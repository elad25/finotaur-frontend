## In plain words

**Inflation** means prices are rising, so each dollar buys less than it did before. **Deflation** is the opposite — prices fall, but economic activity often grinds down with them. **Purchasing power** is the real measure: how much goods and services your money can actually buy, regardless of the number printed on the bill.

## Quick demo

In 2000, a gallon of milk cost roughly $2.78. By 2024 that same gallon cost about $4.00. Your dollar did not shrink — but its purchasing power did. If you kept $100 in cash over those 24 years, it still said "$100," yet it could buy about 30% fewer gallons of milk. The number stayed the same; the real value quietly eroded.

## Full explanation

### What causes inflation

Inflation arises from an imbalance between money supply and the output of goods and services.

- **Demand-pull inflation** — too much money chasing too few goods. Classic example: stimulus checks flooding an economy still constrained by supply-chain problems.
- **Cost-push inflation** — rising input costs (oil, wages, raw materials) force producers to charge more. Energy shocks are the textbook case.
- **Built-in (wage-price spiral)** — workers demand higher wages because prices are up; businesses raise prices to cover wages; repeat.

Central banks measure inflation using indices like the CPI (Consumer Price Index) and PCE (Personal Consumption Expenditures). A target of around 2% annual inflation is considered healthy — enough to encourage spending and investment without punishing savers too badly.

### What causes deflation

Deflation sounds like a bargain, but it is often dangerous:

- If prices are expected to keep falling, consumers delay purchases ("why buy today when it's cheaper tomorrow?").
- Corporate revenues fall, leading to layoffs.
- Falling wages shrink demand further — a downward spiral.

Japan's "lost decades" from the 1990s onward illustrate how hard deflation is to escape once entrenched.

### The real purchasing power calculation

A useful approximation: at 3% inflation, prices double in about **24 years** (the Rule of 72: 72 ÷ 3 = 24). At 7% inflation, prices double in about 10 years. This is why holding only cash is not "safe" — it is a slow guaranteed loss of purchasing power.

### Inflation vs. asset classes

Not all assets respond to inflation the same way:

- **Equities** — mixed. Companies with pricing power can pass costs to customers; those without are squeezed.
- **Bonds** — hurt. Fixed coupon payments are worth less in real terms when inflation rises.
- **Real assets** (real estate, commodities, TIPS) — tend to hold up better because their value is linked to physical goods.
- **Gold** — historically seen as a store of value, though the relationship is imperfect.

### Why it matters for a trader or investor

Inflation is not just a macroeconomic abstraction. It directly affects interest rates (the Fed raises rates to fight inflation), which affects bond prices, equity valuations (higher discount rates compress P/E multiples), and currency strength. Watching the monthly CPI print or the PCE deflator is, for a serious market participant, as important as watching earnings season.
