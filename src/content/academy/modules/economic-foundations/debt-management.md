## In plain words

**Debt** is borrowed money that must be repaid with interest. Not all debt is harmful — **good debt** finances something that appreciates in value or increases your earning power (a mortgage on a property, a student loan for a high-return field). **Bad debt** finances consumption that disappears, at high interest rates (credit cards, buy-now-pay-later). **Personal leverage** is the ratio of what you owe to what you own, and it amplifies both gains and losses — exactly like leverage in trading.

## Quick demo

Two people each have $50,000 in net worth. Person A has no debt. Person B owns a $300,000 rental property with a $250,000 mortgage — net equity also $50,000. If the property rises 10% to $330,000, Person B's equity jumps to $80,000 — a 60% return on their equity, not 10%. But if the property falls 10% to $270,000, Person B's equity collapses to $20,000 — a 60% loss. Leverage is not magic; it magnifies outcomes in both directions.

## Full explanation

### The spectrum from good to bad debt

Debt quality is determined by three factors: the **interest rate**, the **asset purchased**, and the **cash flow** the debt generates.

| Debt type | Typical rate | Asset value | Verdict |
|---|---|---|---|
| Mortgage (primary home) | 5–7% | Appreciates slowly | Generally acceptable |
| Student loan (STEM, medicine) | 4–8% | Raises future income | Potentially good |
| Car loan | 5–10% | Depreciates immediately | Neutral to poor |
| Credit card | 18–29% | Buys consumption | Almost always bad |
| Payday loan | 300–400%+ | Nothing | Avoid entirely |

### The debt avalanche vs. debt snowball

Two popular repayment strategies:

- **Avalanche** — pay minimums on all debts; throw extra money at the highest-interest debt first. Mathematically optimal: minimizes total interest paid.
- **Snowball** — pay minimums on all debts; throw extra money at the smallest balance first. Psychologically effective: quick wins build momentum.

Research shows the snowball performs slightly worse mathematically but better behaviorally — people who use it are more likely to actually complete repayment. Choose based on your own psychology.

### Debt-to-income ratio (DTI)

Lenders assess your **debt-to-income ratio**: total monthly debt payments divided by gross monthly income. Conventional mortgage underwriting typically requires DTI below 43%; the best rates often require below 36%. Your own target should be lower than what a lender will approve — just because a bank offers you more does not mean more is wise.

### The opportunity cost of debt

Every dollar spent on interest is a dollar not compounding in investments. A credit card balance of $8,000 at 22% APR costs $1,760 in interest per year. Eliminating that debt is equivalent to earning a guaranteed 22% return — which no index fund reliably delivers. High-interest debt repayment is almost always the highest-certainty investment available.

### Leverage in personal finance vs. trading

Margin in a brokerage account and a mortgage work the same way mechanically: borrowed capital magnifies exposure. The difference is control. With a mortgage, the bank does not issue a margin call if your home drops 20% — you can hold through the dip. With trading margin, a broker can liquidate your position at the worst moment. Understanding this distinction matters when deciding how much leverage belongs in each area of your financial life.

### Why it matters for a trader or investor

Carrying high-interest consumer debt while simultaneously trying to grow a trading account is a mathematical contradiction — the drag on one side almost always exceeds the returns on the other. Beyond the numbers, debt-induced financial stress impairs judgment exactly when clear thinking is most needed. Cleaning up the balance sheet is not a prerequisite only for beginners; it is a recurring discipline for anyone serious about compounding wealth over time.
