## In plain words

When you profit from an investment, the government typically takes a share. **Capital gains** are profits from selling an asset for more than you paid. **Dividends** are payments companies make to shareholders from their profits. Tax rates differ based on how long you held the asset, what type of account it is in, and your overall income level — which is why the **after-tax return** is the only return that actually ends up in your pocket.

## Quick demo

You buy 100 shares of a stock at $50 ($5,000 total) and sell at $80 ($8,000 total) — a $3,000 gain. If you held for 8 months (short-term), that $3,000 is taxed as ordinary income — potentially at 22–37% depending on your bracket, meaning a tax bill of $660–$1,110. If you held for 14 months (long-term), the same $3,000 is taxed at 0%, 15%, or 20% depending on income — potentially just $450. Same trade, same profit, but holding longer can save hundreds of dollars.

## Full explanation

### Short-term vs. long-term capital gains

The most important distinction in US investment taxation:

- **Short-term capital gains** — assets held 1 year or less. Taxed as **ordinary income** at your marginal rate (10%–37% in 2024).
- **Long-term capital gains** — assets held more than 1 year. Taxed at **preferential rates**: 0% (income under ~$47,000 for single filers), 15% (most taxpayers), or 20% (high earners).

This creates a direct tax incentive for longer holding periods. For an active trader who turns over positions frequently, the short-term rate is a persistent headwind that needs to be overcome by the strategy's edge.

### Wash-sale rule

If you sell a position at a loss and buy back the same or substantially identical security within 30 days before or after the sale, the IRS disallows the loss deduction. The loss is not gone — it is added to the cost basis of the repurchased position — but you cannot use it to offset gains in that tax year. Active traders must track wash sales carefully.

### Dividend taxation

Dividends come in two flavors:

- **Qualified dividends** — paid by US corporations (and some foreign ones) on stock held long enough. Taxed at long-term capital gains rates (0/15/20%).
- **Ordinary dividends** — paid by REITs, money-market funds, and some other sources. Taxed as ordinary income.

Knowing which type a stock pays matters for calculating your true after-tax yield.

### Tax-advantaged accounts

The single biggest legal tool for reducing investment taxes is using the right account type:

- **401(k) / Traditional IRA** — contributions may be pre-tax; growth is tax-deferred; withdrawals taxed as ordinary income in retirement. Best for those expecting a lower tax bracket in retirement.
- **Roth IRA / Roth 401(k)** — contributions are after-tax; growth and qualified withdrawals are tax-free. Best for those expecting a higher bracket in retirement or who want tax-free growth.
- **HSA (Health Savings Account)** — triple tax advantage (pre-tax contributions, tax-free growth, tax-free withdrawals for medical expenses). Often overlooked.
- **Taxable brokerage account** — no special treatment; all capital gains and dividends taxed annually or at sale.

### Net Investment Income Tax (NIIT)

High earners (above $200,000 single / $250,000 married) pay an additional 3.8% Medicare surcharge on net investment income. This raises the effective top rate on long-term capital gains to 23.8% and on short-term gains to 40.8%.

### Why it matters for a trader or investor

Tax drag is one of the largest and most controllable expenses in a trading or investing practice. An active trader paying short-term rates on every gain needs to consistently outperform a buy-and-hold investor just to break even after taxes — before considering transaction costs. Tax-loss harvesting (deliberately realizing losses to offset gains), holding period management, and account selection are legitimate, legal tools that compound meaningfully over decades. After-tax return is the only number that matters; treating it as an afterthought is expensive.
