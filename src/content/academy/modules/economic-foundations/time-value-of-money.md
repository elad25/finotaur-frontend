## In plain words

The **time value of money** is the principle that a dollar available today is worth more than a dollar promised in the future. The reason is opportunity: money in hand right now can be invested, earning a return. So when you compare future sums to present ones, you must account for what that money could have earned in the meantime — the **discount rate**.

## Quick demo

Someone offers you a choice: $10,000 today or $10,000 three years from now. If you can invest at 7% per year, the $10,000 today becomes roughly $12,250 in three years. The future offer is therefore worth only about $8,163 in today's terms ($10,000 ÷ 1.07^3). Taking the money now is objectively better, and by a measurable amount.

## Full explanation

### Present value and future value

Two core calculations flow from this principle:

**Future Value (FV)** — what a sum today will grow into:
> FV = PV × (1 + r)^n

**Present Value (PV)** — what a future sum is worth in today's dollars:
> PV = FV ÷ (1 + r)^n

Where **r** is the discount (or interest) rate and **n** is the number of periods.

These two formulas are mirrors of each other. Master them and you can evaluate almost any financial decision.

### The discount rate — where the judgment lives

The discount rate is not one universal number; it reflects the **opportunity cost** of capital and the **risk** of the cash flows:

- A US Treasury bond uses a very low rate — cash flows are nearly certain.
- A startup's projected revenues might use a 20–30% rate — there is real chance those revenues never materialize.
- Your personal discount rate might be your mortgage rate, your expected market return, or whatever alternative use of the money looks most realistic.

Choosing the wrong discount rate distorts every financial comparison. This is the art hidden inside the math.

### Net Present Value (NPV)

When a decision involves multiple cash flows over time, you discount each one back to today and add them up. This sum is the **Net Present Value**.

- NPV > 0: the investment creates value (in present-dollar terms, you get back more than you put in).
- NPV < 0: the investment destroys value.
- NPV = 0: you earn exactly your required rate — the break-even of time.

NPV is how companies evaluate capital projects, how bond prices are set, and how private equity values a business.

### Annuities and perpetuities

Sometimes cash flows recur regularly:

- **Annuity**: a fixed payment for a finite number of periods (think: mortgage, lease).
- **Perpetuity**: a fixed payment forever (some preferred stocks, infrastructure bonds).

The present value of a perpetuity is simply: **PV = Payment ÷ r**. At a 5% discount rate, a $100 annual payment forever is worth $2,000 today.

### Why it matters for a trader or investor

The time value of money is not just accounting theory — it is the engine of valuation. When a stock's price-to-earnings multiple expands or contracts, the market is repricing what those future earnings are worth today, which is a direct function of interest rates. When the Fed raises rates, the discount rate rises, and future cash flows are worth less — which is exactly why growth stocks (whose value sits mostly in distant future earnings) tend to fall the hardest in rising-rate environments. Every DCF model, every bond price, every option premium embeds this concept. Understanding it at the gut level gives you a framework for judging whether any price is cheap or expensive in absolute terms.
