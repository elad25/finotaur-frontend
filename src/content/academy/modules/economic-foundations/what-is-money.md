## In plain words

Money is just a tool we all agreed to trust. It does three jobs: it lets you
**buy things** (a medium of exchange), it lets you **measure value** (a unit of
account), and it lets you **store value for later** (a store of value). Anything
that does all three well — shells, gold, paper notes, digital balances — can be
money. What makes modern money work isn't gold in a vault; it's shared belief.

## Quick demo

Imagine a fisherman who wants bread. Without money, he has to find a baker who
happens to want fish *right now* — a "double coincidence of wants." Money breaks
that trap: the fisherman sells fish to anyone for $10, then buys bread from the
baker with that $10. The baker never had to want fish. That single invention is
what lets a complex economy exist.

## Full explanation

### The three functions

Every form of money is judged by how well it performs three roles:

- **Medium of exchange** — it's accepted in trade, so you don't need barter.
- **Unit of account** — prices are quoted in it, so value is comparable.
- **Store of value** — it holds purchasing power over time, so you can save.

Gold was good money for thousands of years because it was durable, divisible,
portable, and scarce. The problem was practicality: you can't easily pay for
coffee with gold dust.

### From commodity money to fiat

For most of history, money was **commodity money** (it had value in itself) or
**representative money** (paper that could be redeemed for gold). Since 1971,
the world runs on **fiat money** — currency that is legal tender by government
decree, backed not by metal but by the credibility of the issuing state and its
central bank.

This is the key shift to understand: a modern dollar has value because the
economy that uses it is productive and the central bank manages its supply
responsibly. Trust replaced gold. That trust is powerful — and, as inflation
shows, it can erode.

### Why this matters for a trader

Once you see money as *trust priced in real time*, currencies, bonds, and even
gold stop looking like separate topics. A weakening dollar, a central bank
printing money, and a rising gold price are three views of the same question:
**how much is this unit of trust worth today?** Every market you will ever trade
is, at bottom, denominated in money — so understanding what money *is* is the
first foundation, not an optional one.
