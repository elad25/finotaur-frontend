## In plain words

The **psychology of money** is the study of how emotions, cognitive shortcuts, and social pressures shape financial decisions — often in ways that are irrational, predictable, and costly. We do not make money decisions with a calculator; we make them with a brain wired for survival in a world that no longer exists. Understanding the biases underneath every decision is the first step to overriding them.

## Quick demo

In a study, participants were asked to choose between two options: (A) a guaranteed $900 or (B) a 90% chance of winning $1,000. Most people chose (A) despite option B having a slightly higher expected value ($900 vs. $900). Then they were asked: (C) a certain loss of $900 or (D) a 90% chance of losing $1,000. Most switched to (D). The math is identical, but the framing of "losing" made people suddenly prefer a riskier option. This inconsistency — driven by **loss aversion** — governs countless trading decisions every day.

## Full explanation

### Loss aversion

Studies by Daniel Kahneman and Amos Tversky established that losses feel roughly twice as painful as equivalent gains feel pleasurable. A $500 loss hurts more than a $500 gain delights. In trading, this manifests as:

- Holding losing positions too long ("it's not a loss until I sell")
- Cutting winning positions too early to lock in the good feeling
- Refusing to take necessary losses because it makes the failure "real"

### Overconfidence bias

Most people rate themselves as above-average drivers, above-average investors, and above-average judges of risk. Statistically this is impossible. Overconfidence in markets leads to:

- **Over-trading** — believing your analysis is better than the market's composite judgment
- **Under-diversification** — concentrating in positions you feel certain about
- **Ignoring base rates** — focusing on why this time is different rather than what the historical odds say

### Confirmation bias

Once you form an opinion on a stock or trade, the brain preferentially notices information that confirms the view and discounts information that challenges it. A bullish trader scrolls past bearish headlines and bookmarks bullish ones — not dishonestly, but automatically. The antidote is actively seeking the opposing case before entering a position.

### Recency bias and availability heuristic

Recent events are weighted too heavily in risk assessment. After a crash, risk feels permanent; after a long bull market, risk feels abstract. The crash of 2008 made investors excessively fearful of equities for years, causing them to miss one of the longest bull markets in history. The heuristic: "how easily can I recall a bad outcome?" is mistaken for "how likely is a bad outcome?"

### Anchoring

The first number you see becomes an anchor that distorts all subsequent judgments. If a stock was at $200 and is now at $120, many investors call it "cheap" purely because of the anchor — ignoring that $120 might still be fundamentally expensive. Traders anchor to their entry price and assess gains and losses relative to it rather than relative to current fair value.

### Social proof and herd behavior

Humans are social animals. When everyone around us is buying something, our brain interprets this as evidence that it is a good idea. This is the mechanism behind bubbles: prices rise, which attracts attention, which attracts buyers, which drives prices higher — until the fundamentals collapse. The crowd is often right in the middle of a trend and catastrophically wrong at the extremes.

### Mental accounting

People treat money differently depending on where it came from or what it is labeled for. "House money" (profits from a recent trade) is spent more freely than "earned money." A tax refund gets treated as a bonus even though it was your own money all along. Mental accounting creates irrational decision trees that a single unified balance sheet would dissolve.

### Why it matters for a trader or investor

Every chapter in this curriculum describes a financial concept. This chapter describes the system that applies them — and routinely gets them wrong. Knowing that you are wired to hold losers, cut winners, overtrade, and herd does not make you immune, but it gives you the vocabulary to build rules and processes that protect you from yourself. The most successful trading systems are partly psychological engineering: checklists before entry, pre-defined exits, position sizes set before emotion is involved, and journals that force honest accounting after each trade.
