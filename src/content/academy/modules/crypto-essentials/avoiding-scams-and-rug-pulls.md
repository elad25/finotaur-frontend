## In plain words

Crypto scams cost users billions of dollars every year because blockchain transactions are **irreversible** — once your coins leave your wallet, they cannot be recalled. A **rug pull** is a specific fraud where developers hype a new token, attract investment, then drain the liquidity pool and disappear. Recognizing the patterns before you act is the only protection.

## Quick demo

A Discord server promotes "MoonFrog Token" — limited presale, 10,000% APY, celebrity endorsement, urgency to "buy before the whitelist closes." Tom buys $500 worth. Three days later, the website goes offline, the Discord vanishes, and the token price drops 99.9%. The developers sold their own tokens into Tom's buy pressure and walked away with the liquidity. Tom has no legal recourse because the token was unregulated and the developers were anonymous.

## Full explanation

### Common scam types

**Rug pull**
A development team creates a token, drives hype (social media, influencer shills, fake partnerships), attracts buyers, and then removes all the liquidity at once — leaving holders with a worthless token they cannot sell. Common on decentralized exchanges where anyone can list a token with no vetting.

**Pump and dump**
A group accumulates a coin cheaply, floods social media with hype to attract buyers, drives up the price, then sells their entire position. Late buyers are left holding losses.

**Phishing**
Fake websites, Discord bots, and emails that mimic legitimate platforms. They ask you to "connect your wallet" or enter your seed phrase. Any site asking for your seed phrase is a scam, without exception.

**Romance / "pig butchering" scam**
Someone builds a relationship online over weeks or months, then introduces a "hot investment opportunity." The platform shows fake gains to encourage larger deposits. Withdrawals are blocked until you "pay taxes" or "fees" — which are further theft.

**Fake customer support**
Scammers impersonate exchange or wallet support in Twitter/X replies, Telegram, and Discord. They ask for your seed phrase or remote access to your screen. No legitimate company ever asks for either.

**Airdrop scams**
A wallet interaction or social media post claims you received free tokens. Claiming them requires signing a malicious transaction that grants the scammer access to drain your entire wallet.

### Red flags

- **Promises of guaranteed returns** — no investment guarantees profit
- **Extreme APY** (1,000%+) — only possible through hyperinflation of the token or outright fraud
- **Anonymous team with no verifiable identity or track record**
- **No smart contract audit** from a reputable third party
- **Locked liquidity with no expiry** — or worse, no locked liquidity at all
- **Urgency and artificial scarcity** — "buy now before it's too late"
- **Unsolicited contact** offering investment tips
- **Celebrity endorsements** (most are either fake or paid promotions by the scammer)

### How to protect yourself

- Never enter your seed phrase anywhere, ever
- Use a dedicated browser or device for DeFi interactions
- Verify URLs letter by letter — scammers use near-identical domains
- Research the team: are they public? Do they have a verifiable history?
- Check token contract on-chain: is liquidity locked? Is there a mint function the owner can exploit?
- Start with tiny amounts when interacting with new protocols

### Why it matters

The irreversibility of blockchain is a feature that becomes a vulnerability when you are deceived. Unlike a credit card charge, there is no dispute process. Your best defense is skepticism as a default setting: assume everything new is a scam until proven otherwise, and let time and research do the work of proof.
