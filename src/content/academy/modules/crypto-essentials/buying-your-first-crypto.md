## In plain words

Buying crypto for the first time means converting regular money into a digital asset held on a **blockchain**. You do this through an **exchange** — a platform that matches buyers and sellers. The whole process takes about 15 minutes, but the decisions you make on day one (which exchange, how much, which coin) affect your safety and returns for years.

## Quick demo

Maria decides to buy $100 of Bitcoin. She signs up for Coinbase, verifies her identity with a passport photo, links her bank account, and places a market order for BTC. Within two minutes she owns 0.0014 BTC — the exact amount depends on the price at the moment she clicks buy. Her coins sit in Coinbase's custody until she decides to move them.

## Full explanation

### Step 1: Decide what you're buying

Most beginners start with **Bitcoin (BTC)** or **Ethereum (ETH)** — they are the most established, most liquid, and most widely covered. Avoid jumping straight into unknown altcoins; they carry far higher risk of collapse and fraud.

### Step 2: Pick a regulated exchange

Choose a platform that:
- Is **regulated** in your country (licensed, has legal obligations to users)
- Has **proof of reserves** — assets it holds on behalf of users
- Offers **two-factor authentication (2FA)** by default
- Has a clear history — avoid new or obscure exchanges

Well-known examples include Coinbase, Kraken, and Gemini. Always check that the exchange is available and licensed where you live.

### Step 3: Complete identity verification (KYC)

Regulated exchanges require **Know Your Customer (KYC)** verification — a government ID and sometimes a selfie. This is normal and legally required. It protects you and the platform. Expect it to take minutes to hours.

### Step 4: Fund your account

You can fund with:
- **Bank transfer (ACH/wire)** — slowest, cheapest fees
- **Debit card** — fast, but higher fees (often 1–3%)
- **Credit card** — risky; you pay interest on a volatile asset

Start with an amount you can afford to lose entirely. Crypto can drop 50–80% in a bear market.

### Step 5: Place your first order

- **Market order** — buys immediately at the current price. Good for small amounts.
- **Limit order** — lets you set the exact price you want to pay. Better for larger amounts.

Check the fee schedule before you confirm. Many exchanges charge 0.5–1.5% per trade for beginners.

### Step 6: Understand what you now own

After buying, you hold crypto **in the exchange's custody** — the exchange controls the actual keys. This is fine for getting started, but for any meaningful amount you should eventually move to **self-custody** (see the Self-Custody chapter). Think of the exchange wallet as a bank: convenient, but you're trusting someone else.

### Why it matters

Getting the first buy right builds the right habits: use a regulated platform, verify identity properly, start small, and understand what custody means. Most costly crypto mistakes happen in the first 30 days when people skip steps or trust flashy promotions. A slow, deliberate start is the foundation of a safe crypto journey.
