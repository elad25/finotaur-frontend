## In plain words

A **crypto exchange** is the platform where you buy, sell, and sometimes store digital assets. Not all exchanges are equal — they differ in regulation, fees, supported coins, security track record, and ease of use. Choosing the wrong one can cost you money in hidden fees or, in the worst case, expose your funds to theft or insolvency.

## Quick demo

James compares two exchanges. Exchange A charges a 0.1% trading fee but has thin liquidity on small altcoins, which means his $500 order slips by 0.4% from the listed price. Exchange B charges 0.5% but fills his order exactly. On a $500 trade Exchange A costs James $2.50 in stated fees plus $2 in slippage; Exchange B costs $2.50 flat. The "cheaper" exchange actually cost more.

## Full explanation

### Centralized (CEX) vs. Decentralized (DEX)

- **Centralized exchanges (CEX)** — a company holds your funds and matches orders. Examples: Coinbase, Kraken, Binance. Regulated, easier to use, can freeze accounts, require KYC.
- **Decentralized exchanges (DEX)** — smart contracts match trades directly from your own wallet. Examples: Uniswap, dYdX. No KYC, you keep custody, but they are more complex and have no customer support.

For beginners, a reputable CEX is the right starting point.

### What to evaluate

**Regulation and licensing**
- Is the exchange licensed in your jurisdiction?
- Does it comply with anti-money-laundering rules?
- Is user-fund custody verified (proof of reserves)?

A licensed exchange has legal obligations to you. An unlicensed one has none.

**Security track record**
- Has it been hacked? How did it respond?
- Does it hold the majority of funds in **cold storage** (offline)?
- Does it offer 2FA and withdrawal address whitelisting?

**Fee structure**
- **Trading fees** — maker/taker model; varies 0.05–1.5%
- **Deposit fees** — bank transfers are usually free; cards are not
- **Withdrawal fees** — fixed per-network fee; compare across platforms
- **Spread** — on some exchanges the displayed price already includes a markup

**Liquidity**
- High trading volume means tighter spreads and less slippage
- Low liquidity = your order moves the price against you

**Supported assets and regions**
- Not all coins are available everywhere
- Some exchanges restrict users in certain countries
- Check before signing up, not after

**Customer support and reputation**
- Look for an established track record (5+ years)
- Check independent reviews; search "exchange name + withdrawal issues"
- Avoid exchanges with unresolved insolvency complaints

### Red flags to avoid

- No physical address or unclear ownership
- Unrealistically high interest on deposits ("earn 20% APY on USDT")
- Pressure to deposit quickly for a "bonus"
- No working withdrawal process (the single biggest danger sign)

### The practical shortlist

For most users in most countries, starting with one of the top five exchanges by regulated volume is the safest move. Prioritize regulation and security over slightly lower fees — a 0.1% fee difference is trivial compared to the risk of losing access to your funds.

Choosing carefully means you spend your energy trading, not chasing customer support for a frozen account.
