## In plain words

A **hot wallet** is a crypto wallet connected to the internet — easy to use, but exposed to online attacks. A **cold wallet** stores your private keys completely offline, making remote theft nearly impossible. The tradeoff is straightforward: **hot = convenient, cold = secure**. Where you store your crypto should match the risk you are willing to accept.

## Quick demo

David keeps $200 of ETH in MetaMask (a hot wallet) for paying gas fees and interacting with apps — he needs it accessible daily. His $8,000 in Bitcoin sits on a Ledger hardware wallet in a drawer. When a phishing attack compromises his browser and drains his MetaMask, he loses $200. His $8,000 is untouched because the Ledger never connected to the internet.

## Full explanation

### Hot wallets

**Hot wallets** are any wallet where the private key is stored on a device that connects to the internet:
- Browser extensions (MetaMask, Phantom)
- Mobile apps (Trust Wallet, Coinbase Wallet)
- Exchange wallets (funds held by the exchange, not truly "yours")
- Desktop applications

**Advantages:**
- Instant access for transactions
- Required for DeFi, NFTs, and on-chain applications
- Easy to set up with no hardware purchase

**Disadvantages:**
- Vulnerable to malware, phishing, and browser exploits
- If your device is compromised, your funds may be at risk
- Software bugs in the wallet can expose keys

Hot wallets are appropriate for small amounts you need to use regularly — think of them as a cash wallet you carry in your pocket.

### Cold wallets

**Cold wallets** store private keys on a device or medium that never connects to the internet:

- **Hardware wallets** (Ledger, Trezor, Coldcard) — dedicated physical devices with secure chips. The key is generated and stored on the device; transactions are signed offline and broadcast only the signed output.
- **Paper wallets** — a printed copy of a key or seed phrase. Simple but fragile; vulnerable to fire, water, and physical theft.
- **Steel/metal backups** — seed phrase stamped into metal plates. More durable than paper; purely for backup, not for signing.
- **Air-gapped computers** — an offline computer that has never connected to the internet, used only for key storage and signing. Used by very large holders.

**Advantages:**
- Private key never touches the internet
- Immune to remote hacking
- The best protection for significant holdings

**Disadvantages:**
- Slightly slower to use (need to connect the device to sign a transaction)
- Hardware wallets cost $60–$200
- If you lose both the device and your seed phrase backup, the funds are gone

### Practical allocation

A sensible approach for most holders:

| Amount | Storage |
|---|---|
| Small spending amount | Hot wallet (mobile or browser) |
| Moderate holdings | Reputable exchange with 2FA |
| Significant savings | Hardware (cold) wallet |

### The custody spectrum

There is no single right answer — only the right answer for your situation. The key insight is that **convenience and security are inversely related** in crypto storage. Decide consciously how much of each you need, not by default. Moving crypto to cold storage is one of the highest-impact security steps you can take, and it is not technically difficult.
