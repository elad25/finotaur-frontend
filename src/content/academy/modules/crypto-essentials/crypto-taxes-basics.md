## In plain words

In most countries, crypto is treated as **property** for tax purposes — not currency. This means nearly every time you sell, swap, or spend crypto, you trigger a potential **taxable event**. The gain or loss is calculated from the difference between what you paid (your **cost basis**) and what you received. Failing to track this leads to expensive surprises at tax time.

## Quick demo

Alex buys 1 ETH for $1,800. Six months later he swaps it for a different token when ETH is worth $2,400. In most jurisdictions, he has realized a **$600 capital gain** — even though he never touched his bank account. If he had held longer than 12 months before selling, many tax systems would apply a lower **long-term capital gains rate** instead. Alex needs to record both the buy price and the swap date/price to file correctly.

## Full explanation

### Why crypto creates taxable events

Tax authorities in most major economies (US, UK, EU, Australia, Canada, and others) classify crypto as a **capital asset**. Taxable events occur when you:
- Sell crypto for fiat currency (dollars, euros, etc.)
- Swap one crypto for another (ETH → BTC counts as a sale of ETH)
- Spend crypto on goods or services (paying with BTC is a disposal)
- Receive crypto as income (mining rewards, staking yield, airdrops, freelance payment)

### What is NOT typically taxable (consult your local rules)
- Buying crypto with fiat and holding it
- Transferring your own crypto between wallets you control
- In some jurisdictions: receiving crypto as a gift (though gifting may be taxable for the giver)

*Tax rules vary significantly by country. This chapter explains general principles; always verify the rules in your jurisdiction and consult a qualified tax professional for your specific situation.*

### Key concepts

**Cost basis**
The amount you originally paid for the asset, including any fees. If you buy 0.5 BTC for $10,000 plus $50 in exchange fees, your cost basis is $10,050.

**Capital gain / loss**
> Proceeds (sale price) minus cost basis = gain or loss

A gain adds to taxable income. A loss may be deductible against gains (rules vary by jurisdiction).

**Short-term vs. long-term**
Many tax systems apply lower rates to assets held longer than 12 months. Short-term gains are often taxed as ordinary income — the same as a salary. Holding longer can significantly reduce your tax bill.

**Income events**
Staking rewards, mining income, and crypto received for services are typically taxed as **ordinary income** at the value on the date received, not as capital gains.

### Record-keeping

You need to track for every transaction:
- Date of acquisition
- Amount and asset purchased
- Cost in fiat at time of purchase (including fees)
- Date of disposal
- Proceeds in fiat at time of disposal

Exchanges provide transaction history exports. Use them — do not rely on memory. Crypto tax software (Koinly, CoinTracker, TaxBit) can import exchange data and calculate your gains automatically, which is almost essential once you have more than a handful of trades.

### Common mistakes

- **Ignoring swaps** — "I didn't cash out" is not a defense if you swapped tokens
- **Losing records** — blockchain history is permanent, but off-chain fee records may not be
- **Mixing wallets** — makes cost-basis calculation difficult; keep a record of transfers between your own wallets
- **Not reporting** — tax authorities increasingly receive data from exchanges; non-disclosure is a growing risk

### Why it matters

The tax obligation on crypto is real and increasingly enforced. Understanding the basic structure — property, cost basis, disposal events — lets you plan intelligently: timing sales, offsetting losses, and keeping records from the start. Clean records kept in real time cost minutes; reconstructing them years later can cost days, and errors can cost penalties.
