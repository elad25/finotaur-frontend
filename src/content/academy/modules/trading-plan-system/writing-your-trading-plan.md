## In plain words

A **written trading plan** is a document — not a mental note — that records every rule governing your trading before the market opens. It covers what you trade, when you trade it, how you enter, where you exit, and how much you risk. Writing it forces precision: vague ideas that sound reasonable in your head often fall apart when you try to commit them to paper. A plan that exists only in your mind is a plan you can silently rewrite whenever a trade goes against you.

## Quick demo

A day trader writes: "I trade ES futures between 9:30 and 11:00 AM only. Entry condition: price reclaims VWAP after an opening drive down, with a bullish imbalance on the 3-minute chart. Stop: below the swing low of the reclaim candle. Target: 2× the stop distance. Max two trades per session. If I hit my daily loss limit of 1.5% of account, I close the platform." That paragraph replaces a hundred in-the-moment decisions — market conditions, sizing, entry trigger, risk rule, and quitting criteria are all answered before she touches a button.

## Full explanation

### What goes in a trading plan

A complete trading plan covers six areas. Every area must be specific enough that someone else could execute it without asking you a single question.

#### 1. Markets and instruments
- Which markets will you trade? (equities, futures, forex, crypto)
- Which specific instruments? (a watchlist, a fixed set of contracts, a sector)
- Which you will never trade? (illiquid names, binary-event days, instruments you do not understand)

#### 2. Market conditions
- What broader environment must exist for you to trade your setup? (trending market, above or below a key moving average, certain volatility range)
- What conditions cause you to stand aside entirely? (earnings week, FOMC day, choppy range-bound tape)

#### 3. Entry rules
- Exactly what technical or fundamental condition triggers an entry?
- Is there a confirmation requirement (volume, time, second timeframe agreement)?
- Where is the precise entry price or zone?

#### 4. Exit rules
- Where is your stop loss — and is it a hard stop in the market or a mental one? (Hard stops are almost always better.)
- Where is your profit target?
- Are there partial-exit rules (take half off at 1R, let the rest run)?
- Is there a time-based exit if neither target nor stop is hit?

#### 5. Position sizing
- What percentage of your account do you risk per trade? (Most experienced traders use 0.5%–2%.)
- How do you calculate share or contract size from your account size and stop distance?
- Do you size differently in different market conditions?

#### 6. Daily and session rules
- Maximum loss per day before you stop trading
- Maximum number of trades per session
- Pre-market routine required before trading
- Post-market review required after trading

### Format and maintenance

The plan does not need to be long. A single well-organized page is better than a twenty-page document you never read. Keep it where you can see it while trading. Review it before every session. When you break a rule, note it in your journal — not to punish yourself, but to identify which rules are unclear or which psychological triggers cause the most violations.

### The honesty test

Before finalizing any rule, ask: "Could I actually follow this in a fast-moving market, under pressure?" If a rule requires perfect information or lightning judgment, rewrite it so a slightly slower, more cautious version of you can execute it reliably. The best trading plan is the one you actually follow, not the one that describes ideal behavior.

A written trading plan is what separates the trader who learns from experience from the one who merely accumulates it.
