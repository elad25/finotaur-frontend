## In plain words

**Reviewing and improving your system** is the ongoing practice of using real trade data to deliberately update your plan — removing what does not work, refining what does, and adapting when market conditions change. A trading system is not finished when you first write it; it is a living document that evolves through evidence. The traders who become consistently profitable are not those who found the perfect system on day one — they are those who built a reliable process for improving an imperfect one.

## Quick demo

After three months of live trading, a momentum trader reviews his monthly statistics. His long setups have a positive expectancy of +0.9R per trade, but his short setups average −0.3R — a persistent drag. He looks at the conditions: every losing short was taken in a broadly bullish market environment. He adds one rule to his plan: "No short trades when the broad market index is above its 50-day moving average." He runs the change against his last six months of data — the short-side results flip to slightly positive. He implements the rule. Next quarter, his overall expectancy improves by 0.4R per trade. That is systematic improvement.

## Full explanation

### The improvement cycle

Systematic improvement follows a four-step cycle that repeats indefinitely:

1. **Collect** — trade data, rule adherence logs, journal entries
2. **Analyze** — identify what is working, what is not, and why
3. **Hypothesize** — form a specific, testable change ("if I add condition X, setup Y will perform better")
4. **Test** — backtest the change on historical data, then forward-test before applying live

This cycle prevents two opposite failure modes: changing nothing (stagnation) and changing everything at once (chaos). One change at a time, with evidence, is the operating principle.

### What is worth changing

Not every poor result requires a rule change. Markets have variance. A losing month in a strategy with positive expectancy is expected and says nothing about whether the strategy should be modified. The question is not "did I lose?" — it is "did I lose while following the rules?"

**Change your system when:**
- A rule is consistently broken because it is ambiguous or impossible to execute in live conditions
- A specific setup or condition has underperformed across a statistically meaningful sample (30+ trades)
- Market structure has fundamentally shifted (a strategy built on trending behavior may need adaptation in a range-bound regime)
- Your journal reveals a repeating behavioral pattern that a rule change could prevent

**Do not change your system when:**
- You are in a losing streak within normal statistical variance
- A single trade or a single week performed poorly
- You feel emotional about recent losses (this is the worst time to revise rules)

### How to evaluate a proposed change

Before implementing any rule change, apply these tests:

- **Historical check** — does the change improve results on at least one year of historical data, including out-of-sample periods?
- **Logic check** — does the change make sense for a reason that is independent of the data? A change supported by both evidence and logic is far more robust than one supported by data fitting alone.
- **Complexity check** — does the change add a rule that is genuinely useful, or does it simply add complexity? More rules create more opportunities for confusion and rule-breaking.

### When to discard a strategy entirely

Sometimes a strategy reaches the end of its useful life. Market structure changes, an edge becomes crowded, or the conditions that created the opportunity no longer exist. Signs that a strategy may be finished rather than in a fixable drawdown:

- Performance has declined consistently across multiple market regimes over 6–12 months
- The underlying logic depends on a market behavior that no longer appears in current data
- Even after targeted rule changes, no configuration produces a positive expectancy on recent data

Discarding a strategy is not failure — it is a rational response to evidence. The skills built in developing, testing, and trading one system transfer directly to building the next one, at a fraction of the original time cost.

### The long game

Systematic review and improvement compound over time in the same way that financial returns do. A trader who makes three small, evidence-based improvements per year is operating with a fundamentally different system after five years — not through a single breakthrough, but through accumulated precision. The discipline to improve deliberately rather than react emotionally is, ultimately, the skill that separates traders who last from those who do not.
