## In plain words

**Entry rules** define the exact conditions that must be met before you place a trade. **Exit rules** define exactly when and where you leave — both when the trade works and when it does not. Together they remove the two most destructive questions in trading: "Should I get in?" and "Should I get out?" When rules pre-answer those questions, you execute a process instead of making emotional decisions under pressure.

## Quick demo

A trader's entry rule states: "I buy only when price breaks above the prior day's high, volume is above the 10-day average, and the broad market index is above its 20-day moving average." His exit rules state: "Stop loss is placed 0.5% below the breakout level. First target is 1.5× the stop distance — sell half there. Second target is 3× — sell the remainder, or trail a stop if the move extends." Every trade he takes follows the same logic. Some lose, some win — but over hundreds of trades, he knows whether the system works.

## Full explanation

### Why entry rules matter

Entry is the easiest part to get right and the easiest to abuse. A good entry has three properties:

- **Specificity** — it describes an exact condition, not a direction ("buy dips"). Specific: "buy the first pullback to the 8 EMA after a breakout candle closes above the prior swing high."
- **Falsifiability** — you can look at a chart and say definitively whether the condition is met or not.
- **Replicability** — you would take the same trade at 9:45 AM on a Monday and 2:15 PM on a Friday, given the same conditions.

Vague entries create discretionary chaos. Precise entries create a testable system.

### Entry triggers and confirmations

Most robust entry rules combine two elements:

- **Setup** — the broader condition that puts the trade on your radar (e.g., a stock holding above a key moving average after an upside gap)
- **Trigger** — the specific event that causes you to act (e.g., a bullish candle closing above the high of the prior 15-minute bar)

Requiring a trigger prevents you from entering on anticipation alone, which is one of the most common and costly entry mistakes.

### Why exit rules matter more than entries

Entries determine which trades you are in. Exits determine how much money you make or lose. Most traders spend 90% of their time on entries and almost none on exits — which is exactly backwards.

There are three types of exits every plan needs:

#### Stop loss — the protection exit
This is non-negotiable and must be placed as soon as you enter. A stop loss that exists only in your mind will move. Hard stops in the market do not. Place it at a level where the trade idea is proven wrong — not at a level that feels emotionally comfortable. The difference matters: stops based on risk tolerance rather than market structure almost always get hit unnecessarily.

#### Profit target — the reward exit
A defined target enforces discipline and lets you calculate risk/reward before entering. Common approaches: a fixed multiple of the stop distance (e.g., 2R or 3R), a prior resistance level, or a measured move from the pattern. Pre-defining the target prevents the trap of holding a winner until it becomes a loser.

#### Trailing stop or time exit — the adaptive exit
Some strategies benefit from letting winners run beyond the initial target. A trailing stop (moved up as price rises) captures extended moves while protecting gains. A time-based exit removes trades that are not working within a defined window — "if the trade hasn't moved in my direction within 30 minutes, I exit at market."

### The discipline of following exits

The most common rule-breaking in trading happens at exits: moving a stop "just this once," not taking the target because it "feels like it could go further," or refusing to exit a loser because "it will come back." Every one of these impulses has a rational-sounding justification. None of them survive statistical scrutiny.

Pre-defined, tested exit rules enforce the mathematical expectancy that makes your system work. Break them and you are trading a different, untested system — one with an unknown expectancy that is probably negative.
