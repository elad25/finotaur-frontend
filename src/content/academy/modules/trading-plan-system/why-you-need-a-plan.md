## In plain words

A **trading plan** is a written set of rules that tells you exactly what to trade, when to enter, when to exit, and how much to risk — before the market opens. Without one, every decision gets made in the heat of the moment, where emotion beats logic almost every time. The difference between trading and gambling is not the market you use; it is whether you have a pre-defined, repeatable process.

## Quick demo

Imagine two traders both buy the same stock. Trader A has no plan — he entered because it "looked strong" and has no idea where he will exit. When it drops 5%, he panics and sells. When it bounces, he buys back higher, chasing it. Trader B entered at a specific level, with a stop at a defined risk point and a target at 2× that risk. She follows her rules. Same stock, same day — completely different outcomes, driven entirely by whether a plan existed.

## Full explanation

### Why emotion is the enemy

Markets are designed to trigger emotion. A fast rally tempts you to chase. A sharp dip tempts you to panic-sell or, worse, average into a loser. These impulses feel logical in the moment and are almost always wrong in aggregate. A trading plan moves the decision to a calm, analytical state — before you have skin in the game.

### What a plan actually does

A trading plan is not a prediction. It does not tell you what the market will do. It tells you:

- **What conditions must be present** before you take a trade
- **Exactly where your stop loss sits** before you enter
- **Where you will take profit** or reduce size
- **How much of your account you are willing to lose** on any single trade
- **Which markets, instruments, and times of day** you will trade

Without these answers written down and tested, you are improvising — and improvised trading is expensive.

### Plans vs. gambling

Gambling is a negative-expectancy game. Even if you win sometimes, the house edge means you lose over time. Trading can be a positive-expectancy game — but only if your edge is real and you execute it consistently. The plan is the mechanism that converts a real edge into consistent execution. Skipping the plan is effectively choosing to gamble.

### The psychological contract

A trading plan also serves as a psychological contract with yourself. When you have a documented rule and you break it, you know it. That awareness — uncomfortable as it is — is data. Over time, traders who maintain a plan discover that most of their losses come not from bad setups but from broken rules: exiting too early, moving a stop, sizing up on a hunch. The plan turns those mistakes from invisible habits into visible patterns you can fix.

### What a plan is not

A plan is not rigid dogma. Markets change, and plans get refined. But they get refined through deliberate review — not in the middle of a trade when your account is moving.

For a trader, having a plan is the single highest-leverage thing you can do before you risk a dollar. Every professional trader has one. The absence of one is the clearest marker that someone is not yet trading — they are speculating.
