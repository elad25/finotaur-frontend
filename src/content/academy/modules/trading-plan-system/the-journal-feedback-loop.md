## In plain words

The **journal feedback loop** is the process of recording every trade, reviewing those records regularly, and using what you find to improve your system and your behavior. A trade journal is not a diary — it is a data collection tool. Without it, you accumulate experience but cannot learn from it systematically. The same mistakes repeat because they are invisible. With a journal, patterns surface: which setups are losing money, which rules you consistently break, and under what conditions your best trading happens.

## Quick demo

After a rough week, a trader reviews her journal from the past month. She filters trades by time of day and immediately spots the problem: every trade she took between 2:00 and 3:00 PM was a loser. Her afternoon trades had a negative expectancy of −0.6R per trade, while her morning trades were positive at +1.2R. Without the journal, she would have concluded she had "a bad week." With it, she has an actionable fix: stop trading in the afternoon. She adds a rule to her plan. Next month is measurably better.

## Full explanation

### Why experience without records is nearly worthless

The human mind is a terrible trade recorder. It remembers winning trades more vividly than losing ones. It distorts the size of winners and the frequency of mistakes. It finds patterns that confirm existing beliefs and ignores evidence that contradicts them. These are not personality flaws — they are documented features of human cognition. A journal replaces memory with data.

### What to record in every trade

**Before the trade:**
- Date, time, instrument, and direction
- The setup you identified and why it qualified under your rules
- Entry price, stop price, and target price
- Risk amount in dollars and as a percentage of account
- Your pre-trade emotional state (calm, impatient, uncertain?)

**After the trade:**
- Exit price and reason (stop, target, manual exit)
- Outcome in R-multiples
- Did you follow your entry rule? Exit rule? Position size rule?
- What did the trade look like at entry? (A screenshot is invaluable)
- What would you do differently?

### How to review the journal

Data collection without review is a filing cabinet. Reviews convert data into decisions.

**Daily review** (5–10 minutes): Check the day's trades. Was every rule followed? Note any deviation. Rate your execution, not the outcome — a trade that followed all rules but lost is a good trade; a trade that broke rules but won is a dangerous one.

**Weekly review** (30–60 minutes): Aggregate the week's trades. Calculate win rate, average R per trade, and total R for the week. Look for patterns: specific setups that are underperforming, specific times of day where results are poor, recurring rule-breaks.

**Monthly review** (1–2 hours): Compare the month's results to your backtest and forward-test benchmarks. Are your live results tracking your expected expectancy? If they are significantly worse, is it a variance issue (normal) or a systematic execution problem (fixable)? Update your trading plan based on findings.

### The rule-break analysis

One of the highest-value things a journal reveals is which rules you break most often — and why. Common patterns:

- **Entering too early** — impatience; the trigger has not fired yet but the setup looks promising
- **Widening stops** — fear of being wrong; the trade is at your stop level and you move it
- **Exiting winning trades early** — fear of giving back gains; the trade has not reached the target
- **Oversizing** — overconfidence after a winning streak

Once you know which rules you break and why, you can address the root cause: refine the rule to make it easier to follow, or implement a behavioral check (a checklist before each trade, a brief pause before sizing).

### The compounding effect of review

A trader who reviews consistently improves exponentially. Each review cycle produces one or two small improvements. Those improvements compound: better entries lead to higher win rates, better exits lead to larger average wins, rule-adherence leads to the system actually achieving its tested expectancy. Over one to two years, the difference between a trader who journals and one who does not becomes dramatic — not because of different knowledge, but because of different feedback loops.

The journal is not overhead. It is the mechanism through which trading skill actually develops.
