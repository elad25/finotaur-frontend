## In plain words

An **edge** is any condition that causes you to win more money than you lose over a large number of trades — not on every trade, but across many. It is not a feeling, not a hot tip, and not a pattern you noticed once. A real edge is something you can define precisely, test on historical data, and replicate consistently. Without an edge, a trading plan is just a schedule for losing money in an organized way.

## Quick demo

A swing trader notices that when a stock pulls back to its 20-day moving average after a strong earnings beat, it tends to bounce within two to three days. She studies 200 such setups over two years and finds it works 58% of the time with an average winner of 2.1R and an average loser of 1R. That is an edge: a defined condition with a positive expectancy she can calculate. A trader who just buys "when it feels right near the moving average" has no edge — only a vague intuition that a single bad month will erase.

## Full explanation

### Expectancy — the only number that matters

Edge is not about win rate. A strategy that wins 40% of the time can be highly profitable if the average winner is 3× the average loser. The formula that captures this is **expectancy**:

> Expectancy = (Win Rate × Average Win) − (Loss Rate × Average Loss)

Any positive result means the strategy produces money over time. Any negative result means it destroys money, regardless of how convincing the story sounds. Calculating your expectancy forces you to confront the math, not the narrative.

### Where real edges come from

Edges exist in markets for a few recurring reasons:

- **Behavioral inefficiency** — other traders act emotionally at identifiable moments (earnings panic, overnight gap reversals, first-hour false breakouts). A patient, rule-based trader can be on the other side of those mistakes.
- **Time-of-day patterns** — certain setups perform consistently during specific sessions (e.g., the first 30 minutes of equities, the London open in forex). Time is a variable most traders ignore.
- **Risk tolerance asymmetry** — institutional traders often cannot hold positions through short-term volatility that retail traders can absorb. That asymmetry creates opportunities.
- **Structural patterns** — market microstructure (how orders fill, where stops cluster, how liquidity providers operate) creates repeating price behaviors at specific levels.

### What is not an edge

- A trade that worked last week
- A strategy someone else claims is profitable
- A pattern you spotted on a single chart
- A gut feeling after watching the tape for an hour

These may contain the seed of an edge. They are not one yet.

### How to identify your edge

1. **Observe a specific, repeatable condition** — not "momentum stocks," but "stocks up more than 5% pre-market on earnings beats, first pullback to VWAP after the open."
2. **Collect a meaningful sample** — at minimum 50–100 instances, preferably more.
3. **Calculate the raw statistics** — win rate, average win, average loss, expectancy.
4. **Stress-test the sample** — does it hold across different market regimes (trending, ranging, volatile)?

### Why traders skip this step

Defining an edge is unglamorous, time-consuming work. It is far more exciting to trade. But skipping it means your plan has no foundation. You will be following rules that have no proven reason to produce profit — and when the inevitable losing streak arrives, you will have no evidence to tell you whether to keep going or to stop.

An edge is the reason your plan will eventually make money. Without it, everything else is discipline in service of nothing.
