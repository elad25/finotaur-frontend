## In plain words

Futures, stocks, options, and CFDs all let you speculate on price — but they differ dramatically in how they work, what they cost, and what risks they carry. **Futures** offer exchange-regulated access, built-in leverage, and near-24-hour markets. Understanding when to use each vehicle is as important as having a view on where price is going.

## Quick demo

A trader is bearish on the S&P 500. With stocks, they can only sell short (requiring a margin account, borrowing shares, paying fees). With options, they buy a put — paying a premium that expires worthless if they are wrong. With a futures contract, they simply sell ES short — no borrowing, no premium decay, instant exposure. If they are wrong and ES rises 20 points, they lose $1,000 per contract immediately, with no time buffer. Each instrument has a different risk profile for the same directional view.

## Full explanation

### Futures vs. Stocks

| | Futures | Stocks |
|---|---|---|
| Leverage | Built-in (via margin) | Limited (2:1 reg T, 4:1 intraday in US) |
| Short selling | Native — no borrowing needed | Requires borrowing shares |
| Settlement | Cash or physical, at expiration | No expiration; hold forever |
| Market hours | ~23 hours/day | 6.5 regular hours (pre/after limited) |
| Tax treatment (US) | 60/40 rule | Short-term or long-term based on hold period |
| Counterparty risk | Clearinghouse guarantee | Minimal (exchange-listed) |

Futures are superior for traders who want efficient short exposure, nearly 24-hour access, or leverage without a margin loan. Stocks are better for buy-and-hold investors and anyone who wants equity ownership in a company.

### Futures vs. Options

| | Futures | Options |
|---|---|---|
| Obligation | Both sides are obligated | Buyer has a right; seller has obligation |
| Time decay | None | Theta erodes option value daily |
| Leverage | High, fixed by contract | Variable; changes as price moves (delta) |
| Max loss (buyer) | Unlimited in either direction | Buyer: limited to premium paid |
| Profit profile | Linear with price | Nonlinear (curved) |

Options are better for defined-risk trades, income strategies (selling premium), and positioning for binary events like earnings. Futures are better for pure directional speculation and hedging where a linear payoff is desired. Note: options on futures exist, combining both instruments' characteristics.

### Futures vs. CFDs

| | Futures | CFDs |
|---|---|---|
| Regulation | Heavily regulated (CFTC in US) | Lighter regulation; banned for US retail traders |
| Exchange | Listed on regulated exchanges | OTC — your broker is the counterparty |
| Spread | Exchange-determined, transparent | Broker-set, can widen at will |
| Expiration | Fixed contract expirations | No expiration (but overnight financing costs) |
| Availability | US residents can trade | Not available to US residents |

CFDs offer flexible sizing and no expiration, but the OTC nature means less price transparency and counterparty risk. Futures are the regulated, transparent alternative — and the only option for US-based retail traders seeking leveraged exposure to equity indexes and commodities.

### When to choose futures

- You want clean, levered directional exposure to equity indexes, bonds, commodities, or currencies.
- You want nearly 24-hour access to react to overnight events.
- You want to short an index or commodity without borrowing.
- You want the US 60/40 tax treatment on short-term trading.
- You want exchange-guaranteed counterparty safety.

### Why it matters for a trader

There is no universally superior instrument — the best one is the one that fits your strategy, account size, and regulatory environment. A day trader in the US index market almost always prefers ES or MES futures over stock ETFs (SPY) or options for directional scalps. A swing trader with a specific thesis on an individual stock has no futures contract to express that view. Know the tools available, and match the instrument to the job.
