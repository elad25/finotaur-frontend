## In plain words

**Mini** and **micro** futures contracts are smaller versions of the standard (full-size) contracts, designed for traders with smaller accounts or those who want to size their positions more precisely. A **micro** contract is typically one-tenth the size of its corresponding mini, and the mini is one-fifth the size of the standard. Same markets, same hours, same mechanics — just smaller dollar exposure per contract.

## Quick demo

The standard S&P 500 futures contract (SP) has a $250 multiplier. The E-mini ES has a $50 multiplier — one-fifth the size. The Micro E-mini MES has a $5 multiplier — one-tenth of ES and one-fiftieth of SP. If ES moves 10 points ($500 per contract), MES moves the same 10 points but earns or loses only $50. A trader with a $10,000 account can risk 1% ($100) by using a two-tick stop on MES — something nearly impossible to do with a full ES contract.

## Full explanation

### The size hierarchy

The CME Group offers three tiers for its most popular index futures:

| Contract | Underlying | Multiplier | 1 Point = |
|---|---|---|---|
| SP (standard) | S&P 500 | $250 | $250 |
| ES (E-mini) | S&P 500 | $50 | $50 |
| MES (Micro E-mini) | S&P 500 | $5 | $5 |

The same structure applies to the Nasdaq 100 (NQ / MNQ), Dow Jones (YM / MYM), Russell 2000 (RTY / M2K), crude oil (CL / MCL), and gold (GC / MGC).

### Benefits of micro contracts

**Capital efficiency**: Micro contracts allow traders to take positions with significantly lower margin requirements. MES initial margin is roughly $2,000 vs. $15,000 for ES.

**Precise position sizing**: Instead of being forced to trade one, two, or three full ES contracts, a trader can use 3, 7, or 12 MES contracts to dial in an exact dollar risk that matches their account size and risk tolerance.

**Learning without overexposure**: New traders can learn futures mechanics with real money while keeping risk at a manageable level. Paper trading is useful, but the psychological element only kicks in when real capital is at stake.

**Scaling in and out**: Micro contracts let traders scale their entries and exits. Adding half a position, taking partial profits, or adjusting exposure incrementally is much easier with smaller units.

### The trade-off: spreads and liquidity

Micro contracts are liquid but generally have slightly wider bid-ask spreads (in dollar terms relative to position size) than their mini counterparts. For scalpers doing high-frequency trading where the spread matters enormously, standard minis may be preferred. For swing traders or those holding positions for minutes to hours, micros are an excellent tool.

### Tax treatment

In the US, micro futures contracts receive the same **60/40 tax treatment** as their mini and standard equivalents (60% of gains taxed at long-term capital gains rates, 40% at short-term rates), regardless of how long you held the position.

### Why it matters for a trader

Micros are not training wheels — they are a legitimate instrument used by professional traders to manage risk precisely. For any trader whose account is not large enough to properly size a mini contract (where one tick represents a meaningful percentage of capital), micros are the correct starting point. The goal is to always risk a predefined, small percentage of capital per trade — micro contracts make that possible at any account size.
