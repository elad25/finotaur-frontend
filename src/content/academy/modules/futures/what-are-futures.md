## In plain words

A **futures contract** is a legally binding agreement to buy or sell a specific asset at a **predetermined price** on a **future date**. Both parties — buyer and seller — are locked in: neither can walk away. Futures trade on regulated exchanges, which makes them transparent and standardized.

## Quick demo

In October, a wheat farmer fears prices will fall by harvest time. A bread company fears prices will rise. They agree today: the farmer will sell 5,000 bushels at $5.50 each in December. Come December, if wheat is at $5.00, the farmer still gets $5.50 — a win. If it's at $6.00, the bread company still pays $5.50 — their win. Both locked in certainty at the cost of upside.

## Full explanation

### What makes a futures contract

A futures contract specifies four things:

- **The underlying asset** — wheat, crude oil, the S&P 500 index, gold, etc.
- **The quantity** — one contract of crude oil covers 1,000 barrels.
- **The delivery date** — called the expiration or settlement date.
- **The agreed price** — locked in at the time of the trade.

Unlike a spot trade (where you pay and receive the asset immediately), a futures contract defers both payment and delivery to a later date.

### Who uses futures and why

Futures were invented for **hedgers** — producers and consumers who want to lock in a price and remove uncertainty. A corn farmer, an airline (hedging jet fuel), and a gold miner are all natural hedgers.

**Speculators** took notice because futures also offer an efficient way to bet on price direction. A day trader buying one ES (S&P 500 e-mini) contract is speculating that the index will rise — they have no intention of receiving 50 times the S&P 500 in shares.

**Arbitrageurs** use futures to exploit price discrepancies between the futures price and the underlying spot market, keeping the two in alignment.

### Exchange-traded and standardized

All listed futures trade on regulated exchanges — the CME Group in the US is the largest. Every contract is standardized: the exchange defines the size, the delivery terms, and the settlement method. This standardization creates deep liquidity and price transparency.

A **clearinghouse** sits between every buyer and seller, guaranteeing both sides of the trade. This eliminates counterparty risk — you don't need to worry whether the person on the other side of your trade will honor it.

### Cash settlement vs. physical delivery

Most financial futures (equity index, interest rate) settle in **cash** — no one delivers actual stocks. Most commodity futures allow physical delivery, though the vast majority of speculative traders close or roll their contracts before expiration, so delivery rarely occurs in practice.

### Why it matters for a trader

Futures are one of the most efficient and liquid trading vehicles available. Low commissions, nearly 24-hour markets, tax advantages (60/40 long-term/short-term in the US), and tight spreads make them the instrument of choice for many active traders. Understanding what a futures contract *is* — a binding forward agreement with a clearinghouse guarantee — is the foundation for everything else in this module.
