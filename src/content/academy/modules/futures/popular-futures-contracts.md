## In plain words

While hundreds of futures contracts exist, active traders concentrate in a small number of highly liquid markets. The most popular are **ES** (S&P 500), **NQ** (Nasdaq 100), **CL** (crude oil), and **GC** (gold). Each has its own personality — volatility level, session behavior, and the type of trader it attracts. Knowing which market fits your style is more important than trading all of them.

## Quick demo

On a typical day, ES trades over one million contracts. One ES contract moved 20 points ($1,000) in a session. NQ moved 80 points on the same day ($1,600 per contract) — NQ is more volatile than ES, which attracts traders who want bigger moves but demands tighter risk control. Meanwhile, CL barely moved $0.40 ($400 per contract) — a slow crude oil day. Different instruments, different energy levels, different approaches required.

## Full explanation

### Equity index futures

**ES — E-mini S&P 500**
The most liquid futures contract in the world. It tracks the S&P 500 index with a $50 multiplier (one point = $50). ES is the benchmark instrument for macro traders, hedgers, and day traders. It reacts to every major economic event, earnings season, and Federal Reserve announcement. Spread is typically one tick ($12.50) in normal conditions.

**NQ — E-mini Nasdaq 100**
Tracks the Nasdaq 100. With a $20 multiplier (one point = $20), NQ moves in larger point terms than ES but has a similar dollar value per point when sized in micros (MNQ multiplier = $2). NQ is more sensitive to technology sector news and growth-rate expectations. It tends to be more volatile than ES and attracts momentum-oriented traders.

**YM — E-mini Dow Jones**
Tracks the DJIA. $5 multiplier (one point = $5). Less common among active day traders due to lower volatility relative to ES and NQ, but popular among traders who prefer slower-moving markets.

**RTY — E-mini Russell 2000**
Tracks small-cap US stocks. More sensitive to domestic economic conditions than the large-cap indices. Popular during risk-on/risk-off rotation trades.

### Energy futures

**CL — Crude Oil (WTI)**
One of the most actively traded commodity futures. Each contract covers 1,000 barrels; one tick ($0.01/barrel) = $10. CL can be highly volatile around OPEC announcements, inventory reports (EIA Wednesday report), and geopolitical events. It requires respect for gap risk and can move several dollars ($1 = $1,000 per contract) in minutes.

### Precious metals futures

**GC — Gold**
100 troy ounces per contract; one tick ($0.10/oz) = $10. Gold is a macro instrument — it reacts to real interest rates, dollar strength, and geopolitical risk. It tends to trend well and is a favorite among swing traders. MGC (10 oz micro) makes it accessible to smaller accounts.

**SI — Silver**
5,000 troy ounces per contract. More volatile than gold in percentage terms and often trades with higher relative spreads. Follows gold but can deviate sharply.

### Interest rate futures

**ZN — 10-Year Treasury Note**
Tracks US 10-year bond prices. Each full point = $1,000, but bonds move in 32nds (one 32nd = $31.25). A critical instrument for macro traders and anyone tracking the yield curve. Inversely related to interest rate expectations.

**ZB — 30-Year Treasury Bond**
Similar to ZN but for the long end of the yield curve. More duration sensitivity; larger moves on rate shock days.

### Choosing your instrument

Most successful futures traders master one or two contracts deeply rather than jumping between them. Match your instrument to your:

- **Account size** (use micros if account is under $25,000)
- **Preferred session** (ES and NQ are most active during US hours; CL active during European and US morning)
- **Volatility tolerance** (NQ > ES > YM for equity index volatility)
- **Time horizon** (GC and ZN suit swing trades; ES/NQ suit intraday scalping)

### Why it matters for a trader

The instrument you choose shapes your entire trading experience — the setups available, the psychology required, and the capital at risk per tick. Spend time watching a market in a simulator before committing real capital. Each of these contracts has a distinct rhythm that only becomes obvious with observation.
