## In plain words

When futures contracts for delivery in the future are priced **higher** than the current spot price, the market is in **contango**. When futures are priced **lower** than spot, the market is in **backwardation**. These two states reveal what the market expects about supply, demand, storage, and the cost of carrying an asset forward in time.

## Quick demo

Crude oil spot is at $80 per barrel. The contract expiring in three months trades at $82, and the one expiring in six months trades at $84. That upward-sloping curve is contango — storage costs and financing charges are baked in. Now imagine a supply shock: the spot price jumps to $90 while the six-month contract stays at $83. The curve slopes downward. That is backwardation — scarce supply today commands a premium over future delivery.

## Full explanation

### The futures curve

If you plot the prices of futures contracts for the same commodity across different expiration months, you get the **futures curve** (also called the term structure). Its shape — upward, flat, or downward sloping — tells a story about market conditions.

### Contango explained

**Contango** is the normal state for most commodities and financial assets when markets are stable. It exists because of the **cost of carry**:

- Storage costs (paying to warehouse wheat or oil)
- Insurance
- Financing costs (the interest rate on capital tied up in the commodity)

If you can buy oil today and store it for six months, you need a higher price in six months to make that worthwhile. The futures price at six months reflects that total carrying cost. Contango is essentially the market pricing in "ownership isn't free."

### Backwardation explained

**Backwardation** occurs when near-term supply is tight or demand is urgent. The spot price is elevated above future prices because people need the commodity *now*, not in six months. It is common in:

- Crude oil during supply disruptions
- Agricultural commodities before harvest
- Gold during periods of intense physical demand

Backwardation can also occur because of the **convenience yield** — the non-financial benefit of holding a physical commodity (being able to meet production demand immediately). When convenience yield is high, it overwhelms storage costs and pushes spot above futures.

### The roll yield

For traders who hold futures positions and roll them forward (closing the expiring contract and opening the next one), the shape of the curve determines whether rolling is a headwind or a tailwind:

- **In contango**: rolling is expensive. You close the cheaper near-month contract and open the more expensive later-month contract — a negative roll yield.
- **In backwardation**: rolling is profitable. You close the more expensive near-month and open the cheaper later-month — a positive roll yield.

This roll yield significantly affects the long-run performance of commodity ETFs and any trader holding futures through multiple expiration cycles.

### Why it matters for a trader

Understanding the curve shape is especially important for anyone trading commodity futures, crude oil, or volatility products (VIX futures are almost always in contango, which explains why long-VIX ETFs decay over time). The spot price and the futures price are not the same — and the gap between them, and its direction, is information.
