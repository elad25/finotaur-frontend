## In plain words

The **halving** is a scheduled event in Bitcoin's code that cuts the reward paid to miners for adding a new block — exactly in half — approximately every four years. Because miners are the primary source of new Bitcoin entering circulation, halving directly reduces the pace of new supply. Historically, the crypto market has exhibited a rough four-year rhythm loosely correlated with halving events, though the relationship is debated and the pattern has varied each cycle.

## Quick demo

In April 2024, Bitcoin's block reward dropped from 6.25 BTC to 3.125 BTC per block. At Bitcoin's typical pace of ~144 blocks per day, that reduced daily new issuance from roughly 900 BTC per day to roughly 450 BTC. If demand stays constant and supply drops by half, basic economics suggests upward price pressure. Whether and when markets price this in — before the event, after, or not at all — is the subject of ongoing debate, but the supply shock is mathematically certain.

## Full explanation

### What exactly happens at a halving

Every 210,000 blocks (roughly 4 years at a 10-minute block time), Bitcoin's code automatically reduces the block subsidy. The sequence:

- Block 0–209,999: 50 BTC per block
- 210,000–419,999: 25 BTC per block
- 420,000–629,999: 12.5 BTC per block
- 630,000–839,999: 6.25 BTC per block (March 2020)
- 840,000+: 3.125 BTC per block (April 2024)

This continues until the subsidy reaches zero, projected around 2140. After that, miners are compensated only by transaction fees.

### Stock-to-flow ratio

The **stock-to-flow (S2F)** model measures how many years of production it would take to double the existing supply. Halving events sharply increase S2F. Gold has a high S2F (roughly 60); Bitcoin's S2F rose to roughly 120 after the 2024 halving, exceeding gold on this metric. Proponents argue high S2F correlates with price appreciation; critics note the model's predictions have been inconsistent.

### The four-year cycle narrative

Observers have described a recurring pattern across Bitcoin's history:

1. **Accumulation** — bear market lows, little public interest, long-term holders acquire
2. **Bull run** — often beginning 6–12 months before or after a halving, extending into a new all-time high
3. **Blow-off top** — parabolic price rise followed by sharp reversal (typically 75–85% peak-to-trough drawdown)
4. **Bear market** — multi-year consolidation and decline before the next cycle

Past halvings occurred in November 2012, July 2016, May 2020, and April 2024. Each was followed by a significant bull market — though with different timing, magnitude, and subsequent drawdown.

### Why the pattern may weaken over time

As Bitcoin's market cap grows, it takes larger capital flows to produce the same percentage moves. Institutional participation has also smoothed some of the speculative extremes. Additionally, the supply shock from each halving is proportionally smaller as the outstanding supply grows — the 2024 halving reduced annualized issuance from roughly 1.7% to 0.85% of total supply. Future halvings will be even smaller relative to supply.

### Altcoin cycles

Historically, a pattern repeated across cycles: Bitcoin rallies first, then Ethereum, then large-cap altcoins, then small-cap "alt season" with the broadest speculative participation. This rotation is not guaranteed and has varied between cycles. It reflects capital moving sequentially through risk tiers as confidence grows — and retreating in the same order when sentiment reverses.

### Why it matters for a trader

The halving is a known event — it creates a structured framework for thinking about crypto market phases. Whether you believe the cycle pattern repeats predictably or not, understanding it is essential because most crypto market participants trade around this narrative. Their behavior — accumulating ahead of halvings, expecting post-halving rallies — can partially self-reinforce the pattern. Trading with and against cycle expectations is a legitimate strategy, but requires clear risk management given the volatility.
