## In plain words

**Bitcoin** is the first cryptocurrency: a fixed-supply digital currency that runs on a public blockchain with no central issuer. Its creator (known only as Satoshi Nakamoto) designed it so that only 21 million coins can ever exist, making it the first digitally **scarce** asset. Transactions are settled by a decentralized network of computers without any bank or government involved.

## Quick demo

Picture gold — finite in supply, not controlled by any government, and valued partly because digging it up requires real effort. Bitcoin mirrors that model digitally: new coins are created only by "miners" who expend computing power to validate transactions, and the reward they earn is cut in half roughly every four years. In May 2020, the reward dropped from 12.5 to 6.25 BTC per block; in April 2024, it dropped again to 3.125. Each cut shrinks the new supply entering the market, even as existing demand continues.

## Full explanation

### Origin and purpose

Bitcoin launched in January 2009, nine months after a whitepaper appeared under the name Satoshi Nakamoto. The stated goal was a "peer-to-peer electronic cash system" that let two parties transact without relying on a trusted intermediary. In practice, Bitcoin has evolved into something closer to a store of value — "digital gold" — rather than everyday cash, though the debate about its ideal use case continues.

### The 21 million cap

The supply schedule is encoded in Bitcoin's software and enforced by the network. Approximately 19.7 million BTC have been mined as of mid-2026, leaving fewer than 1.5 million still to be issued. The final bitcoin is projected to be mined around the year 2140. This hard cap is maintained by:

- A **difficulty adjustment** every 2,016 blocks that keeps the average block time at 10 minutes regardless of how much computing power joins or leaves the network
- The **halving** mechanism that reduces block rewards on a fixed schedule

### Mining and security

Bitcoin's security comes from **Proof of Work**. Miners compete to find a hash below a target value by iterating through billions of inputs per second. The winning miner adds the block and receives the block reward plus transaction fees. This competition means that to alter the ledger, an attacker would need to outpace the combined computational power of the rest of the network — currently in the hundreds of exahashes per second.

### Keys and ownership

Bitcoin ownership is controlled by **cryptographic key pairs**. Your **private key** is a secret number that authorizes transactions; the matching **public key** is your receiving address. "Owning bitcoin" means controlling a private key — not holding a balance in an account maintained by someone else. If you lose the private key, the funds are inaccessible permanently.

### Bitcoin's role in the market

Bitcoin is the largest crypto asset by market capitalization and functions as the primary benchmark for the rest of the market. When Bitcoin falls sharply, most other cryptocurrencies tend to fall harder. When Bitcoin is in a sustained uptrend, it often draws capital into the broader crypto market. Traders track Bitcoin **dominance** — Bitcoin's share of total crypto market cap — as a signal of whether capital is rotating toward or away from higher-risk altcoins.

### Why it matters for a trader

Bitcoin is the risk-on/risk-off barometer of the crypto market. Its on-chain metrics (active addresses, exchange inflows, miner behavior), its correlation to macro factors (real interest rates, dollar strength), and its supply schedule (halving cycles) are all inputs professional crypto traders monitor. Understanding Bitcoin's mechanics lets you read those signals rather than just watching price.
