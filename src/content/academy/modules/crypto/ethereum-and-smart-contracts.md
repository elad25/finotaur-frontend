## In plain words

**Ethereum** is a blockchain that goes beyond storing balances: it runs **smart contracts**, which are programs that execute automatically when predefined conditions are met — no human intermediary required. Think of it as a global computer where anyone can deploy software that handles money, ownership, and agreements without trusting a company to run the server. The native currency that powers this network is called **Ether (ETH)**.

## Quick demo

Suppose you want to sell a digital concert ticket to a stranger online. Normally you'd worry: what if I send the ticket and they don't pay? What if they pay and I don't send the ticket? A smart contract solves this by holding the ticket and the payment in escrow simultaneously. When both sides fulfill their conditions — buyer deposits funds, seller sends the ticket — the contract releases them automatically. Neither party needs to trust the other; they both trust the publicly visible code.

## Full explanation

### What makes Ethereum different from Bitcoin

Bitcoin's scripting language is deliberately limited: it can transfer value but can't run complex logic. Ethereum's co-founder Vitalik Buterin proposed in 2013 a blockchain with a **Turing-complete** virtual machine — meaning it can run any computable program. This turned the blockchain from a ledger into a platform.

### Smart contracts

A smart contract is code stored on the blockchain that:

- Executes automatically when its conditions are met
- Cannot be altered after deployment (immutability)
- Is visible to anyone (transparency)
- Handles value without a trusted custodian

They power most of what exists in the crypto ecosystem: **decentralized exchanges**, **lending protocols**, **NFT marketplaces**, and more.

### Gas fees

Running code on Ethereum costs **gas** — a unit measuring computational work. Users pay gas fees in ETH to compensate validators for processing their transactions. When the network is congested, gas fees spike. This is a key usability and cost issue that drives competition from alternative blockchains (called "Layer 1s") and **Layer 2 networks** that batch transactions to reduce fees.

### The merge: Proof of Stake

In September 2022, Ethereum transitioned from Proof of Work to **Proof of Stake** in an upgrade known as "The Merge." Validators now lock up (stake) ETH as collateral to propose and attest to blocks. The switch reduced Ethereum's energy consumption by approximately 99.95% and made ETH issuance deflationary in some conditions, because a portion of every gas fee is **burned** (permanently removed from supply).

### The Ethereum ecosystem

Ethereum hosts the largest ecosystem of decentralized applications (dApps):

- **DeFi** — lending, borrowing, and trading protocols with billions in locked assets
- **NFTs** — non-fungible tokens representing unique digital ownership
- **Stablecoins** — most major stablecoins (USDC, DAI) are ERC-20 tokens on Ethereum
- **Layer 2s** — networks like Arbitrum, Optimism, and Base that inherit Ethereum's security while processing transactions faster and cheaper

### ETH as an asset

ETH is both a currency (used to pay gas) and a yield-bearing asset (stakers earn rewards). Post-Merge, the net issuance of ETH can go negative during high-activity periods because more ETH is burned in fees than is issued to validators. Traders track ETH supply changes, gas fee levels, and Layer 2 activity as demand signals.

### Why it matters for a trader

Ethereum is the foundation of most on-chain activity in crypto. Its health — gas fees, validator count, TVL (total value locked in DeFi), and Layer 2 adoption — is a leading indicator of overall ecosystem demand. Understanding smart contracts helps you evaluate any crypto project: what does the contract actually do, can it be audited, and what happens if it fails?
