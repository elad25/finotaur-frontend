## In plain words

**Relative strength** is the comparison of how one asset performs against another — or against a benchmark — over a given period. It answers the question: is this asset outperforming or underperforming its peers? A **Relative Rotation Graph (RRG)** is a visual tool that takes relative strength analysis one step further by showing both the level of relative performance and whether that performance is improving or deteriorating — all in a single, rotating chart.

## Quick demo

Suppose two stocks both rise 10% in a month, but the broader S&P 500 rises 15%. Both stocks had positive returns, but both underperformed the market — their relative strength was negative. Now imagine you plot their relative strength scores and momentum on an RRG. If one of them is moving from the "lagging" quadrant into the "improving" quadrant, it signals that even though it has underperformed recently, the gap is closing — a potential early entry for rotation traders. Without the relative lens, you'd see only two charts trending up and draw no distinction.

## Full explanation

### What relative strength measures

Relative strength (RS) is calculated by dividing the price of an asset by the price of a benchmark (often a broad index or a sector ETF). The resulting ratio tells you whether the asset is gaining or losing ground relative to that benchmark.

- **Rising RS ratio** — the asset is outperforming; money is preferring this name or sector.
- **Falling RS ratio** — the asset is underperforming; money is rotating away.

The key insight: **absolute price is not the same as relative performance**. An asset can rise in price while underperforming its benchmark, which is a hidden warning sign. Conversely, an asset that falls less than the market during a downturn has strong relative strength — institutional money is defending it.

### How to use relative strength in practice

**Step 1 — Choose a benchmark.** For individual stocks, use the S&P 500 or the sector ETF. For sectors, use SPY. For commodities, use a broad commodity index or a sector-specific benchmark.

**Step 2 — Plot the ratio.** Divide the asset price by the benchmark price. The chart of this ratio (sometimes called a "ratio chart" or "relative chart") shows the trend of outperformance or underperformance over time.

**Step 3 — Look for trend changes.** A relative strength line that has been declining and then begins to form a base and turn up is a classic signal that rotation into that asset may be beginning. Combining this with improving price momentum creates a high-probability setup.

### The Relative Rotation Graph (RRG)

Developed by Julius de Kempenaer, the RRG plots assets on a two-dimensional chart:

- **X-axis — RS-Ratio:** the level of relative strength. Right of center = outperforming.
- **Y-axis — RS-Momentum:** whether relative strength is improving or deteriorating. Above center = momentum improving.

This creates four quadrants:

| Quadrant | RS Level | RS Momentum | Interpretation |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Leading** | Strong | Improving | Best position — leaders |
| **Weakening** | Strong | Deteriorating | Beginning to lose ground |
| **Lagging** | Weak | Deteriorating | Avoid or short |
| **Improving** | Weak | Improving | Early rotation opportunity |

Assets in the RRG rotate clockwise through these quadrants over time as their relative performance waxes and wanes. The speed and direction of rotation is as important as the current quadrant.

### Using RRG in sector rotation

When applied to the eleven S&P sectors against SPY, the RRG provides an instant snapshot of which sectors are leading, which are about to peak, which are lagging, and which may be bottoming. Combined with business cycle awareness and intermarket signals, the RRG becomes a powerful decision-support tool:

- A sector moving from **Improving → Leading** is the classic rotation buy.
- A sector moving from **Leading → Weakening** is a signal to reduce exposure.
- A sector stuck in **Lagging** with no improving momentum should be avoided.

### Why it matters for a trader or investor

Relative strength thinking rewires how you approach market analysis. Instead of asking "is this stock going up?", you ask "is this stock going up more than everything else?" That shift from absolute to relative thinking is what separates traders who catch the early part of a rotation from those who buy what has already run. The RRG makes this analysis visual and dynamic — a tool that encodes the entire sector rotation picture into a single glance, turning what used to require hours of chart comparison into an immediate, actionable read on where money is flowing.
