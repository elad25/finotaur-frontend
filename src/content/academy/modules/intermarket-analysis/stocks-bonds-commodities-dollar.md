## In plain words

Four markets sit at the center of intermarket analysis: **stocks**, **bonds**, **commodities**, and the **U.S. dollar**. Each one reflects a different aspect of the economy — corporate growth, interest rates, real demand, and the cost of money itself. Understanding how they typically relate to each other is the foundation of reading markets as a system rather than as isolated charts.

## Quick demo

Consider the commodity boom of 2021–2022. Supply-chain disruptions and massive fiscal stimulus pushed oil, copper, and grain prices sharply higher. This commodity surge signaled rising inflation, which forced the Federal Reserve to raise rates. Higher rates drove bond yields up and bond prices down. The strong dollar that followed commodity-inflation tightening crushed emerging-market currencies and put pressure on U.S. multinational corporate earnings. The stock market eventually repriced lower. One trend — rising commodity prices — telegraphed pain across all three other pillars before stocks confirmed it.

## Full explanation

### Stocks: the growth barometer

Equities reflect investor expectations for future corporate earnings, which depend on economic growth and the cost of capital. Stock markets tend to lead the real economy by six to twelve months, making them a forward-looking signal rather than a current one. When stocks are rising in broad, sustained fashion, it generally confirms that the economy is expanding and credit conditions are supportive.

### Bonds: the rate and inflation barometer

Bond prices move inversely to yields. When yields rise, bond prices fall — and vice versa. Because the bond market is far larger than the stock market, it tends to be an earlier and more accurate signal of economic turning points. Key things to watch:

- **Rising yields** signal expectations of stronger growth or higher inflation — potentially negative for equities (especially growth stocks) and for commodities via the dollar.
- **Falling yields** signal risk aversion, expectations of weaker growth, or a flight to safety.
- **Credit spreads** (the yield gap between corporate and government bonds) are a particularly sensitive early-warning indicator: widening spreads mean investors are demanding more compensation for default risk, which often precedes equity weakness.

### Commodities: the real-economy and inflation barometer

Commodities — energy, metals, and agriculture — reflect actual physical demand. Rising commodity prices signal economic expansion and building inflation pressure. Falling commodity prices suggest a slowdown. Key commodities carry specific signals:

- **Copper** is sometimes called "Dr. Copper" because its industrial uses make it a sensitive gauge of global manufacturing and construction activity.
- **Crude oil** signals energy costs and, through them, consumer and corporate margin pressure.
- **Gold** is unusual: it often rises with inflation fears but also rises in genuine risk-off environments, making it a multi-purpose signal.

### The U.S. dollar: the global cost of money

Because most commodity contracts are priced in dollars, a stronger dollar mechanically makes commodities more expensive for foreign buyers — which typically suppresses demand and prices. The dollar also reflects the relative attractiveness of U.S. assets: when U.S. rates are rising faster than those abroad, capital flows to the dollar, strengthening it.

The dollar's most important intermarket relationship:
- **Dollar up → commodities down** (typical)
- **Dollar up → emerging markets under pressure** (dollar-denominated debt becomes more expensive)
- **Dollar down → commodities often rise** (historically observed)

### Why these relationships shift

These are tendencies, not laws. The most important driver of *which* relationship dominates at any time is whether the market is primarily concerned with **growth** (stocks-bonds diverge, commodities matter) or **inflation** (stocks and bonds can fall together, commodities rise). Knowing which regime you are in determines which intermarket signals to weight most heavily.

### Why it matters for a trader or investor

Reading all four pillars at once prevents tunnel vision. If you are long equities and you notice bonds and the dollar are both surging while commodities are rolling over, you have a macro warning sign — not a reason to panic, but a reason to tighten risk and look for confirmation before adding exposure.
