## In plain words

**Sector rotation** is the process by which capital moves from one industry sector to another as economic conditions change. Because different sectors of the stock market are sensitive to different economic forces — interest rates, commodity prices, consumer spending, capital expenditure — they tend to lead or lag the broader market at predictable points in the business cycle. Recognizing where money is flowing between sectors gives both investors and active traders an edge in identifying which parts of the market are positioned to outperform.

## Quick demo

In 2022, as the Federal Reserve began its most aggressive rate-hiking cycle in decades, the rotation was textbook. High-growth technology stocks — which had dominated the prior decade — collapsed as rising rates crushed their high multiples. Capital rotated into **energy** (oil and gas prices surging), **healthcare** (defensive earnings), and **utilities** (dividend income more attractive relative to now-higher bond yields). An investor who watched sector flows rather than just the headline index could have dramatically reduced drawdown by trimming tech and adding energy exposure months before the S&P 500 peak.

## Full explanation

### Why sectors rotate

Each of the eleven GICS sectors has a different fundamental driver:

- **Technology and growth sectors** are most sensitive to interest rates and long-duration earnings expectations. They thrive in low-rate, high-growth environments and suffer when rates rise.
- **Financials** benefit from a steepening yield curve (they borrow short, lend long) and suffer when the curve inverts.
- **Energy and materials** are linked to commodity prices and global industrial demand.
- **Consumer discretionary** follows consumer confidence and employment.
- **Consumer staples, healthcare, and utilities** are defensive — their earnings are stable regardless of the cycle, making them attractive when growth slows.
- **Real estate (REITs)** is highly sensitive to interest rates; rising rates increase borrowing costs and pressure valuations.
- **Industrials** tend to peak in mid-cycle as capital expenditure accelerates.

### The classic rotation sequence

Following the business cycle framework, the traditional rotation looks roughly like this:

1. **Early cycle** — Financials, consumer discretionary, and industrials lead as credit loosens and spending recovers.
2. **Mid cycle** — Technology and materials outperform as earnings growth accelerates and commodity demand builds.
3. **Late cycle** — Energy and healthcare lead; staples and utilities begin to attract defensive flows.
4. **Recession** — Consumer staples, utilities, and healthcare hold up while everything cyclical falls.

This sequence is a framework, not a rigid script. Exogenous events and policy shifts can accelerate, delay, or reverse parts of the rotation.

### Practical ways to track rotation

**Relative performance charts** — comparing a sector ETF (e.g., XLE for energy, XLK for technology) against the S&P 500 (SPY) shows whether that sector is outperforming or underperforming the broad market. A sector that is rising in both absolute price and relative strength terms is genuinely leading.

**Sector breadth** — how many stocks within the sector are participating in a move matters. A sector where only two or three large-cap names are pulling up the average is weaker than one with broad participation.

**Money flow** — tracking weekly fund flows into sector ETFs can give early signals of institutional repositioning before the price move becomes obvious.

**The S&P heatmap** — a quick visual of which sectors are green and which are red on a given day or week reveals the prevailing bias of institutional money.

### Sector rotation and intermarket signals

Sector rotation doesn't happen in isolation. It is triggered by the same macro forces visible in the intermarket framework:

- **Rising bond yields** → rotate toward financials, out of utilities and tech.
- **Rising commodity prices** → rotate toward energy and materials.
- **Flattening/inverted yield curve** → rotate toward defensives.
- **Dollar weakness** → benefits multinationals and commodity producers.

When sector rotation and intermarket signals point in the same direction, the signal is stronger.

### Why it matters for a trader or investor

Even if you only trade individual stocks, knowing which sector is leading the cycle gives you a tailwind. Being in the right sector at the right phase of the cycle is often worth more than picking the single best stock in the wrong sector. Sector rotation is macro analysis made actionable: instead of predicting exact economic data, you simply follow where the money is already going.
