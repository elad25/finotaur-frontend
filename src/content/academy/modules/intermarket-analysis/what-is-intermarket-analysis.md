## In plain words

**Intermarket analysis** is the practice of watching multiple markets at the same time to understand what they are collectively signaling about the economy and risk appetite. Stocks, bonds, commodities, and currencies are not independent — they respond to the same underlying forces and constantly push and pull on each other. When you learn to read those relationships, a move in one market becomes a clue about what is likely to happen in another.

## Quick demo

In early 2022, the U.S. Federal Reserve began aggressively raising interest rates to fight inflation. Bond prices fell sharply, the U.S. dollar surged, and gold — which pays no yield — sold off as real yields climbed. Stocks, especially high-growth technology shares, dropped hard because their valuations depended on low rates. Four separate markets, one cause. A trader watching only the stock market saw a sell-off with no obvious explanation; a trader watching all four saw it coming from the bond market first.

## Full explanation

### Why markets are connected

Every financial market is ultimately a bet on the same thing: future economic conditions. Interest rates, growth expectations, inflation, and risk appetite flow through all asset classes simultaneously. When these forces shift, capital moves — sometimes from bonds into stocks, sometimes from stocks into commodities, sometimes from everything into cash. Intermarket analysis is the discipline of tracking those flows.

The framework was popularized by John Murphy, a technical analyst who observed that major turning points in one market are almost always preceded or confirmed by moves in related markets. His key insight: **technical analysis applied to only one market is like driving while looking through a keyhole.**

### The four pillars

- **Stocks** — reflect growth expectations and corporate earnings. They tend to lead the real economy.
- **Bonds** — reflect interest rate expectations and inflation. Rising bond prices (falling yields) signal slowing growth or falling inflation; falling bond prices signal the opposite.
- **Commodities** — reflect real economic demand and inflation pressure. Rising commodities often confirm economic expansion and signal inflation risk.
- **The U.S. Dollar** — acts as the global reserve currency and a key input price. A strong dollar tends to suppress commodity prices (which are dollar-denominated) and pressure emerging-market assets.

### What intermarket analysis is not

It is not a mechanical system that generates buy and sell signals. It is a framework for **context** — a way to confirm or question what a single chart is telling you. When the stock market rallies but bonds are selling off, credit spreads are widening, and commodities are rolling over, that rally deserves skepticism. When all four pillars align, a move tends to have more staying power.

### Historical relationships to know

- Bonds and stocks usually move in opposite directions (when growth fears dominate), but can move together when inflation is the main driver.
- Commodities and the dollar tend to move in opposite directions.
- Commodity-linked currencies (Australian dollar, Canadian dollar) often track commodity prices.
- Credit spreads — the difference between corporate bond yields and Treasury yields — are one of the earliest warnings of stress in the stock market.

### Why it matters for a trader or investor

Whether you trade equities, commodities, or currencies, you are operating inside a system, not in isolation. Intermarket analysis gives you the ability to identify the macro backdrop your trades are sitting in, spot divergences that warn of trend exhaustion, and avoid being long the wrong asset class at the wrong phase of the cycle. It is not a shortcut to perfect timing — it is the map that keeps you oriented.
