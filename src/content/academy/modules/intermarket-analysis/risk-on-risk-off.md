## In plain words

**Risk-on / risk-off** (often written RORO) describes the two broad modes that markets cycle between based on how investors feel about uncertainty. In **risk-on** mode, investors are confident and chase higher returns: they buy stocks, high-yield bonds, commodities, and currencies from growth-sensitive economies. In **risk-off** mode, fear or uncertainty dominates and capital flows toward safety: U.S. Treasuries, the dollar, the yen, gold, and cash. The shift between the two modes is one of the most visible and tradeable patterns in intermarket analysis.

## Quick demo

In March 2020, when COVID-19 lockdowns began, markets flipped violently to risk-off in a matter of days. U.S. Treasury bonds surged (yields collapsed), the dollar spiked, gold initially sold off (due to forced liquidity selling) then rebounded, and stocks around the world fell 30–40%. Commodity-linked currencies like the Australian and Canadian dollar crashed. When the Federal Reserve announced unlimited quantitative easing in late March, the switch flipped back to risk-on almost overnight: stocks rallied, the dollar weakened, copper and oil recovered, and high-yield spreads compressed. Recognizing the regime saved traders from fighting the tide.

## Full explanation

### What drives the shift

Risk sentiment is driven by the perceived balance between reward and uncertainty. Catalysts that trigger risk-off include:

- **Geopolitical shocks** (wars, elections, sanctions)
- **Credit events** (a major bank failure, sovereign debt crisis)
- **Economic data surprises** (a sudden spike in unemployment or a manufacturing collapse)
- **Central bank policy tightening** that threatens to slow growth

Risk-on conditions tend to prevail when growth is expanding, inflation is manageable, and central banks are accommodative.

### Risk-on assets and behaviors

| Asset / behavior | Risk-on |
|---|---|
| Equities | Rise (especially cyclicals and small-caps) |
| High-yield bonds | Spreads narrow |
| Commodities (copper, oil) | Rise |
| AUD, NZD, CAD | Strengthen |
| JPY, CHF | Weaken (sold as funding currencies) |
| U.S. Treasuries | Sell off (yields rise) |
| VIX | Falls |

### Risk-off assets and behaviors

| Asset / behavior | Risk-off |
|---|---|
| Equities | Fall (especially cyclicals; defensives outperform) |
| High-yield bonds | Spreads widen |
| Commodities | Generally fall (gold is the exception) |
| AUD, NZD, CAD | Weaken |
| JPY, CHF, USD | Strengthen |
| U.S. Treasuries | Rally (yields fall) |
| VIX | Rises |

### Why risk sentiment is a spectrum, not a binary

True "pure" risk-on or risk-off conditions where everything moves in lockstep are relatively rare. Most of the time, markets are in a mixed regime — some sectors risk-on, some defensively positioned. The usefulness of the framework is not that it predicts every move perfectly, but that it gives you a **baseline expectation for correlations**. When the Australian dollar is surging and Treasury yields are falling simultaneously, that divergence is worth questioning — one of them is likely wrong.

### Identifying the current regime

Practical signals to track:

- **VIX level and direction** — a falling VIX suggests risk-on; a rising VIX signals growing caution
- **Credit spreads** — tightening = risk-on; widening = risk-off
- **JPY and CHF** — safe-haven currencies strengthen in risk-off
- **AUD/USD** — often called a "risk barometer" because Australia exports commodities; it rises in risk-on and falls in risk-off

### Why it matters for a trader or investor

Identifying the regime your market is operating in helps you select the right trades and the right sizing. Fighting a risk-off environment by buying heavily cyclical stocks or commodity-linked currencies is swimming against a powerful current. Aligning your exposure with the prevailing risk sentiment — or hedging with safe-haven assets when signals are mixed — dramatically improves your win rate on broader market moves.
