## In plain words

The **business cycle** is the recurring pattern of economic expansion, peak, contraction, and recovery that most developed economies move through over time. Different asset classes tend to perform best at different phases of this cycle — not because of magic, but because the forces driving each phase (growth momentum, interest rates, inflation) directly affect corporate earnings, borrowing costs, and commodity demand in predictable ways. Knowing where you are in the cycle is one of the highest-value inputs an investor can have.

## Quick demo

Think about the 2009–2020 U.S. expansion. In the early phase after 2009, stocks led the recovery, credit spreads collapsed, and rate-sensitive sectors like financials and industrials outperformed. By the middle phase, commodities recovered as industrial demand picked up, and inflation crept higher. In the late phase (2017–2019), defensives like healthcare and utilities started to outperform cyclicals, credit spreads began widening from their tightest levels, and short-term rates rose above long-term rates (yield curve inversion) — a classic late-cycle warning. By early 2020, the recession arrived (accelerated by COVID-19, but the cycle had already aged). Each phase played out largely as the historical framework suggested.

## Full explanation

### The four phases

#### 1. Early cycle (recovery)
The economy emerges from recession. Growth picks up from a low base, monetary policy is still accommodative (rates are low), and credit is loosening. This is typically the strongest period for equities overall, especially:

- **Financials** (benefit from steepening yield curve and lower credit losses)
- **Consumer discretionary** (pent-up demand is released)
- **Industrials** (capex recovery begins)

Commodities are still weak because demand hasn't fully recovered. Bonds have already priced in the rate cuts that occurred during recession.

#### 2. Mid cycle (expansion)
The most sustained phase. Growth is solid, inflation is moderate, and earnings are rising. Credit is readily available. Broad equity exposure tends to work well. **Technology** and **materials** often lead. Commodity demand begins to pick up as industrial activity accelerates. Central banks may begin tightening gradually.

#### 3. Late cycle (slowdown)
Growth is still positive but slowing. Inflation is often elevated and central banks are actively tightening. Profit margins come under pressure from rising input costs (wages, energy). The yield curve typically flattens or inverts. Asset class rotation begins to favor:

- **Energy** (commodities at peak demand)
- **Healthcare and utilities** (defensive earnings stability)
- **Shorter-duration bonds** (less sensitive to rate rises)

High-yield credit spreads widen as debt service becomes more burdensome.

#### 4. Recession
Economic output contracts. Earnings fall. Unemployment rises. Central banks eventually cut rates to stimulate recovery. Safe-haven assets outperform:

- **Long-duration government bonds** (flight to quality + rate cuts)
- **Gold** (store of value + uncertainty)
- **Defensive equities** (consumer staples, utilities)

Commodities sell off due to collapsing demand. High-yield spreads spike.

### Asset class leadership summary

| Cycle phase | Leaders | Laggards |
|---|---|---|
| Early | Equities, high yield, cyclicals | Commodities, defensives |
| Mid | Tech, materials, equities broadly | Short-duration bonds |
| Late | Energy, defensives, commodities | High yield, cyclicals |
| Recession | Treasuries, gold, staples | Equities, commodities |

### Important caveats

The cycle is a framework, not a clock. Phases can last much longer than historical averages. Exogenous shocks (pandemics, wars, policy errors) can compress or distort the sequence. And financial markets move on *expectations* — they price in the next phase before economic data confirms it. This is why equities typically bottom during recession, not after it.

### Why it matters for a trader or investor

Knowing the cycle phase helps you tilt allocations toward the asset classes with the structural tailwind at that moment — and avoid those facing headwinds. Even a rough sense of "early vs. late" dramatically improves the hit rate of sector and asset-class decisions. Combined with intermarket signals from bonds, commodities, and currencies, the business cycle framework provides one of the strongest macro overlays available to any market participant.
