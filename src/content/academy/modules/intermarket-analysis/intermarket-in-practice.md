## In plain words

Intermarket analysis only earns its keep when it changes what you do — not just what you know. **Putting the relationships to work** means building a simple, repeatable routine for checking the four pillars (stocks, bonds, commodities, dollar), identifying the current regime (risk-on vs. risk-off, early vs. late cycle), spotting divergences between markets, and using that context to filter or confirm individual trade setups. Theory without application is just interesting reading.

## Quick demo

In late 2023, the U.S. stock market was rising strongly, but beneath the surface, a careful intermarket reader could see a warning developing. The 10-year Treasury yield climbed above 5% for the first time since 2007 — a major resistance level that had historically preceded equity weakness. Credit spreads began widening quietly. The dollar was strengthening, applying pressure to commodity exporters. The VIX, though low, was beginning to creep up. Each signal alone was dismissible; together they painted a picture of late-cycle stress. Traders who used these signals to reduce equity risk or hedge with put options avoided the sharp 10% pullback that followed in October 2023.

## Full explanation

### Building a weekly intermarket checklist

The most practical way to apply intermarket analysis is to review the same set of charts every week — not to predict the future, but to update your understanding of which regime you are in and whether that has changed.

**Suggested weekly checklist:**

1. **Treasury yields (2-year and 10-year)** — Direction and whether the curve is steepening or flattening.
2. **Credit spreads (HYG or LQD vs. Treasuries)** — Tightening = risk-on; widening = stress.
3. **DXY (U.S. Dollar Index)** — Trend and momentum.
4. **Commodities (DJP, CL for crude, GC for gold, HG for copper)** — Growth signal and inflation signal.
5. **VIX** — Fear gauge; compare to its 20-day moving average for context.
6. **Sector relative strength** — Which sectors are leading and lagging this week?
7. **S&P 500 breadth** — Advance/Decline line, percentage of stocks above their 200-day moving average.

This review takes 15–30 minutes and dramatically improves the quality of decisions made throughout the week.

### Reading divergences

The most actionable intermarket signals come from **divergences** — situations where two markets that should be moving together are not.

Common divergences worth watching:

- **Stocks rising, but credit spreads widening** — equity market is ignoring credit stress; usually resolves by stocks falling.
- **Commodity currencies (AUD, CAD) falling, but equities rising** — growth narrative is being questioned in FX while equities are complacent.
- **Gold and stocks rising together** — suggests uncertainty is elevated even as risk appetite appears high; often precedes volatility.
- **Yield curve inverting while equities make new highs** — historically one of the most reliable warnings of late-cycle fragility.

Divergences don't provide exact timing, but they raise the appropriate question: *which market is right?*

### Applying context to individual trades

Intermarket analysis doesn't tell you when to buy Apple or what strike to sell on a crude oil option. What it does is give you a **regime filter** for your directional bias:

- In a clear risk-on, early-cycle environment: favor long cyclicals, be comfortable with equity exposure, avoid heavy cash drag.
- In a late-cycle environment with widening credit spreads: reduce position sizes, favor defensives, consider hedges.
- In genuine risk-off (spreads spiking, VIX surging, safe havens rallying): the first rule is capital preservation; wait for stabilization before re-entering.

The classic mistake is having a technically perfect long setup on a cyclical stock while the macro regime is screaming risk-off. The individual setup may be valid but the wind is in your face.

### Common pitfalls

- **Paralysis by analysis** — watching too many signals and being unable to act. Solve this by narrowing your checklist to five core inputs and ranking them by current importance.
- **Treating lagging signals as leading signals** — GDP data, for example, confirms what the market already knew six months ago. Bond yields and credit spreads move faster.
- **Over-relying on historical relationships that have temporarily broken** — relationships shift, especially during regime changes (e.g., 2022 when stocks and bonds fell together as inflation dominated). Know why a relationship exists, not just that it exists.

### Why it matters for a trader or investor

Every trade you make is embedded in a macro context whether you acknowledge it or not. The question is whether that context works for you or against you. A consistent intermarket review practice is the highest-leverage habit a market participant can build — not because it eliminates risk, but because it systematically identifies which direction the current environment is pushing, and lets you trade with the tide rather than against it.
