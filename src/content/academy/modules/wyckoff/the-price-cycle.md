## In plain words

Wyckoff observed that markets move through a repeating four-phase cycle driven by the activity of large operators. The phases are **accumulation** (big money quietly buys at low prices), **markup** (price trends upward as the public joins in), **distribution** (big money quietly sells at high prices), and **markdown** (price falls as selling overwhelms demand). Recognizing which phase you are in changes almost every decision you make.

## Quick demo

Think of a single stock over two years. For several months it trades sideways near its lows, with occasional false dips that snap back quickly — that is accumulation. Then it begins a clear uptrend over six months — markup. Near the highs it again goes sideways, but now dips are not bought aggressively and rallies stall on high volume — distribution. Then it rolls over into a sustained downtrend — markdown. The cycle then repeats.

## Full explanation

### Phase 1: Accumulation

Accumulation is the first and most complex phase. After a prolonged downtrend, price enters a wide trading range. Large operators — the "composite operator" in Wyckoff's terminology — begin absorbing shares from the public, which is either panic-selling or simply indifferent at these levels.

Key characteristics:
- **Price range:** wide but non-trending; often volatile at the extremes.
- **Volume:** heavy at the lows (absorption of supply) and during rallies (testing demand), then diminishing as the range matures.
- **False moves:** downside breaks that reverse quickly (springs), designed to shake out weak holders.

Accumulation ends when supply is exhausted and the operator has built a full position.

### Phase 2: Markup

With supply absorbed, even modest demand drives price higher. The uptrend begins, often slowly at first, then accelerating as trend-followers and the general public recognize the move and add their buying.

Key characteristics:
- **Price:** series of higher highs and higher lows.
- **Volume:** generally expands on advancing waves and contracts on pullbacks.
- **Pullbacks:** normal and healthy; often shallow, as demand is strong.

Markup is the phase where those who correctly identified accumulation are rewarded. It ends when price has reached a level where the operator judges that the public's enthusiasm can absorb their inventory.

### Phase 3: Distribution

Distribution mirrors accumulation but in reverse. At the top of the markup, price enters another trading range. Large operators now switch from buying to selling — quietly unloading their holdings into the public's demand.

Key characteristics:
- **Price range:** wide, choppy, often at elevated levels.
- **Volume:** heavy on rallies (operators selling into strength) and on downside breaks.
- **False moves:** upside breaks that quickly fail (upthrusts), designed to attract fresh buying that can be sold into.

Distribution ends when the operator has exited and supply dominates the market.

### Phase 4: Markdown

With large buyers now out or short, even modest selling drives price lower. The downtrend unfolds, often accelerating as stop-losses are triggered and the public capitulates.

Key characteristics:
- **Price:** series of lower highs and lower lows.
- **Volume:** typically expands on declining waves.
- **Rallies:** weak and short-lived; supply hits any attempt to recover.

Markdown ends with a **selling climax** — a high-volume capitulation that exhausts sellers and sets the stage for a new accumulation phase, restarting the cycle.

### Why it matters for a trader

The cycle framework solves one of trading's hardest problems: knowing whether to buy, hold, short, or stay flat. A trader who can identify distribution, for example, will not be buying breakouts near the top. One who recognizes accumulation will not be panic-selling the false dips. Phase identification is not always easy — the edges are fuzzy and real markets are noisy — but even a rough read dramatically improves the quality of your decisions.
