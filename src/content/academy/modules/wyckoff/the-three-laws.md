## In plain words

Wyckoff built his entire framework on three foundational principles. The **Law of Supply and Demand** says price rises when demand exceeds supply and falls when supply dominates. The **Law of Cause and Effect** says that the size of the next trend is proportional to the preparation that preceded it. The **Law of Effort versus Result** says that volume (effort) should confirm price movement (result) — when they diverge, a reversal is often near.

## Quick demo

A stock trades in a tight range for twelve weeks while volume gradually dries up to almost nothing. That long sideways base is the "cause." When it finally breaks out and runs 30 %, that is the proportionate "effect." Had the base only lasted two weeks, Wyckoff would expect a much smaller move. The size of the preparation roughly predicts the size of the campaign.

## Full explanation

### Law 1: Supply and Demand

This is the bedrock. Every price move — on any instrument, in any era — is ultimately the product of supply and demand imbalance:

- **Demand exceeds supply** → price must rise to find willing sellers.
- **Supply exceeds demand** → price must fall to find willing buyers.
- **Supply equals demand** → price moves sideways; a condition Wyckoff calls **equilibrium**, which is unstable and will resolve in one direction.

What makes Wyckoff's application of this law distinctive is his focus on *who* is supplying and demanding. Random retail activity largely cancels out. What matters is the net activity of large, informed operators. Their supply and demand campaigns are what push prices into trends.

### Law 2: Cause and Effect

Wyckoff argued that trending moves do not appear from nowhere. They are the direct effect of a preceding cause built up during a sideways phase:

- **Accumulation** (buying campaign) is the cause → **Markup** (uptrend) is the effect.
- **Distribution** (selling campaign) is the cause → **Markdown** (downtrend) is the effect.

The longer and more absorptive the sideways phase, the more shares have changed hands at similar prices, and the more fuel there is for the eventual move. Wyckoff analysts use **Point and Figure charts** to estimate the horizontal count of a trading range as a rough measure of the potential move — a technique called "counting the cause."

### Law 3: Effort versus Result

Volume is Wyckoff's proxy for effort — the collective force being applied to price. Price movement is the result. These two should be in harmony:

- **High volume + significant price move** → effort and result agree; move has conviction.
- **High volume + small price move** (or a reversal candle) → high effort produced little result; supply or demand is being absorbed; likely turning point.
- **Low volume + healthy price move** → little effort, easy move; trend has little opposition.

This law is why Wyckoff analysts pay close attention to climax bars — moments of extremely high volume that produce little net price progress. A **buying climax** (high volume, price barely moves higher, or reverses) signals that smart money is unloading into the public's enthusiasm. A **selling climax** signals the opposite.

### How the three laws work together

In practice, the three laws are inseparable. You use the Law of Supply and Demand to identify who controls the market at any moment. You use the Law of Cause and Effect to estimate how far the resulting move might go. You use the Law of Effort versus Result to confirm whether the move has genuine backing or is losing steam. Together they form a complete diagnostic lens for reading any chart.

### Why it matters for a trader

Most retail traders act on price alone — the direction of the last few candles. The three Wyckoff laws force you to look deeper: at who is buying and selling, at how long preparation has been building, and at whether volume is confirming what price is doing. That shift in attention — from reaction to analysis — is the difference between chasing the market and reading it.
