## In plain words

**Distribution** is accumulation in reverse: the phase in which large operators quietly sell their long positions at high prices before a sustained downtrend begins. Unable to dump all at once without crashing the price against themselves, they spread the selling campaign over weeks or months, unloading into the public's bullish enthusiasm. By the time the downtrend is obvious, the operators are already out.

## Quick demo

A stock has climbed 80 % over the past year and everyone is bullish. It hits $95, pulls back, then bounces back to $97 on heavy volume — but closes in the middle of its range rather than near the highs. It does this three or four times over eight weeks: volume is big, but price can't hold its gains. That pattern is the distribution campaign. The operator is selling every rally into the eager buyers. When support finally gives way, the stock falls to $60 in two months — the public is stunned; the operator exited months ago.

## Full explanation

### The Wyckoff Distribution Schematic

Like accumulation, distribution has a set of recognizable structural events. The major landmarks are:

- **Preliminary Supply (PSY):** after a long uptrend, unusually large volume appears as operators begin selling into strength. Price can still make new highs, but the effort required is increasing.
- **Buying Climax (BC):** an accelerating, high-volume price surge — often triggered by good news — where the public chases the move and operators sell aggressively. This frequently marks the highest price of the range.
- **Automatic Reaction (AR):** a sharp decline after the BC as buying is exhausted. The AR establishes the lower boundary of the distribution range.
- **Secondary Test (ST):** price rallies back toward the BC high to test whether new demand can push the market to new highs. If volume contracts and price fails to match the BC high, supply is still dominant.
- **Upthrust (UT) / Upthrust After Distribution (UTAD):** a deliberate false breakout above the BC high, designed to attract fresh buyers who can then be sold into. Volume is typically heavy on the upthrust bar but price reverses back into the range quickly. The UTAD is the distribution equivalent of the spring.
- **Sign of Weakness (SOW):** a broad decline on expanding volume that breaks through the AR low, signaling that demand is losing control.
- **Last Point of Supply (LPSY):** a weak, low-volume rally after the SOW — the final chance for operators to sell remaining inventory before the markdown accelerates.
- **Breakdown:** price leaves the range to the downside on strong volume, confirming the transition to markdown.

### How distribution differs from accumulation

The structural events are mirror images, but the psychology is different:

- In accumulation, the dominant emotion in the public is **fear and disinterest** — that is what keeps supply available at low prices.
- In distribution, the dominant emotion is **greed and optimism** — that is what keeps demand available at high prices for operators to sell into.

This is why distribution is often described as the hardest phase to identify: markets are at their most bullish when operators are most aggressively selling.

### Volume clues to watch for

- **Heavy volume on rallies that fail to hold** — the effort (volume) is not producing the result (new highs).
- **Wide-spread down bars on increasing volume** — supply is accelerating.
- **Weak rallies on low volume** — demand is thinning out.
- **Upthrusts that reverse within the bar** — classic supply overwhelming a breakout attempt.

### Why it matters for a trader

Distribution is the most dangerous environment for long traders. The chart still looks constructive — price is near highs, news is positive — but the underlying dynamics have shifted. A trader who can recognize the signs of distribution avoids the worst draw-downs and, if appropriate, can position for the markdown that follows. Catching a short near the LPSY or after the SOW breakout is a classic high-probability Wyckoff trade.
