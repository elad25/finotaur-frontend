## In plain words

Every business has two kinds of costs: **fixed costs** that do not change with output (rent, salaries, insurance) and **variable costs** that rise as you produce more (raw materials, packaging, commission). The **break-even point** is the sales volume where total revenue exactly covers total costs — neither profit nor loss. Below that point, every unit sold still loses money; above it, each extra unit contributes to profit.

## Quick demo

A coffee shop pays $8,000/month in rent, salaries, and insurance — its fixed costs. Each cup of coffee uses $1.50 in ingredients and take-away cups. The shop sells each cup for $5.00, so it earns $3.50 in **contribution margin** per cup. Break-even = $8,000 ÷ $3.50 = approximately 2,286 cups per month. Sell 2,285 cups — losing money. Sell 2,287 — profitable. Every cup above that threshold produces $3.50 in profit.

## Full explanation

### Fixed vs. variable costs

- **Fixed costs (FC)** remain constant regardless of output level: a factory lease, annual software licenses, executive salaries. They exist whether you produce zero units or 10,000.
- **Variable costs (VC)** scale with output: direct labor on an assembly line, raw materials, merchant processing fees. Total variable cost = variable cost per unit × units produced.
- **Total cost (TC)** = FC + VC.

### Contribution margin

**Contribution margin per unit** = selling price per unit − variable cost per unit.

It is the amount each sale contributes toward covering fixed costs, and then toward profit once those fixed costs are fully covered. Contribution margin is the operating lever that makes volume matter so much.

**Contribution margin ratio** = contribution margin per unit ÷ selling price. A ratio of 0.60 means 60 cents of every dollar in revenue flows toward fixed costs and profit.

### The break-even formula

**Break-even quantity** = Fixed costs ÷ Contribution margin per unit.

**Break-even revenue** = Fixed costs ÷ Contribution margin ratio.

### Operating leverage

Businesses with high fixed costs relative to variable costs have **high operating leverage**. Once break-even is cleared, additional revenue drops disproportionately to profit — a small increase in sales produces a large increase in operating income. The flip side: below break-even, losses accumulate fast. Airlines, hotels, and semiconductor fabs are classic high-operating-leverage businesses. Consulting firms or pure software-as-a-service companies at scale can also exhibit this profile.

### Margin of safety

**Margin of safety** = (actual revenue − break-even revenue) ÷ actual revenue.

A 30% margin of safety means revenue can fall 30% before the business stops being profitable. This is a useful stress-test metric when evaluating how a company will hold up in a downturn.

### Beyond the simple model

Real businesses have more nuance:

- **Step-fixed costs** jump at certain output thresholds (hiring a second shift, opening a new warehouse).
- **Semi-variable costs** have a fixed floor plus a variable component (a phone plan with a base fee and per-minute charges).
- **Non-linear variable costs** — bulk discounts from suppliers change the cost curve at volume.

These complexities do not change the core logic; they just require a more granular model.

### Why it matters for a trader or investor

Break-even analysis sits at the heart of every earnings model. When a company is pre-profitability, the key question is: at what revenue run rate does it flip positive? When a profitable company faces a revenue slowdown, the question is: how far can revenue fall before losses appear? Knowing a company's fixed-cost base, contribution margin, and current distance from break-even lets you build a much more resilient earnings estimate — and anticipate where guidance will be cut before it happens.
