## In plain words

**Supply** is how much of something sellers are willing to offer at a given price; **demand** is how much buyers are willing to purchase. When these two forces meet, they set a **market price** and a **quantity traded**. If either side shifts — a drought cuts wheat supply, or a trend spikes demand for sneakers — the price moves until balance is restored.

## Quick demo

A city has 1,000 apartments. At $1,500/month, all are rented. A new tech campus opens and 400 more people want apartments. Landlords see a waiting list and raise rents. Over time rents climb to $2,000, some renters leave or double up, and the market settles at a new equilibrium. Nothing more complex than that drives most price changes in the real world.

## Full explanation

### The demand curve

The demand curve slopes downward: the higher the price, the fewer units buyers want. Factors that shift the entire curve (not just move along it) include:

- **Income** — higher incomes raise demand for most goods.
- **Substitutes and complements** — if coffee gets cheaper, demand for tea may fall.
- **Tastes and expectations** — news that a product will be scarce tomorrow raises demand today.

### The supply curve

The supply curve slopes upward: higher prices give sellers a bigger incentive to produce more. Curve-shifting factors include:

- **Input costs** — cheaper raw materials let producers supply more at the same price.
- **Technology** — better manufacturing lowers cost and shifts supply outward.
- **Number of sellers** — more competitors increase market supply.

### Equilibrium and disequilibrium

**Equilibrium** is where the supply and demand curves cross — the price where quantity supplied equals quantity demanded. Prices are rarely stuck at equilibrium; shocks create temporary surpluses (price above equilibrium, excess supply) or shortages (price below equilibrium, excess demand). The market continuously works back toward balance as buyers and sellers adjust.

### Price controls and distortions

When governments set a **price ceiling** (below equilibrium, e.g., rent control) a shortage forms — quantity demanded exceeds supply. A **price floor** (above equilibrium, e.g., minimum wage) creates a surplus — quantity supplied exceeds demand. Both prevent the signal from reaching producers and consumers cleanly.

### Why it matters for a trader or investor

Supply and demand analysis applies well beyond economics classrooms. A company that controls a scarce resource can protect margins even as input costs rise — pricing power is just demand inelasticity meeting constrained supply. Commodity traders watch crop reports (supply shocks) and industrial data (demand signals) to front-run price moves. Even equity prices are, at the most basic level, the outcome of buy orders meeting sell orders in real time. Understanding the mechanism — what shifts curves, what creates disequilibrium, how fast markets clear — gives you a structural lens to evaluate any price move, not just a reactive one.
