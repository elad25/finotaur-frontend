## In plain words

**Demand elasticity** measures how much the quantity demanded changes when the price changes. If a 10% price hike causes a 20% drop in purchases, demand is **elastic** — buyers are sensitive. If the same hike barely moves purchases, demand is **inelastic** — buyers have little choice. Knowing elasticity tells you whether raising a price increases or decreases a company's total revenue.

## Quick demo

A gasoline station raises prices by 10%. Most commuters still fill up — they need to get to work — so quantity sold drops only 3%. Demand is inelastic (elasticity ≈ 0.3). A luxury restaurant raises prices by 10%. Diners switch to cheaper alternatives, cutting covers by 15%. Demand is elastic (elasticity ≈ 1.5). The gas station earns more revenue; the restaurant earns less. Same price action, opposite results.

## Full explanation

### The elasticity formula

**Price elasticity of demand (PED)** = % change in quantity demanded ÷ % change in price.

- PED > 1 → **elastic**: a price rise hurts revenue (e.g., consumer electronics, airline tickets in economy).
- PED < 1 → **inelastic**: a price rise boosts revenue (e.g., insulin, tobacco, utilities).
- PED = 1 → **unit elastic**: revenue stays flat regardless of the price move.

The sign is almost always negative (higher price, lower quantity), so analysts typically quote the absolute value.

### What drives elasticity?

- **Availability of substitutes** — the more options buyers have, the more elastic demand is.
- **Necessity vs. luxury** — necessities (medicine, electricity) are inelastic; luxuries are elastic.
- **Budget share** — items that consume a large slice of income (cars, houses) tend to be more elastic.
- **Time horizon** — demand is usually more elastic over the long run. If oil prices spike, consumers can't switch cars immediately, but eventually they do.

### Cross-price and income elasticity

- **Cross-price elasticity**: how demand for Product A changes when Product B's price changes. Positive = substitutes (Coke and Pepsi); negative = complements (printers and ink cartridges).
- **Income elasticity**: how demand changes as income rises. **Normal goods** have positive income elasticity; **inferior goods** (instant noodles, cheap beer) see demand fall as incomes rise; **luxury goods** have income elasticity > 1.

### Elasticity and pricing power

A company with inelastic demand can raise prices without losing many customers — that is the definition of **pricing power**. It is one of the most valuable characteristics an analyst looks for in a business. Brands like Hermes, Coca-Cola, or Novo Nordisk sustain high margins partly because their customers have few satisfactory substitutes.

### Why it matters for a trader or investor

When a company announces a price increase, the critical question is whether unit volumes will hold. A business with elastic demand trading at a high multiple is vulnerable — any volume miss following a price hike can collapse the revenue story quickly. Inelastic-demand companies tend to be more resilient during recessions and rate hike cycles, which is why consumer staples and healthcare often outperform late in an economic cycle. Elasticity is not just theory — it is embedded in every earnings model.
