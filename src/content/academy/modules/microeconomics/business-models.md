## In plain words

A **business model** is the mechanism by which a company creates value for customers and captures some of that value as revenue. The same product can be delivered through radically different business models — a piece of software can be sold once for a flat fee, licensed monthly, offered free with ads, or used as a loss-leader to lock customers into a platform. The model chosen determines cost structure, cash flow predictability, scalability, and ultimately how the market should value the business.

## Quick demo

Adobe used to sell Photoshop as a one-time $700 purchase. Revenue was lumpy: big spikes at each version release, then silence. In 2012 they shifted to a $55/month Creative Cloud subscription. Year one, revenue dipped — fewer customers bought in at the lower monthly price. By year three, monthly recurring revenue had eclipsed the old boxed-software peaks, customer churn was measurable and manageable, and Adobe's stock price had roughly tripled. The product barely changed. The business model transformed the company's value.

## Full explanation

### Core business model archetypes

**SaaS (Software as a Service)**
Customers pay a recurring fee — monthly or annually — for access to software hosted in the cloud. Key metrics: **Monthly Recurring Revenue (MRR)**, **Annual Recurring Revenue (ARR)**, **churn rate**, **Net Revenue Retention (NRR)**. High-quality SaaS businesses have NRR above 120%, meaning existing customers expand spending faster than others churn. This produces compounding revenue growth from the existing base alone. Slack, Salesforce, and Snowflake are examples.

**Subscription (non-software)**
The same recurring-payment logic applied to media, physical goods, or services — Netflix, Spotify, Dollar Shave Club. Revenue is predictable; customer acquisition cost (CAC) must be recovered over the **customer lifetime** (LTV). The fundamental test: LTV >> CAC, with a payback period (months to recover CAC from gross profit) under 18-24 months for most healthy businesses.

**Marketplace / Platform**
The company connects two sides — buyers and sellers, riders and drivers, hosts and guests — and takes a **take rate** (typically 10-30%) of each transaction. Revenue scales with **Gross Merchandise Volume (GMV)**. Marketplaces benefit from network effects on both sides and are asset-light once at scale. Risk: disintermediation (buyers and sellers transact directly once they meet, bypassing the platform).

**Transactional / E-commerce**
Revenue recognized per transaction, not recurring. Margins depend on inventory ownership (higher risk, lower margin for merchants that hold stock) versus a pure marketplace model. Key metrics: gross margin, inventory turns, customer repeat rate, basket size.

**Advertising**
Revenue from displaying ads to users whose attention has been aggregated. Scale is essential — ad revenue per user is low, so the business must be enormous to generate meaningful cash. Facebook, Google, and X (Twitter) operate this way. User growth and engagement drive inventory; CPMs (cost per thousand impressions) reflect advertiser demand.

**Hardware / Razor-and-Blades**
A device (the razor) is sold at or below cost; consumables (blades, ink, pods) are sold at high margin indefinitely. Requires customers to be locked into a proprietary consumable. Printers, Nespresso machines, and gaming consoles follow this pattern. The income statement looks weak at the hardware layer; the real business is the recurring consumable.

**Licensing and Royalties**
Intellectual property — patents, brand licenses, software libraries — is leased to third parties. Revenue is high-margin and requires minimal capital; the creator captures value without manufacturing or distribution. QUALCOMM (chip IP), ARM Holdings, and luxury brand licensors operate this way.

### Key metrics across models

| Model | Primary revenue metric | Health indicator |
|---|---|---|
| SaaS | ARR / MRR | NRR, churn, CAC payback |
| Subscription | Subscriber count | LTV/CAC, retention |
| Marketplace | GMV | Take rate, liquidity |
| Advertising | DAU/MAU | CPM, ARPU |
| Transactional | Revenue | Gross margin, repeat rate |
| Licensing | Royalty revenue | IP expiry calendar |

### Why it matters for a trader or investor

The business model determines how to read financial statements, which metrics actually matter, and what multiple the market should assign. A SaaS company should be valued on ARR multiples and NRR trajectory — not on trailing GAAP earnings that may understate true economic performance. A transactional retailer is valued on normalized margins and inventory discipline. Misapplying the wrong framework leads to obvious valuation errors. When a company pivots its business model — as Adobe did, as Microsoft did with Office 365 — the market often misprices the transition because it looks backward at old metrics. Recognizing the model shift early and re-rating the business correctly is a repeatable edge.
