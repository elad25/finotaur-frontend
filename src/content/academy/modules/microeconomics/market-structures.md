## In plain words

**Market structure** describes how many competitors exist in an industry and how much power any single seller has over price. The four main structures range from **perfect competition** (many sellers, no pricing power) to **monopoly** (one seller, maximum control). Understanding where a business sits in this spectrum explains its margins, its risks, and how durable its competitive position really is.

## Quick demo

Consider three businesses: a wheat farmer, a mobile phone carrier, and a local water utility. The farmer sells identical wheat alongside thousands of other farmers and takes whatever the market price is — perfect competition, razor-thin margin. The carrier competes with two or three national rivals — an oligopoly — so they can sustain higher margins without one firm immediately stealing all customers. The water utility has no rivals in its region — a regulated monopoly — so it earns a capped but stable return. Same economy, three entirely different business realities.

## Full explanation

### The four structures

**1. Perfect competition**
- Many buyers and sellers, homogeneous (identical) product.
- No single firm controls price; all are **price takers**.
- Long-run economic profit trends to zero as new entrants compete away margins.
- Examples: commodity agriculture, some foreign-exchange markets.

**2. Monopolistic competition**
- Many firms, but each sells a **differentiated** product (brand, quality, location).
- Firms have limited pricing power — enough to charge a small premium.
- Easy entry and exit; excess profits attract rivals who chip away at them.
- Examples: restaurants, clothing brands, consumer electronics accessories.

**3. Oligopoly**
- A small number of large firms dominate the market.
- Actions of one firm directly affect the others — firms watch each other closely.
- Prices tend to be sticky (reluctant to cut because rivals would match; reluctant to raise unilaterally for fear of losing share).
- Can lead to **tacit collusion** (parallel pricing without explicit agreement).
- Examples: commercial aviation, mobile telecom, major credit-card networks.

**4. Monopoly**
- One firm supplies the entire market.
- Sets its own price, constrained only by how demand falls at higher prices.
- Pure monopolies are rare in private markets; they often arise from regulation, patents, or infrastructure (natural monopoly).
- Examples: regulated utilities, some pharmaceutical patents, certain operating systems.

### Barriers to entry

What keeps a market from flooding with new competitors? Key barriers include:

- **Capital requirements** — building a semiconductor fab costs tens of billions.
- **Economies of scale** — existing giants produce at lower unit cost than any startup could match early on.
- **Network effects** — a platform becomes more valuable as more people use it (see the Network Effects chapter).
- **Regulatory licenses** — banking, telecom, broadcasting all require government approval.
- **Switching costs** — enterprise software vendors retain customers because migration is expensive and risky.
- **Brand loyalty and patents** — legal and psychological barriers that blunt competitive pressure.

### Market power and margins

The less competitive a market structure, the more **market power** firms hold, and the wider the sustainable margin. Analysts measure this through the **Lerner Index** (price minus marginal cost, divided by price) or simply by tracking gross and operating margins relative to industry peers. Persistently high margins in an industry with few players are a strong signal of structural market power — and a moat worth investigating.

### Why it matters for a trader or investor

Market structure is the first filter when evaluating a sector. Airlines are an oligopoly with brutal price competition, high fixed costs, and commodity fuel exposure — structurally difficult. Cloud infrastructure is approaching a three-player oligopoly with rising switching costs — structurally advantaged. Whenever margins in a sector start to compress unexpectedly, ask whether a new entrant has changed the structure or whether an existing competitor has become more aggressive. The most enduring equity returns come from companies that operate in structurally favorable markets and actively defend their position within them.
