## In plain words

**Capital structure** is the mix of **debt** and **equity** a company uses to fund its operations and growth. Equity means selling ownership stakes; debt means borrowing money that must be repaid with interest. Every company has to choose how much of each to use. That choice affects risk, returns, tax bills, and how much control founders and shareholders retain.

## Quick demo

Two identical companies each need $10 million to open a chain of restaurants. Company A raises $10 million by selling equity to investors — no interest payments, but ownership is diluted. Company B borrows $7 million at 6% annual interest and puts in $3 million of equity. In a good year with $1.5 million operating profit, Company B pays $420,000 in interest and keeps $1,080,000 — a 36% return on its $3M equity. Company A keeps the full $1.5 million — a 15% return on its $10M equity. Leverage amplified Company B's return. But in a bad year with only $300,000 operating profit, Company B still owes $420,000 in interest — a loss. Company A survives.

## Full explanation

### The two building blocks

**Equity financing** — selling shares or retaining earnings. Equity holders own a portion of the business and share in profits (dividends, capital gains) but absorb losses. There are no mandatory payments; equity is permanent capital. The cost of equity is implicit — shareholders expect a return commensurate with the risk they bear, typically measured using the **Capital Asset Pricing Model (CAPM)**.

**Debt financing** — borrowing from banks, bond markets, or other lenders. Debt must be repaid on schedule with interest. Interest payments are usually tax-deductible, giving debt a structural cost advantage over equity in most tax jurisdictions. The interest rate demanded reflects the lender's assessment of default risk.

### The leverage effect

Debt amplifies both gains and losses — this is **financial leverage**. The key metric is the **Debt-to-Equity (D/E) ratio**:

- A D/E of 0 means the company is 100% equity-financed (very conservative).
- A D/E of 1 means debt equals equity — moderate leverage.
- A D/E of 3 or more is high leverage — common in capital-intensive industries like real estate, utilities, and airlines.

When operating income exceeds the cost of debt, leverage boosts **Return on Equity (ROE)**. When operating income falls below the cost of debt, leverage accelerates losses — and at the extreme, triggers insolvency.

### Modigliani-Miller and its real-world limits

In a world with no taxes and no bankruptcy costs, Modigliani and Miller proved that capital structure is irrelevant to firm value — what matters is the underlying business. In the real world, two forces pull in opposite directions:

- **Tax shield** — interest is tax-deductible, so debt has a real cost advantage over equity. This pushes companies toward more leverage.
- **Distress costs** — too much debt raises the probability of financial distress, which destroys value (customers lose confidence, suppliers tighten terms, managers make short-sighted decisions). This pushes companies toward less leverage.

The optimal capital structure balances the two. Most companies target a debt level consistent with investment-grade credit ratings to preserve access to capital at reasonable rates.

### Credit ratings and bond markets

Rating agencies (Moody's, S&P, Fitch) assess default risk and assign ratings from AAA (safest) to D (in default). Investment-grade (BBB- and above) bonds carry lower yields; high-yield or "junk" bonds (BB+ and below) demand higher returns. A company that slips below investment grade faces sharply higher borrowing costs and some institutional investors may be forced to sell its bonds — a negative feedback loop during stress.

### Why it matters for a trader or investor

Capital structure shapes almost every financial metric you use. High debt magnifies earnings per share in good times — which can make a leveraged company look cheap on a P/E basis — but that same leverage creates fragility when rates rise or revenue falls. During credit market stress, highly leveraged companies' stocks can gap down dramatically as bond markets price in distress. Always check the balance sheet: net debt, interest coverage (EBIT divided by interest expense), and debt maturity profile. A great business with dangerous leverage is not a great investment.
