## In plain words

**Economic news releases** are scheduled events that reveal the health of a country's economy — things like employment figures, inflation readings, and central bank decisions. Forex markets react to these releases instantly and violently because currency values depend on economic fundamentals. **Trading the news** means positioning around these events, either by anticipating the direction or by reacting to the actual data as it prints.

## Quick demo

The US Non-Farm Payrolls (NFP) report is released on the first Friday of each month at 8:30 AM Eastern. If the consensus estimate is 180,000 new jobs but the actual figure comes in at 280,000, USD strengthens immediately — sometimes 50–100 pips in the first 30 seconds. EUR/USD drops, USD/JPY surges, GBP/USD falls. A trader holding a long EUR/USD position through NFP without a plan can lose a significant portion of their account before they can close the position.

## Full explanation

### The economic calendar

Every trading week contains scheduled news releases with pre-announced times. The most widely followed services are Forex Factory, DailyFX, and Bloomberg. Each release is rated for its likely market impact:

- **High impact (red):** Fed decisions, NFP, CPI, GDP — can move major pairs 50–150 pips instantly.
- **Medium impact (orange):** Retail sales, housing data, trade balance.
- **Low impact (yellow):** Usually ignored by the market or only relevant to exotic pairs.

### The most market-moving releases

| Release | Frequency | Pairs most affected |
|---|---|---|
| Non-Farm Payrolls (NFP) | Monthly (1st Friday) | All USD pairs |
| CPI (Consumer Price Index) | Monthly | All USD pairs (or EUR, GBP depending on country) |
| Federal Reserve decision | 8x per year | All USD pairs |
| ECB decision | ~8x per year | EUR pairs |
| GDP | Quarterly | Country-specific |
| ISM Manufacturing/Services PMI | Monthly | USD pairs |
| Retail Sales | Monthly | USD, GBP, EUR |

### How news moves price

Markets trade on **expectations**, not just results. Prices before a release reflect the consensus estimate. The actual result moves price only by the amount it **deviates from expectations**:

- **Beat:** data comes in better than expected → currency strengthens
- **Miss:** data worse than expected → currency weakens
- **In-line:** data matches expectations → minimal reaction (sometimes a "buy the rumor, sell the fact" reversal)

### The "spike and reverse" pattern

A common phenomenon during high-impact news:

1. The report prints — price spikes violently in one direction.
2. Within seconds to minutes, algorithms and institutional traders assess whether the move was justified.
3. Price reverses partially or fully if the initial move was an overcorrection or if secondary details in the report contradict the headline.

This makes news moments extremely dangerous for retail traders using market orders — slippage and spread widening can turn a correct directional bet into a loss.

### Strategies for trading news

**Pre-news positioning:** Take a position before the release based on expectation of a beat or miss. High risk — if wrong, you exit into a spiking market.

**The straddle:** Place a buy-stop above and a sell-stop below current price before the release, aiming to catch the move in whichever direction. The risk is a spike in both directions that triggers both orders, resulting in two losses.

**Post-spike reaction:** Wait for the initial spike to settle (30–60 seconds to several minutes), then trade the continuation or reversal based on the technical structure after the move. This is the approach most professional retail traders prefer — it sacrifices some of the initial move but avoids the worst slippage.

**Avoidance:** Simply close positions or stay flat during high-impact releases. This is a legitimate risk management choice, not a failure of nerve.

### Why it matters for a trader

Even traders who never intentionally trade the news must understand the calendar. Being in a position when a high-impact event prints without a plan — no stop, no intention — is one of the most common ways retail accounts take large unexpected losses. Marking the economic calendar and deciding consciously whether to be in a trade before each major event is a basic discipline in professional forex trading.
