## In plain words

**Currency correlations** describe how consistently two pairs move in the same or opposite direction. A **positive correlation** means the pairs tend to move together; a **negative correlation** means they tend to move in opposite directions. Understanding correlations prevents you from accidentally doubling your risk by holding positions in two pairs that effectively move as one.

## Quick demo

EUR/USD and GBP/USD are historically positively correlated — often above 0.85 — because both pairs share USD on one side and both track broader dollar sentiment. A trader who is long EUR/USD and long GBP/USD, thinking they are two separate trades, is actually running nearly double the dollar exposure. When the dollar strengthens, both positions lose simultaneously. What looked like diversification was concentration in disguise.

## Full explanation

### Measuring correlation

Correlation is measured on a scale from −1 to +1:

- **+1.0** — perfect positive correlation: pairs move identically, tick for tick.
- **0** — no correlation: moves are independent.
- **−1.0** — perfect negative correlation: when one rises, the other falls by the same amount.

In practice, forex correlations range from about −0.95 to +0.95 and are never truly fixed. They shift with market conditions, central bank cycles, and global risk sentiment. Correlations are typically calculated over rolling windows — 1 month, 3 months, 1 year — and shorter windows are more relevant for active traders.

### Common positive correlations

| Pair A | Pair B | Typical correlation | Reason |
|---|---|---|---|
| EUR/USD | GBP/USD | +0.80 to +0.95 | Both priced vs. USD; European macro linkage |
| AUD/USD | NZD/USD | +0.80 to +0.90 | Both commodity-linked, similar risk profile |
| AUD/USD | Gold | +0.60 to +0.80 | Australia is a major gold exporter |
| EUR/USD | AUD/USD | +0.50 to +0.80 | Both rise vs. USD in risk-on environments |

### Common negative correlations

| Pair A | Pair B | Typical correlation | Reason |
|---|---|---|---|
| EUR/USD | USD/CHF | −0.80 to −0.95 | USD is base in one, quote in the other |
| EUR/USD | USD/JPY | −0.50 to −0.80 | USD base vs. USD quote; varies with risk |
| AUD/USD | USD/JPY | −0.60 to −0.80 | Risk-on/risk-off axis; yen and AUD move opposite |

### The commodity-currency link

**AUD, NZD, and CAD** are often called commodity currencies because their economies are heavily tied to raw material exports. They tend to correlate positively with:
- Oil price (especially CAD/USD, since Canada exports oil)
- Gold and iron ore (AUD)
- Dairy and agricultural prices (NZD)

When commodity prices rise globally, these currencies often strengthen against the dollar.

### The safe-haven currencies

**JPY** and **CHF** strengthen in risk-off environments as capital flows into perceived safe havens. They tend to be negatively correlated with risk-sensitive assets during crises, making them natural diversifiers — or instruments for hedging equity exposure.

### Practical implications for position sizing

If you are long EUR/USD and long GBP/USD with 1 standard lot each, and the correlation is 0.90, you are effectively running 1.9 lots of USD exposure — not 2 independent trades. This matters for:

- **Maximum portfolio risk** — correlated positions compound losses.
- **Drawdown math** — a single macro event can hit all correlated positions simultaneously.
- **Hedge logic** — a short USD/CHF partly hedges a long EUR/USD because they are negatively correlated.

### Why it matters for a trader

Correlation awareness is not optional in forex because all pairs share currencies with each other. Ignoring correlations leads to false diversification and accidental overexposure to a single theme — usually the dollar's strength or weakness. Checking correlation before opening a second position is a simple practice that materially reduces the risk of getting wiped out by a single macro move.
