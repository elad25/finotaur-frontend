## In plain words

The **carry trade** is a strategy of borrowing money in a currency with a low interest rate and investing it in a currency with a high interest rate, pocketing the **interest rate differential** — called the **carry** — as profit. It is one of the most widely used strategies by institutional forex traders and hedge funds. The trade earns every day the position is held, but it comes with a significant risk: sharp reversals when market sentiment turns.

## Quick demo

The Reserve Bank of Australia sets its rate at 4.35% while the Bank of Japan holds at 0.10%. A trader borrows JPY at near-zero cost, converts to AUD, and deposits it — earning roughly 4.25% annually on the interest differential. On a $100,000 position, that is approximately $4,250 per year just from the carry, before any currency price movement. If AUD/JPY also rises, the directional gain adds on top. If AUD/JPY falls sharply, however, those currency losses can wipe out months of carry income in days.

## Full explanation

### The mechanics

Carry works because currencies carry the interest rate of their home central bank. When you hold a long position in a higher-yielding currency overnight, your broker credits the interest rate differential to your account (the **positive swap**). If you hold the lower-yielding currency long, you pay the differential.

**Positive carry:** Long AUD/JPY — receive the Australian rate, pay the Japanese rate. Net credit daily.
**Negative carry:** Short AUD/JPY — pay the Australian rate, receive the Japanese rate. Net debit daily.

Swap rates are quoted in pips per day by your broker. They compound over time in extended positions.

### Why it works — and why it fails

The carry trade is structural, not speculative. Central banks set rates based on domestic inflation and growth cycles, and rate differentials can persist for months or years. Funds pour into high-yield currencies, which tends to push those currencies higher — reinforcing the trade on two fronts (carry income + price appreciation).

The failure mode is called **carry unwind**:

- A global risk shock (financial crisis, geopolitical event, pandemic) triggers a flight to safety.
- Investors rush to close high-risk, high-leverage carry positions simultaneously.
- High-yield currencies collapse; low-yield safe havens (JPY, CHF) surge.
- The unwind is violent and fast — years of carry income can evaporate in a week.

The 2008 financial crisis saw AUD/JPY fall over 45% in six months as the carry trade violently reversed.

### Popular carry pairs

| Long (high yield) | Short (low yield) | Historical carry differential |
|---|---|---|
| AUD | JPY | Variable; often 2–4%+ |
| NZD | JPY | Similar range |
| MXN | JPY | High carry, high risk |
| TRY | JPY / EUR | Very high carry, very high risk |

Emerging market currencies like MXN and TRY offer larger differentials but come with political and economic instability that can overwhelm the carry income.

### Signals that carry trades are under stress

- **VIX spikes** — rising volatility signals risk-off, the enemy of carry.
- **JPY strengthening broadly** — unwinding of yen-funded positions.
- **Correlation breakdown** — when multiple high-yielders fall simultaneously.

### Why it matters for a trader

Even if you never explicitly run a carry trade, understanding carry explains why certain pairs trend persistently in one direction, why the yen strengthens violently in crises, and why emerging market currencies have such extreme boom-bust cycles. When you trade AUD/JPY, GBP/JPY, or exotic pairs, carry is part of the landscape — whether you are aware of it or not. Awareness turns noise into signal.
