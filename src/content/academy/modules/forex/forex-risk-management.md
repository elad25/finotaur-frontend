## In plain words

Forex risk management is the set of rules and habits that determine how much you can lose on any trade, any day, and in any drawdown period. Because forex offers high leverage and 24-hour exposure, losses can compound far faster than in most other markets. **Surviving** long enough to improve is the prerequisite to everything else — and it requires hard limits on position size, daily loss, and leverage use.

## Quick demo

A trader with a $10,000 account uses a 1% risk rule: no more than $100 at risk per trade. Their stop loss is 30 pips away on EUR/USD. They calculate the correct lot size: $100 ÷ (30 pips × $10/pip) = 0.33 standard lots. Even if they lose five trades in a row — an ordinary losing streak — they have lost $500, or 5% of their account. They are still in the game. The trader who sizes based on "how much I can afford to lose" rather than this formula often blows up within weeks.

## Full explanation

### The foundation: risk per trade

The most important single number in forex risk management is your **risk per trade** expressed as a percentage of your account. Common professional guidelines:

- **1% per trade:** a 10-trade losing streak = 10% drawdown. Recoverable.
- **2% per trade:** same streak = 20% drawdown. More painful but manageable.
- **5% per trade:** same streak = 40% drawdown. Requires a 67% gain just to break even.

New traders should start at 0.5%–1%. Sizing discipline is more important than win rate.

### Stop losses — mandatory, not optional

In forex, a stop loss is not optional. Holding positions without stops in a leveraged, 24-hour market means a gap or overnight spike can exceed your entire account balance.

Key principles:
- **Place stops based on the chart** — at the level where your trade idea is invalidated, not at a round pip distance.
- **Never move a stop wider** once the trade is open to avoid being stopped out.
- **Account for spread** — your stop executes at the bid/ask, not the mid, so place it slightly beyond the structural level.

### Daily loss limits

Professional traders set a hard daily loss limit — a maximum they will accept losing in a single session:
- **3–5% of account** is a common benchmark for retail traders.
- When the daily limit is hit, stop trading entirely for the day.

This prevents the catastrophic behavior of revenge trading after losses, which is one of the primary ways accounts blow up.

### Leverage management

Never use the maximum leverage your broker offers. The practical formula:

**Effective leverage = Total open notional ÷ Account equity**

Keep effective leverage below 10:1 as a general rule. At 10:1 effective leverage, a 1% adverse move = 10% account loss. At 50:1, a 1% move = 50% account loss.

### Managing correlated positions

Do not hold multiple positions that are positively correlated without accounting for combined exposure. Long EUR/USD + long GBP/USD is nearly 2x USD short exposure, not two independent 1% risks — it is closer to 2% per correlated move.

### Overnight and weekend risk

- **Overnight holds:** accumulate swap costs and expose you to news from other time zones.
- **Weekend holds:** the market can gap significantly on Monday's open if major news breaks Saturday or Sunday. Consider reducing position size or closing before Friday close if you cannot manage the gap risk.

### The drawdown recovery math

Understanding this table makes conservative sizing feel rational:

| Drawdown | Gain needed to recover |
|---|---|
| 10% | 11% |
| 20% | 25% |
| 30% | 43% |
| 50% | 100% |
| 75% | 300% |

Protecting capital is not defensive — it is mathematically superior to chasing returns with large risk.

### Why it matters for a trader

Forex is not more profitable than other markets; it is faster and more leveraged. Without a risk management framework enforced before each trade, the leverage that attracts traders becomes the mechanism that destroys them. Every professional forex trader operates within explicit risk rules — not because they lack confidence, but because they understand that staying in the game is the compounding edge that no strategy or analysis can replace.
