## In plain words

Every forex trade involves buying one currency and selling another simultaneously — the two are always quoted together as a **currency pair**. Pairs are grouped into **majors** (the most traded, all involving USD), **minors** (cross pairs without USD), and **exotics** (one major currency paired with a currency from a smaller economy). The first currency listed is the **base currency**; the second is the **quote currency**.

## Quick demo

EUR/USD quoted at 1.0870 means 1 euro buys 1.0870 US dollars. If you think the euro will strengthen, you buy EUR/USD (buy euros, sell dollars). If the rate moves from 1.0870 to 1.0920, you gained 50 pips. If instead it falls to 1.0820, you are down 50 pips. You never trade euros in isolation — you always trade them against something.

## Full explanation

### Reading a pair

In any pair like EUR/USD:
- **EUR** = base currency (the one you are buying or selling)
- **USD** = quote currency (the one you are pricing the base in)
- Price = how many units of the quote currency buy one unit of the base

When you **buy** EUR/USD, you buy euros and sell dollars. When you **sell** EUR/USD, you sell euros and buy dollars. The pair always moves in one direction — there is no "neutral" position.

### Major pairs

Majors involve USD on one side and account for the bulk of daily forex volume:

| Pair | Nickname | Description |
|---|---|---|
| EUR/USD | "The Fiber" | Euro vs. US Dollar — highest volume globally |
| GBP/USD | "Cable" | British Pound vs. US Dollar |
| USD/JPY | "Ninja" | US Dollar vs. Japanese Yen |
| USD/CHF | "Swissy" | US Dollar vs. Swiss Franc |
| USD/CAD | "Loonie" | US Dollar vs. Canadian Dollar |
| AUD/USD | "Aussie" | Australian Dollar vs. US Dollar |
| NZD/USD | "Kiwi" | New Zealand Dollar vs. US Dollar |

Majors have the tightest spreads, deepest liquidity, and the most reliable technical behavior.

### Minor pairs (cross pairs)

Minors are pairs of two major currencies with no USD:
- EUR/GBP, EUR/JPY, GBP/JPY ("The Dragon"), EUR/CHF, AUD/JPY

These tend to have wider spreads and slightly less liquidity than majors, but they offer diversified opportunities — especially when you have a view on two non-USD economies.

### Exotic pairs

Exotics pair a major currency with one from an emerging or smaller economy:
- USD/MXN, EUR/TRY, USD/ZAR, USD/SGD, USD/HUF

Characteristics of exotics:
- **Wide spreads** — your entry cost is much higher
- **Lower liquidity** — slippage is more common
- **Political and economic volatility** — central bank interventions and policy surprises hit harder

Exotics are not beginner instruments. Many retail traders ignore them entirely.

### Why pairs move the way they do

A pair's price reflects the relative strength of two economies, two interest rate policies, and two risk environments simultaneously. EUR/USD, for example, rises when:
- European economic data beats expectations while US data disappoints, or
- The Fed cuts rates while the ECB holds, or
- Global risk-off sentiment drives flows away from USD

Understanding which forces dominate in a given market environment is the deeper skill in forex analysis.

### Why it matters for a trader

Choosing which pairs to trade is as important as choosing when to trade. New traders often scatter across too many pairs. Professionals often specialize in two or three pairs they understand deeply — knowing their typical daily range, how they react to news, and which sessions they move in. Start with majors; master the mechanics before moving to minors or exotics.
