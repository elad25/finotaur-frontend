## In plain words

**Leverage** in forex lets you control a position much larger than the cash you deposit. A 50:1 leverage ratio means $1,000 in your account can control $50,000 worth of currency. **Margin** is the deposit your broker holds as collateral while the position is open — it is not a fee, but a security against potential losses. Leverage amplifies both profits and losses by the same multiple.

## Quick demo

You have $2,000 and use 50:1 leverage to open a $100,000 (1 standard lot) EUR/USD position. Your required margin is $2,000 — your entire account. EUR/USD moves 100 pips against you, equaling a $1,000 loss (100 pips × $10/pip). You have lost 50% of your account in a single trade. With no leverage, that same 100-pip move on $2,000 worth of euros would have cost $20 — a 1% loss. The leverage multiplied the pain by 50.

## Full explanation

### How leverage works

Leverage is expressed as a ratio:
- **50:1** means $1 controls $50 of notional value
- **100:1** means $1 controls $100
- **10:1** means $1 controls $10

Retail leverage limits vary by region. In the United States, regulated retail forex leverage is capped at 50:1 for majors and 20:1 for minors. In the EU, ESMA caps are 30:1 for majors. Some offshore brokers offer 500:1, which is nearly guaranteed to destroy inexperienced accounts.

### Margin — the collateral

**Initial margin** is the amount locked up to open a position. It is calculated as:

**Margin required = (Trade size) ÷ (Leverage ratio)**

Example: 1 standard lot (100,000 units) at 50:1 leverage = $2,000 margin required.

**Free margin** = Equity − Used Margin. This is what remains available to open new trades or absorb losses.

**Margin level** = (Equity ÷ Used Margin) × 100%. Brokers typically issue a **margin call** when margin level falls below 100% and force-close positions (a **stop-out**) around 50% — though exact levels vary by broker.

### The real cost of leverage

Leverage does not add risk by itself — it multiplies the risk relative to your capital. Consider:

| Leverage | Position size | 1% move against you | Loss as % of $2,000 |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1:1 | $2,000 | $20 | 1% |
| 10:1 | $20,000 | $200 | 10% |
| 50:1 | $100,000 | $1,000 | 50% |
| 100:1 | $200,000 | $2,000 | 100% (wiped) |

### Overnight financing (swap)

When you hold a leveraged forex position overnight, you pay or receive **swap** (also called rollover). The rate depends on the interest rate differential between the two currencies in the pair. Holding high-leverage positions for days accumulates swap costs that can erode profits.

### Using leverage responsibly

Professional retail traders typically use effective leverage of 5:1 to 10:1 regardless of what the broker allows. The standard framework:

1. Decide your max risk per trade in dollars (e.g., 1% of account = $20 on a $2,000 account).
2. Set your stop loss distance in pips based on the chart — not the margin limit.
3. Calculate the position size that makes those dollars match that stop.
4. The leverage ratio falls out of the math automatically.

The broker's maximum leverage is the ceiling. Your position sizing process is the floor you actually use.

### Why it matters for a trader

Leverage is the most common reason new forex traders lose everything in weeks rather than months. Understanding that leverage amplifies losses at the same rate as profits — and that brokers will close your positions automatically if your margin runs out — changes how you approach every single trade. The goal is not to maximize leverage; it is to calibrate risk so that a string of losses does not end your ability to trade.
