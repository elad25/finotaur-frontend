## In plain words

**Central banks** are the single most powerful driver of currency values over the medium and long term. When a central bank raises **interest rates**, its currency typically strengthens — higher rates attract foreign capital seeking better returns. When it cuts rates or expands its money supply, the currency tends to weaken. Every major currency pair reflects an ongoing comparison of two central banks' policies and credibility.

## Quick demo

In 2022, the US Federal Reserve began one of the most aggressive rate-hike cycles in decades, raising rates from near 0% to over 5% in roughly 18 months. During the same period, the Bank of Japan maintained near-zero rates. The result: USD/JPY surged from roughly 115 to 151 — a 31% move — driven almost entirely by the growing interest rate gap between the two central banks. That single policy divergence was one of the largest forex trends in a generation.

## Full explanation

### Why interest rates move currencies

When a country's rates rise, its bonds and cash deposits offer better returns. Foreign investors sell their domestic assets, convert to that currency, and invest in the higher-yielding market. This demand strengthens the currency. The mechanism is called **capital flows**.

The key variable is not the absolute rate but the **rate differential** between two countries — and, crucially, the *expected future path* of that differential.

### What traders watch

**Policy meetings:** Central banks hold scheduled meetings (the Fed meets eight times per year) and release rate decisions, statements, and press conferences. Prices often move most violently at these events.

**Forward guidance:** Markets price future rate expectations, not just current rates. If the Fed signals it will hold rates at current levels for longer, the dollar can strengthen even if it does not raise rates at that meeting.

**Inflation data:** Since central banks target inflation (typically 2%), CPI and PCE readings directly influence rate expectations — and therefore currencies.

**Growth data:** GDP, employment, and PMI readings shape expectations for how long a tightening or easing cycle will last.

### Market pricing tools

The **Fed funds futures** market and **overnight index swaps (OIS)** allow traders to see what probability the market assigns to a rate move at the next meeting. When actual decisions diverge from these expectations, the largest currency moves happen. A "hawkish surprise" (more tightening than expected) strengthens the currency; a "dovish surprise" (less tightening or more easing) weakens it.

### Central bank intervention

Central banks sometimes intervene directly in the forex market — buying or selling their own currency. The Bank of Japan has intervened multiple times to slow yen weakness. The SNB famously abandoned its EUR/CHF peg in January 2015, causing CHF to surge 30% in minutes. These events are rare but can be market-moving within hours.

### Why it matters for a trader

No chart pattern or technical indicator overrides a central bank policy shift. A trader who understands the divergence between two central banks — one hiking, one cutting — has a macro tailwind behind their position that can last months. Ignoring central banks in forex is like ignoring earnings season in equities. The fundamentals drive the trend; the technicals help you time the entry.
