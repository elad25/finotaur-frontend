## In plain words

In forex, price moves are measured in **pips** — typically the fourth decimal place of a currency pair — and position sizes are measured in **lots**. A standard lot is 100,000 units of the base currency, a mini lot is 10,000 units, and a micro lot is 1,000 units. Getting these numbers right is the foundation of **position sizing**: knowing exactly how much money you risk per trade before you place it.

## Quick demo

You buy EUR/USD at 1.08500 and your stop loss is at 1.08300 — a distance of 20 pips. You are trading 1 mini lot (10,000 EUR). Each pip on a mini lot is worth approximately $1 (in a USD-denominated account). Your total risk is 20 pips × $1 = $20. If your account is $2,000 and you want to risk no more than 1%, you should risk at most $20 per trade — exactly what this position risks. The math lines up cleanly.

## Full explanation

### What is a pip?

A **pip** (percentage in point) is the conventional smallest price increment quoted for most currency pairs. For pairs quoted to four decimal places — like EUR/USD (1.0850) or GBP/USD (1.2740) — one pip is 0.0001, or 1 in the last quoted digit.

**JPY pairs** are the main exception: they are quoted to two decimal places (USD/JPY at 149.80), so one pip = 0.01.

Many brokers now quote a fifth decimal place (called a **pipette** or fractional pip): 1.08504. The pipette is one-tenth of a pip and is used for tighter spread display, not your primary measurement.

### Pip value — the dollars behind the pips

The monetary value of a pip depends on three things:
1. The pair being traded
2. The lot size
3. Your account's base currency

For pairs where USD is the quote currency (EUR/USD, GBP/USD):
- **Standard lot (100,000 units):** 1 pip = $10
- **Mini lot (10,000 units):** 1 pip = $1
- **Micro lot (1,000 units):** 1 pip = $0.10

For pairs where USD is the base currency (USD/JPY, USD/CAD), the pip value fluctuates slightly with the exchange rate but is approximately:
- Standard lot: ~$9–$10 per pip (varies with current rate)

### Lots explained

| Lot type | Units | Approx. pip value (EUR/USD) |
|---|---|---|
| Standard | 100,000 | $10 |
| Mini | 10,000 | $1 |
| Micro | 1,000 | $0.10 |
| Nano | 100 | $0.01 |

Retail platforms typically let you trade in fractions of a lot (e.g., 0.25 lots = 25,000 units = $2.50/pip on EUR/USD).

### Connecting pip value to position sizing

The correct position sizing formula:

**Lots = (Account Risk $) ÷ (Stop Distance in Pips × Pip Value per Lot)**

Example:
- Account: $5,000
- Risk per trade: 1% = $50
- Stop distance: 25 pips
- Instrument: EUR/USD, pip value = $10/pip per standard lot

Lots = $50 ÷ (25 × $10) = $50 ÷ $250 = 0.20 standard lots (= 2 mini lots)

This is not optional math — it is how professional forex traders size every single trade.

### Why it matters for a trader

Pips and lots are the language of forex risk. Without understanding them, you are guessing at your actual dollar exposure — and with leverage available in forex, guessing at exposure is how accounts get wiped quickly. Mastering this math transforms abstract chart moves into concrete dollars-at-risk numbers you can control before every trade.
