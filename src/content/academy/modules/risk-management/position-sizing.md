## In plain words

**Position sizing** is deciding how many shares, contracts, or units to buy or sell on a single trade. It answers the question: "given my account and this setup, how much should I put at risk?" Getting this number right matters more than picking the right entry — a great trade in too large a size can still blow up an account.

## Quick demo

Suppose you have a $20,000 account and decide to risk no more than 1% per trade — that is $200. Your stop loss is $4 below your entry price. Divide the dollar risk by the stop distance: $200 ÷ $4 = 50 shares. You buy exactly 50 shares. If you're wrong and price hits your stop, you lose $200. If you sized to "feel," you might have bought 500 shares and lost $2,000 instead.

## Full explanation

### Why size is the most important variable

Most traders obsess over entry signals, but the brutal arithmetic of trading is this: a losing trade in a position that is too large can erase weeks of small wins. Position sizing is the control knob that determines how much any single trade can hurt you.

### The percent-risk method

The most widely taught approach:

1. **Set your max risk per trade** — typically 1% or 2% of account equity (see the Money Management chapter for the logic).
2. **Identify your stop price** — the level at which the trade idea is invalidated.
3. **Calculate the stop distance** — entry price minus stop price (for longs).
4. **Divide:** position size = (account × risk%) ÷ stop distance.

Example: $50,000 account, 1% risk = $500 max loss. Stop is $2.50 away. Size = $500 ÷ $2.50 = 200 shares.

### Fixed dollar vs. percent of equity

- **Fixed dollar** — risk the same dollar amount every trade (e.g., always $300). Simple, but as your account grows or shrinks the dollar amount stays constant, so effective risk percentage drifts.
- **Percent of equity** — risk a fixed percentage of your *current* balance each time. The size compounds with your growth and contracts during drawdowns, naturally reducing exposure when you're losing.

### Volatility-adjusted sizing

A stock that swings 5% a day is not the same risk as one that swings 1% a day, even if the dollar stop is identical. Some traders scale size inversely with the asset's Average True Range (ATR) — tighter-range assets get larger size, wider-range assets get smaller size — so each trade carries approximately equal volatility exposure.

### Common mistakes

- **Over-sizing on conviction** — feeling certain does not change the math. A 100% confident trade that goes wrong at a large size is catastrophic.
- **Ignoring liquidity** — a large size in a thinly traded stock can move the price against you or trap you on exit.
- **Inconsistent sizing** — ad-hoc sizes make it impossible to measure your true edge over time.

### Why it matters for a trader

Consistent position sizing is what turns a positive edge into actual profits over time. Without it, even a system that wins more than it loses can produce a losing equity curve if big losses outpace small wins. Proper sizing also keeps you psychologically stable — when no single trade can seriously damage the account, decision-making stays cleaner.
