## In plain words

**Diversification** means spreading money across multiple positions so that no single loss can devastate the whole account. **Allocation** is the deliberate choice of how much of the portfolio to assign to each position, sector, or asset class. Together they reduce the impact of any one bad outcome — not by preventing losses, but by ensuring that losses stay local rather than systemic.

## Quick demo

You put 100% of your trading capital into a single tech stock. It drops 40% after a bad earnings report. Your account is down 40%. Now imagine you had spread the same capital across ten stocks in five different sectors. That same stock still drops 40%, but it represents only 10% of the account — the damage is 4%, not 40%. The trade was just as bad; the outcome was four times less damaging.

## Full explanation

### The logic of uncorrelated bets

Diversification works when positions move somewhat independently of each other. If you hold five different tech stocks and tech sells off, all five fall together — that is concentration masquerading as diversification. True diversification requires **low correlation**:

- Stocks and government bonds often move in opposite directions during risk-off events.
- Different sectors (energy, healthcare, industrials) respond to different economic drivers.
- Different time frames (a day trade and a long-term position) have different risk windows.

### Asset allocation frameworks

**Allocation** structures this at a higher level:

- **By asset class** — e.g., 60% equities, 30% bonds, 10% alternatives. The classic "60/40" portfolio is a simple allocation.
- **By sector** — ensuring no single industry represents too large a share. Many risk frameworks cap a single sector at 20–25%.
- **By position** — no single trade exceeds a set percentage of the portfolio (commonly 2–5% per position in a diversified stock portfolio).
- **By strategy** — active traders sometimes run separate buckets: a swing-trading book, a long-term book, and a cash reserve.

### Concentration vs. diversification

Diversification has real costs:

- It limits upside — if one position doubles, a diversified portfolio benefits less.
- Over-diversification ("deworsification") — owning 50 positions dilutes any genuine edge.
- False diversification — buying many assets that behave similarly adds complexity without real protection.

Concentrated traders (e.g., 5–10 high-conviction positions) accept more volatility for potentially higher returns. Diversified portfolios seek smoother equity curves at the cost of capped upside. Neither is objectively superior — the right approach depends on your edge, capital size, and risk tolerance.

### Correlation during crises

One critical warning: correlations across assets tend to increase during market crises. In the 2008 financial crash and the 2020 COVID sell-off, almost all risk assets fell together — stocks, real estate, corporate bonds, commodities. Diversification reduced, but did not eliminate, drawdowns. Cash and short positions were the only true hedges.

### Practical rules for active traders

- Avoid more than 20–30% of capital in trades that share the same catalyst (e.g., all move with oil prices).
- Scale back total exposure when the overall market is in a high-correlation, risk-off regime.
- Keep a cash reserve. Cash is a position — it provides optionality when better setups appear.

### Why it matters for a trader

Diversification and allocation are the architectural choices that determine how much a bad period can damage your account. A sound allocation means that even when a strategy underperforms for weeks, the account remains intact and the trader can continue. Survival is the prerequisite for compounding.
