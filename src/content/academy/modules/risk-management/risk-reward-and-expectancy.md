## In plain words

**Risk/reward ratio** compares how much you stand to gain on a trade to how much you stand to lose. **Expectancy** takes that one step further by weighing those gains and losses against your actual win rate, giving you the average dollar result per trade over time. A high win rate does not guarantee profits; a low win rate does not guarantee losses — expectancy is the number that really matters.

## Quick demo

You win 40% of your trades. Each winner returns $300; each loser costs $100. Your expectancy = (0.40 × $300) + (0.60 × −$100) = $120 − $60 = **+$60 per trade**. Now flip it: you win 70% of trades but winners make $50 and losers cost $200. Expectancy = (0.70 × $50) + (0.30 × −$200) = $35 − $60 = **−$25 per trade**. The "high win rate" system is actually a money-loser.

## Full explanation

### Risk/reward ratio

The **R/R ratio** is the ratio of potential profit to potential loss on a specific trade:

- Entry at $50, stop at $48, target at $56.
- Risk = $2, Reward = $6 → R/R = 3:1 (or 3R).

A 3:1 R/R means you only need to be right 25% of the time to break even (ignoring commissions). The math: 1 win × $6 = $6; 3 losses × $2 = $6. Any win rate above 25% is profitable.

### Why minimum R/R matters

Most professional traders require at least a 1.5:1 or 2:1 ratio before entering a trade. This discipline:

- Creates a margin for error — you can be wrong more often than right and still profit.
- Forces you to identify a realistic target before entering, not after.
- Prevents chasing low-reward setups simply because they look "safe."

### Expectancy formula

**Expectancy = (Win rate × Average win) − (Loss rate × Average loss)**

This is the single most important number to know about your trading system. A positive expectancy means the system makes money over a large enough sample. A negative expectancy means the system loses money regardless of how many trades you take.

### How to use expectancy in practice

1. **Track every trade** with entry, exit, result in R (multiples of initial risk). This normalizes for position size.
2. After 50–100 trades, calculate your average win in R (e.g., 2.1R) and average loss (e.g., 0.9R) plus win rate.
3. Plug into the formula. If expectancy is below zero, your system — not your luck — is the problem.

### The interplay between win rate and R/R

| Win Rate | Minimum R/R to break even |
|----------|--------------------------|
| 30%      | 2.3:1                    |
| 40%      | 1.5:1                    |
| 50%      | 1:1                      |
| 60%      | 0.67:1                   |

Scalpers often target 60–70% win rates with small R/R. Trend followers often see 30–40% win rates but target 3R or more. Both can be profitable; neither approach is universally superior.

### Why it matters for a trader

Understanding expectancy lets you evaluate your system objectively instead of by feel. A two-week losing streak hurts less when you know that a 40% win rate produces losing streaks of five or more trades regularly — and that the edge still exists. Without this framework, traders tend to abandon profitable systems after normal variance, or cling to losing ones because the win rate "feels" good.
