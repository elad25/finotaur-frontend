## In plain words

A **trading journal** is a structured record of every trade you take — including why you entered, where you planned to exit, what actually happened, and how you felt. It is the primary tool for converting trading experience into learning. Without a journal, you repeat the same mistakes at increasing costs; with one, you accumulate a data set that reveals exactly where your edge exists, where it breaks down, and what emotional patterns undermine your execution.

## Quick demo

After 60 trades, a trader reviews their journal and notices: trades taken in the first 30 minutes of the session have a win rate of 55% and average +1.8R. Trades taken after 2 PM have a win rate of 38% and average −0.6R. Without the journal, this pattern is invisible — it just feels like "some days are bad." With the journal, the fix is immediate: stop trading after 2 PM. No strategy change required, just a schedule change. The journal found the leak.

## Full explanation

### What to log on every trade

A minimum useful journal entry includes:

- **Date and time** of entry and exit.
- **Instrument** (ticker, contract, pair).
- **Direction** — long or short.
- **Entry price, stop price, target price** as planned before entry.
- **Actual exit price** and reason for exit (stop hit, target hit, manual exit, time stop).
- **Position size** and **dollar risk**.
- **Result** — profit/loss in dollars and in R (multiples of initial risk).
- **Setup type** — label the pattern or strategy used (e.g., "breakout," "pullback to MA," "earnings fade").
- **Market context** — was the broad market trending, ranging, or in a risk-off day?
- **Emotional state** — one word: calm, anxious, frustrated, confident. This is the data most traders skip and most need.

### What to review weekly

Aggregate analysis produces insights that individual trade reviews cannot:

- **Win rate by setup type** — which setups actually perform?
- **Average R by time of day** — when are you sharpest?
- **Average R by market regime** — trend vs. range day performance.
- **Adherence score** — what percentage of trades followed the pre-defined rules exactly?
- **Emotional correlation** — do trades taken when "anxious" or "frustrated" underperform?

This analysis identifies both strategy edges and behavioral edges.

### Screenshots and chart marks

Adding a screenshot of the chart at entry (and optionally at exit) accelerates learning by making the pattern review concrete. Over 100 entries, a folder of annotated chart screenshots becomes a personal pattern library — your own visual database of what your setups actually look like in real markets, not in idealized textbook form.

### Grading your adherence to plan

One of the most valuable metrics in a trading journal is plan adherence:

- **A-grade trade** — entry, size, stop, and management all followed the plan exactly, regardless of outcome.
- **B-grade trade** — minor deviation (e.g., sized slightly off) but thesis intact.
- **C-grade trade** — material deviation from plan (moved a stop, chased, sized up emotionally).

Tracking this separately from P&L reveals whether losses are coming from a flawed strategy or flawed execution. A strategy that would be profitable with perfect execution but is unprofitable due to poor execution is a very different problem than a genuinely bad strategy.

### Digital vs. paper journals

Both work. Digital journals (spreadsheets, dedicated software) make quantitative analysis easier and aggregate reports automatic. Paper journals make the reflection process slower and more deliberate, which some traders find produces better qualitative insights. Many experienced traders use both: a quick digital log for the numbers and a brief written post on the day's lessons.

### Why it matters for a trader

The journal is how experience compounds. Without it, a trader who has been active for three years has three years of unprocessed noise. With it, they have a structured archive of what works, what doesn't, and exactly what their own behavioral failure patterns look like. The journal transforms loss from pure cost into tuition — but only if you actually use it.
