## In plain words

A **stop loss** is a pre-set price level at which you exit a trade to prevent further losses. When price reaches that level, the trade closes automatically (or you close it manually). It is the mechanism that enforces the maximum loss you calculated during position sizing — without it, a "bad trade" can turn into an account-ending disaster. Placing a stop loss is an act of planning, not weakness.

## Quick demo

You buy shares at $100 and place a stop at $95 — a $5 risk. Overnight, bad news sends the stock to $80. Because your stop was in place, you exit at or near $95 and lose $5 per share. Without the stop, you lose $20 per share — four times as much. The stop loss didn't prevent loss; it capped it at a survivable amount.

## Full explanation

### Types of stop losses

- **Hard stop (stop-market order)** — an order waiting on your broker's system. When the trigger price is touched, a market order fires. Guaranteed to exit but not at a guaranteed price, especially in fast markets or gaps.
- **Stop-limit order** — a limit order fires at trigger. You control the exit price but risk not being filled if the market blows through your limit.
- **Mental stop** — you plan to exit at a level but rely on manual execution. Vulnerable to hesitation and rationalization; unreliable under pressure.
- **Trailing stop** — moves with price in your favor (e.g., always 5% below the highest price reached). Locks in profits as the trade moves for you.
- **Time stop** — exit if the trade has not moved as expected by a certain time (e.g., after two hours or by end of day). Used when a thesis depends on a specific catalyst.

### Where to place a stop

Stop placement should be based on the **chart**, not on a round dollar amount or a fixed percentage:

- **Below support** — for a long trade, place the stop just below a key support level or recent swing low where your thesis is invalidated.
- **Outside volatility** — use the Average True Range (ATR) to place stops at least 1–1.5 ATR away from entry so normal price noise doesn't trigger an exit.
- **After the candle structure** — a stop below a pin bar or inside bar low keeps you in while respecting the pattern's logic.

Placing a stop too tight relative to normal price movement ("stop-hunting zone") is one of the most common beginner mistakes — the trade is stopped out, then moves in the originally intended direction.

### Avoid moving stops against yourself

One of the most destructive habits in trading is moving a stop further away from entry when price approaches it. This converts a defined risk into an undefined one. Moving a stop in your favor (trailing it) is appropriate; moving it against your favor violates the entire purpose of having a stop.

### Gap risk

A stop loss does not protect against overnight gaps or flash crashes. If a stock closes at $100, you hold with a stop at $95, and it opens the next morning at $80 due to news, you exit near $80 — not $95. This is called **slippage** in the context of gaps. Reducing overnight size or using options as a hedge can address this, but the risk cannot be entirely eliminated.

### Why it matters for a trader

The stop loss is the only structural guarantee that you stay in the game. A trader who loses 50% of their account needs a 100% gain to recover. By capping individual losses, stops ensure no single trade erases the work of dozens of winning trades. The discipline to honor a stop — not moving it, not ignoring it — is a foundational trading skill that separates professionals from gamblers.
