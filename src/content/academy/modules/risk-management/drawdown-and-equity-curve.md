## In plain words

A **drawdown** is the decline from an account's peak value to a subsequent trough — the "how far you fell" number. The **equity curve** is the visual record of your account balance over time: the shape of the line tells you whether your strategy is working, struggling, or deteriorating. Understanding both is essential because losing streaks are normal, but understanding their math lets you manage through them without panic or ruin.

## Quick demo

Your account peaks at $30,000. A losing streak drops it to $24,000. Your drawdown is $6,000, or 20%. To return to the $30,000 peak, you need a 25% gain from the trough — not 20%. This asymmetry is the core math of drawdown: percentage recoveries are always larger than the percentage losses that caused them. A 50% loss requires a 100% gain to recover.

## Full explanation

### Measuring drawdown

- **Dollar drawdown** — the raw dollar amount lost from peak to trough.
- **Percentage drawdown** — (peak − trough) ÷ peak × 100. This is the standard metric for comparing across account sizes and systems.
- **Maximum drawdown (MDD)** — the worst peak-to-trough decline over a specific period. A key metric in evaluating any trading strategy.

### The recovery asymmetry

| Drawdown | Recovery Needed |
|----------|----------------|
| 10%      | 11.1%          |
| 20%      | 25%            |
| 30%      | 42.9%          |
| 40%      | 66.7%          |
| 50%      | 100%           |

This asymmetry explains why capital preservation is not a defensive mindset — it is mathematically necessary for long-term compounding.

### What the equity curve tells you

An equity curve rising steadily in the upper-right direction indicates a consistent edge being applied with consistent sizing. Key patterns to recognize:

- **Smooth upward slope** — consistent edge and sizing. Healthy.
- **Staircase pattern** — gains cluster around certain market conditions and flatten in others. Understand which conditions drive your edge.
- **Declining slope** — the edge has degraded or market conditions have changed. Stop trading real money and re-evaluate.
- **High volatility around trend** — high variance but trending upward. The strategy works but needs tighter sizing.

### Distinguishing normal variance from a broken system

Every profitable system has losing streaks. The question is whether a drawdown is within the expected statistical range or signals a real breakdown.

- If your historical maximum drawdown is 15%, and current drawdown is 12%, you are likely within normal variance. Stay disciplined.
- If current drawdown significantly exceeds historical max (e.g., 25% when max was 15%), something has changed. Reduce size, stop live trading, investigate.

One useful circuit breaker: set a **drawdown limit** (e.g., 10–15% of account equity) at which you automatically step back, trade on paper only, and reassess.

### Consecutive losses vs. drawdown depth

Losing streaks are predictable. With a 50% win rate, the probability of 5 consecutive losses is about 3% — it happens roughly 3 times in every 100 trade sequences. A 40% win rate makes a 6-loss streak even more common. Knowing this in advance reduces the psychological shock that leads to over-sizing and revenge trading during drawdowns.

### Why it matters for a trader

Drawdown is the price of participation. Every strategy that generates returns also accepts periods of loss. The traders who survive are those who quantify what they can tolerate in advance, size accordingly, and do not abandon sound strategies during normal variance. The equity curve is the report card of process, not a single-session grade.
