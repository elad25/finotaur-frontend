## In plain words

**Cognitive biases** are systematic patterns of thinking that cause people to make decisions that feel rational but are objectively flawed. In trading, three biases do the most damage: **loss aversion** (the pain of a loss feels twice as bad as the pleasure of an equal gain, leading to holding losers too long), **FOMO** (fear of missing out, which drives impulsive entries), and **confirmation bias** (only noticing information that supports what you already believe). These are not character flaws — they are built into human cognition, and knowing them is the first step to managing them.

## Quick demo

You are in a trade that is down $500. Rationally, you should exit — your thesis is broken. But the thought of locking in a real loss is so painful that you hold, telling yourself "it'll come back." This is loss aversion in action. Meanwhile, you scroll social media and only notice posts from people bullish on the same stock — confirmation bias. You now feel better about holding. The stock drops another 20%. You just experienced two of the most expensive biases in trading.

## Full explanation

### Loss aversion

The Nobel-winning research by Kahneman and Tversky showed that losses feel psychologically about twice as painful as equivalent gains feel pleasurable. This means:

- Traders hold losing positions far longer than winning ones, hoping to "get even."
- They take profits too early to lock in a certain gain, cutting winners short.
- They avoid admitting a thesis is wrong because admitting the loss feels unbearable.

The antidote is a pre-defined stop loss — an exit rule set before the trade, when you are not yet emotionally invested. Once in a trade, the brain is compromised.

### FOMO — Fear of Missing Out

FOMO drives traders to enter after a move has already happened: buying a stock that is already up 30% because it "might keep going," or chasing a breakout that is already extended. Symptoms include:

- Entering without a defined plan or stop loss.
- Increasing position size on a move already in progress.
- Opening a trade simply because others are talking about it.

The cure is a pre-set entry criteria list. If a trade does not meet the criteria before entry, it is not taken — regardless of how it looks in the moment.

### Confirmation bias

The human brain filters information to reinforce existing beliefs. A trader long on a stock will:

- Seek out and remember bullish articles.
- Dismiss or rationalize bearish data.
- Interpret ambiguous news as positive.

This makes it very difficult to objectively reassess a losing trade. Deliberate practice: before entering any trade, actively search for the best argument *against* it. If you cannot articulate why you might be wrong, you probably have not done adequate research.

### Other notable biases

- **Recency bias** — overweighting recent events. A winning streak leads to overconfidence; a losing streak leads to excessive caution, even if the setup quality is unchanged.
- **Anchoring** — fixating on a purchase price ("it was at $200, so $150 must be cheap"). Price has no memory; value is determined by future cash flows, not what you paid.
- **Overconfidence** — believing your analysis is more accurate than it is, leading to oversizing and ignoring stop levels.
- **Gambler's fallacy** — believing that after several losses, a win is "due." Each trade is statistically independent.

### Building structural defenses

Knowing about biases is not enough — the emotional brain acts faster than the rational brain under pressure. Structural defenses include:

- Pre-trade checklists (forces systematic thinking before entry).
- Pre-defined exits (removes the exit decision from the emotional moment).
- Trade journaling with post-trade analysis (builds a feedback loop to catch bias patterns).
- Cooling-off rules (no trading for 15–30 minutes after a loss).

### Why it matters for a trader

Every technical system can be destroyed by a biased operator. A trader who understands their own cognitive patterns and builds process-level defenses around them will consistently outperform an equally knowledgeable trader who does not. The edge in markets is often not about finding better information — it is about processing the same information less emotionally than everyone else.
