## In plain words

**Money management rules** are the hard limits you set in advance on how much you risk per trade, per day, and in total. The most widely cited are the **1% rule** (risk no more than 1% of your account on any single trade) and the **2% rule**. These are not suggestions for beginners — they are the structural constraints that keep you alive long enough for your edge to play out.

## Quick demo

A trader has a $25,000 account and applies the 2% rule: maximum $500 risk per trade. In a brutal week, they lose 5 trades in a row — a $2,500 loss, leaving $22,500. The account is down 10%, painful but survivable. A friend with the same account ignored rules and risked 10% per trade. Five losses wipe $12,500, leaving $12,500 — a 50% drawdown that requires a 100% gain to recover. Same losing streak, radically different outcomes.

## Full explanation

### The 1% and 2% rules

These are standard risk-per-trade limits used across professional trading:

- **1% rule** — risk no more than 1% of total account equity on any single trade. For a $20,000 account: $200 maximum per trade.
- **2% rule** — a slightly more permissive variant, common among swing and position traders with larger accounts.

The dollar amount from these rules feeds directly into position sizing (see the Position Sizing chapter). The point is not that 1% is magic — it is that having *any* consistent, pre-set limit prevents the "just this once" exception that destroys accounts.

### Daily and weekly loss limits

Beyond per-trade limits, professional traders apply daily and weekly stop-outs:

- **Daily loss limit** — if you lose X% in a single day, stop trading for the day. A common threshold is 2–3× the average daily risk (e.g., if you take 3 trades per day at 1% each, a 4% daily loss triggers a stop).
- **Weekly loss limit** — if the week exceeds a defined drawdown (e.g., 5–6%), reduce size or stop for the rest of the week.

These limits protect against the single deadliest pattern in trading: doubling down on a losing day out of frustration.

### Maximum account drawdown threshold

Beyond daily limits, set an absolute account-level floor. If the account drops below a certain level (e.g., 15–20% from peak), enter a review mode: stop live trading, trade on paper only, and evaluate what changed. This is not failure — it is a systematic circuit breaker.

### Scaling rules up and down

Money management rules should scale with the account, not remain fixed in dollar terms:

- **Scale up cautiously** — as account grows, increase absolute dollar risk only after consistent performance across 50–100+ trades. Psychological comfort with a larger risk level must precede the actual increase.
- **Scale down automatically** — if the account is in drawdown, the percent rule automatically reduces dollar risk as equity falls, which is a natural and correct response.

### The compounding argument for conservative limits

The rule is conservative by design. With 1% risk, a 10-trade losing streak costs approximately 9.6% of the account (due to compounding the smaller base). At 5% risk, the same streak costs 40%. The deeper the drawdown, the harder the recovery and the worse the psychology. Conservative sizing keeps the compounding engine running even through rough periods.

### Why it matters for a trader

Most traders who fail do not fail because their strategy was wrong — they fail because one or two oversized trades overwhelmed the account before the strategy's edge could express itself. The 1% and 2% rules exist to ensure that no single decision, no matter how confident you feel, can structurally damage the account. Capital preservation is not timidity; it is the foundation on which all long-term performance is built.
