## In plain words

**Debt** is money borrowed that must be repaid with interest. Not all debt is equal: a low-interest mortgage on an appreciating asset is very different from a high-interest credit card balance that grows faster than you can pay it. Paying off debt strategically means sequencing your payoff efforts to **minimize total interest paid** or to **maximize motivation** — two approaches with different math but both valid depending on your psychology.

## Quick demo

Elena has three debts: a credit card at 22% interest with a $1,200 balance, a personal loan at 9% with a $5,000 balance, and a student loan at 4% with a $12,000 balance. Using the **avalanche method**, she attacks the credit card first — this saves the most money. Using the **snowball method**, she pays the credit card first too (it's also the smallest), gets a quick win, and stays motivated. In this case both methods point to the same first target, but the logic behind each differs meaningfully.

## Full explanation

### Good debt vs. bad debt

Not all borrowing is destructive. A useful mental model:

- **Good debt** — interest rate is low, the asset purchased tends to grow in value or generate income (a mortgage, a business loan, a student loan for a high-earning profession).
- **Bad debt** — interest rate is high, money was spent on consumption with no lasting asset (credit card balances, buy-now-pay-later loans, payday loans).

The priority is to eliminate high-interest consumer debt as fast as possible. Low-interest debt can be managed more patiently alongside investing.

### The two main payoff strategies

#### Avalanche Method (mathematical optimum)
List all debts by **interest rate, highest to lowest**. Pay minimums on all, then direct every extra dollar to the highest-rate debt. Once paid off, roll that payment into the next highest rate. You will pay the least total interest over time.

#### Snowball Method (psychological optimum)
List all debts by **balance, smallest to largest**. Pay minimums on all, then attack the smallest balance first regardless of rate. Each paid-off account delivers a tangible win that builds momentum. Research shows this method works better for people who struggle with motivation, even though it costs slightly more in interest.

**Which should you choose?** If you can commit to either method with discipline, avalanche saves more money. If you need early wins to stay on track, snowball keeps you going. A hybrid approach — knocking out one small balance first for momentum, then switching to rate-order — is also reasonable.

### Practical acceleration tactics

- **Stop adding new debt** to the accounts you are paying off. Paying down a credit card while continuing to use it is like bailing out a boat with the plug still out.
- **Round up payments.** Paying $250 instead of the $180 minimum on a loan dramatically cuts both the payoff timeline and total interest.
- **Direct windfalls to debt.** Tax refunds, bonuses, and gifts applied directly to principal have an outsized impact.
- **Negotiate rates.** For credit cards, calling and requesting a lower rate works more often than most people expect, especially with a good payment history.

### The interest math

On a $5,000 credit card balance at 22% APR, paying the minimum (~2% of balance) each month takes over 20 years to pay off and costs more than $7,000 in interest alone — more than the original balance. Doubling the monthly payment cuts the timeline to under 3 years and saves thousands.

### Why this matters

High-interest debt is the single greatest obstacle to building wealth for most households. Every dollar paid toward a 22% credit card balance earns a guaranteed 22% return — better than almost any investment available. Eliminating consumer debt is therefore the highest-return financial move most people can make before investing a single dollar in the market.
