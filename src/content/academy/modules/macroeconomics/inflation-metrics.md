## In plain words

**Inflation** is the rate at which the general level of prices rises over time. Governments track it using price indexes — surveys of what a representative basket of goods and services costs compared to a prior period. The two most important measures in the U.S. are the **Consumer Price Index (CPI)** and the **Personal Consumption Expenditures (PCE)** price index. The difference between **headline** and **core** inflation is whether volatile food and energy prices are included.

## Quick demo

In June 2022, the U.S. CPI hit 9.1% year-over-year — the highest in 40 years. But **core CPI** (excluding food and energy) was 5.9%. The gap existed because oil prices had spiked after Russia invaded Ukraine. The Fed focused on core because food and energy prices are volatile and often reverse without any policy action. When oil prices fell in late 2022, headline inflation dropped quickly while core remained stubbornly elevated — exactly why the distinction matters.

## Full explanation

### CPI — Consumer Price Index

Published monthly by the Bureau of Labor Statistics (BLS), CPI measures price changes for a fixed basket of goods and services typically purchased by urban consumers. The basket includes:

- Housing (the largest weight, ~33%)
- Food at home and away from home (~15%)
- Transportation (~15%)
- Medical care, education, recreation, and others

**CPI-U** is the broadest version (all urban consumers). **CPI-W** covers urban wage earners and is used to adjust Social Security payments.

### PCE — Personal Consumption Expenditures

Published monthly by the Bureau of Economic Analysis (BEA), PCE is the Fed's **preferred inflation measure** because:

- It has a broader scope than CPI (covers more spending categories).
- It uses a **chain-weighted** methodology — the basket adjusts as consumer behavior changes (substitution effect). If beef gets expensive and people switch to chicken, PCE captures that; CPI is slower to adjust.
- Historically, PCE runs about 0.3–0.5 percentage points below CPI.

The Fed's 2% inflation target refers to **PCE**, not CPI.

### Headline vs. core

- **Headline** — includes all items in the basket, including food and energy.
- **Core** — excludes food and energy because those prices are driven by short-term supply shocks (weather, oil production cuts) unrelated to underlying demand.

Policymakers and traders use core as a better signal of **trend inflation** — what the Fed can actually influence with interest rates.

### Other inflation indicators

- **PPI (Producer Price Index)** — measures prices at the wholesale/producer level. A leading indicator: cost pressures on producers often pass through to consumer prices with a lag.
- **Import Price Index** — tracks prices of imported goods; useful for assessing inflationary pressures from abroad or a weakening currency.
- **Breakeven inflation rates** — derived from TIPS (Treasury Inflation-Protected Securities) vs. nominal Treasuries; reflects the market's real-time inflation expectations.

### Why it matters for a trader or investor

Inflation data releases (CPI and PCE) are among the most market-moving events on the economic calendar. A surprise high print triggers rate-hike expectations, sells off bonds, and often pressures growth stocks with high valuations. A surprise low print does the opposite. Understanding which measure the Fed targets, how it is computed, and what "transitory" vs. "entrenched" inflation looks like is essential context for reading every central bank decision.
