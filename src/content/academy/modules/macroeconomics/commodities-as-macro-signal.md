## In plain words

**Commodities** — raw materials like oil, copper, and gold — are not just things to trade. They are also real-time signals about what the global economy is doing. Because commodities are physically consumed in production and daily life, their prices reflect actual supply-demand conditions rather than just investor sentiment. **Oil** signals global energy demand and geopolitical risk; **copper** (nicknamed "Dr. Copper") signals industrial and construction activity; **gold** signals inflation fears, currency distrust, and risk aversion.

## Quick demo

In 2008, copper fell from $4.00 per pound in early summer to $1.25 by December — a 69% decline in about six months, before the worst of the financial crisis hit official GDP data. Copper's collapse was an early warning: the global economy was seizing up. Investors who watched copper were not surprised by the equity crash that followed. Similarly, from late 2020 to mid-2022, copper surged from $2.50 to $4.80, signaling the strongest industrial demand rebound in decades — which ultimately showed up in GDP figures and earnings for materials and industrials months later.

## Full explanation

### Oil (crude) as a macro barometer

Crude oil is the world's most-traded commodity by value. Its price reflects:

- **Global growth** — when economies are expanding, demand for energy rises; when they slow, demand falls.
- **Geopolitical risk** — supply disruptions from conflicts in major producing regions (Middle East, Russia) can spike prices regardless of demand.
- **OPEC+ production decisions** — the cartel that controls roughly 40% of global supply can engineer price moves by cutting or increasing output.

High oil prices act as a **tax on consumers and businesses** — they raise input costs, slow consumer spending on discretionary goods, and compress corporate margins. The energy sector benefits directly; most other sectors are hurt by sustained high oil prices.

### Copper ("Dr. Copper") as a growth proxy

Copper is used in construction (wiring, plumbing), manufacturing (motors, electronics), and infrastructure. Because it appears in so many productive activities, copper demand closely tracks industrial output and capital investment.

- **Rising copper** — often signals accelerating global manufacturing, infrastructure investment, or China demand (China consumes roughly half the world's copper).
- **Falling copper** — often a warning of slowing construction and manufacturing activity.

Unlike oil, copper has fewer geopolitical supply disruptions, making its price a cleaner growth signal.

### Gold as a macro/risk signal

Gold has no major industrial use — its demand is primarily monetary (jewelry, central bank reserves, and investment). Its price reflects:

- **Real interest rates** — gold pays no yield, so it competes poorly when real rates (nominal rates minus inflation) are high. When real rates fall, gold becomes more attractive. This is the primary quantitative driver of gold prices.
- **Currency debasement fears** — when investors doubt the value of fiat money (during QE, heavy deficits, or high inflation), gold demand rises.
- **Safe-haven demand** — during crises (financial crashes, geopolitical conflicts), investors buy gold as insurance.

Gold rising alongside stocks often signals inflation anxiety; gold rising while stocks fall typically signals crisis and flight to safety.

### Agricultural commodities and the inflation signal

Corn, wheat, and soybeans feed people and livestock, and their prices feed into the food component of CPI. Supply shocks (drought, conflict disrupting exports as Russia's 2022 invasion of Ukraine disrupted wheat) can cause sharp food-price spikes that hit the headline inflation number and squeeze lower-income consumers most.

### The commodity-dollar relationship

Most globally traded commodities are priced in U.S. dollars. When the dollar strengthens, commodity prices tend to fall (it takes fewer dollars to buy the same amount). When the dollar weakens, commodities often rise. This inverse relationship is not perfect, but it is persistent enough that dollar index movements are a standard first screen when reading commodity price action.

### Why it matters for a trader or investor

Commodity prices are early, real-economy signals that cut through the noise of financial market speculation. Watching oil, copper, and gold together — and comparing them to the dollar and bond yields — gives a richer, more textured read on the economic environment than any single indicator provides alone. For sector allocation, commodities signal when to overweight or underweight energy, materials, industrials, and utilities relative to the cycle phase.
