## In plain words

**GDP** (Gross Domestic Product) is the total dollar value of all goods and services produced in a country over a given period, usually a year or a quarter. When GDP grows, the economy is expanding — businesses earn more, employment rises, and incomes tend to increase. When GDP shrinks for two consecutive quarters, that is a **recession**. GDP is the broadest single scorecard economists have for an economy's health.

## Quick demo

The U.S. GDP in 2023 was roughly $27 trillion. That figure represents every car built, every haircut given, every software subscription sold, and every government service delivered across the country in one year. If next year that number rises to $28 trillion (about 3.7% growth), the economy expanded. If it falls to $26.5 trillion, the economy contracted — and the Fed, investors, and employers all start acting differently.

## Full explanation

### What GDP actually counts

GDP adds up four main components (the expenditure approach):

- **Consumption (C)** — household spending on goods and services; typically the largest component, around 70% in the U.S.
- **Investment (I)** — business spending on capital (equipment, buildings) and residential construction.
- **Government spending (G)** — federal, state, and local purchases of goods and services (not transfer payments like Social Security).
- **Net Exports (NX)** — exports minus imports; a trade surplus adds to GDP, a deficit subtracts.

The formula is simply: **GDP = C + I + G + NX**.

### Nominal vs. real GDP

**Nominal GDP** is measured in current prices. If prices rose 5% and output stayed flat, nominal GDP still grew 5% — but nothing real changed. **Real GDP** strips out inflation, giving a true picture of output growth. This is why economists and traders focus on real GDP and on the **GDP deflator** used to make the adjustment.

### GDP growth rate and the business cycle

The annualized quarterly growth rate (e.g., "the economy grew 2.4% in Q1") is the number markets react to. Consensus estimates are published before the release; surprises — higher or lower than expected — move equity indices, bonds, and the dollar.

- **Strong growth (>3%)** — usually bullish for equities, bearish for long-duration bonds (higher rate expectations), and often supportive of the dollar.
- **Weak or negative growth** — often triggers a flight to safety (bonds, gold), rate-cut expectations, and pressure on cyclical sectors.

### Limitations of GDP

GDP does not measure wealth distribution, environmental costs, or well-being — it measures market activity. A country can have high GDP growth while inequality worsens. Traders care about it not because it is a perfect measure but because central banks, government policy, and market sentiment all respond to it.

### Why it matters for a trader or investor

GDP is the macro backdrop against which every other number is read. Earnings season, central bank decisions, sector rotation, and credit conditions all make more sense when you know whether the economy is accelerating, decelerating, or in contraction. A 2% GDP miss can erase weeks of equity gains in a single session — understanding why is not optional knowledge for anyone trading macro-sensitive assets.
