## In plain words

A **central bank** is the institution that manages a country's money supply and sets the baseline interest rate for that economy. In the United States, that institution is the **Federal Reserve** (the Fed). It does not lend to consumers or businesses directly; instead, it controls the cost of money for the banks that do — and through them, for everyone else. Its two official mandates are **maximum employment** and **stable prices** (low, steady inflation).

## Quick demo

In March 2022, U.S. inflation reached 8.5% — far above the Fed's 2% target. The Fed responded by raising the federal funds rate from near 0% to 5.25–5.50% over 16 months. As borrowing costs rose, mortgages became more expensive, consumer spending cooled, and business investment slowed. By late 2023, inflation had fallen to around 3.2%. The entire sequence — data in, rate decision out, economy responds — is the Fed's policy transmission mechanism in action.

## Full explanation

### Structure of the Federal Reserve

The Fed is not a single entity but a system:

- **Board of Governors** — seven members appointed by the President and confirmed by the Senate; based in Washington, D.C.
- **12 Regional Federal Reserve Banks** — located in cities like New York, Chicago, and San Francisco; the New York Fed is the most operationally important.
- **Federal Open Market Committee (FOMC)** — the body that actually sets monetary policy. It meets eight times a year. Its decisions move every major market.

### What the Fed controls

- **The federal funds rate** — the overnight lending rate between banks. This is the primary policy tool. Raising it makes credit more expensive; cutting it makes credit cheaper.
- **Reserve requirements** — how much cash banks must hold (currently 0% for most deposits in the U.S., making this tool less relevant today).
- **Open market operations** — the Fed buys or sells Treasury securities to expand or contract the money supply.
- **The balance sheet** — through **quantitative easing (QE)**, the Fed buys assets to inject liquidity; through **quantitative tightening (QT)**, it shrinks its balance sheet to drain liquidity.

### Other major central banks

Every major economy has one: the **European Central Bank (ECB)**, **Bank of England (BoE)**, **Bank of Japan (BoJ)**, and **People's Bank of China (PBoC)**. When these institutions act in concert — or in opposite directions — cross-currency dynamics, carry trades, and global capital flows all shift.

### The FOMC meeting and market reaction

The FOMC announcement (usually a Wednesday at 2:00 PM ET) and the Fed Chair's press conference that follows are among the highest-volatility events in financial markets. The key inputs traders watch:

- The rate decision itself (hike, cut, or hold).
- The **dot plot** — each member's anonymous forecast of where rates will be over the next three years.
- The Chair's language: "data-dependent," "restrictive," "neutral" — every word is parsed.

### Why it matters for a trader or investor

Almost every asset class has a relationship with interest rate expectations. Equities, bonds, real estate, gold, and the dollar all re-price when the market updates its view of what the Fed will do next. Understanding the Fed is not esoteric macro theory — it is the single most important factor driving multi-week and multi-month price trends across nearly every market.
