## In plain words

The **yield curve** is a chart that plots the interest rates (yields) on U.S. Treasury bonds across different maturities — from one month out to thirty years. Under normal conditions, longer-term bonds pay higher yields than short-term ones (investors demand more compensation for tying up money longer). When that relationship flips — short-term yields rise above long-term yields — the curve is said to be **inverted**. Inversion has preceded every U.S. recession in the past 50 years, making it one of the most watched signals in all of finance.

## Quick demo

In July 2022, the 2-year Treasury yield rose above the 10-year yield for the first time since 2019. By October 2023, the **2s10s spread** (2-year minus 10-year yield) reached -1.07% — the deepest inversion in decades. This single relationship, called the 2s10s, had accurately signaled each of the prior four recessions. Equity investors, bond traders, bank analysts, and the Fed all watched it daily. It does not tell you *when* the recession arrives — historically there is a 6- to 18-month lag — but it tells you the risk is rising.

## Full explanation

### Normal, flat, and inverted

- **Normal (upward-sloping)** — longer maturities yield more. Banks borrow short and lend long profitably. The economy is growing; inflation is moderate.
- **Flat** — all maturities yield roughly the same. Often a transition signal: the Fed has raised rates and the market is uncertain about growth ahead.
- **Inverted (downward-sloping)** — short maturities yield more than long ones. The classic recession signal. It means the market expects rates to fall in the future (i.e., the Fed will cut to fight a slowdown).

### The most-watched spreads

- **2s10s (2-year vs. 10-year)** — the most cited spread. Inverts when the Fed has raised short-term rates to a level that the market believes will damage growth.
- **3-month vs. 10-year** — the Fed's preferred recession indicator, based on the academic research of economist Arturo Estrella. Has a slightly stronger historical track record than 2s10s.
- **5s30s (5-year vs. 30-year)** — useful for reading long-term inflation expectations.

### Why inversion signals recession

The mechanism is straightforward: when short-term borrowing costs exceed long-term lending rates, banks' **net interest margins** compress or go negative. Banks become reluctant to make new loans. Credit contracts. Business investment slows. Consumer spending follows. The lag between inversion and recession is typically 6–24 months because these effects accumulate gradually.

### What the yield curve is NOT

- It is a **probabilistic signal**, not a certainty. An inverted curve raises recession odds; it does not guarantee one.
- Timing is uncertain. The 2022 inversion did not produce a 2023 recession as many had feared — the labor market and consumer spending remained resilient.
- The curve can **disinvert** without a recession if the Fed cuts rates in time to engineer a soft landing.

### Trading around the yield curve

- **Steepening trade** — positioning for the spread to widen (longer yields rising faster than short ones, or short yields falling faster). Often a bet on recovery or Fed easing.
- **Flattening trade** — positioning for the spread to narrow. Often a bet on rate hikes continuing or growth slowing.
- **Impact on sectors** — banks benefit from a steep curve (borrow short, lend long); utilities and REITs are typically pressured when long yields rise.

### Why it matters for a trader or investor

The yield curve is the market's real-time vote on the future of growth and interest rates. It affects bank lending, mortgage rates, corporate borrowing costs, equity discount rates, and currency flows. Any trader in equities, bonds, or rates needs to know where the curve is, what direction it is moving, and what that implies for the economic outlook over the next 12–18 months.
