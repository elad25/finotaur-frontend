## In plain words

The **Purchasing Managers' Index (PMI)** is a monthly survey of corporate purchasing managers — the people who buy raw materials and services for their companies. Because purchasing managers act before economic data is officially tallied, the PMI is a **leading indicator**: it often signals where the economy is heading before GDP figures confirm it. In the U.S., the **ISM Manufacturing** and **ISM Services** reports are the most closely watched versions. A reading above 50 means expansion; below 50 means contraction.

## Quick demo

In November 2022, the ISM Manufacturing PMI came in at 49.0 — the first reading below 50 in over two years. At the same time, ISM Services remained at 56.5. Markets interpreted the divergence clearly: manufacturing was already contracting (as rate hikes bit into goods demand) while the service sector — the larger part of the economy — was still healthy. That split told investors the economy was slowing but not collapsing, giving the Fed room to keep hiking. A single PMI print shaped weeks of positioning.

## Full explanation

### How the survey works

Each month, the Institute for Supply Management (ISM) surveys hundreds of purchasing managers across industries. Respondents answer whether key conditions are **better, the same, or worse** compared to the prior month, across five equally weighted sub-indexes:

1. **New Orders** (most forward-looking) — demand in the pipeline.
2. **Production** — current output levels.
3. **Employment** — hiring activity.
4. **Supplier Deliveries** — delivery times (longer = higher demand).
5. **Inventories** — stockpile levels.

Each sub-index is calculated as: % reporting "better" + (% reporting "same" × 0.5). The composite PMI is the equal-weighted average of all five.

### ISM Manufacturing vs. ISM Services

- **ISM Manufacturing PMI** — covers factories and goods producers. More cyclically sensitive and often an earlier signal of turning points. Released on the first business day of each month.
- **ISM Services PMI** — covers the broader service sector (finance, healthcare, retail, etc.), which accounts for roughly 80% of U.S. GDP. Released a few days later.

Both matter, but because services dominate the economy, a services contraction below 50 is a more serious signal than a manufacturing one.

### The new orders sub-index

Among the components, **new orders** is the most watched because it indicates future production. If new orders are rising while inventories are declining, businesses will need to ramp output — a positive signal. The opposite combination (falling new orders, rising inventories) often precedes production cuts and layoffs.

### Global PMIs

The ISM reports have equivalents in every major economy:

- **Eurozone, UK, Japan** use the **S&P Global / Markit PMI** (formerly IHS Markit).
- **China** publishes both an official PMI (state survey) and a **Caixin PMI** (private survey of smaller manufacturers).

Comparing PMIs across regions reveals where global manufacturing is strongest and where demand is weakest — critical context for commodity prices and multinational earnings.

### PMI vs. GDP: the lead time

The PMI is released within days of month-end; GDP is released weeks or months after the quarter closes. This lead time makes PMI one of the most time-sensitive tools for tracking the business cycle in real time.

### Why it matters for a trader or investor

PMI releases move equity markets, particularly in industrial, materials, and energy sectors. A surprise manufacturing PMI contraction can reprice cyclical stocks sharply lower and push bond yields down (growth scare). A resilient services PMI can keep rate-hike expectations alive. Because PMI is released early, timely, and globally comparable, it is one of the core instruments in any macro trader's toolkit.
