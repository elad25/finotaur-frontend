## In plain words

The **labor market** describes the supply of workers and the demand for their work across the economy. Key metrics include the **unemployment rate** (the share of people looking for work but not finding it), **nonfarm payrolls (NFP)** (how many jobs the economy added or lost in the prior month), and **wage growth** (how fast average earnings are rising). These numbers sit at the center of the Fed's dual mandate — maximum employment — and they move markets every month.

## Quick demo

On the first Friday of each month at 8:30 AM ET, the Bureau of Labor Statistics releases the NFP report. In January 2023, economists expected 185,000 jobs added; the actual number came in at 517,000 — a massive beat. Within minutes, the 2-year Treasury yield jumped 0.20%, the dollar strengthened, and stocks initially sold off because investors concluded the Fed would have to raise rates further to cool an overheating labor market. A single data point rewrote the narrative for weeks.

## Full explanation

### The unemployment rate

The headline unemployment rate (U-3) measures people who are **jobless, available to work, and actively looked for a job in the past four weeks**. It does not count people who have given up looking (discouraged workers) or those working part-time involuntarily.

A broader measure, **U-6**, includes both discouraged workers and the underemployed, and typically runs 3–5 percentage points higher than U-3.

The Fed watches both, but U-3 is the headline number markets react to.

### Nonfarm Payrolls (NFP)

NFP is the monthly count of jobs added or lost across all non-agricultural sectors. It is the most-watched labor market release globally. Key details:

- **Revisions matter** — prior months are often revised significantly; the revision can move markets as much as the headline number.
- **Sector breakdown** — gains in manufacturing vs. healthcare vs. leisure & hospitality tell different stories about the economy.
- **Government vs. private payrolls** — private sector jobs are more cyclically sensitive and more meaningful for growth signals.

### Wage growth

Average hourly earnings growth (also in the NFP report) is a critical inflation input. When wages rise faster than productivity, businesses face higher costs and often pass them through to prices. The Fed watches wage growth closely as a leading indicator of service-sector inflation, which is the most persistent component of core PCE.

### Other labor market indicators

- **Initial jobless claims** — released every Thursday; how many people filed for unemployment the prior week. A timely weekly signal; rising claims are a recession warning.
- **JOLTS (Job Openings and Labor Turnover Survey)** — monthly count of unfilled job openings, hires, and quits. High quits (the "quit rate") signal worker confidence and wage pressure.
- **ADP private payrolls** — released two days before NFP; a preview from a private payroll processor. Useful as a rough guide but has diverged sharply from NFP in both directions.
- **Labor force participation rate** — the share of working-age adults who are employed or seeking work. A declining participation rate can mask unemployment weakness.

### The full-employment concept

"Full employment" does not mean zero unemployment — there is always some **frictional unemployment** (people between jobs) and **structural unemployment** (skills mismatches). The Fed estimates the natural rate of unemployment (sometimes called NAIRU) at around 4–4.5%. Employment below that level typically signals a tight labor market with wage-price inflation risk.

### Why it matters for a trader or investor

Labor data feeds directly into Fed decisions. A strong labor market with rising wages gives the Fed cover to maintain or raise rates. A weakening labor market signals the policy may shift toward cuts. NFP Fridays and JOLTS releases create sharp intraday moves in equities, Treasuries, and the dollar. Understanding whether the labor market is tightening or loosening — and what that means for the rate cycle — is core macro literacy for anyone trading rate-sensitive assets.
