## In plain words

Economies do not grow in a straight line — they move through recurring **cycles** of expansion, peak, contraction, and recovery. Understanding where we are in the cycle helps investors know which assets and sectors tend to perform best. During expansions, cyclical stocks (consumer discretionary, industrials) tend to lead. During contractions, defensive sectors (utilities, healthcare, consumer staples) hold up better. The cycle does not follow a precise schedule, but its broad phases are recognizable and consequential for every asset class.

## Quick demo

Between June 2009 and February 2020, the U.S. experienced its longest economic expansion on record — nearly 11 years. During that period, the S&P 500 rose from 666 to over 3,380. Then, in February–March 2020, the COVID-19 shock compressed an entire recession into about two months — the shortest on record — before the next recovery began. Investors who rotated from defensive to growth assets in April 2020, recognizing the early-recovery phase, captured much of the subsequent bull market. Cycle awareness was the edge.

## Full explanation

### The four phases

**1. Expansion**
GDP grows above trend, unemployment falls, consumer and business confidence rises, credit conditions ease, and corporate earnings grow. Risk assets (equities, high-yield bonds, commodities) typically outperform. Central banks may begin tightening to prevent overheating.

**2. Peak**
Growth reaches its maximum rate. Inflation is often elevated. The yield curve may be flattening or beginning to invert. Valuations are typically high. The transition to contraction can be triggered by excessive tightening, an external shock, or the natural exhaustion of credit-driven demand.

**3. Contraction (Recession)**
GDP falls for two or more consecutive quarters. Unemployment rises, corporate earnings decline, credit conditions tighten, and consumer confidence falls. Defensive assets (government bonds, gold, consumer staples) tend to outperform. Central banks typically pivot toward easing.

**4. Recovery (Trough → Early Expansion)**
The economy stabilizes and begins growing again. Unemployment starts declining. Credit eases. Early-cycle sectors — financials, consumer discretionary, industrials — often lead. Equities tend to anticipate recovery before the GDP data confirms it (markets are forward-looking).

### Leading, lagging, and coincident indicators

- **Leading indicators** — signal turning points before they happen: PMI, yield curve, stock prices, building permits, jobless claims.
- **Coincident indicators** — move with the cycle in real time: GDP, industrial production, personal income, retail sales.
- **Lagging indicators** — confirm the cycle has turned: unemployment rate, prime rate, commercial loans.

The Conference Board's **Leading Economic Index (LEI)** aggregates 10 leading indicators into a single composite, released monthly. A sustained decline in the LEI is one of the most reliable recession warning signals.

### Sector rotation through the cycle

A classic framework maps sector performance to cycle phase:

| Phase | Outperforming sectors |
|---|---|
| Early recovery | Financials, Consumer Discretionary, Technology |
| Mid expansion | Industrials, Materials, Energy |
| Late cycle | Energy, Staples, Healthcare |
| Recession | Utilities, Staples, Healthcare, Government bonds |

This rotation is probabilistic, not deterministic — it is a guide, not a guarantee.

### The NBER definition

In the U.S., recessions are formally dated by the **National Bureau of Economic Research (NBER)** Business Cycle Dating Committee. The NBER's definition is broader than the common "two consecutive quarters of negative GDP" rule — it considers the depth, diffusion, and duration of the decline across multiple indicators. NBER recession calls are often made months after the fact, which is why markets rely on leading indicators rather than waiting for official confirmation.

### Why it matters for a trader or investor

Every long-term investment strategy performs differently across cycle phases. Knowing whether the economy is in early, mid, or late expansion — and what risks signal a transition — informs sector allocation, asset-class weighting, and hedging decisions. Traders use the cycle as a macro backdrop for position sizing and direction bias; investors use it for portfolio tilt and rebalancing triggers.
