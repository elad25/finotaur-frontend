## In plain words

**Fiscal policy** is how a government uses its taxing and spending decisions to influence the economy. When the government spends more than it collects in taxes, it runs a **deficit** — borrowing the difference by issuing bonds. During a downturn, governments often spend aggressively (a **stimulus**) to replace lost private demand. During expansions, they may try to reduce deficits. Unlike monetary policy, fiscal policy is set by elected legislators, not central bankers — which means it is often slower, messier, and more politically driven.

## Quick demo

In 2020–2021, the U.S. government injected roughly $5 trillion in fiscal stimulus — direct checks to households, expanded unemployment benefits, and loans to businesses. The federal deficit hit $3.1 trillion in 2020 alone, more than triple the prior record. Consumer spending surged; so did inflation. The episode shows both the power of fiscal stimulus (it prevented a depression) and its risk (it contributed to the inflation surge that followed).

## Full explanation

### The two levers of fiscal policy

- **Government spending** — infrastructure, defense, healthcare, education, transfer payments (Social Security, Medicare, unemployment benefits). Direct demand injected into the economy.
- **Taxation** — income taxes, corporate taxes, payroll taxes, capital gains taxes. Lower taxes leave more money with households and businesses; higher taxes pull money back out.

The **fiscal multiplier** captures how much GDP changes for each dollar of government spending or tax cut. Multipliers vary by the type of spending and the state of the economy.

### Deficits, debt, and bond issuance

When the government spends more than it collects, the **U.S. Treasury** issues bonds (T-bills, T-notes, T-bonds) to fund the gap. The total accumulated shortfall is the **national debt** — over $34 trillion in the U.S. as of 2024.

Heavy bond issuance affects markets directly:

- **Supply of bonds increases** → prices fall, yields rise (all else equal).
- **Higher yields** → higher borrowing costs for businesses and consumers, and a stronger dollar as foreign investors buy U.S. debt.

This is the mechanism through which large deficits can "crowd out" private investment.

### Automatic stabilizers vs. discretionary stimulus

- **Automatic stabilizers** — programs that expand automatically when the economy weakens without a new congressional vote. Unemployment insurance and food stamps are examples. They cushion downturns in real time.
- **Discretionary stimulus** — deliberate legislative acts (tax cuts, infrastructure bills, relief packages). These take months or years to pass and deploy, often arriving after the recession has already ended.

### Fiscal vs. monetary policy interaction

Fiscal and monetary policy can reinforce or fight each other:

- **Both loose** (high spending + low rates) — maximum stimulus, as in 2020–2021. Risk: inflation.
- **Tight fiscal + loose monetary** — government cuts spending while the central bank keeps rates low.
- **Loose fiscal + tight monetary** — government spends heavily while the central bank raises rates; upward pressure on yields and the dollar.

The interplay between the two shapes the macro backdrop for every asset class.

### Why it matters for a trader or investor

Large spending packages, tax bills, and deficit projections move bond markets (via yield expectations) and equities (via after-tax earnings, sector spending priorities, and growth outlook). Traders watch the Congressional Budget Office (CBO), Treasury auction schedules, and political negotiation timelines the same way they watch Fed meeting dates — because fiscal decisions can reprice markets just as dramatically as rate decisions.
