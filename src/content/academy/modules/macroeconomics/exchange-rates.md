## In plain words

An **exchange rate** is the price of one currency expressed in terms of another — how many Japanese yen one U.S. dollar buys, or how many euros one British pound buys. Exchange rates fluctuate constantly based on trade flows, interest rate differentials, inflation expectations, and geopolitical risk. A strong dollar makes U.S. exports more expensive abroad (and hurts multinational earnings) while making imports cheaper. Currency moves ripple through every asset class, from commodity prices to emerging market debt.

## Quick demo

In 2022, the U.S. Dollar Index (DXY) rose from about 96 to 114 — a roughly 18% appreciation — as the Fed raised rates aggressively while other central banks moved more slowly. The euro fell to parity with the dollar (1:1) for the first time in 20 years. For a U.S. investor holding European stocks, that meant even if those stocks were flat in euros, they were down significantly in dollar terms. Meanwhile, companies like Apple and Microsoft reported that the strong dollar would reduce their reported earnings by hundreds of millions of dollars. Exchange rate moves are not abstract — they reshape returns and costs across the global economy.

## Full explanation

### How exchange rates are quoted

- **Direct quote** — units of domestic currency per unit of foreign currency. In the U.S., EUR/USD = 1.08 means one euro costs $1.08.
- **Indirect quote** — units of foreign currency per unit of domestic currency. USD/JPY = 150 means one dollar buys 150 yen.
- **Currency pairs** — major pairs involve the dollar (EUR/USD, GBP/USD, USD/JPY, USD/CHF). Cross-pairs exclude the dollar (EUR/GBP, AUD/JPY).

### What drives exchange rates

- **Interest rate differentials** — the dominant driver. Capital flows toward the currency with the higher yield. If U.S. rates are 5% and eurozone rates are 3%, capital moves into dollar-denominated assets, strengthening the dollar. This is the core of **carry trade** dynamics.
- **Inflation differentials** — a country with higher inflation sees its currency depreciate over time (purchasing power parity). In the long run, currencies of high-inflation economies weaken against lower-inflation ones.
- **Current account balance** — a country that imports far more than it exports must continually sell its currency to pay for imports, creating downward pressure.
- **Risk appetite** — in crises, investors flee to "safe haven" currencies (USD, JPY, CHF). In risk-on environments, higher-yielding and emerging market currencies strengthen.
- **Central bank intervention** — governments occasionally buy or sell their own currency to influence the exchange rate (e.g., Japan's interventions to defend the yen in 2022–2023).

### The U.S. Dollar Index (DXY)

The DXY measures the dollar against a basket of six major currencies, weighted heavily toward the euro (~58%). It is the most-watched single proxy for dollar strength. A rising DXY typically pressures commodities (priced in dollars), emerging market debt (harder to service), and U.S. multinational earnings.

### Currency wars and competitive devaluation

A **currency war** occurs when countries deliberately weaken their currencies to gain export competitiveness. A cheaper currency makes exports more attractive to foreign buyers. The risk is that trading partners retaliate, and the result is a race to the bottom that destabilizes global trade and capital flows.

### Carry trade

The **carry trade** borrows in a low-interest-rate currency (historically the Japanese yen or Swiss franc) and invests in a high-interest-rate currency (historically the Australian dollar or emerging market currencies). The trader earns the interest rate differential as long as the exchange rate stays stable. In August 2024, an unexpected Bank of Japan rate hike triggered a massive carry trade unwind, sending global equity markets sharply lower in a matter of days — a reminder that currency positions can be a source of systemic risk.

### Why it matters for a trader or investor

Currency moves affect the real returns of any international investment. A strong dollar headwind can erase gains in foreign equities when translated back to dollars. Commodity prices (oil, gold, copper) are inversely correlated with the dollar in many environments. Knowing where the dollar cycle is — strengthening or weakening — gives macro context for positioning in equities, commodities, bonds, and emerging markets simultaneously.
