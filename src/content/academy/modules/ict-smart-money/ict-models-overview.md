## In plain words

ICT has developed several named **trade models** — structured, repeatable sequences of conditions that combine its core concepts into specific setup types. Each model has a name (such as the **Silver Bullet**, the **Unicorn**, or the **2022 Model**), a defined time window, and a specific combination of liquidity sweep, displacement, and order block or fair value gap. The models are essentially pre-packaged versions of the entry model checklist applied to particular sessions and instruments.

## Quick demo

The **Silver Bullet** is one of the most widely discussed ICT models. It runs during three one-hour windows: 3:00–4:00 AM, 10:00–11:00 AM, and 2:00–3:00 PM New York time. During the window, the trader waits for price to sweep buy-side or sell-side liquidity, then return and form a fair value gap on the 1-minute chart in the opposite direction. A position is entered at the FVG with a stop beyond the sweep candle. The entire setup, entry to exit, occurs within one hour. The specific time constraint is what defines the model and distinguishes it from a generic FVG trade.

## Full explanation

### Why named models exist

Named models serve an important pedagogical purpose: they force specificity. Without a defined model, a trader applying ICT concepts can rationalize almost any trade as a valid setup after the fact. A named model has explicit conditions — time window, required liquidity event, required entry structure — that either are or are not present. This makes the model testable, trackable, and improvable.

Models also reduce decision fatigue. Rather than scanning the entire day for setups, a model-based trader monitors only the specific session windows where their preferred model is designed to operate.

### Common ICT models

#### The Silver Bullet
- **Windows:** 3:00–4:00 AM, 10:00–11:00 AM, or 2:00–3:00 PM New York time.
- **Mechanics:** Wait for price to take out a liquidity pool within the window, then identify a fair value gap on the 1-minute chart formed after the sweep. Enter at the FVG in the direction opposite to the sweep.
- **Instruments:** Works across forex, equity indices, and futures.

#### The 2022 Model (also called the New York Session Model)
- **Window:** New York AM session, 9:30–11:00 AM.
- **Mechanics:** After the open, identify the previous day's high and low as liquidity targets. Watch for a sweep of one target (e.g., sell-side below the prior day's low), then look for a 15-minute order block or FVG in the reversal direction. Enter on the retest.
- **Instruments:** Primarily equity index futures (ES, NQ).

#### The Unicorn Model
- **Mechanics:** A two-legged retracement setup. The market breaks structure, retraces to an order block, bounces, then pulls back a second time into a fair value gap overlapping with the original order block. The entry is the second touch (the "unicorn" — two overlapping validation zones).
- **Characteristic:** Higher confluence than a single order block or FVG because two ICT concepts overlap at the same level.

#### The London Swing and London Close models
- **London Swing:** Runs during the 3:00–5:00 AM kill zone. Designed to catch the first directional move of the London session after an Asian session range is established.
- **London Close:** Targets the 10:00 AM–12:00 PM window when London institutions are closing positions, often creating a short-term reversal against the London session's primary move.

### How models fit together

Models do not all operate simultaneously. A practitioner typically chooses one or two models that match their schedule, risk tolerance, and preferred instruments, then studies those models in depth rather than jumping between all of them. The models share the same underlying ICT framework — market structure, liquidity, displacement, timing — but apply it with different time constraints and trigger specifics.

### The evolution of ICT models

ICT has released and renamed models over many years of public teaching, and the community has also developed variations independently. Terminology and model specifics have changed across different ICT teaching periods (2010s vs. early 2020s vs. post-2022 content). When studying a specific model, it is important to identify which time period the source material is from, as earlier references may use different names for the same concepts or apply slightly different rules.

### Why it matters for a trader

Named models provide the structure that raw concept knowledge lacks. A trader who understands order blocks, fair value gaps, and kill zones in isolation may still struggle to execute consistently because they do not know exactly what to wait for. A model defines the exact sequence and timing, turning abstract knowledge into a repeatable daily process. Once a model is internalized, backtesting it becomes straightforward: either the defined conditions appeared and were traded, or they did not.
