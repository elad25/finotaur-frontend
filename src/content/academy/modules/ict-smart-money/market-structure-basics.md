## In plain words

**Market structure** is the sequence of highs and lows that price prints over time. When each new high is higher than the last, and each pullback stops above the previous low, the market is in an **uptrend** (also called a **bullish market structure**). The reverse pattern — lower highs and lower lows — defines a **downtrend**. A **break of structure** (BOS) or **market structure shift** (MSS) is the moment price violates the pattern and suggests control is changing hands.

## Quick demo

Imagine S&P 500 futures printing four swing lows, each one higher: 4,800 → 4,820 → 4,850 → 4,875. Each rally then makes a higher high. That is textbook bullish structure. Now price falls sharply below 4,820 — the previous higher low. That is a **break of structure**: the market has taken out a key swing low, which signals that sellers may have gained control. ICT traders watch for this shift before hunting for short entries.

## Full explanation

### The building blocks

Market structure analysis starts with identifying three things on a price chart:

- **Swing highs** — a candle whose high is higher than the candles on both sides of it.
- **Swing lows** — a candle whose low is lower than the candles on both sides of it.
- **The sequence** — whether those highs and lows are trending up, down, or ranging sideways.

ICT uses specific terminology for these:
- **Higher High (HH)** and **Higher Low (HL)** = bullish structure.
- **Lower High (LH)** and **Lower Low (LL)** = bearish structure.

### Break of Structure vs. Change of Character

ICT distinguishes between two important events:

- **Break of Structure (BOS)** — price breaks a swing point *in the direction of the existing trend*. This confirms the trend is continuing. For example, in an uptrend, a BOS is a new higher high being made after a pullback.
- **Change of Character (ChoCh)** or **Market Structure Shift (MSS)** — price breaks a swing point *against* the trend. This is the first warning that the trend may be reversing. In an uptrend, the ChoCh is when price breaks below a significant higher low for the first time.

The distinction matters because BOS is not a reversal signal — it is a continuation signal. Traders who mistake every BOS for a reversal will frequently fight a healthy trend.

### Timeframe hierarchy

Structure on a higher timeframe always takes precedence. A 15-minute chart may show a bearish ChoCh while the 4-hour chart remains in a clear uptrend. ICT practitioners generally use:

- **Weekly / Daily** — define the macro bias (are institutions positioned bullish or bearish?).
- **4-hour / 1-hour** — identify the swing structure to trade within.
- **15-minute / 5-minute** — find the precise entry after structure shifts.

Operating with the higher-timeframe bias behind you significantly increases the quality of setups.

### Liquidity at swing points

One of ICT's key additions to classical market structure analysis is the recognition that swing highs and swing lows are *not just technical levels* — they are **liquidity pools**. Retail traders place their stop-loss orders just beyond these points (stops above a swing high in a short position; stops below a swing low in a long position). Institutions are aware of this clustering and often push price into those zones to fill their own orders before the real move begins.

### Why it matters for a trader

Reading market structure correctly tells you the dominant direction so you trade with institutional flow, not against it. Missing a ChoCh — staying long after the market has shifted bearish at the higher timeframe — is one of the most common reasons traders hold losing positions too long and take large drawdowns.
