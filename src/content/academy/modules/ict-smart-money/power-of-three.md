## In plain words

**Power of Three (PO3)** is an ICT concept describing the three phases that price tends to move through within a trading day (or any significant time period): **accumulation**, **manipulation**, and **distribution**. Accumulation is the quiet period where institutions build their position. Manipulation is a deceptive move against the anticipated direction — a stop hunt — that tricks retail traders into the wrong side. Distribution is the real, sustained directional move that follows once institutions have filled their positions.

## Quick demo

It is 2:00 AM New York time. EUR/USD consolidates in a tight range through the Asian session — that is accumulation. At the London open (3:00 AM), price dips sharply below the Asian session low, triggering stop-losses from overnight longs. That dip is manipulation: it looks like a breakdown but quickly reverses. By 4:30 AM, EUR/USD is surging higher, rallying 80 pips through the New York morning — that is distribution. Retail traders who sold the "breakdown" are now stopped out or trapped, while the institution that sold the Asian low accumulated longs during the raid.

## Full explanation

### The three phases in detail

#### Phase 1 — Accumulation

During accumulation, institutions are quietly building their position in a market. Because they are working large orders, they cannot simply buy all at once — they spread purchases across time to avoid moving the market against themselves. Price during this phase typically appears to consolidate or chop, with no clear directional bias. ICT associates accumulation with the Asian trading session, when volume is lower and large players can position without causing obvious moves.

Visually, accumulation often looks like a rectangle or a series of inside bars. Retail traders tend to ignore this phase or see it as "nothing happening."

#### Phase 2 — Manipulation (the stop hunt)

Manipulation is the move designed to create the liquidity needed to complete the institutional position. If institutions are accumulating longs during the Asian session, the manipulation phase involves pushing price *downward* — below the Asian session low — to trigger the stop-losses of early long traders and the sell orders of breakout traders who shorted the breakdown.

This provides the final batch of sell orders that institutions buy from, completing their position. The manipulation move typically reverses quickly. Key characteristics:
- It occurs at the transition into a high-volume session (London or New York open).
- It sweeps an obvious liquidity level (session low, overnight high, a round number).
- It does not sustain: the move reverses within minutes to hours.
- Volume on the manipulation candles is often elevated but lacks follow-through.

#### Phase 3 — Distribution

With the position fully established and opposing retail orders absorbed, institutions allow price to move in the true direction. Distribution is the sustained directional move — the one most retail traders wish they had caught, and missed because they were on the wrong side during manipulation.

Distribution tends to be faster and more directional than either of the first two phases. ICT practitioners look for entry opportunities at the beginning of distribution, typically after a retracement to an order block or fair value gap formed during the manipulation phase.

### Daily PO3 vs. weekly PO3

The concept applies at multiple timeframes. On a weekly chart:
- **Monday** often represents accumulation (price sets the weekly range).
- **Tuesday/Wednesday** can bring the manipulation move (a false break of Monday's high or low).
- **Thursday/Friday** deliver the distribution (the week's real directional move).

This is why many ICT traders are cautious about Monday reversals and look for their highest-conviction trades on Tuesday through Thursday.

### Limitations and context

PO3 is a narrative framework, not a mechanical rule. Not every trading day follows the three-phase sequence cleanly, and identifying which phase you are in requires broader context: the higher-timeframe trend, the current liquidity targets, and session timing. Applying PO3 without those anchors leads to misidentifying every consolidation as "accumulation" and every dip as "manipulation."

### Why it matters for a trader

Understanding PO3 reframes what looks like random noise. The Asian session consolidation is not indecision — it is positioning. The early London spike is not a genuine breakout — it is a trap. Once a trader internalizes this cycle, they stop chasing early moves and instead wait for the manipulation to complete before entering in the direction of distribution.
