## In plain words

An ICT **entry model** is the complete sequence of conditions that must align before taking a trade — it is how you combine market structure, liquidity, order blocks or fair value gaps, timing, and a specific trigger candle into one decision. Rather than entering on any single signal, ICT traders build a checklist: the macro bias is set, a liquidity sweep has occurred, a retracement is in progress, and a specific candle pattern on the entry timeframe confirms the turn. All pieces need to be present; a partial setup is not a setup.

## Quick demo

It is 10:00 AM New York time (inside the New York AM kill zone). The daily chart is bullish. On the 15-minute chart, price swept below the previous session's low (a sell-side liquidity grab), then formed a bullish fair value gap between 10:02 and 10:06 AM. Price retraces back into that gap at 10:15 AM and prints a bullish displacement candle closing above the gap. The entry model is complete: daily bias (bullish) + liquidity sweep (below session low) + FVG identified + retracement into FVG + entry candle confirmed. The trader enters long, stop below the session low, target at the prior daily high.

## Full explanation

### The layers of an ICT entry model

An entry model is a hierarchical checklist applied top-down across timeframes. The typical structure has four layers:

#### Layer 1 — Higher-timeframe bias (Weekly / Daily)
Before any trade, the trader determines the dominant institutional direction. Are we making higher highs and higher lows (bullish) or lower highs and lower lows (bearish)? This layer determines whether to look for longs or shorts exclusively. Trading against this bias is a red flag regardless of how good the lower-timeframe setup looks.

#### Layer 2 — Intermediate context (4-hour / 1-hour)
On the intermediate timeframe, the trader identifies:
- Where the current swing is within the higher-timeframe range (discount vs. premium).
- Whether a liquidity sweep has recently occurred (buy-side or sell-side depending on bias).
- The most recent order block or fair value gap that was left behind by the move that broke structure.

This layer answers: *where is price heading and from what level?*

#### Layer 3 — Entry timeframe (15-minute / 5-minute)
On the entry timeframe, the trader waits for price to retrace into the confluent zone identified in Layer 2 (order block, fair value gap, OTE zone). Within that zone, they look for a specific lower-timeframe event that signals the retracement is ending:
- A bullish or bearish order block on the 1-minute or 5-minute chart.
- A displacement candle — a strong candle that moves rapidly in the intended direction, closing above (for longs) or below (for shorts) the recent swing.
- A market structure shift on the lower timeframe (a lower high taken out for longs; a higher low taken out for shorts).

#### Layer 4 — Trigger and execution
The trigger is the specific candle or pattern that prompts the actual entry. Common ICT triggers include:
- A **breaker block** — a failed order block that has flipped to the opposite polarity.
- A **mitigation block** — when price returns to an opposing candle before the original order block.
- A **displacement** followed by consolidation and then another displacement in the same direction.

### Stop placement and targets

- **Stop-loss:** placed below the liquidity sweep candle (for longs) or above it (for shorts), or below the lowest point of the entry zone. The stop should be beyond the range that, if taken, would invalidate the trade's thesis.
- **Initial target:** the opposing liquidity pool — the nearest pool of buy-side liquidity (previous swing high) for longs, or sell-side liquidity (previous swing low) for shorts.
- **Extended target:** a 1:3 or better risk/reward using the daily high or weekly high as a reference.

### Entry model discipline

ICT emphasizes that the entry model must be fully complete before taking a trade. Partial setups — where the liquidity sweep occurred but there is no clear order block or FVG, or where the session timing is wrong — should be passed. This discipline is where many practitioners struggle: the components look close enough, so they take the trade anyway. Tracking setups that were passed (and what happened) is a useful practice for calibrating patience.

### Why it matters for a trader

An entry model converts abstract concepts into a repeatable, specific process. Without it, ICT analysis becomes post-hoc rationalization: you see a move happen and point to the order block it respected, but you never had a plan before price got there. The entry model forces you to pre-identify the level, the trigger, and the stop before price arrives — which is the only way to know whether a method actually has edge.
