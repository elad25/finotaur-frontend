## In plain words

**Liquidity** in ICT refers to the concentration of resting orders — stop-losses and pending limit orders — sitting just beyond obvious price levels. **Smart money** is ICT's shorthand for large institutional participants (banks, hedge funds, central banks) who need to transact in size. Because institutions cannot fill enormous orders at a single price without moving the market against themselves, they engineer price moves toward the levels where retail orders are clustered, using those orders as the opposing liquidity to fill their own positions.

## Quick demo

Gold has been falling for two weeks. A clear swing low sits at $2,280. Thousands of retail traders have placed their long entries and stop-losses just below that level. Gold dips to $2,274 — momentarily breaching $2,280, triggering all those stops — then sharply reverses and rallies $40 in two days. ICT calls this a **liquidity sweep** (also called a **stop hunt** or **liquidity raid**). The institution sold to those panicking retail traders on the way down, then bought back at $2,274 when retail stops provided the supply.

## Full explanation

### Why liquidity matters to institutions

A hedge fund wanting to buy $500 million of EUR/USD cannot simply place a market order — that order would push the price up as it fills, making the average purchase price worse. Instead, institutions seek moments when a large supply of sellers exists at a price they want to buy. Retail stop-losses below swing lows are exactly that supply. ICT theory holds that institutions deliberately push price into those zones to access the liquidity.

### The anatomy of a liquidity pool

ICT identifies several recurring types of liquidity:

- **Buy-side liquidity (BSL)** — stop-losses from short sellers, and breakout buy orders, cluster above swing highs and equal highs. When price sweeps above these levels, it is collecting buy-side liquidity.
- **Sell-side liquidity (SSL)** — stop-losses from long buyers, and breakdown sell orders, cluster below swing lows and equal lows. A sweep below these levels collects sell-side liquidity.
- **Equal highs and equal lows** — two or more price peaks at the same level create a magnet because retail traders see them as strong resistance and place stops just above. ICT treats equal highs as particularly attractive liquidity targets.

### How price hunts liquidity before reversing

The typical ICT liquidity sequence runs in three phases:

1. **Accumulation** — institutions quietly build a position over time, often during ranging price action, without moving the market significantly.
2. **The raid / manipulation** — price is pushed into a liquidity pool (above a swing high or below a swing low), triggering retail orders and allowing institutions to complete their position at good prices.
3. **Distribution / the real move** — with their position full and retail traders stopped out, institutions allow price to move in their intended direction.

Recognizing Phase 2 is the central skill. A trader who sells a break below a swing low may be providing exactly the liquidity an institution needed to complete its long position.

### Smart money divergence as a signal

ICT also uses the concept of **Smart Money Technique (SMT)** — comparing two correlated assets (such as ES and NQ futures, or EUR/USD and GBP/USD) during a swing. If one asset makes a new low but the correlated asset does not confirm it, that non-confirmation (divergence) suggests the low in the first asset was a liquidity grab, not genuine breakdown.

### Why it matters for a trader

Understanding liquidity pools changes where you place your own stops and entries. Rather than placing stops just below a swing low — directly where everyone else parks their orders — you either accept that the stop may be raided before the trade works, or you use the raid itself as your entry signal. Traders who internalize this concept stop being surprised by apparent "false breakouts" and start recognizing them as structured institutional behavior.
