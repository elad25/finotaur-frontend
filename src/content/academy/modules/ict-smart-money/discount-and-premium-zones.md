## In plain words

**Discount and premium zones** describe where price sits within a defined trading range. The lower half of any range — from the low up to the 50% midpoint — is the **discount zone**: price is "cheap" relative to the range. The upper half — from the 50% midpoint up to the high — is the **premium zone**: price is "expensive." ICT's principle is straightforward: look to **buy in discount** and **sell (or short) in premium**. This prevents chasing price at extremes and aligns entries with institutional cost-basis thinking.

## Quick demo

NQ futures establish a daily range with a swing low at 18,400 and a swing high at 18,800 — a 400-point range. The 50% midpoint (the **equilibrium**) is 18,600. If price pulls back from the high down to 18,490, it is now trading at the 77.5% retracement level — deep into discount territory. An ICT trader looking for longs would be interested in this area rather than buying back near 18,750 (premium). The setup is cleaner because price has returned to a level where value is on their side.

## Full explanation

### Defining the range and midpoint

The range can be drawn from any meaningful swing: a daily range (day's high to day's low), a weekly range, or a swing from a major high to a major low on any timeframe. The boundaries are:

- **High of the range** = 100%
- **Low of the range** = 0%
- **Midpoint / Equilibrium** = 50%

Anything below 50% is discount. Anything above 50% is premium.

### Fibonacci alignment

ICT links discount and premium zones directly to Fibonacci retracement levels drawn from the range low (0%) to the range high (100%):

- **Discount zone:** 50% to 100% retracement (i.e., the lower half of the range). Key levels within this zone include 62%, 70.5%, 79%, and 88.6%.
- **Premium zone:** 0% to 50% retracement (i.e., the upper half of the range).
- **Equilibrium (EQ):** The 50% level itself, which often acts as a magnet and a decision point.

Traders use these Fibonacci levels to identify *where within the discount zone* price is likely to find support. A 62–79% retracement into a discount zone, aligned with an order block or fair value gap, is considered a high-quality entry area.

### Applying the concept

The framework operates on a multi-timeframe basis:

1. **Identify the higher-timeframe range and trend.** If the daily chart shows a bullish structure, you are looking for longs.
2. **Wait for price to pull back into discount.** A retracement below the 50% level of the most recent swing is required before considering a long entry.
3. **Identify a confluent entry zone within discount.** Look for an order block, fair value gap, or optimal trade entry (OTE) level in the 62–79% retracement area.
4. **Enter at the confluent zone with a stop beyond the range low.**

The mirror logic applies for shorts in a bearish structure: wait for price to rally into premium (above 50% of the range), find a bearish order block or FVG in the 62–79% zone of the rally, and enter short.

### Common misapplication

Traders new to this concept sometimes apply the range boundaries incorrectly by using arbitrary reference points rather than significant swing highs and lows. The quality of the discount/premium framework depends entirely on using *meaningful* structural swings — ideally those that involved a liquidity sweep or a break of structure. Using a random minor consolidation as the range boundaries produces unreliable zones.

### Why it matters for a trader

Buying in discount and selling in premium is the fundamental logic of any mean-reversion or value-based approach to markets. ICT formalizes this instinct with specific Fibonacci levels and connects it to liquidity sweeps and order blocks, giving traders a precise framework for timing entries. Entering a position in the discount zone rather than chasing a breakout at premium significantly improves the risk/reward ratio — the stop can be tighter (just below the range low) while the reward target is the full range or beyond.
