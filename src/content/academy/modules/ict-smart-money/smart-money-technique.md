## In plain words

**Smart Money Technique (SMT)** is an ICT tool for identifying false breakouts by comparing two highly correlated assets. If Asset A makes a new swing high but its correlated partner, Asset B, fails to confirm that new high (or vice versa), the divergence suggests that the new extreme in Asset A is a **liquidity grab** rather than a genuine breakout. The non-confirming asset reveals that institutional momentum is not truly behind the move.

## Quick demo

ES futures (S&P 500) rally above yesterday's high to 5,250. At the same moment, NQ futures (Nasdaq 100) — which normally move in lockstep with ES — fail to make a new high and instead are still trading below yesterday's NQ high. An SMT divergence has formed: ES is above its old high, NQ is not. This suggests the ES breakout is a stop hunt collecting buy-side liquidity, not a genuine institutional breakout. Shortly after, both markets reverse lower. A trader using SMT would have avoided chasing the ES long and instead prepared for a short.

## Full explanation

### The logic of correlated pairs

Correlated markets tend to move together because they share common macro drivers. When they diverge meaningfully at a key structural level, something unusual is happening. ICT's SMT framework interprets that divergence as evidence of manipulation — one asset is being pushed to collect stops or trigger orders while the other, which cannot be controlled as easily, reveals the true directional pressure.

### Common SMT pairs

ICT practitioners use SMT most commonly with:

- **ES (S&P 500) and NQ (Nasdaq 100)** — both U.S. equity index futures, highly correlated.
- **EUR/USD and GBP/USD** — both positively correlated against the dollar.
- **EUR/USD and DXY (Dollar Index)** — inversely correlated; if EUR/USD makes a higher high but DXY fails to make a new lower low, that is an SMT divergence.
- **Gold (XAU/USD) and Silver (XAG/USD)** — both precious metals with strong historical correlation.

The pairs must be genuinely correlated over the relevant timeframe. Using SMT with poorly correlated instruments produces false signals.

### Identifying an SMT divergence

An SMT setup requires the following conditions:

1. **Both assets are trending in the same direction** (e.g., both equity indices rising).
2. **One asset makes a new swing high (or low)** at a structurally significant level — typically above a prior swing high where stop-losses cluster.
3. **The correlated asset does not confirm** — it fails to make a corresponding new swing high at the same time.
4. **Price then reverses** from the new extreme in the diverging asset.

The divergence is most meaningful at higher-timeframe liquidity levels (daily or weekly swing highs/lows), during active trading sessions (London or New York), and when the broader market structure is aligned with the anticipated reversal direction.

### Bullish vs. bearish SMT

- **Bearish SMT** — Asset A makes a new swing high that Asset B does not confirm. This signals a potential top and reversal lower. The trader looks for short entries.
- **Bullish SMT** — Asset A makes a new swing low that Asset B does not confirm. This signals a potential bottom and reversal higher. The trader looks for long entries.

### Combining SMT with other ICT tools

SMT is rarely used as a standalone entry signal. It is typically combined with:
- A higher-timeframe market structure bias (confirming the anticipated direction of reversal).
- An order block or fair value gap in the reversal area (providing the precise entry zone).
- Session timing (London or New York kill zones, where institutional activity is highest).

When all three align — an SMT divergence, a structural order block, and the right session — ICT traders consider this a higher-probability setup.

### Limitations

SMT requires judgment. Correlations between pairs are not constant — they can break down during risk-off events or fundamental divergences (e.g., if U.S. tech earnings are very strong, NQ may legitimately outperform ES without it being manipulation). A trader who applies SMT mechanically without understanding the macro context will generate false signals. Always check whether there is a fundamental reason the two assets might be decoupling before interpreting a divergence as a liquidity grab.

### Why it matters for a trader

SMT adds a second data point — a second market — to your analysis. A new high or low confirmed by two correlated markets is significantly more likely to be real than one where only one asset reaches the new extreme. This second layer of confirmation helps traders avoid chasing false breakouts, one of the most damaging habits in active trading.
