## In plain words

Three concepts form the structural core of ICT setups. An **order block** is a specific candle (or small group of candles) where institutional buying or selling is believed to have begun, and price tends to return to that zone for re-entry. A **fair value gap (FVG)** is a three-candle price imbalance where a rapid move left a range with little trading activity — price often returns to partially or fully fill that gap. An **imbalance** is the broader category: any area on the chart where buying and selling were so one-sided that price left a void that the market tends to revisit.

## Quick demo

On a 1-hour EUR/USD chart, a large bearish candle prints at 8:30 AM (New York open). The candle before it has a low of 1.0850 and the candle after it has a high of 1.0820. The space between 1.0820 and 1.0850 traded on only one side — sellers were so aggressive that buyers never filled orders in that range. That gap is a **bearish fair value gap**. Hours later, price retraces up into the 1.0820–1.0850 zone and then resumes falling. An ICT trader who anticipated this fill used that return as a short entry.

## Full explanation

### Order Blocks

An **order block (OB)** is the last opposing candle before a strong directional move. The logic is that institutions placed their orders in that candle, and when price returns to that zone, unfilled portions of the original institutional order are re-triggered.

- **Bullish order block** — the last bearish (down) candle before a strong rally. This candle's body range is the zone to watch for future long entries.
- **Bearish order block** — the last bullish (up) candle before a strong decline. Its body range becomes a zone for short entries on retracement.

Not every opposing candle qualifies. ICT practitioners look for order blocks that:
- Immediately precede a significant move (a break of structure or strong momentum).
- Have not been fully revisited and violated since the move began.
- Align with the higher-timeframe bias.

### Fair Value Gaps (FVG)

A **fair value gap** forms when a candle's body moves so far that the previous candle's low and the next candle's high do not overlap. The three-candle sequence creates an "untouched" price range:

- **Bullish FVG** — Candle 1's high is below Candle 3's low. The gap between them was not traded two-way.
- **Bearish FVG** — Candle 1's low is above Candle 3's high. Again, the range was not traded two-way.

Markets have a statistical tendency to return to these gaps (often called "filling the gap") because resting limit orders and price discovery naturally pull toward untested prices. ICT traders use FVGs as entry zones, placing trades at the gap's edge in the direction of the larger trend.

### Imbalances and Inefficiency

"Imbalance" is the umbrella term: any price range where aggressive one-directional movement left little two-way trading. Fair value gaps are the most precise form. ICT also references:

- **Consequent encroachment (CE)** — the midpoint of a fair value gap, which is often the minimum retrace before the market respects the gap and continues.
- **Inversion** — when a bullish FVG is fully violated, it can flip to act as resistance (bearish); a bearish FVG, once violated, can become support.

### Combining the concepts

In practice, these concepts are stacked. A trader might look for:
1. A higher-timeframe bullish structure (higher highs and higher lows).
2. A pullback that sweeps buy-side liquidity below a swing low (the liquidity raid).
3. A bullish order block or fair value gap in that pullback area.
4. An entry at the OB or FVG with a stop below the liquidity sweep candle.

### Why it matters for a trader

Order blocks and fair value gaps give ICT practitioners specific, bounded entry zones rather than vague "support areas." When a retracement returns to a well-defined OB or FVG that aligns with the larger structure, the trade has a clear invalidation level (the bottom of the OB or below the FVG) and a defined risk. That specificity is one of the methodology's practical strengths.
