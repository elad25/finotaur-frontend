## In plain words

**ICT** stands for "Inner Circle Trader," a trading methodology developed by Michael J. Huddleston that analyzes markets through the lens of institutional behavior. The core premise is that large institutions — banks, hedge funds, and central banks — leave predictable footprints in price action as they build and exit positions. By learning to read those footprints, a retail trader can align with institutional activity rather than trade against it.

## Quick demo

Suppose EUR/USD has been rising steadily. Just before it reverses sharply lower, price briefly spikes above a recent swing high, triggering the stop-loss orders of traders who were short. That spike handed liquidity to institutions looking to sell at high prices. An ICT practitioner would identify that spike as a **liquidity grab** — a deliberate move to collect orders before the real direction reasserts itself.

## Full explanation

### The institutional framing

ICT begins with a simple observation: retail traders collectively place their stops and entries at obvious technical levels — round numbers, previous highs and lows, trendline touches. Institutions need large amounts of liquidity to fill their enormous orders. ICT theory holds that institutions engineer moves toward those obvious levels specifically to trigger retail orders, which provides the opposing liquidity they need to enter or exit.

This does not mean markets are rigged in an illegal sense. It means that understanding *where orders are clustered* gives you a probabilistic edge about where price is likely to move next.

### Key premises of the methodology

- **Price is drawn to liquidity.** Every swing high and swing low has stop orders stacked just beyond it. Price tends to reach those clusters before reversing.
- **Time matters as much as price.** ICT places heavy emphasis on specific trading sessions (London, New York) and specific times within those sessions (kill zones) when institutional participation is highest.
- **Fair Value Gaps and imbalances.** When price moves very quickly in one direction, it leaves behind a range where little trading occurred. Institutions often return to fill that gap before continuing the original move.
- **Multi-timeframe analysis is essential.** Higher timeframes (weekly, daily) define the macro direction. Lower timeframes (15-minute, 5-minute, 1-minute) provide the entry trigger within that context.

### What ICT is and is not

ICT is a **discretionary** methodology, meaning it relies on human judgment to interpret setups — it cannot be reduced to a simple mechanical rule. It draws on core technical analysis concepts (support, resistance, market structure) but interprets them through an institutional lens rather than a purely retail one.

It is worth noting that ICT concepts are widely used in the retail trading community but are not independently verified by academic research in the way that, for example, factor-based equity strategies have been. Practitioners report varying results. Like any methodology, it requires significant study, backtesting, and disciplined risk management before live application.

### Why it matters for a trader

Whether or not you adopt ICT fully, its core insight — that price moves toward liquidity — is broadly useful. Understanding that stop clusters exist above swing highs and below swing lows, and that institutions need those clusters to fill orders, changes how you interpret price movement and where you place your own stops and entries.
