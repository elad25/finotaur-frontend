## In plain words

ICT places heavy emphasis on *when* you trade, not just *where*. The global forex and futures markets have predictable surges in institutional activity tied to the opening and overlap of major trading sessions. ICT calls the periods of peak activity **kill zones** — windows during which institutional participants are most actively moving price and setups are most likely to follow through. Trading outside these windows increases exposure to low-liquidity, choppy price action that does not respect technical levels in the same way.

## Quick demo

A trader has identified a bearish order block on the EUR/USD 1-hour chart at 1.0850. At 1:00 AM New York time (3:00 AM London), the asset is approaching that level — but it is the dead of the Asian session, volume is low, and the order block is being tested with weak, indecisive candles. The trader waits. At 3:00 AM New York time (the New York AM kill zone begins), the same order block is retested. A strong bearish displacement candle forms and price sells off 60 pips. Same level, different time — entirely different outcome, because now institutional participants are actively engaged.

## Full explanation

### The global trading sessions

The forex and futures markets operate 24 hours on weekdays, but institutional participation is not uniform across the day. Four major sessions drive the majority of volume and directional movement:

- **Sydney (Asia/Pacific):** 5:00 PM – 2:00 AM New York time. Lower volume; tends to set ranges that later sessions exploit.
- **Tokyo:** 7:00 PM – 4:00 AM New York time. Some Yen pairs and commodity currencies are most active.
- **London:** 3:00 AM – 12:00 PM New York time. Highest single-session volume globally; EUR, GBP, and CHF pairs are most active.
- **New York:** 8:00 AM – 5:00 PM New York time. Highest U.S. equity and futures activity; overlaps with London from 8:00 AM to 12:00 PM, creating the most liquid window of the trading week.

### ICT Kill Zones

Kill zones are the specific high-probability entry windows within each session:

| Kill Zone | Time (New York EST) | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| London Open | 2:00 AM – 5:00 AM | First major directional push; often the daily high or low is set here. |
| New York AM | 7:00 AM – 10:00 AM | Most active for U.S. equities and futures; overlaps London. |
| New York Lunch | 10:00 AM – 12:00 PM | Lower reliability; liquidity thins and chop increases. |
| New York PM | 1:30 PM – 4:00 PM | Can produce secondary moves aligned with the day's trend. |

ICT treats the **London Open** and **New York AM** kill zones as the primary windows for most setups. The majority of daily range is established during these two windows.

### The Asian session as a range-setter

Rather than trying to trade the Asian session, ICT practitioners use it to identify the day's developing liquidity pools. The Asian session high and low are particularly important:

- Buy-side liquidity rests just above the Asian high.
- Sell-side liquidity rests just below the Asian low.

London and New York sessions frequently manipulate price into one of these extremes (the Power of Three manipulation phase) before reversing and running the true direction of the day. A trader who marks the Asian session high and low before London opens has a ready-made road map for where liquidity raids may occur.

### Seasonal and day-of-week tendencies

ICT also observes tendencies at a higher temporal resolution:

- **Monday** tends to set the weekly range (accumulation / early positioning).
- **Tuesday and Wednesday** are historically the most directional days of the week.
- **Thursday** often continues the week's move or sets up a reversal.
- **Friday** sees position squaring ahead of the weekend; setups are less reliable.

These are tendencies, not rules. They are most useful as a tie-breaker: if a setup appears on Friday afternoon with mixed confluence, the day-of-week tendency is one more reason to pass.

### Combining timing with structural analysis

Timing context does not replace structural analysis — it filters it. A perfectly structured order block setup at the wrong time of day (e.g., during the New York lunch lull or the dead of the Asian session for EUR/USD) has lower expectancy than the same setup appearing at the London Open. Kill zones raise the probability that price will move decisively from the target level.

### Why it matters for a trader

Respecting timing is one of the most immediately actionable changes a new ICT trader can make. Many early practitioners find that their setups appear valid but stop out or chop before working. Restricting entries to London Open and New York AM kill zones eliminates a large proportion of low-probability setups and reduces overtrading — two of the most common destructive habits in active trading.
