## In plain words
A **pip** (forex) and a **tick** (futures/stocks) are the smallest standardized price increments in a given market — the atomic unit of price movement.

## Quick demo
In EUR/USD, one pip is 0.0001 — if the price moves from 1.0850 to 1.0860, it moved 10 pips. In the ES futures contract, one tick is 0.25 index points, worth $12.50.

## Full explanation
**Pips** are used in forex. Most major pairs are quoted to four decimal places, and a pip is the fourth decimal (0.0001). Some brokers quote to five decimal places — the fifth is called a "pipette" or fractional pip. For JPY pairs, a pip is the second decimal (0.01) because yen is quoted differently.

**Ticks** are used in futures (and sometimes stocks). Each contract has a defined minimum tick size and a dollar value per tick:

| Contract | Tick Size | Tick Value |
|---|---|---|
| ES (S&P 500) | 0.25 points | $12.50 |
| NQ (Nasdaq) | 0.25 points | $5.00 |
| CL (Crude Oil) | $0.01 | $10.00 |
| GC (Gold) | $0.10 | $10.00 |

Why it matters: knowing the tick or pip value lets you quickly calculate the dollar impact of a move. If ES moves 10 ticks, that is $125 per contract. Thinking in ticks rather than dollar amounts helps traders express stops and targets precisely and consistently across different position sizes.
