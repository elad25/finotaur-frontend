## In plain words
**Volatility** is a measure of how much and how quickly an asset's price moves over a given period — high volatility means large, rapid swings; low volatility means slow, steady price action.

## Quick demo
Stock A moves an average of 0.5% per day; Stock B moves 3% per day. Stock B is six times more volatile — presenting both larger profit opportunities and larger risks on any given trade.

## Full explanation
Volatility is typically expressed as an annualized standard deviation of returns. There are two types:

- **Historical (realized) volatility** — calculated from actual past price moves. A 20-day HV uses the last 20 sessions.
- **Implied volatility (IV)** — derived from option prices. It reflects what the market expects future volatility to be, not what has already happened.

Traders use volatility for several purposes:
- **Position sizing** — more volatile instruments require smaller position sizes to keep dollar risk constant.
- **Options pricing** — IV is the dominant variable in option premiums. High IV = expensive options; low IV = cheap options.
- **Strategy selection** — some strategies profit from expanding volatility (long straddles); others profit from contraction (premium selling).

**VIX** is the most commonly cited volatility index, measuring 30-day implied volatility on the S&P 500. Readings above 20 are broadly considered elevated; above 30 signals stress or fear in the market.

Volatility is not the same as risk — but it is one of its most important dimensions.
