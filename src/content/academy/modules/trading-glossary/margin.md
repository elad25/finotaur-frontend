## In plain words
**Margin** is the collateral you deposit with your broker to open and maintain a leveraged position — it is not the cost of the trade, but a good-faith deposit.

## Quick demo
To buy one ES futures contract worth roughly $250,000 in notional value, a broker might require $12,000 in initial margin. That $12,000 is not spent — it is held as a deposit while the trade is open.

## Full explanation
There are two key margin levels:

- **Initial margin** — the minimum amount you must have in your account to open a position.
- **Maintenance margin** — the minimum required to keep a position open. If your account falls below this threshold due to losses, you receive a margin call.

Margin exists in every leveraged market: stocks (on a margin account), futures, forex, and crypto perpetuals. The specific requirements vary by broker and by instrument.

Important distinctions:
- **Margin is not a fee.** It is your own money, held as collateral, and released when you close the trade.
- **Margin and leverage are related but different.** Margin is the input (collateral); leverage is the output (position size relative to that collateral).
- **Margin requirements can change.** Brokers can raise intraday requirements during volatile sessions without notice.

Monitoring your margin utilization is essential — running too close to the minimum leaves no buffer for normal fluctuation.
