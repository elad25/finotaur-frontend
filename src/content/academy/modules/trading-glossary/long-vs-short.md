## In plain words
**Going long** means buying an asset expecting its price to rise. **Going short** means selling an asset you don't own, expecting its price to fall so you can buy it back cheaper.

## Quick demo
You go long 100 shares of a stock at $50. It rises to $60 — you profit $1,000. If instead you shorted 100 shares at $50 and it falls to $40, you profit $1,000 by buying the shares back at the lower price.

## Full explanation
These are the two fundamental positions in any market.

**Long:**
- You profit when price goes up.
- Maximum loss is limited to your investment (price can only go to zero).
- The natural direction for most buy-and-hold investors.

**Short:**
- You borrow shares (or enter a futures/CFD position) and sell them now, hoping to repurchase later at a lower price.
- Profit = sell price minus buy-back price.
- **Risk is theoretically unlimited** — a stock can rise indefinitely, and every dollar above your short entry is a dollar of loss.
- Short sellers pay borrowing fees and must return shares on demand.

In derivatives markets (futures, options, perpetuals), going short does not require borrowing — you simply take the opposite side of the contract. This makes short exposure easier and cheaper to access than in the stock market.

Understanding both directions is foundational: every trade has a long side and a short side.
