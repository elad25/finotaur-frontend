## In plain words
**The spread** is the gap between the best price a buyer will pay (the bid) and the best price a seller will accept (the ask) at any moment in the market.

## Quick demo
If the bid on a stock is $49.98 and the ask is $50.02, the spread is $0.04. A trader who buys at the ask and immediately sells at the bid loses $0.04 per share before any other costs.

## Full explanation
Every quoted market has two prices simultaneously: the bid (highest price a buyer is willing to pay) and the ask or offer (lowest price a seller is willing to accept). The spread is the distance between them.

The spread is an implicit cost of trading — you pay it every time you enter and exit using market orders. In liquid, high-volume markets like large-cap stocks or major forex pairs, spreads are extremely tight (often a single cent or less). In thinly traded stocks, small-cap names, or after-hours sessions, spreads can be wide enough to represent a meaningful percentage of the trade's value.

Factors that affect spread width:
- **Liquidity** — more participants = tighter spreads
- **Volatility** — during fast moves, market makers widen spreads to manage risk
- **Time of day** — spreads are tightest near the open and during peak volume hours
- **Asset type** — exotic forex pairs and micro-cap stocks have inherently wider spreads

Understanding the spread helps traders choose when to use limit orders (to avoid crossing the spread) versus market orders (when speed matters more than cost).
