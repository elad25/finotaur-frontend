## In plain words
**Paper trading** is simulated trading using a virtual account with fake money — you practice placing trades and tracking results without any real capital at risk.

## Quick demo
You open a paper trading account with $25,000 in simulated funds. You "buy" 200 shares of a stock at $50, it rises to $58, and you "sell" — recording an $1,600 gain on paper, with zero real money involved.

## Full explanation
Paper trading (also called simulated or demo trading) replicates the mechanics of real trading — order entry, position tracking, P&L — using a broker's or platform's virtual environment instead of a live account.

What paper trading is good for:
- **Learning order types** — practicing market, limit, and stop orders without cost.
- **Testing a strategy** — running a new setup across many trades before risking real capital.
- **Familiarizing with a platform** — navigating charting tools, hotkeys, and workflows.
- **Building a track record** — logging paper trades to evaluate edge before going live.

What paper trading does not replicate:
- **Emotional reality** — there is no psychological pressure when the money is not real. Fear, greed, and the urge to revenge trade are absent from the simulation.
- **Execution conditions** — paper fills often assume perfect liquidity; real orders face slippage, partial fills, and varying bid-ask spreads.
- **Order flow dynamics** — large paper orders execute at stated price; in real markets they move the book.

Paper trading is a useful starting tool, but most traders find their live performance differs meaningfully from their simulated results — primarily because of the emotional dimension that simulation cannot reproduce.
