## In plain words
**Hedging** means taking a second position designed to offset the risk of an existing position — reducing potential losses at the cost of also reducing potential gains.

## Quick demo
You own 500 shares of a tech stock ahead of an earnings report. You buy put options on that stock with a strike near the current price. If the stock drops 20%, your puts gain value and offset most of the loss.

## Full explanation
A hedge is a deliberate, structured offset — not just diversification. The goal is not to maximize return but to limit downside on a specific exposure.

Common hedging methods:

- **Options** — buying puts to protect a long stock position; buying calls to protect a short position.
- **Inverse ETFs** — products that move opposite to an index, used to hedge portfolio exposure without selling holdings.
- **Futures** — a producer selling oil futures to lock in a price; a portfolio manager shorting index futures to reduce beta exposure.
- **Currency hedging** — converting foreign earnings back to home currency at a fixed rate to eliminate FX risk.
- **Correlated pairs** — going long one asset and short a correlated asset to isolate a specific risk factor.

Hedges come with costs: option premiums, roll costs in futures, or the opportunity cost of profits forgone if the original position moves favorably.

Perfect hedges are rare; most hedges reduce, not eliminate, risk. The art is in finding hedges that are cost-effective relative to the risk they remove.
