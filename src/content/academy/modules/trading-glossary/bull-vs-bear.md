## In plain words
A **bull market** is a sustained period of rising prices driven by optimism and buying; a **bear market** is a sustained period of falling prices driven by pessimism and selling.

## Quick demo
The S&P 500 rises 30% over 18 months as corporate earnings grow and investors are optimistic — that is a bull market. When the index falls more than 20% from its peak over several months, it officially enters bear market territory.

## Full explanation
The conventional definition of a bear market is a decline of 20% or more from a recent peak. A bull market lacks a strict numerical threshold but typically describes a rising trend lasting months to years.

**Bull market characteristics:**
- Rising prices across broad market indices
- Strong corporate earnings and economic growth
- High investor confidence and increasing market participation
- Low unemployment, expanding credit

**Bear market characteristics:**
- Sustained price declines of 20%+ from highs
- Contracting earnings expectations, rising unemployment
- Elevated volatility and fear (VIX spikes)
- Credit tightening, risk-off rotation

The terms come from how each animal attacks: a bull thrusts upward with its horns; a bear swipes downward with its paw.

Important nuances:
- Bull and bear markets exist within a trend — short bear market rallies (called "bear market rallies" or "dead cat bounces") occur inside broader downtrends.
- Sector-level bull and bear markets can run independently of the broader index.
- For traders, identifying the regime correctly helps select appropriate strategies — trend-following in bull markets, mean-reversion or short strategies in confirmed bear markets.
