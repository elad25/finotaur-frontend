## In plain words
A **margin call** is a demand from your broker to deposit more funds — or face forced liquidation of your positions — because your account equity has fallen below the required maintenance margin.

## Quick demo
You hold a futures position with $15,000 in your account and a $12,000 maintenance margin requirement. After a $3,500 loss, your equity drops to $11,500 — below the threshold. The broker issues a margin call for at least $500, or begins liquidating your position.

## Full explanation
A margin call is triggered when losses erode your account equity to the point that it no longer satisfies your broker's minimum margin requirements.

What happens next depends on the broker and market:

- **Futures and forex brokers** typically liquidate positions automatically, without waiting for you to respond. The speed can be near-instant.
- **Stock margin accounts** may give you a short window (sometimes just same-day) to deposit funds before they sell your holdings.

Liquidation compounds losses: positions are often sold at market price into adverse conditions, locking in the loss and potentially leaving you with less than you started with.

How to avoid a margin call:
- Keep leverage well below the maximum allowed
- Use stop-loss orders to exit before losses reach the maintenance threshold
- Monitor account equity continuously during open positions
- Never fund a margin account to exactly the minimum — maintain a meaningful buffer

A margin call is not a warning; by the time it arrives, liquidation may already be underway.
