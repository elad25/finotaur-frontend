## In plain words
**Risk-on / risk-off** describes the market's prevailing appetite for risk — in a risk-on environment, investors buy growth assets; in risk-off, they rotate into safer ones.

## Quick demo
A strong jobs report comes in, the Fed signals no rate hikes, and equities rally while bonds sell off and the dollar weakens. That is a risk-on day. A geopolitical shock hits; stocks fall, gold and Treasuries surge. That is risk-off.

## Full explanation
Risk-on and risk-off (often written "RORO") are macro-level states that describe how investors are allocating capital across the risk spectrum at any given time.

**Risk-on assets** (bought in risk-on environments):
- Equities, especially growth and emerging markets
- High-yield (junk) bonds
- Commodities like oil and copper
- Riskier currencies (AUD, NZD, emerging market currencies)

**Risk-off assets** (bought in risk-off environments):
- US Treasuries and sovereign bonds of stable economies
- Gold and silver
- US dollar and Japanese yen
- Defensive equities (utilities, staples)

The shift between states is driven by: economic data surprises, central bank policy signals, geopolitical events, credit market stress, or simply broad sentiment changes.

Traders watch RORO as a cross-market filter. A setup in tech stocks looks different if the overall market is in a risk-off mode — the macro headwind may overwhelm the technical signal. Understanding the risk environment adds important context to individual trade decisions.
