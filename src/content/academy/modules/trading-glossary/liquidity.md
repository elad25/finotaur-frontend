## In plain words
**Liquidity** describes how easily an asset can be bought or sold without meaningfully moving its price. A liquid market has many active buyers and sellers at any given moment.

## Quick demo
Apple stock trades hundreds of millions of shares per day — a $50,000 order executes in milliseconds at close to the quoted price. A thinly traded micro-cap might gap $0.50 on the same order.

## Full explanation
Liquidity has two related meanings. In market terms, it refers to the depth of a market — how much volume sits at or near the current price, and how quickly new orders arrive to replace the ones that get filled.

Key indicators of liquidity:
- **Volume** — total shares or contracts traded per session
- **Bid-ask spread** — tighter spreads signal more liquid markets
- **Market depth** — the size of orders resting in the book near the current price
- **Slippage** — illiquid markets produce larger slippage on the same order size

In a broader financial sense, liquidity also describes how quickly any asset converts to cash. Cash is perfectly liquid; a piece of real estate is not.

For traders, liquidity matters because it determines execution quality. Illiquid instruments are harder to enter and exit, carry wider spreads, and can move sharply on relatively small orders — increasing risk, especially when you need to exit fast.
