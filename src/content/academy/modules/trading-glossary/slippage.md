## In plain words
**Slippage** is the difference between the price you expected to pay (or receive) and the price your order actually filled at. It typically works against you.

## Quick demo
You place a market order to buy 500 shares expecting $100.00. The order fills at an average of $100.14 — that $0.14 per share is slippage, costing you $70 on the trade.

## Full explanation
Slippage occurs because markets are not static. From the instant you submit an order to the moment it executes, prices can shift — especially in fast-moving or thinly traded markets.

The main causes:

- **Market orders in illiquid stocks** — your order consumes available liquidity and "walks up" the book, filling at progressively worse prices.
- **News spikes or high volatility** — prices move faster than your order can arrive.
- **Large order size relative to available volume** — even in liquid markets, a very large order will eat through multiple price levels.

**Positive slippage** does exist — occasionally you fill better than expected — but on average slippage is a cost that traders should account for in their edge calculations.

To minimize slippage: use limit orders when possible, trade during peak liquidity hours, and size positions relative to average daily volume. Slippage is an especially important consideration for scalpers and high-frequency strategies where margins are thin.
