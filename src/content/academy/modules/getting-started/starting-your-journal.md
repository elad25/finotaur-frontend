## In plain words

A **trading journal** is a structured record of every trade you take — the setup, the entry, the exit, the result, and your reasoning. Without a journal, you are flying blind: you will repeat the same mistakes because you have no data showing you what those mistakes are. With a journal, patterns emerge — good ones to reinforce, bad ones to eliminate. It is the single most important tool for improvement that costs nothing.

## Quick demo

After her first week of trading, Priya reviews her journal and notices something: every trade she took in the first 10 minutes after the open was a loser. Every trade she took after 10:00 AM was flat or a winner. She never would have noticed this by memory alone. She adds a rule to her trading plan: no entries in the first 10 minutes. That one insight, found in five minutes of journal review, is worth more than any paid course.

## Full explanation

### What to record on every trade

You do not need elaborate software to start. A spreadsheet or even a notebook works. Capture these fields from day one:

- **Date and time** of entry and exit.
- **Instrument** — ticker, contract, or pair.
- **Direction** — long or short.
- **Entry price** and **exit price**.
- **Position size** — number of shares, contracts, or units.
- **Stop-loss level** and **target level** (as planned before entry).
- **Actual P&L** in dollars and as a percentage of account.
- **Setup type** — what pattern or signal triggered the trade (e.g., "breakout above resistance," "oversold bounce").
- **Why I took it** — one or two sentences. The reasoning at the time, not after you know the outcome.
- **What happened** — brief note on how the trade played out.
- **What I would do differently** — honest self-assessment.

### Screenshots: underrated and underused

Take a screenshot of the chart at entry and at exit, annotated with your levels. Looking back at your own trades visually is far more instructive than reading numbers. You will quickly see whether you were buying at the top of a move, entering too early, or sizing correctly relative to the range.

### Reviewing your journal

The data is worthless if you do not review it. Build these habits:

- **Daily** — spend 10 minutes after the close reading today's entries. Were you disciplined?
- **Weekly** — look for patterns. What setup type had the best win rate? Which instruments? What time of day?
- **Monthly** — review the equity curve of your trades. Are you improving? What is your biggest recurring mistake?

### What good journaling reveals

Over time, a journal will tell you:
- Your actual win rate (not the one you imagine).
- Whether your winners are larger than your losers on average (expectancy).
- Which setups work and which do not.
- Emotional patterns — do you trade worse after a loss? Do you cut winners too early?

### Starting simple

Do not wait for the perfect journal template. Start with a Google Sheet with the columns listed above. Commit to logging every trade, no exceptions. The habit of logging matters more than the format. Once you have 30–50 trades recorded, you will have enough data to start learning about yourself as a trader — which is the whole point.
