## In plain words

A **daily trading routine** is a repeatable sequence of actions you perform before, during, and after the market session — regardless of what the market is doing. Professionals do not improvise each day; they follow a consistent process that keeps them prepared, objective, and out of impulsive trades. Building a routine early is one of the highest-leverage habits a new trader can develop.

## Quick demo

Alex trades US stocks. At 8:00 AM he checks overnight news and futures. At 8:30 AM he reads the economic calendar for scheduled releases. By 9:15 AM he has a watchlist of 3–5 setups with specific entry levels noted. At 9:30 AM the market opens; he watches but does not trade for the first 15 minutes. He acts on setups as they develop. At 12:00 PM, if nothing is working, he stops trading. At 4:30 PM he reviews his trades, updates his journal, and sets alerts for tomorrow. This structure takes the guesswork out of every day.

## Full explanation

### Pre-market (60–30 minutes before open)

This is your research and preparation window. Rushing straight to the open without preparation is one of the most common beginner mistakes.

- **Check overnight news and macro events.** Did futures gap up or down significantly? Was there major news (Fed speech, geopolitical event, earnings)?
- **Review the economic calendar.** Sites like Investing.com or the Fed calendar list scheduled releases — CPI, NFP, FOMC announcements. These move markets; know when they land.
- **Build your watchlist.** Identify 3–5 instruments you might trade today. Write down the specific price level at which a setup becomes valid and the level at which it fails. Do not improvise this during market hours.
- **Assess your own state.** Tired? Frustrated from yesterday's loss? These are real risk factors. Some traders keep a simple mood log: if they rate themselves below a threshold, they reduce size or sit out.

### Market open (first 15–30 minutes)

The opening range is often the most volatile and least predictable period of the day. Bid-ask spreads are wider, gaps are running, and institutions are positioning.

- **Observe before acting.** Watch how your watchlist instruments behave. Do not trade in the first 5–15 minutes unless this is part of a specific, tested strategy.
- **Wait for confirmation.** Let the initial chaos settle. The best setups often become clear 15–30 minutes after the open.

### Mid-session management

- Execute only the setups you identified pre-market. If a setup was not on your list, it requires a very strong reason to trade it.
- Manage open positions according to your plan — not according to how you feel about them in the moment.
- If you hit your daily loss limit (decide this in advance — for example, 2% of account), stop trading for the day.

### Post-market review (30 minutes after close)

This is where improvement actually happens.

- **Log every trade.** Entry price, exit price, P&L, the setup type, and why you took it.
- **Evaluate process, not just outcome.** Did you follow your rules? A losing trade where you followed your rules is a success. A winning trade where you broke your rules is a warning.
- **Set alerts for tomorrow.** Mark your levels so you are not scrambling in the morning.

A routine turns trading from a reactive, stressful activity into a systematic one. The goal is to reach a point where the routine runs on autopilot — freeing your mental energy for the actual decision-making.
