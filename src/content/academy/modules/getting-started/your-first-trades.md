## In plain words

Your first real trades should be intentionally small, rule-governed, and treated as learning experiments — not income attempts. The goal in the first weeks is not to make money; it is to practice executing a plan without letting emotion override it. **Position size**, **entry criteria**, and **exit rules** must be decided before you click buy. The market will test your discipline immediately.

## Quick demo

Priya has $2,000 in her account and wants to buy shares of a stock trading at $40. She decides to risk no more than 1% of her account on any single trade — that is $20 of risk. She places a stop-loss $2 below her entry at $38. So she can buy at most 10 shares ($20 risk / $2 stop distance). She enters a **limit order** at $40.05 and sets a **stop order** at $38 immediately after. She is not checking the screen every minute. She has a plan, and the plan runs itself.

## Full explanation

### The cardinal rule: size down

New traders consistently overtrade and oversize. A loss of 20% requires a 25% gain just to break even. The practical approach:

- **Risk no more than 1% of your total account per trade** while learning. For a $2,000 account, that is $20 per trade.
- This means losses are small enough to be instructive rather than devastating.
- Use position size, not luck, to control how much you lose when wrong.

### Always use limit orders

A **market order** buys or sells at whatever price is available right now. On thinly traded stocks or volatile opens, that can be far from what you expected.

A **limit order** only executes at your specified price or better. It gives you price control at the cost of not always getting filled. For beginners, limit orders are almost always the right choice.

### Set your exit before you enter

Before you click buy, you must know:
1. **Where you are wrong** — your stop-loss price. If the trade goes here, you exit. No exceptions, no "let me see how it closes."
2. **Where you take profit** — your target. At least 1.5x to 2x your risk (a $20 risk should aim for at least a $30–40 gain).

Place your stop order immediately after entry, not after you watch it for a while.

### What to avoid in the first weeks

- **Chasing stocks already up big** — you are buying other people's momentum, not a setup.
- **Trading earnings announcements** — binary events are gambling for beginners; the stock can move 10–20% either way.
- **Using margin** — you are not ready for borrowed money until you have a positive track record over dozens of trades.
- **Trading based on tips** — Reddit posts, chat rooms, and hot takes are not a strategy.
- **More than 1–2 trades per day** — overtrading is the fastest way to turn small losses into large ones through commissions, spread, and emotional fatigue.

### Paper trading first

Most brokers offer simulated ("paper") trading with real market data and no real money at risk. Spend at least two weeks paper trading your strategy. Track your trades in a journal. Only move to real money when:
- You have a clear, written set of rules for entries and exits.
- You are following those rules consistently in simulation.
- You understand why each trade made sense before you entered it.

The market will be there tomorrow. There is no edge in rushing.
