## In plain words

A **broker** is the intermediary between you and the market — they route your orders to exchanges and hold your assets in custody. Choosing one is not permanent (you can always transfer accounts later), but it does shape what instruments you can trade, how fast your orders execute, what tools you get, and what hidden costs you pay. The right broker depends on what you actually plan to do.

## Quick demo

Two traders, same strategy: both day-trade US stocks. Trader A picks a well-known app broker with a slick interface but no direct-access routing and charges $0 commissions by selling order flow. Trader B pays $0.005 per share at a direct-access broker with Level 2 data included. On a 1,000-share trade, Trader B pays $5 in commissions — but gets price improvement that saves $15 on slippage. Trader A "paid nothing" and lost $15 quietly. The cheaper label was not the cheaper outcome.

## Full explanation

### The dimensions that actually matter

#### 1. Instruments available
Make sure the broker covers what you plan to trade:
- Stocks and ETFs — universal.
- Options — most retail brokers offer them; tiers vary.
- Futures and forex — not all retail brokers offer these. Dedicated futures brokers (e.g., NinjaTrader, Tradovate, AMP) are often better for active futures traders.
- Crypto — separate exchanges (Coinbase, Kraken) or brokers with crypto access.

#### 2. Commission and fee structure
Most US stock brokers charge $0 commissions on stocks and ETFs. Watch for:
- **Options contracts** — typically $0.50–$0.65 per contract; some brokers charge $0.
- **Inactivity fees** — charged if you don't trade enough.
- **Margin interest rates** — if you borrow, the rate matters a lot.
- **Payment for order flow (PFOF)** — your broker sells your order to a market maker. Legal, but it can mean slightly worse fills, especially on options.

#### 3. Platform and execution quality
- **Retail/app brokers** (Robinhood, Webull, Tastytrade) — great UX, limited advanced tools.
- **Full-service retail** (TD Ameritrade/thinkorswim, Fidelity Active Trader Pro, Charles Schwab) — solid platforms, research, options tools.
- **Direct-access brokers** (Interactive Brokers, Lightspeed, TradeStation) — choose your routing venue, Level 2, real-time data. Better for active or professional traders.
- **Futures-focused** (Tradovate, NinjaTrader, AMP Futures) — built specifically for futures, CME/CBOT markets, lower per-contract fees.

#### 4. Data and research
Live Level 1 quotes are standard. Level 2 (order book depth), historical data, and real-time news often cost extra — or come free with certain account sizes. Paper trading access is a big plus for beginners.

#### 5. Customer support and reliability
Low-cost brokers sometimes have poor support and platform outages during volatile markets. Read reviews around high-volatility dates (e.g., meme stock events, major Fed days) to see how a broker performed under pressure.

### A simple decision framework

| Your style | Good starting point |
|---|---|
| Long-term stock/ETF investor | Fidelity, Schwab |
| Options trader | Tastytrade, thinkorswim |
| Active stock day trader | Interactive Brokers, Lightspeed |
| Futures trader | Tradovate, NinjaTrader, AMP |
| Total beginner wanting to learn | Webull (paper trading) or Schwab |

Start with paper trading on any platform before committing real capital. Once you know your style, you can move your account — ACATS transfers between brokers typically take 5–7 business days and cost nothing at the receiving broker.
