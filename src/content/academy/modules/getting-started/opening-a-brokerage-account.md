## In plain words

A **brokerage account** is the account you open at a licensed firm that gives you access to buy and sell financial instruments — stocks, ETFs, options, futures, and more. Before any real trading can happen, you need to open one, fund it, and understand what you agreed to. The process typically takes one to three business days, and most modern brokers let you do it entirely online.

## Quick demo

Marcus decides to start trading stocks. He goes to a broker's website, fills out a 10-minute application with his name, address, Social Security number, and employment status. The broker runs a soft identity check. Marcus then links his bank account and transfers $500. Two days later the money settles and he can place his first trade. Total cost to get here: $0 in fees at most retail brokers, zero minimum balance required.

## Full explanation

### What you need to apply

Every regulated broker must verify your identity under anti-money-laundering (AML) laws. Have these ready before you start:

- **Government-issued ID** — driver's license or passport.
- **Social Security Number (SSN)** or Tax ID — required for US tax reporting.
- **Bank account details** — routing and account numbers for your initial transfer.
- **Employment and financial information** — income, net worth, and investment experience. Brokers use this to determine which products you can access (for example, margin and options require additional approval).

### Account types

- **Individual taxable account** — the standard. You own the assets; gains and dividends are taxable in the year they occur.
- **IRA (Traditional or Roth)** — tax-advantaged retirement account. Strict contribution limits and withdrawal rules apply. Powerful for long-term investing, not for active day trading.
- **Joint account** — shared ownership with a partner or family member.
- **Paper trading account** — simulated, no real money. Many brokers offer this; it is the safest place to practice before risking capital.

### Funding your account

You can fund via:
- **ACH transfer** from your bank — free, takes 1–3 business days.
- **Wire transfer** — faster (same day), may carry a small bank fee.
- **Check** — slowest option, rarely used now.

Most brokers hold your transferred funds for 2–5 business days before they are "settled" and fully available for withdrawals. You can often trade immediately with the funds, but you cannot withdraw them until they clear.

### What to watch for

- **Margin account vs. cash account.** A cash account lets you only trade what you have. A margin account lets you borrow — which amplifies both gains and losses. Start with a cash account.
- **Options approval tiers.** Brokers gate options trading by experience level (Tier 1–4). Answer the application questions honestly; you can always apply for higher tiers later.
- **SIPC protection.** US brokerage accounts are protected up to $500,000 in securities and $250,000 in cash by the Securities Investor Protection Corporation — not against market losses, but against broker failure.

Opening and funding an account is the mechanical first step. The real work — choosing a broker that fits your style, understanding the platform, and placing your first trade with intention — comes next.
