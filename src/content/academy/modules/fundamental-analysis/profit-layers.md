## In plain words

A company's income statement contains four distinct layers of profit, each one peeling away another category of cost. **Revenue** is what you charged. **Gross profit** is what's left after the direct cost of making the product. **Operating profit** is what's left after running the whole business. **Net profit** is what's left after paying lenders and the government. Each layer answers a different question about the business's health.

## Quick demo

A sneaker brand earns $100M in revenue. It costs $40M to manufacture the shoes — gross profit is $60M (60% margin). After spending $30M on marketing and salaries, operating profit is $30M (30% margin). After $5M in interest on factory loans and $6M in taxes, net profit is $19M (19% margin). The gross margin tells you about pricing power; the operating margin reveals operational efficiency; the net margin reflects the full financial picture including how the business is financed.

## Full explanation

### Layer 1: Revenue (the "top line")

Revenue is simply total sales — everything billed to customers in the period. It is the starting point. A growing top line is encouraging, but a company can grow revenue while destroying value if costs grow faster.

### Layer 2: Gross Profit

**Gross Profit = Revenue − Cost of Goods Sold (COGS)**

COGS includes only the direct costs of producing what you sell: raw materials, direct labor, manufacturing overhead. The **gross margin** (Gross Profit / Revenue) is one of the most revealing ratios in fundamental analysis:

- **High gross margins (>60%)** suggest pricing power, strong brand, or scalable software economics.
- **Low gross margins (<20%)** suggest a commodity business or thin-margin distribution model.
- **Declining gross margins** is often the first sign that competition is intensifying or input costs are rising.

### Layer 3: Operating Profit (EBIT)

**Operating Profit = Gross Profit − Operating Expenses**

Operating expenses include selling, marketing, research and development, and general and administrative costs. These are the costs of running the business above and beyond producing the product. The **operating margin** (Operating Profit / Revenue) shows whether management is efficiently converting sales to profit through operational discipline.

Companies with high gross margins but low operating margins are often over-spending on sales, marketing, or overhead relative to their size. Companies with widening operating margins are often gaining scale leverage — their costs are growing slower than their revenue.

**EBITDA** is a variant that adds back depreciation and amortization — a proxy for operating cash earnings before accounting adjustments and financing choices. It is widely used for comparing companies in asset-heavy industries.

### Layer 4: Net Income (the "bottom line")

**Net Income = Operating Profit − Interest Expense − Taxes**

Net income is what remains for shareholders. It is what generates **Earnings Per Share (EPS)**:

EPS = Net Income / Diluted Shares Outstanding

The **net margin** (Net Income / Revenue) is the most comprehensive profitability measure, but it is also the most easily distorted by one-time items, interest rate changes, and tax-rate differences. Always compare companies with similar capital structures when using net margins.

### Reading the layers together

| Layer | What a change signals |
|---|---|
| Gross margin falling | Pricing pressure or rising input costs |
| Operating margin falling faster than gross | Overhead growing out of control |
| Net margin falling while operating margin holds | Increased debt burden or higher tax rate |
| Net margin rising while operating margin is flat | Lower debt or favorable tax item — may not be repeatable |

### Why it matters for a trader and investor

Earnings surprises that drive stock moves on earnings day are usually about whether margins expanded or contracted — not just whether revenue grew. A company that beats EPS because its tax rate dropped is far less interesting than one that beat because gross margins expanded. Learning to read each profit layer separately lets you distinguish durable improvement from one-time noise, which is the difference between a trade and a thesis.
