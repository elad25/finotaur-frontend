## In plain words

**Profit margins** express how many cents of profit a company keeps for every dollar it generates in revenue. There are three primary margins — **gross**, **operating**, and **net** — each peeling back a different layer of costs. High and stable (or expanding) margins signal a business with pricing power and operational discipline. Falling margins are often the earliest visible symptom of a deteriorating business, sometimes appearing quarters before a stock declines.

## Quick demo

A software company grows revenue from $100M to $130M in one year — a 30% increase that looks impressive. But gross margin dropped from 75% to 65%, and operating margin fell from 25% to 15%. The absolute dollars of operating profit actually fell from $25M to $19.5M even as revenue surged. The stock deserves to drop, not rally. Revenue growth masked margin compression. Anyone who read only the top-line number missed the whole story.

## Full explanation

### The three core margins

**Gross Margin = Gross Profit / Revenue**
This is the most fundamental indicator of pricing power and competitive positioning. It reflects how much the business keeps after paying the direct cost of its product or service. Gross margin rarely lies — it reflects the underlying economics of the business model.

- High gross margins (>60%): software, pharmaceuticals, luxury goods — the company sells intangibles, IP, or brand at a large premium to production cost.
- Low gross margins (<20%): grocery retail, commodity manufacturing — thin margins demand operational scale.
- Declining gross margin: usually means pricing pressure from competition, rising input costs, or a shift in product mix toward lower-margin offerings.

**Operating Margin = Operating Income / Revenue**
Operating margin reveals whether the business is efficiently converting gross profit into operating earnings after paying for sales, marketing, R&D, and overhead. Two businesses with the same gross margin can have very different operating margins based on how disciplined they are with overhead.

Operating leverage is a key concept here: as revenue scales, fixed costs (R&D, G&A) get spread over more sales, and operating margin typically expands. A company showing strong revenue growth with expanding operating margins has genuine operating leverage — one of the most attractive fundamental characteristics.

**Net Margin = Net Income / Revenue**
Net margin captures the full picture including interest expense, taxes, and any extraordinary items. It is the most comprehensive but also the most volatile — a change in the tax rate or interest burden can move net margin without any operational change.

### What margin trends tell you

Analysts often focus more on the **direction and trend** of margins than their absolute level:

| Trend | Interpretation |
|---|---|
| Expanding gross + expanding operating | Strong pricing power + operating leverage — rare and valuable |
| Stable gross, falling operating | Cost discipline breaking down — watch SG&A and R&D |
| Falling gross, stable operating | Cutting overhead to compensate for eroding product economics |
| Falling at all three levels | Deteriorating business — high probability of a guidance cut ahead |

### Efficiency ratios alongside margins

Margins gain context when paired with turnover ratios:
- **Asset turnover** (Revenue / Assets): how hard assets are working to generate sales.
- **Inventory turnover** (COGS / Inventory): how quickly inventory moves through the business.
- **Receivables turnover** (Revenue / Accounts Receivable): how fast customers pay.

A business with thin margins but very high asset turnover (e.g., a fast-food chain) can still generate strong returns on capital. Margins and efficiency together — not margins alone — tell the full story.

### Industry benchmarks matter

A 10% operating margin is considered excellent in grocery retail; it is mediocre in enterprise software. Always benchmark margins against the relevant industry peer group and against the company's own historical range.

### Why it matters for a trader and investor

Margin analysis is where most earnings surprises are won or lost. A company that beats the revenue consensus but misses on gross margin is usually punished heavily because the market extrapolates the deterioration forward. Conversely, a company that delivers margin expansion alongside growth gets disproportionately rewarded — the market upgrades its view of the business's long-term earnings power. Tracking margins each quarter is one of the most reliable ways to stay ahead of major fundamental turning points.
