## In plain words

**Fundamental analysis** is the practice of estimating what a business is actually worth by studying its finances, industry, and management — then comparing that estimate to the current market price. The goal is to find stocks trading below their true value and avoid those trading far above it. It is the foundation of **value investing**, pioneered by Benjamin Graham and popularized by Warren Buffett.

## Quick demo

Suppose a small hardware store earns $100,000 per year in profit. A local buyer would probably pay $300,000–$500,000 to own it outright (3–5 years of earnings). Now imagine that same store's stock trades on an exchange at a price that implies a $200,000 valuation. A fundamental analyst would call that a potential bargain — the market is pricing it below what a rational buyer would pay. That gap between price and value is the opportunity.

## Full explanation

### The core question

Fundamental analysis starts with one question: **what is this business worth?** Not what the stock traded at yesterday, not what the headlines say — but what the underlying business would fetch if sold to a rational buyer with full information.

This is different from technical analysis, which asks "where is this price likely to go based on its chart pattern?" Fundamental analysis largely ignores the chart and focuses on the income statement, the balance sheet, and the cash flow statement.

### Two schools within fundamentals

- **Bottom-up**: Start with a single company, study its financials deeply, and decide whether it is cheap or expensive regardless of the macro environment. Most stock pickers work this way.
- **Top-down**: Start with the economy, then the sector, then find the best company within the best sector. Macro investors and portfolio managers often blend this with bottom-up work.

### What analysts actually look at

- **Revenue and earnings growth** — is the business expanding?
- **Profit margins** — how much of each dollar of revenue does it keep?
- **Balance sheet strength** — does it have too much debt relative to equity?
- **Cash flow** — is reported profit backed by real cash coming in the door?
- **Valuation multiples** — how expensive is the stock versus peers and history?
- **Competitive position** — does the business have a durable edge (a "moat")?
- **Management quality** — are the people running it honest and capable capital allocators?

### The margin of safety

Graham's most important concept is the **margin of safety**: only buy when the price is meaningfully below your estimated intrinsic value. Estimates are always imprecise. A margin of safety — buying at, say, 70 cents for every dollar of estimated value — absorbs the inevitable errors in your analysis.

### Why it matters for a trader and investor

Fundamental analysis is the language of long-term wealth creation. Even shorter-term traders benefit from it: a company with deteriorating fundamentals is unlikely to be a good long candidate regardless of the chart. A stock with expanding margins and a cheap valuation has an underlying wind at its back. Understanding what a business is actually worth helps you hold through volatility, size positions with conviction, and avoid the traps that destroy capital — overpaying for hype, ignoring debt loads, and misreading one-time gains as durable earnings.
