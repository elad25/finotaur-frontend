## In plain words

A **dividend** is a cash payment a company makes directly to its shareholders from its earnings. It is how mature, profitable businesses share their success with the people who own them. The **dividend yield** tells you what percentage of the stock's price you receive annually in dividends. The **payout ratio** tells you what fraction of earnings are paid out as dividends. Together, these two metrics reveal whether a dividend is sustainable, growing, or at risk of being cut.

## Quick demo

A utility company pays an annual dividend of $2.00 per share. The stock trades at $40, giving a **dividend yield** of 5% ($2 / $40). The company earned $2.50 per share, so the **payout ratio** is 80% ($2 / $2.50). That payout is high but manageable for a stable, regulated business. Now suppose a tech company also offers a $2 dividend but earned only $1.80 per share — an unsustainable 111% payout ratio. The first dividend is safe; the second is likely to be cut.

## Full explanation

### Why companies pay dividends

A company that consistently earns more cash than it can profitably reinvest faces a choice: hoard the cash (often wasteful), make acquisitions (often value-destructive), or return it to shareholders via dividends or buybacks. Established companies in mature industries — utilities, consumer staples, telecoms — typically pay dividends because their reinvestment opportunities are limited relative to their cash generation.

Growth companies, by contrast, tend to pay no dividend: they reinvest every dollar into expansion because they believe the return on reinvestment exceeds what shareholders could earn elsewhere. A company's dividend policy is therefore a signal about its stage in the business lifecycle and management's assessment of reinvestment opportunities.

### Key dividend metrics

**Dividend Yield = Annual Dividend per Share / Stock Price**
Yield rises when either the dividend increases or the stock price falls. A yield spike caused by a collapsing stock is a danger signal, not an opportunity — the market may be pricing in a dividend cut.

**Payout Ratio = Dividends per Share / Earnings per Share**
A payout ratio below 50% is generally considered conservative and sustainable. Above 80% leaves little room for error. Above 100% means the company is paying dividends from debt or asset sales — almost always unsustainable.

**Cash Payout Ratio = Dividends Paid / Free Cash Flow**
More reliable than the earnings-based payout ratio, because some companies generate strong earnings but weak cash flow (or vice versa). A company that pays out 90% of its earnings but only 40% of its free cash flow is actually in a much stronger position than the earnings-based ratio suggests.

### Dividend growth vs. high yield

Investors often choose between two strategies:

- **High yield investing**: seeking current income from stocks with yields of 5–8%+. The risk is that high yields often reflect market skepticism about sustainability.
- **Dividend growth investing**: seeking companies with lower initial yields (1–3%) but consistent annual dividend growth of 5–10%+. These "dividend growers" tend to be high-quality businesses with growing earnings. A 3% yield growing at 8% annually doubles to 6% yield on your original cost in 9 years.

The **Dividend Aristocrats** (S&P 500 companies that have increased dividends for 25+ consecutive years) and **Dividend Kings** (50+ years) are the benchmarks for dividend growth quality.

### Dividend cuts and their consequences

A dividend cut is one of the most punished events in the stock market. A stock can fall 20–40% on the day a dividend is reduced because:
1. Income-focused investors sell immediately.
2. The cut signals management's admission that the business is underperforming.
3. It removes the stock from dividend-screened funds.

Monitoring payout ratio trends, free cash flow coverage, and debt levels helps anticipate dividend risk before the market does.

### Dividends vs. buybacks

Dividends and share buybacks are both ways to return capital, but they differ in flexibility and tax treatment. Dividends are contractual — cutting one is painful and visible. Buybacks can be paused without the same stigma. In many jurisdictions, buybacks are also more tax-efficient for shareholders because gains from buybacks (via price appreciation) are taxed as capital gains rather than ordinary income.

### Why it matters for a trader and investor

For income investors, dividends provide cash returns that compound over time independently of stock price. For all investors, dividend policy and payout ratio analysis provide a lens into management's confidence in future earnings and the true cash generation of the business. A company that raises its dividend consistently is making a very public commitment about its future — one worth monitoring.
