## In plain words

Return on capital measures how efficiently a company converts the money invested in it into profit. The three main ratios — **ROE** (Return on Equity), **ROIC** (Return on Invested Capital), and **ROA** (Return on Assets) — each answer a slightly different version of the same question: is management creating value with the resources it controls? A business that consistently earns more than its cost of capital is compounding wealth for shareholders; one that earns less is quietly destroying it.

## Quick demo

Two companies each earn $10 million in net income. Company A achieved this with $50 million of equity invested — an ROE of 20%. Company B needed $200 million of equity to earn the same $10M — an ROE of 5%. If the cost of equity (what shareholders expect) is around 10%, Company A is creating real value; Company B is destroying it. Same earnings, radically different quality. Return on capital ratios are the x-ray that reveals this.

## Full explanation

### Return on Equity (ROE)

**ROE = Net Income / Shareholders' Equity**

ROE tells you how much profit the business generates per dollar of shareholders' capital. A consistently high ROE (above 15–20%) suggests the business has strong competitive advantages — it can earn high profits without needing to reinvest huge amounts of capital.

**The DuPont decomposition** breaks ROE into three components:
- **Net Profit Margin** (Net Income / Revenue) — profitability of each sale
- **Asset Turnover** (Revenue / Assets) — efficiency of assets in generating sales
- **Equity Multiplier** (Assets / Equity) — the leverage used

ROE = Net Profit Margin × Asset Turnover × Equity Multiplier

This is powerful: a company can boost ROE by increasing leverage (borrowing more), which artificially inflates the number without improving the underlying business. Always check whether a high ROE is earned through genuine efficiency or funded by debt.

### Return on Invested Capital (ROIC)

**ROIC = Net Operating Profit After Tax (NOPAT) / Invested Capital**

Invested Capital = Equity + Interest-Bearing Debt − Cash

ROIC is considered the most complete measure of capital efficiency because it includes both equity and debt capital in the denominator, removing the distortion from leverage that affects ROE. Businesses with sustained ROIC well above their **Weighted Average Cost of Capital (WACC)** are creating shareholder value. ROIC below WACC means the business is destroying value even if it is growing.

Warren Buffett's best investments (See's Candies, Coca-Cola) were characterized by very high ROIC sustained over decades — the hallmark of a genuine economic moat.

### Return on Assets (ROA)

**ROA = Net Income / Total Assets**

ROA measures how efficiently a company uses all its assets (regardless of how they are financed) to generate profit. It is most useful for:
- **Banks and financial institutions** (where leverage is structural)
- **Capital-heavy industries** where asset efficiency is the core variable
- **Quick comparisons** across companies without worrying about capital structure

A declining ROA over several years can signal asset bloat, acquisition dilution, or a business losing its efficiency edge.

### Comparing the three

| Metric | Denominator | Best used for |
|---|---|---|
| ROE | Equity only | Profitability per shareholder dollar |
| ROIC | Equity + Debt − Cash | True capital efficiency (leverage-neutral) |
| ROA | All assets | Asset utilization, cross-leverage comparison |

### Benchmarks and context

Return ratios must be compared to the **cost of capital** and to **industry peers**. A 12% ROE for a regulated utility is excellent (their cost of equity is ~6–8%). A 12% ROE for a software company is mediocre (investors expect 10–12% cost of equity, and peers routinely deliver 30%+). Context determines everything.

### Why it matters for a trader and investor

High and stable ROIC is one of the best long-term predictors of stock outperformance. A business that repeatedly earns 25% on capital can grow by reinvesting at those returns — compounding wealth at rates that justify elevated valuations. Conversely, capital-intensive businesses with low ROIC tend to be value traps: they always need more money just to stay in place. Learning to screen for return-on-capital metrics helps identify the compounders worth holding for years versus the mediocre businesses worth avoiding regardless of price.
