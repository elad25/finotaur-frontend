## In plain words

The **Price-to-Earnings ratio (P/E)** compares a company's stock price to its earnings per share — it tells you how much the market is willing to pay for each dollar of profit. A P/E of 20 means investors are paying $20 for every $1 of annual earnings. It is the most widely quoted valuation metric in investing, and also one of the most frequently misused. Context — industry, growth rate, interest rates, and accounting quality — determines whether a P/E is cheap or expensive.

## Quick demo

Company A earns $5 per share and trades at $100. Its P/E is 20x. Company B earns $5 per share and trades at $50. Its P/E is 10x. At first glance B looks cheaper. But if A is growing earnings at 25% per year and B is growing at 0%, the market is rational — it is paying a premium for A's future earnings. A low P/E on a declining business is a **value trap**; a high P/E on a fast-growing quality business may be entirely justified. That is the P/E's central trap.

## Full explanation

### How P/E is calculated

**P/E = Stock Price / Earnings Per Share (EPS)**

There are two common variants:
- **Trailing P/E**: uses the last 12 months of actual EPS. Grounded in reported history.
- **Forward P/E**: uses analyst consensus estimates for the next 12 months. More forward-looking, but depends on forecast accuracy.

EPS itself can be reported on a **basic** basis (simple share count) or **diluted** basis (including options, warrants, and convertible securities). Diluted EPS is the more conservative and more commonly used figure.

### What the P/E actually measures

The P/E ratio is the market's implicit answer to: "How many years of current earnings am I willing to pay for today?" A P/E of 15 means 15 years of flat earnings to "pay back" the price — obviously simplistic, but it illustrates why growth expectations dominate.

The inverse of the P/E — **1 / P/E** — is the **earnings yield**. A P/E of 20 implies a 5% earnings yield. Investors compare this to the 10-year Treasury yield to judge whether stocks are fairly priced relative to bonds.

### The P/E's main traps

**1. Cyclical earnings distortion**
At the top of a business cycle, companies report peak earnings, making P/Es look low. At the bottom, earnings collapse, making P/Es look high — just when stocks are actually cheap. The **cyclically adjusted P/E (CAPE or Shiller P/E)** uses a 10-year average of inflation-adjusted earnings to smooth this distortion.

**2. Accounting earnings are not cash**
A company can inflate earnings through aggressive revenue recognition, capitalized expenses, or pension accounting choices. Always sanity-check EPS against operating cash flow per share.

**3. One-time items**
A large legal settlement gain or an asset sale can inflate EPS for a quarter. Always strip those out when calculating a "normalized" P/E.

**4. Different industries, different norms**
Banks trade at 8–12x earnings because they are slow-growing and capital-intensive. Software companies routinely trade at 30–60x because of high margins and recurring revenue. Comparing across sectors without adjustment is meaningless.

### P/E relative to growth — the PEG ratio

The **PEG ratio** adjusts for growth: PEG = P/E / Expected Earnings Growth Rate (%). A PEG below 1.0 is often considered cheap relative to growth. A stock with a P/E of 30 and a 30% growth rate has a PEG of 1.0 — arguably fairly valued.

### Why it matters for a trader and investor

Stocks move when earnings or the multiple (P/E) changes — or both. Understanding whether a stock is re-rating (multiple expanding or contracting) versus simply reporting better numbers helps distinguish durable moves from temporary ones. A stock whose P/E is compressing even as earnings grow is telling you something: the market is losing confidence in the forward story. Tracking P/E alongside earnings growth is one of the most practical habits in fundamental stock analysis.
