## In plain words

The P/E ratio is just one tool in a valuation toolkit. **P/B** (Price-to-Book) compares market value to accounting net assets — useful for banks and asset-heavy businesses. **P/S** (Price-to-Sales) values a company relative to revenue — used when earnings are negative or volatile. **EV/EBITDA** compares the entire enterprise value (debt + equity) to operating earnings — the preferred metric for comparing companies with different capital structures. Each multiple fits some situations well and others poorly.

## Quick demo

A pharmaceutical startup has no earnings yet, so P/E is meaningless. But it has $50M in annual revenue and trades at a market cap of $200M — that's a P/S of 4x. Comparable drug developers trade at 6–10x sales. At 4x, the startup looks cheap relative to peers. Meanwhile, a mature industrial company is best valued on EV/EBITDA: its enterprise value is $1B and it generates $100M in EBITDA — a 10x multiple. Peers trade at 8–12x, so it sits in the middle of the range. Same logic, different metric, different business.

## Full explanation

### Price-to-Book (P/B)

**P/B = Market Price per Share / Book Value per Share**

Book value per share is shareholders' equity divided by shares outstanding — essentially what each share would theoretically be worth if the company liquidated at accounting values.

**When it works well:**
- **Financial companies** (banks, insurers): their assets are largely financial instruments with knowable market values.
- **Asset-heavy industries**: steel, real estate, shipping.
- **Distressed investing**: buying below book value (P/B < 1) can signal deep value — or permanent impairment.

**When it breaks down:**
- **Software, media, consumer brands**: most of the value is in intangible assets (brand, IP, customer relationships) that accounting does not fully capture. These companies routinely trade at 10–30x book with good reason.

### Price-to-Sales (P/S)

**P/S = Market Capitalization / Annual Revenue**

P/S is particularly useful for high-growth, pre-profit companies where earnings are temporarily negative due to investment in expansion.

**When it works well:**
- Early-stage tech, biotech, or any hypergrowth company burning cash to build market share.
- Comparing companies in the same industry with similar margin profiles.

**When it breaks down:**
- A company with a 5% net margin and a 5x P/S is trading at 100x earnings — that matters. P/S ignores profitability entirely. Two companies at 3x P/S with 40% and 5% gross margins respectively are not equivalent.

### EV/EBITDA — the enterprise multiple

**EV = Market Cap + Total Debt − Cash**
**EV/EBITDA = Enterprise Value / EBITDA**

This is the most commonly used metric in mergers and acquisitions because it measures what a buyer actually pays (enterprise value) relative to operating earnings power before financing and accounting choices.

**Why EV instead of market cap?**
A buyer who acquires a company also inherits its debt and receives its cash. Using enterprise value makes the comparison capital-structure-neutral. A company with $1B in market cap and $500M in net debt has an EV of $1.5B — a critical difference.

**Typical EV/EBITDA ranges by industry:**
- Mature industrials: 6–10x
- Consumer staples: 10–14x
- Technology (mid-growth): 15–25x
- High-growth SaaS: 20–50x+

### Choosing the right multiple

| Situation | Best multiple(s) |
|---|---|
| Profitable, mature company | P/E, EV/EBITDA |
| Pre-profit, high growth | P/S, EV/Revenue |
| Bank or insurer | P/B, P/TBV |
| Comparing across leverage levels | EV/EBITDA, EV/FCF |
| Asset-heavy cyclicals | EV/EBITDA, P/B |

### Why it matters for a trader and investor

Using the wrong multiple leads to systematically mispricing stocks. Applying P/E to a loss-making company produces a meaningless result. Applying P/S to a low-margin business creates an illusion of cheapness. Professional analysts always benchmark a company's current multiple against its own history and its peer group — and they choose the multiple that most cleanly reflects the source of value for that specific business. Learning to switch between multiples based on context is a core skill in fundamental analysis.
