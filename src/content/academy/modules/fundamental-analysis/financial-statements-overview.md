## In plain words

Every public company files three core financial statements that together paint a complete picture of its health. The **income statement** shows whether the business is profitable. The **balance sheet** shows what it owns and owes at a point in time. The **cash flow statement** shows the actual cash moving in and out. These three documents are the raw material of all fundamental analysis.

## Quick demo

Think of a food truck. The income statement tells you it sold $200,000 worth of tacos and netted $30,000 in profit after paying for ingredients, labor, and permits. The balance sheet tells you the truck itself is worth $40,000, the owner still owes $15,000 on the loan, and has $10,000 cash in the bank. The cash flow statement reconciles everything — showing that even though it reported $30,000 in profit, the real cash collected was $27,000 because some catering clients haven't paid yet. All three views are necessary.

## Full explanation

### Why three statements instead of one?

Accounting rules allow companies to **recognize revenue and expenses** before or after cash actually changes hands. This means profit on the income statement and cash in the bank can diverge significantly. The three-statement system exists precisely to capture what pure cash accounting and pure accrual accounting each miss on their own.

### The income statement (P&L)

- Covers a **period** of time (a quarter or a year).
- Shows: Revenue → Cost of goods sold → Gross profit → Operating expenses → Operating income → Interest and taxes → Net income.
- Tells you: Is the business growing? Are margins improving or shrinking?

### The balance sheet

- A **snapshot** at a single moment in time (the end of the quarter or fiscal year).
- Structured as: **Assets = Liabilities + Shareholders' Equity**.
- Assets: cash, receivables, inventory, property, intangibles.
- Liabilities: debt, accounts payable, deferred revenue.
- Equity: what shareholders actually own after subtracting all debts.
- Tells you: Is the company financially strong? Can it survive a downturn?

### The cash flow statement

- Also covers a **period** of time, matching the income statement.
- Divided into three sections:
  - **Operating cash flow** — cash from the core business (the most important section).
  - **Investing cash flow** — cash spent on or received from long-term assets (capex, acquisitions).
  - **Financing cash flow** — cash from debt, equity issuances, dividends, and buybacks.
- Tells you: Is profit real? Is the business self-funding or burning cash?

### How the three statements connect

The net income from the income statement feeds into the equity section of the balance sheet. The cash flow statement starts with net income and adjusts it for non-cash items (like depreciation) to arrive at real cash. The ending cash balance on the cash flow statement must match the cash line on the balance sheet. These links are called the **articulation** of the statements — professional analysts check them as a basic integrity test.

### Why it matters for a trader and investor

Executives and sales teams can tell a compelling story. Financial statements are harder to fake — when the cash flow statement doesn't support the income statement's profit claims, that divergence is a red flag worth acting on. Learning to read all three statements, even at a high level, separates investors who understand a business from those who are just reading headlines.
