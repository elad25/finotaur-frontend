## In plain words

The **cash flow statement** tracks the actual cash moving into and out of a business over a period. Unlike profit, cash cannot be manipulated by accounting rules — it either arrived in the bank or it didn't. A company can report accounting profits while running out of cash; the cash flow statement is the document that reveals this. The rule every analyst knows: **profit is an opinion, cash is a fact.**

## Quick demo

A construction company reports $5 million in net income for the year. Impressive — until you check the cash flow statement. It turns out $3 million of that income came from a one-time land sale, and the actual operating cash flow was only $800,000. Worse, the company spent $4 million buying new equipment, so **free cash flow** (operating cash minus capital expenditures) was negative $3.2 million. The business burned cash even while reporting profit. Without the cash flow statement, you'd have no idea.

## Full explanation

### The three sections

**1. Cash Flow from Operations (CFO)**
This is the most important section. It starts with net income and adjusts for:
- Non-cash charges added back (depreciation, amortization — expenses that reduced profit but didn't cost cash).
- Changes in working capital: rising receivables reduce cash (customers haven't paid yet); rising payables increase it (suppliers are being paid later).

A healthy business should consistently generate positive operating cash flow that is close to or greater than net income. When CFO persistently lags net income, it is a serious warning sign.

**2. Cash Flow from Investing (CFI)**
This section records cash spent on long-term assets and investments:
- **Capital expenditures (capex)** — buying property, equipment, or technology. Usually negative.
- Acquisitions and divestitures.
- Purchases or sales of investment securities.

High capex is not inherently bad — it often funds future growth — but it does consume cash that could otherwise be returned to shareholders.

**3. Cash Flow from Financing (CFF)**
This section records transactions with lenders and shareholders:
- Debt raised (positive) or repaid (negative).
- Equity issued (positive) or shares repurchased (negative).
- Dividends paid (negative).

A business that constantly raises new debt or equity just to stay afloat is a red flag; one that consistently repurchases shares and pays dividends from operations is demonstrating financial strength.

### Free Cash Flow (FCF) — the most-watched metric

**Free Cash Flow = Operating Cash Flow − Capital Expenditures**

FCF is the cash the business generates that is truly free: it can repay debt, fund acquisitions, pay dividends, or buy back stock. Many professional investors consider FCF the purest measure of a company's value-creating ability. High FCF yield (FCF / market cap) often signals an undervalued company.

### The cash conversion cycle

Analysts also watch the **cash conversion cycle** — how many days it takes from spending cash on inventory to collecting cash from customers. A shorter cycle means the business is efficient; a lengthening cycle means cash is tied up in the business longer, which can signal operational problems.

### Common patterns to recognize

| Pattern | What it might mean |
|---|---|
| High net income, low CFO | Aggressive revenue recognition, slow collections |
| Negative FCF, high capex | Growth investment phase — judge based on return on that investment |
| Positive CFO, negative CFF | Mature business returning cash to shareholders |
| Negative CFO every quarter | Business may be burning through reserves |

### Why it matters for a trader and investor

Earnings can be engineered within accounting rules. Cash cannot be created from nothing. Companies that have imploded in corporate scandals (Enron, Wirecard) often showed deteriorating cash flow years before the stock collapsed, while earnings looked fine. Learning to read the cash flow statement — especially the gap between net income and operating cash — is one of the highest-return skills in fundamental analysis.
