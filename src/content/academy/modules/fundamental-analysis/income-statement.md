## In plain words

The **income statement** (also called the profit and loss statement, or P&L) is a running record of a company's revenue and expenses over a specific period. It answers one question: did the business make more money than it spent? It starts at the top with **revenue** (all sales), subtracts every category of cost, and ends at the bottom with **net income** — the famous "bottom line."

## Quick demo

A software company reports $10 million in annual revenue. It cost $2 million to deliver the software (hosting, support), leaving $8 million in **gross profit**. After paying $4 million in salaries, marketing, and rent, **operating income** is $4 million. After $500,000 in interest on a loan and $700,000 in taxes, **net income** is $2.8 million. That $2.8 million is what the company "earned" for shareholders — but reading only the bottom line hides the margin story embedded in every line above it.

## Full explanation

### The anatomy of an income statement

**1. Revenue (Sales)**
The total amount billed or earned from customers. This is often called the "top line." Growth here is a positive signal, but revenue alone tells you nothing about profitability.

**2. Cost of Goods Sold (COGS)**
The direct costs of producing or delivering the product or service — raw materials, manufacturing labor, cloud hosting for a SaaS company. Subtract COGS from revenue to get **gross profit**.

**3. Gross Profit & Gross Margin**
- Gross Profit = Revenue − COGS
- Gross Margin = Gross Profit / Revenue
- A high gross margin (e.g., 70%+ for software) means the business keeps most of what it charges before paying overhead.

**4. Operating Expenses (OpEx)**
Costs not directly tied to production: sales and marketing, research and development, general and administrative (G&A). Subtract these from gross profit to get **operating income** (also called EBIT: Earnings Before Interest and Taxes).

**5. EBITDA**
A widely used metric: Operating Income + Depreciation + Amortization. It approximates operating cash generation before accounting adjustments and financing choices. Useful for comparing companies across different capital structures, but not a substitute for actual cash flow.

**6. Interest and Other Income/Expense**
Interest paid on debt reduces the pre-tax income. Interest earned on cash or investments adds to it. This section reflects the cost of the company's capital structure.

**7. Taxes**
The effective tax rate on pre-tax income. It varies by jurisdiction, deductions, and deferred tax treatments.

**8. Net Income**
The famous bottom line. Net income = all revenue − all expenses − taxes. This feeds directly into earnings per share (EPS), which drives most stock valuations.

### Key relationships to memorize

| Metric | Formula |
|---|---|
| Gross Profit | Revenue − COGS |
| Operating Income | Gross Profit − OpEx |
| Net Income | Operating Income − Interest − Taxes |

### Common pitfalls

- **One-time items** can inflate or deflate net income. Asset sales, legal settlements, and restructuring charges are not part of the core business. Analysts often strip these out to assess "normalized" earnings.
- **Non-cash charges** like depreciation and amortization reduce net income without using cash. This is why the cash flow statement is essential alongside the P&L.
- **Revenue recognition timing** — some companies book revenue aggressively early; others conservatively. Read the notes in the filing for the company's policy.

### Why it matters for a trader and investor

The income statement is the most-watched statement for a reason: it drives EPS, which drives stock price in the short term. But a sophisticated analyst goes beyond EPS to track gross margin trends (is pricing power holding?), operating leverage (are costs growing slower than revenue?), and whether "beat" quarters are driven by real performance or accounting choices. Knowing how to read a P&L end to end puts you in command of the numbers that move stocks on earnings day.
