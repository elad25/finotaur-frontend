## In plain words

**Financial leverage** means using borrowed money alongside equity to run a business. Debt can amplify returns when a company earns more on its assets than it pays in interest — but it also amplifies losses and creates a fixed obligation that must be met regardless of business conditions. The **Debt-to-Equity ratio** is the most direct measure of how much leverage a company is carrying. Reading the balance sheet for debt levels and coverage ratios is one of the most important risk-management habits in fundamental analysis.

## Quick demo

A real estate developer buys a $1 million property using $200,000 of its own equity and $800,000 of bank debt. If the property rises to $1.1 million, the equity return is 50% ($100K gain on $200K invested) — leverage amplified the return fivefold. But if the property falls to $900,000, the equity is wiped out ($200K loss on $200K) while the bank still demands its $800K back. The business is insolvent. Same leverage, two scenarios: spectacular gain or complete failure. Debt cuts both ways, always.

## Full explanation

### Types of debt

- **Short-term debt**: matures within one year — commercial paper, revolving credit facilities, the current portion of long-term debt. Short-term debt is risky because it must be refinanced frequently; if credit markets tighten or the company's creditworthiness deteriorates, refinancing may be impossible or very expensive.
- **Long-term debt**: bonds or term loans with maturities beyond one year. More predictable but often subject to **covenants** — financial restrictions the company must satisfy (e.g., maintaining a minimum interest coverage ratio).

### Key leverage ratios

**Debt-to-Equity (D/E) = Total Debt / Shareholders' Equity**
The most commonly cited leverage ratio. What is "too high" depends heavily on the industry:
- Utilities and real estate: 1–3x is normal (stable cash flows support high leverage).
- Technology: 0–0.5x is typical (growth companies prefer flexibility).
- Airlines and retailers: variable, but high D/E is a persistent bankruptcy risk factor.

**Net Debt = Total Debt − Cash and Equivalents**
A company with $500M in debt and $300M in cash has net debt of $200M — far more manageable than the gross debt figure suggests. Net debt is always the more relevant figure.

**Net Debt / EBITDA**
The standard measure of leverage in credit analysis. It answers: how many years of operating cash earnings would it take to repay all net debt?
- Below 1x: conservative, low risk.
- 1–3x: moderate, manageable for most businesses.
- 4–6x: elevated, requires stable cash flows.
- Above 6x: distressed territory — common in leveraged buyouts but dangerous without visibility.

**Interest Coverage Ratio = EBIT / Interest Expense**
Measures how comfortably operating profit covers the interest bill. A ratio above 3x is considered healthy. Below 1.5x, there is real risk of interest payments squeezing the business into financial distress.

### Good debt vs. bad debt

Context determines whether leverage is value-creating or value-destroying:

- **Good leverage**: borrowing at 4% to invest in projects earning 15% ROIC. The spread creates value for shareholders.
- **Bad leverage**: borrowing to fund operating losses, pay dividends that are not earned, or finance expensive acquisitions that never generate the promised returns.
- **Cyclical risk**: leverage that is sustainable at the top of the business cycle may become existential at the bottom. Companies with high fixed debt obligations and cyclical revenues are the most vulnerable.

### Reading the debt maturity schedule

A company's debt does not all come due at once — it is spread across years, creating a **maturity schedule**. If a large debt tranche matures in a single year, the company faces a "wall" of refinancing risk at that moment. Always check whether significant debt matures during a period when the company's cash flow might be weakest.

### Why it matters for a trader and investor

Excessive debt is the most common cause of permanent capital loss in investing. A declining business with no debt has time to recover; a declining business with heavy debt can face a death spiral. During market downturns, highly leveraged companies see the largest declines because investors price in the risk of covenant breaches, forced asset sales, and equity dilution from distressed refinancing. Monitoring the D/E ratio, net debt/EBITDA, and interest coverage ratio as a basic filter protects you from the most dangerous situations in your watchlist.
