## In plain words

The **primary market** is where new securities are created and sold for the first time — the company receives the money. The **secondary market** is where investors then trade those securities among themselves — the company receives nothing from these trades. When you buy shares of Apple on NASDAQ today, you are trading in the secondary market; the last time Apple used the primary market was its IPO in 1980.

## Quick demo

A biotech firm needs $200 million for clinical trials. It works with an investment bank, prices 10 million new shares at $20 each, and sells them to institutional investors in an **IPO** (initial public offering) — that's the primary market in action. The next morning, those investors can sell their shares to anyone on the stock exchange. From that point on, every trade between investors is secondary-market activity, and the biotech firm's bank account is unaffected.

## Full explanation

### The primary market

The primary market is the fundraising layer of the capital markets. Key events:

- **IPO (Initial Public Offering)** — a private company sells shares to the public for the first time. Investment banks underwrite the deal: they buy the shares from the company at a guaranteed price, then resell them to investors, pocketing the spread.
- **Follow-on offering (FPO / secondary offering)** — a company that is already public issues additional shares to raise more capital. Dilutes existing shareholders.
- **Rights issue** — existing shareholders get the right to buy new shares at a discount before outsiders can.
- **Bond issuance** — governments and corporations sell newly created bonds directly to investors (typically institutional buyers) through a primary offering process called a **debt capital markets (DCM)** deal.

The key characteristic: proceeds go to the issuer.

### The secondary market

Once securities exist, the secondary market allows continuous trading between investors. This is the market you interact with every day through your brokerage account.

**Organized exchanges** (NYSE, NASDAQ) operate with centralized order books, listing standards, and real-time price dissemination.

**Over-the-counter (OTC) markets** are decentralized — trades are negotiated directly between parties (or through dealer networks) without a central exchange. Most bonds, currencies, and derivatives trade OTC.

### Why the secondary market matters for the primary market

Companies can only raise money in the primary market because investors know they can exit in the secondary market. Without liquidity, no rational investor would lock up capital forever. The existence of secondary markets is what makes primary capital formation possible in the first place.

### Key concepts to know

- **Underwriting** — the investment bank guarantees the issuer a price, taking on the risk of selling the shares to the public.
- **Book building** — the process of gauging investor demand to set the IPO price range.
- **Lock-up period** — insiders (founders, early investors) typically cannot sell their shares for 90–180 days after an IPO, preventing an immediate dump.
- **Greenshoe option (overallotment)** — allows underwriters to sell up to 15% more shares than planned if demand is strong, stabilizing the post-IPO price.

### Why it matters for a trader or investor

IPO and secondary offering announcements move prices — new shares dilute existing shareholders, and the pricing signals where smart institutional money sees value. Recognizing which type of transaction is happening (does cash go to the company, or to a selling shareholder?) tells you something real about the company's motivation and financial health.
