## In plain words

Financial markets do not trade around the clock. US stock exchanges are open from **9:30 AM to 4:00 PM Eastern Time**, Monday through Friday. Outside those hours, **pre-market** (4:00 AM – 9:30 AM ET) and **after-hours** (4:00 PM – 8:00 PM ET) sessions exist but are governed by different rules, with far less liquidity and far wider spreads — making them significantly riskier for most traders.

## Quick demo

A company reports earnings after 4:00 PM on Tuesday. The stock, which closed at $80, immediately trades to $95 in after-hours on an earnings beat. You see the move and try to buy at $95 — but the spread is $94.50 / $96.00 because there are very few participants. You buy at $96. When the regular session opens at 9:30 AM Wednesday, early institutional sellers push the price back to $92 before it stabilizes. You paid $4 more than the open because you acted in thin after-hours conditions. Understanding trading hours would have led you to wait.

## Full explanation

### Standard trading hours by market

| Market | Regular session |
|---|---|
| US equities (NYSE, NASDAQ) | 9:30 AM – 4:00 PM ET |
| US options | 9:30 AM – 4:00 PM ET (some to 4:15 PM) |
| US futures (CME e-mini) | Nearly 24 hours, 5 days a week (closed ~1 hr/day) |
| Forex (spot) | 24 hours, Sunday 5 PM – Friday 5 PM ET |
| Crypto | 24 hours, 7 days |
| European equities (LSE) | 8:00 AM – 4:30 PM GMT |
| Asian equities (TSE) | 9:00 AM – 3:30 PM JST |

### Pre-market and after-hours sessions

**Pre-market** runs from roughly 4:00 AM to 9:30 AM ET. **After-hours** runs from 4:00 PM to 8:00 PM ET. Both are available through many US brokers using Electronic Communication Networks (ECNs) — electronic order books that match orders without a centralized exchange.

Key characteristics of extended-hours trading:

- **Lower volume** — fewer participants, so orders are harder to fill without moving the price.
- **Wider spreads** — market makers post wider quotes to compensate for higher inventory risk.
- **Limit orders only** — most brokers do not accept market orders outside regular hours, protecting you from severe slippage in illiquid conditions.
- **Incomplete price discovery** — the extended-hours price is often not predictive of where the regular session will open, because institutional order flow (which dominates price discovery) tends to wait for the open.

### Key market events around the open and close

- **Opening Auction** (9:30 AM ET) — all orders that accumulated overnight are matched at a single opening price through a call auction. This is when the gap-up or gap-down you see in charts actually occurs.
- **Market on Close (MOC) orders** — orders submitted for execution at the closing auction price. Index funds and ETFs often rebalance using MOC orders, creating volume spikes at 3:55–4:00 PM.
- **Halt** — a stock can be halted at any time by an exchange (pending news, circuit breaker triggered) or by regulators. Trading stops completely until the halt is lifted.

### Circuit breakers

US markets have market-wide circuit breakers to slow panics:

- **Level 1** — S&P 500 drops 7%: trading halted 15 minutes.
- **Level 2** — S&P 500 drops 13%: trading halted 15 minutes.
- **Level 3** — S&P 500 drops 20%: trading halted for the rest of the day.

Individual stocks also have **Limit Up-Limit Down (LULD)** bands that pause trading when a stock moves too fast in either direction within a 5-minute window.

### Global session overlap

Because different markets are open at different times, currency and futures traders pay close attention to session overlaps, where volume tends to be highest:

- **London / New York overlap** (8:00 AM – 12:00 PM ET) — the most liquid period for EUR/USD and major forex pairs.
- **Asian open** — relevant for JPY pairs, Nikkei futures, and commodities with Asian demand (copper, iron ore).

### Why it matters for a trader or investor

Trading in extended hours without understanding the risks is a common mistake. Earnings releases, Fed statements, and economic data often drop outside regular hours — when spreads are widest and liquidity is thinnest. For most participants, waiting for the regular session open (even 15–30 minutes in, once initial volatility settles) produces better fills and clearer price action than chasing moves in the pre-market dark.
