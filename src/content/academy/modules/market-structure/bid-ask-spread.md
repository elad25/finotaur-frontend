## In plain words

The **bid-ask spread** is the gap between the highest price a buyer will pay (the **bid**) and the lowest price a seller will accept (the **ask**). Every time you trade, you pay this gap as an invisible cost — you buy at the ask and sell at the bid, never at the midpoint. It is the most common hidden trading cost, and it compounds silently across every trade you make.

## Quick demo

A stock is quoted at $49.97 bid / $50.03 ask. You buy 100 shares at $50.03, then immediately try to sell — you can only get $49.97. You lost $0.06 per share, or $6 total, without the price moving a single cent. Do that 20 times a month on $5,000 positions and you've paid $120 in spread costs alone, before any commissions. On an illiquid small-cap with a $0.50 spread, that same round-trip would cost $50 on 100 shares — 1% of the position gone before you even take a directional view.

## Full explanation

### What drives the spread

The size of the bid-ask spread is determined by:

- **Liquidity** — the more buyers and sellers competing, the narrower the spread. Apple trades millions of shares a day; its spread is often $0.01. A thinly-traded micro-cap might have a $0.50 or wider spread.
- **Volatility** — in fast-moving markets, market makers widen spreads to compensate for the risk that the price moves against them before they can offset a position. News releases often widen spreads sharply.
- **Market maker competition** — more competing market makers = narrower spreads.
- **Time of day** — spreads tend to be widest at the open and close, when uncertainty is highest, and tightest during mid-session when liquidity is deepest.

### Types of trading costs

The spread is just one of several costs embedded in trading:

- **Bid-ask spread** — the cost of crossing the market.
- **Commissions** — explicit fees charged by the broker per trade. Many retail brokers now charge $0, but zero-commission doesn't mean zero-cost (see PFOF below).
- **Slippage** — the difference between expected price and actual fill price, caused by moving through multiple levels of the order book or by price moving during the order's transmission.
- **Market impact** — for large orders, the act of buying moves the price up (or selling moves it down), resulting in a worse average fill.

### Payment for order flow (PFOF) and the spread

Many zero-commission retail brokers route your orders to **market makers** (often wholesale firms) who pay for the privilege of executing your trades. In return, those market makers execute your order at a price slightly better than the quoted spread — but not as good as the midpoint. The broker earns the PFOF payment; you get a fill that looks good on paper but may still leave money on the table relative to direct exchange routing.

The key insight: "free trading" redistributes the cost rather than eliminating it. The spread still exists; it is just allocated differently.

### Effective spread vs. quoted spread

The **quoted spread** is the difference between the best bid and best ask. The **effective spread** is what you actually paid — it accounts for the fact that your order may have received **price improvement** (a fill inside the quoted spread, common with PFOF arrangements) or may have been filled at a worse level due to slippage.

### How to minimize spread costs

- **Trade liquid securities** — large-cap stocks, major currency pairs, and front-month futures have the tightest spreads.
- **Use limit orders** — posting a limit order at the midpoint means you pay zero spread if filled; you are providing liquidity rather than consuming it. The risk is non-execution.
- **Avoid trading at the open and close** — spreads are often 2–3x wider in the first and last 15 minutes of the session.
- **Be aware of total round-trip cost** — not just the spread on entry, but the spread on exit, plus commissions, multiplied by your trading frequency.

### Why it matters for a trader or investor

The spread is a headwind on every trade. A day trader making 20 round trips a day on a $0.05-spread stock is paying $1.00 per share per day in spread costs alone. Understanding and minimizing spread cost is not optional — it is a core component of a positive expected-value trading strategy.
