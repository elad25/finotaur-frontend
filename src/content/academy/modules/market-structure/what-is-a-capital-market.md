## In plain words

A **capital market** is any place — physical or electronic — where buyers and sellers trade financial assets like stocks and bonds. It channels money from people who have it (investors) to businesses and governments that need it (issuers). **Exchanges** like the NYSE and NASDAQ provide the regulated venues; **regulators** like the SEC set and enforce the rules that keep the system fair and transparent.

## Quick demo

A startup wants $50 million to build a factory. Rather than beg a single bank, it sells 5 million shares at $10 each to thousands of investors through a stock exchange. Each investor owns a tiny piece of the factory and can sell their shares to someone else tomorrow — without asking the company for the money back. The company got its capital; the investors got a liquid asset. That's the capital market doing its job.

## Full explanation

### What a capital market actually is

Capital markets connect two groups:

- **Suppliers of capital** — individuals, pension funds, insurance companies, and sovereign wealth funds that have savings and want a return.
- **Demanders of capital** — corporations that want to expand, governments that need to fund spending, and agencies that need to refinance debt.

The market is the mechanism that sets the price at which those two groups meet.

### The main venues

**Stock exchanges** (equity markets) let companies sell ownership stakes and let investors trade those stakes with each other. The NYSE, NASDAQ, London Stock Exchange, and Tokyo Stock Exchange are the largest.

**Bond markets** (debt markets) let governments and corporations borrow money by issuing bonds — promises to pay back principal plus interest. The global bond market is actually larger than the global equity market by total value.

**Derivatives exchanges** (futures, options) let participants hedge risk or speculate on the future price of an underlying asset, using instruments described in other modules.

### Key players

- **Issuers** — companies and governments that raise money.
- **Investors** — institutions (mutual funds, hedge funds, pension plans) and retail participants (individual traders).
- **Intermediaries** — investment banks that underwrite new securities, brokers who execute orders, and market makers who provide liquidity.
- **Regulators** — bodies like the SEC (US), FCA (UK), or ESMA (EU) that set conduct rules, require disclosure, and punish fraud.
- **Exchanges and clearinghouses** — the infrastructure that matches orders and guarantees settlement.

### Regulation and transparency

Regulation exists because information asymmetry is dangerous: if insiders can trade on facts the public doesn't have, the market is not a fair game and public trust collapses. Rules like mandatory earnings disclosure, insider-trading bans, and position-reporting requirements force information into the open.

### Why it matters for a trader or investor

Every trade you place rides on this infrastructure — the exchange matching your order, a regulator ensuring the counterparty can't walk away, and a clearinghouse guaranteeing settlement. Understanding who each player is and what incentive they have helps you interpret market events: a regulatory crackdown, an exchange outage, or a new disclosure rule all affect price and liquidity in predictable ways.
