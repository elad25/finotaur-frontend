## In plain words

A **market index** is a calculated number that tracks the combined performance of a selected group of securities. The S&P 500, for example, tracks 500 large US companies and is used as the benchmark for "how the stock market did today." Indices are not directly tradable — but **ETFs** and **futures** that track them are, making indices central to both measurement and trading.

## Quick demo

The S&P 500 is **market-cap weighted**: Apple, with a $3 trillion market cap, has roughly 7% weight in the index, while a $10 billion company has about 0.02%. On a day when Apple rises 3% and that small company rises 10%, the index barely registers the small company's gain but rises meaningfully because of Apple. This weighting means the S&P 500 is not really "500 equal companies" — it is, in practice, a large-cap growth index where the top 10 stocks drive most of the day's movement.

## Full explanation

### What an index tracks

An index defines a **universe** (which securities are included) and a **weighting method** (how much influence each security has on the total). The combination determines what the index actually measures.

### The major US indices

- **S&P 500** — 500 large US companies, market-cap weighted. Considered the best single proxy for the US stock market. The most widely tracked benchmark.
- **Dow Jones Industrial Average (DJIA)** — 30 large blue-chip US companies, **price-weighted** (a higher-priced stock has more influence, regardless of company size). The oldest index and still heavily media-cited, but less representative than the S&P 500.
- **NASDAQ Composite** — all ~3,000+ companies listed on the NASDAQ exchange, market-cap weighted. Heavily skewed toward technology and growth.
- **NASDAQ 100 (NDX / QQQ)** — the 100 largest non-financial companies on NASDAQ. Even more concentrated in mega-cap tech.
- **Russell 2000** — 2,000 small-cap US companies. The benchmark for small-cap performance, often moves differently from large-cap indices.

### International indices

- **FTSE 100** (UK), **DAX** (Germany), **CAC 40** (France), **Nikkei 225** (Japan), **Hang Seng** (Hong Kong), **CSI 300** (China mainland) — each measures its local market.
- **MSCI World** and **MSCI Emerging Markets** — global indices used by institutional investors to benchmark international equity exposure.

### Weighting methods

| Method | How it works | Example |
|---|---|---|
| Market-cap weighted | Each stock weighted by its market capitalization | S&P 500, NASDAQ Composite |
| Price-weighted | Stocks with higher share prices have more influence | Dow Jones Industrial Average |
| Equal-weighted | Every stock has the same weight, rebalanced periodically | S&P 500 Equal Weight (RSP) |
| Factor-weighted | Weighted by a characteristic (dividends, volatility, etc.) | Dividend-focused, low-vol indices |

### Index rebalancing

Indices are reviewed periodically (quarterly or annually) and constituents change. When a company is added to the S&P 500, every index fund tracking it must buy that stock — creating a predictable demand surge. When a company is removed, index funds must sell. This **index effect** is well-documented and creates short-term trading opportunities around rebalancing dates.

### How traders and investors use indices

- **Benchmarking** — every active manager is measured against an index. Beating the S&P 500 (generating **alpha**) is the stated goal of active management.
- **Trading index futures and ETFs** — S&P 500 futures (ES, MES) and ETFs (SPY, IVV, VOO) let you trade the entire index in a single instrument.
- **Sector indices** — the S&P 500 is divided into 11 GICS sectors (Technology, Financials, Health Care, etc.), each with its own sub-index, allowing sector rotation analysis.
- **Volatility index (VIX)** — derived from S&P 500 options prices, the VIX measures implied volatility of the index and is a widely used measure of market fear.

### Why it matters for a trader or investor

No stock trades in isolation. Understanding which index a stock belongs to, how heavily it is weighted, and what the index itself is doing provides critical context. When the S&P 500 is in a confirmed downtrend, the odds of any individual long trade working are lower. When index rebalancing approaches, knowing which stocks are entering or leaving creates an edge. Indices are the map of the market — reading them is a foundational skill.
