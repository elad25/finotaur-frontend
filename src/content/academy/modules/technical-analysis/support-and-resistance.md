## In plain words

**Support** is a price level where buying has historically stepped in and stopped a decline. **Resistance** is a level where selling has historically capped a rally. Think of them as floors and ceilings in a building: price bounces off the floor (support) and hits the ceiling (resistance). When price breaks through either level, the old floor often becomes a new ceiling and vice versa — a concept called **role reversal**.

## Quick demo

Nvidia's stock bounces off $800 three times over two months without breaking below it. That $800 level is support: each time sellers pushed price down to $800, buyers absorbed the pressure and drove it back up. A trader watching this recognizes a pattern — and on the fourth test, buys near $800 with a stop-loss just below it, knowing that a break below $800 would mean the support level has failed.

## Full explanation

### What creates support and resistance

Price levels become significant for a few overlapping reasons:

- **Previous highs and lows** — areas where price has reversed before. Other traders remember these levels and act on them again.
- **Round numbers** — $100, $500, $50,000 attract orders because people naturally gravitate to round figures when placing limit orders.
- **Heavy volume zones** — if a lot of buying or selling occurred at a price, many traders have open positions near that level and will act again if price returns to it.
- **Moving averages** — widely watched moving averages (50-day, 200-day) act as dynamic support and resistance.

### How to identify levels

Look for price zones, not exact lines. Support and resistance are rarely a single precise price — they are zones, typically spanning a small range. A level is stronger when:

- Price has touched it multiple times without breaking through.
- High volume accompanied the prior reaction at that level.
- The level aligns with a round number, a moving average, or a prior high/low.

### Role reversal (polarity change)

One of the most reliable principles in TA is **role reversal**: when a support level is broken decisively, it often becomes a resistance level on the next rally, and vice versa. The reasoning: traders who bought at the old support are now sitting on losses; if price rallies back to that level, they may sell to break even — creating resistance where support once existed.

### Breakouts and false breakouts

A **breakout** occurs when price closes convincingly beyond a support or resistance level, often accompanied by higher volume. This signals that the balance of supply and demand has shifted. A **false breakout** (or fakeout) occurs when price briefly pierces a level then reverses — a common trap for traders who chase the move.

Waiting for a confirmed close beyond the level (rather than just a wick through it) reduces the risk of trading false breakouts.

### Support and resistance on different timeframes

A level that has held on the weekly chart carries more weight than one that formed on a 5-minute chart. When a daily-chart resistance aligns with a weekly-chart resistance, traders from multiple timeframes are watching the same level — making it self-reinforcing.

### Why it matters for a trader

Support and resistance are the single most widely used concept in technical analysis. They define where to enter trades, where to place stop-losses, and where to take profit. Even traders who disagree on every other aspect of TA tend to agree that these levels exist and influence price. Understanding them is not optional — it is foundational.
