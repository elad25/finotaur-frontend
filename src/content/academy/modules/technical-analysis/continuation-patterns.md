## In plain words

**Continuation patterns** are chart formations that appear during a trend and suggest the trend will resume after a brief pause. Think of them as the market "catching its breath" before moving further in the same direction. The most common examples are **flags**, **pennants**, **triangles**, and **rectangles**. Recognizing them helps traders stay in a trade — or enter a new one — rather than panicking during a normal consolidation.

## Quick demo

Amazon climbs 12% in three days on strong earnings — a sharp "flagpole." Then, for the next five days, price drifts sideways and slightly downward in a narrow band: a **bull flag**. Volume drops during the consolidation, then spikes on day six as price breaks higher and continues its climb. A trader who recognized the flag bought near the top of the breakout and measured a target equal to the height of the flagpole added to the breakout point.

## Full explanation

### Flags and pennants

Both form after a sharp, near-vertical price move (the "flagpole"):

- **Bull flag** — after a strong rally, price consolidates in a tight downward-sloping channel. Volume contracts. When price breaks out upward with a volume surge, the flag resolves in the direction of the prior trend.
- **Bear flag** — after a strong decline, price consolidates in a tight upward-sloping channel, then breaks down again.
- **Pennant** — similar to a flag but the consolidation forms converging trendlines (a small symmetrical triangle) rather than a parallel channel. Resolves in the same direction as the prior move.

Measuring the target: add the height of the flagpole to the breakout point to estimate the minimum expected move.

### Triangles

Triangles form as price makes higher lows and lower highs (or one side flat), compressing into a point. There are three types:

- **Ascending triangle** — horizontal resistance on top, rising lows on the bottom. Typically bullish: buyers keep pushing higher while sellers hold at the same level until buyers break through.
- **Descending triangle** — horizontal support on the bottom, falling highs on the top. Typically bearish: sellers keep pressing lower while buyers defend the same floor until sellers overwhelm it.
- **Symmetrical triangle** — converging trendlines with no flat side. Direction is less predetermined; traders wait for the breakout to confirm direction. Volume should contract during formation and expand on the breakout.

### Rectangles (trading ranges)

A rectangle (or box) forms when price bounces between a flat support level and a flat resistance level. When price finally breaks through either side with volume, the move often travels at least the height of the rectangle.

### How to trade continuation patterns

1. **Confirm the prior trend** — a flag in an uptrend is bullish; the same formation after a downtrend may be a bear flag.
2. **Watch volume** — volume should contract during the pattern and expand on the breakout.
3. **Wait for the breakout** — entering before the pattern resolves is aggressive. The pattern could break in either direction or simply fail.
4. **Set a stop** — place a stop below the low of the pattern (bull pattern) or above the high (bear pattern).
5. **Measure a target** — use the flagpole height or the width/height of the pattern to project the move.

### Common mistakes

- **Trading against the prior trend** — a bull flag is a *continuation* of the uptrend, not a reversal signal.
- **Entering too early** — price can oscillate inside the pattern for much longer than expected.
- **Ignoring volume** — a breakout without volume expansion is much more likely to fail (false breakout).

### Why it matters for a trader

Continuation patterns help traders distinguish between a healthy pause and a genuine reversal. If you exit a trending position the moment price starts to consolidate, you may miss the rest of a significant move. Recognizing that a pullback is merely a flag or triangle — not a breakdown — is a skill that directly improves trade management and profitability.
