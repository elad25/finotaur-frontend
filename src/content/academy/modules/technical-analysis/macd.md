## In plain words

**MACD** (Moving Average Convergence Divergence) is a trend-following momentum indicator that shows the relationship between two exponential moving averages. It has three components: the **MACD line** (the gap between a fast and slow EMA), the **signal line** (a smoothed average of the MACD line), and the **histogram** (the difference between the two, shown as bars). When the MACD line crosses the signal line, traders use that as a buy or sell signal.

Many traders interpret this as a momentum shift back to bullish and use it as a buy signal — confirmed if price is also above its 50-day MA.

## Full explanation

### MACD structure

The MACD was created by Gerald Appel in the late 1970s. Its standard parameters are:

- **Fast EMA**: 12 periods
- **Slow EMA**: 26 periods
- **Signal line**: 9-period EMA of the MACD line
- **MACD line** = 12-period EMA − 26-period EMA
- **Histogram** = MACD line − Signal line

When the fast EMA is above the slow EMA, the MACD line is positive. When they converge (come together), the MACD line approaches zero. When they diverge (move apart), the MACD line moves farther from zero.

### Reading the MACD

**MACD crossovers:**
- **Bullish crossover (MACD line crosses above the signal line)** — indicates that the short-term momentum is accelerating faster than the longer-term trend. Common buy signal.
- **Bearish crossover (MACD line crosses below the signal line)** — indicates that the short-term momentum is decelerating. Common sell signal.

**The histogram:**
- Positive histogram (MACD line above signal line) and growing = strengthening bullish momentum.
- Positive histogram but shrinking = bullish momentum is decelerating — potential crossover ahead.
- Negative histogram and growing in magnitude = strengthening bearish momentum.
- Negative histogram but shrinking = bearish momentum is decelerating.

**Zero-line crossover:**
- MACD line crossing above zero = the fast EMA has crossed above the slow EMA — a longer-term bullish signal.
- MACD line crossing below zero = the fast EMA has crossed below the slow EMA — a longer-term bearish signal.

### MACD divergence

Like RSI, MACD can show divergence with price:

- **Bearish divergence** — price makes a higher high, but the MACD histogram makes a lower high. Momentum is fading.
- **Bullish divergence** — price makes a lower low, but the MACD histogram makes a higher low. Selling momentum is exhausting.

MACD divergence is often clearer and more visually obvious than RSI divergence, making it a popular warning signal for trend exhaustion.

### Limitations

MACD is a **lagging indicator** — it is built entirely from moving averages of past prices, so it always reflects recent history rather than predicting future moves. In choppy, trendless markets, MACD generates frequent false crossover signals. It works best in clearly trending environments.

The standard 12/26/9 settings can be adjusted: shorter periods (e.g., 5/13/6) create a more responsive but noisier MACD suitable for shorter timeframes. Longer periods produce fewer, more reliable signals.

### Why it matters for a trader

MACD is one of the most widely used technical indicators because it combines two ideas — trend direction and momentum strength — into a single visual display. The histogram makes it easy to see whether momentum is building or waning, and crossovers provide clear, actionable signals. Used alongside price action, support/resistance levels, and other indicators, MACD is a powerful tool for timing entries and exits in trending markets.
