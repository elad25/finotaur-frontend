## In plain words

**Technical analysis (TA)** is the study of past price and volume data to anticipate future price movement. It rests on three core beliefs: that price already reflects all known information, that prices move in **trends**, and that human behavior repeats — so chart patterns recur. TA does not tell you *why* a stock moves; it tries to tell you *where* price is likely to go next.

## Quick demo

Apple trades at $180. An analyst reads no earnings reports, no news — only the chart. She notices price has bounced three times off the $175 level over two months. She bets that $175 is strong support and buys, placing a stop below it. Her decision was made entirely from price history, no financial statement needed. That is TA in practice.

## Full explanation

### The three pillars

Technical analysis is built on three foundational assumptions:

- **The market discounts everything.** Price already reflects all public information — earnings, rumors, sentiment, macroeconomics. Analysts who accept this believe there is nothing to gain from re-reading the 10-K that the market hasn't already priced in.
- **Price moves in trends.** Once a trend is established — upward, downward, or sideways — it is more likely to continue than to reverse. TA seeks to identify and follow trends rather than predict turning points from scratch.
- **History repeats.** Markets are driven by human emotion: greed, fear, hope, regret. Because human psychology is consistent, price patterns that appeared in the past (a flag, a head-and-shoulders, a support zone) tend to appear again and carry similar implications.

### What TA is not

TA does not predict the future with certainty. It is a probabilistic framework: it identifies situations where the historical odds favor one direction over another. No pattern has a 100% hit rate.

TA also does not explain fundamental value. A stock can be deeply overvalued by every financial metric and still trend higher for months — and TA can capture that trend without caring why.

### TA vs. fundamental analysis

Fundamental analysts ask "What is this worth?" and look for price to converge to value. Technical analysts ask "Which way is price moving?" and look to ride that movement. Many professionals combine both: fundamentals to decide *what* to trade, technicals to decide *when* to enter and exit.

### The honest limitations

TA is partly self-fulfilling: if millions of traders watch the same moving average or support level, they may collectively act on it, making the pattern work — at least temporarily. Critics argue that many chart patterns have no predictive edge once you adjust for randomness. The academic literature is mixed.

### Why it matters for a trader

Whether you believe in TA completely, partially, or skeptically, it is the dominant language of short-term markets. Most active traders use it. Levels that many participants watch become levels where orders cluster — and where price actually reacts. Understanding the assumptions behind TA helps you use it with appropriate confidence and recognize when it is likely to fail.
