## In plain words

**Bollinger Bands** are three lines drawn around price: a middle **moving average** (usually 20-period SMA) flanked by an upper and lower band set two **standard deviations** above and below it. The bands automatically widen when price is volatile and narrow when price is calm. When the bands squeeze tightly together — called the **Bollinger Squeeze** — it often signals that a large move is building; when the bands expand widely, it signals that a volatile move is underway.

## Quick demo

The EUR/USD currency pair has been in a tight range for three weeks. The Bollinger Bands have squeezed to their narrowest width in months — the upper and lower bands are almost parallel. Then, a surprise inflation report drops. The euro surges, price breaks above the upper band, and the bands expand rapidly. Traders using the squeeze as a setup had positioned for a breakout and caught the full move.

## Full explanation

### How Bollinger Bands are constructed

Developed by John Bollinger in the 1980s, the bands use standard deviation — a statistical measure of how much prices are dispersed from their average — to set dynamic boundaries:

- **Middle band**: 20-period SMA (the baseline)
- **Upper band**: Middle band + (2 × standard deviation of the 20-period close)
- **Lower band**: Middle band − (2 × standard deviation of the 20-period close)

Because standard deviation is a measure of volatility, the bands self-adjust: more volatility → wider bands; less volatility → narrower bands.

Statistically, if price were normally distributed, approximately 95% of price action would fall within the two standard deviation bands. In practice, markets are not perfectly normal, but the bands capture most price action most of the time.

### Key signals and interpretations

**The Bollinger Squeeze:**
When the bands contract to a very narrow width (sometimes measured using the Bandwidth indicator = (Upper − Lower) / Middle), it signals a period of low volatility — which historically precedes a significant directional move. The squeeze does not predict which direction the breakout will be; traders often wait for the actual breakout confirmation.

**Price touching or closing outside the bands:**
- Touching the upper band is not a sell signal by itself. In a strong uptrend, price can "walk the upper band," repeatedly closing near or above it for extended periods.
- Closing outside the bands, then returning inside, can signal the end of an extreme move.

**Mean reversion:**
In ranging markets, price hitting the upper band and turning down, or hitting the lower band and turning up, can be used as a mean-reversion trade back toward the middle band. However, this strategy fails badly in trending markets.

**Riding the band (trend confirmation):**
In a strong uptrend, price repeatedly touches or slightly exceeds the upper band. A drop back below the middle band (20 SMA) in this context signals weakening momentum, not necessarily a full reversal.

### Band width

The **Bandwidth** indicator isolates the distance between the bands as a percentage:

Bandwidth = (Upper Band − Lower Band) / Middle Band × 100

Very low bandwidth = squeeze (low volatility). High bandwidth = expansion (high volatility). Historical comparisons of bandwidth percentile help identify squeezes in the context of a stock's own history.

### Common settings variations

While 20 periods / 2 standard deviations is the classic setting, traders adjust these:

- **Shorter period (10)**: More responsive bands, useful on intraday charts.
- **Wider bands (2.5 σ)**: Fewer "outside" readings, useful for identifying truly extreme moves.
- **Narrower bands (1.5 σ)**: More sensitive to normal price swings.

### Why it matters for a trader

Bollinger Bands are among the most versatile indicators because they adapt to changing market conditions automatically — they are inherently dynamic. Unlike a fixed level, they tell you not just where price is but *how unusual* that position is relative to recent behavior. The squeeze setup in particular is a practical, widely used tool for anticipating volatility expansions, which often produce the best risk-reward trading opportunities.
