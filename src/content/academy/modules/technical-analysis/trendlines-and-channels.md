## In plain words

A **trendline** is a straight line drawn on a chart connecting a series of higher lows (in an uptrend) or lower highs (in a downtrend). It visually defines the direction and slope of a move. When you add a parallel line on the opposite side, connecting the highs (in an uptrend) or lows (in a downtrend), you get a **channel** — price oscillates between the two rails. Trendlines show where the trend holds; channels show where to expect bounces within it.

## Quick demo

Oil has been climbing for six weeks. Drawing a line connecting the three lowest points of each pullback produces an upward-sloping trendline at roughly $70, $73, and $77. A parallel line across the swing highs sits at roughly $80, $84, and $88. The channel is clear: traders buy near the lower rail and take profit near the upper rail — as long as price stays inside the channel, the trend is intact.

## Full explanation

### Drawing a valid trendline

A trendline requires at least two points to draw and a third to **confirm** it. More touches make the line more significant — a trendline that has been respected six times is more powerful than one respected twice.

Rules for clean trendlines:
- Connect **swing highs to swing highs** (downtrend) or **swing lows to swing lows** (uptrend).
- Do not force a line through candle bodies if it doesn't also pass through or near the wicks.
- The steeper the trendline, the more vulnerable it is — very steep trends often break and re-establish at a flatter angle.

### Uptrends and downtrends

- **Uptrend** — price makes higher highs and higher lows. The trendline connects the higher lows (the "floor" of the move). As long as price respects this line on pullbacks, the uptrend is intact.
- **Downtrend** — price makes lower highs and lower lows. The trendline connects the lower highs (the "ceiling" of the move).
- **Sideways trend** — price oscillates within a horizontal range. In this case, support and resistance levels replace diagonal trendlines.

### Channels

A **channel** is formed by drawing a second line parallel to the trendline, offset to capture the highs (in an uptrend) or the lows (in a downtrend). The two lines define the **upper and lower rails** of the channel.

- Price touching the lower rail of an upward channel is a potential buy zone.
- Price touching the upper rail is a potential target or exit zone.
- Channels can be ascending, descending, or horizontal.

### Trendline breaks

A trendline break — where price closes convincingly on the other side of the line — is one of the most commonly used signals in TA. However, not every break is significant:

- A break with **high volume** on the breaking candle is more credible.
- A close through the line, not just a wick, carries more weight.
- After a break, many traders wait for price to **retest** the old trendline from the other side (it may now act as resistance) before entering a new position.

### Common pitfalls

- **Fitting the line to the data** — drawing trendlines that barely touch the relevant points by angling the line to "work." A good trendline fits naturally; you should not need to cherry-pick which lows to connect.
- **Ignoring the timeframe** — a trendline break on a 1-minute chart is far less significant than one on a daily chart.
- **Trading the break prematurely** — false breaks are common; patience after confirmation saves many bad trades.

### Why it matters for a trader

Trendlines and channels give you a dynamic framework for where price should and should not go if a trend is healthy. They define logical entry points (at the lower rail), logical targets (at the upper rail), and a clear invalidation level (a close outside the channel). Combined with support and resistance levels, they are among the most fundamental tools in any trader's chart-reading toolkit.
