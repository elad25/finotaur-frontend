## In plain words

A **chart** is a visual record of price over time. The three most common types are: the **line chart** (connects closing prices), the **bar chart** (shows open, high, low, and close for each period), and the **candlestick chart** (same data as a bar, but displayed with a color-coded body that makes patterns easier to read at a glance). Most active traders use candlesticks.

## Quick demo

On Monday, Tesla opens at $200, rises to $215, drops to $195, and closes at $210. On a line chart you see only the dot at $210. On a bar chart you see a vertical line from $195 to $215 with tick marks for open and close. On a candlestick chart you see a green body from $200 to $210 (open to close) with thin wicks above ($215) and below ($195). The candlestick makes the day's story immediately visible.

## Full explanation

### Line charts

A line chart plots a single price point — usually the **closing price** — for each time period and connects the dots. It is the simplest and clearest way to see the overall direction of a market. Line charts are useful for big-picture trend analysis but discard most of the price action that happened during the period.

### Bar charts (OHLC charts)

A bar chart plots four values for each period:

- **Open** — where price started the period (left tick mark)
- **High** — the highest price reached (top of the vertical bar)
- **Low** — the lowest price reached (bottom of the vertical bar)
- **Close** — where price ended the period (right tick mark)

Bar charts are compact and widely used in traditional finance, but the open-to-close relationship is harder to read at a glance than in candlesticks.

### Candlestick charts

Developed in 18th-century Japan for rice trading, **candlestick charts** display the same OHLC data as bar charts but in a more visual format:

- The **body** is a filled rectangle between the open and close. A green (or white) body means price closed *higher* than it opened. A red (or black) body means it closed *lower*.
- The **wicks** (also called shadows) are the thin lines extending above and below the body, showing the high and low.

The width of the body immediately tells you whether bulls or bears won the period. A tall green body with small wicks = buyers dominated. A small body with long wicks = indecision. This visual clarity is why candlesticks became the dominant chart type for active traders.

### Timeframes

Every chart type can be displayed on any timeframe: 1-minute, 5-minute, hourly, daily, weekly, monthly. Each candle or bar represents one period. A 15-minute chart shows each 15-minute interval as one candle. The choice of timeframe depends on your trading style: scalpers use 1–5 minute charts; swing traders use daily or weekly.

### Choosing a chart type

Most traders default to candlesticks because patterns are easier to recognize. Line charts are useful when you want to reduce visual noise and focus purely on trend. Bar charts remain common in certain institutional and legacy environments. There is no universally "correct" type — what matters is reading the chart consistently.

### Why it matters for a trader

Every TA concept — support, resistance, patterns, indicators — is read on a chart. Understanding what each chart type shows and hides helps you interpret price action accurately. Misreading a candlestick wick as a close, or confusing open with close, leads to wrong conclusions about whether bulls or bears controlled a session.
