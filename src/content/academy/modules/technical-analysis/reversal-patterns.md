## In plain words

**Reversal patterns** are chart formations that suggest a prevailing trend is losing momentum and may be about to change direction. Unlike continuation patterns — which signal a pause before resuming — reversal patterns signal a potential turning point. The most studied examples are the **head and shoulders** (a top-reversal pattern) and **double tops / double bottoms** (which form when price fails twice at the same level).

## Quick demo

Meta stock makes three peaks during a rally: a left shoulder at $480, a higher head at $510, then a lower right shoulder at $480 again. Between each peak, price dips to a common support level ($460) — the **neckline**. When price breaks below $460 on heavy volume, the head-and-shoulders pattern is confirmed. Traders measure the target by subtracting the height of the pattern (510 − 460 = $50) from the neckline: a projected decline to $410.

## Full explanation

### Head and shoulders (top reversal)

The head-and-shoulders is one of the most recognized patterns in all of technical analysis:

- **Left shoulder** — price rallies, peaks, then pulls back.
- **Head** — price rallies again to a higher peak, then pulls back to roughly the same level as the first pullback.
- **Right shoulder** — price rallies one more time but fails to reach the head's peak, then pulls back again.
- **Neckline** — the support level connecting the two pullback lows. The pattern is confirmed when price closes below this line.

Volume behavior matters: volume is often highest on the left shoulder, lower on the head, and lowest on the right shoulder — showing declining participation in the uptrend. Volume typically surges on the neckline break.

**Inverse head and shoulders** is the mirror image at the bottom — a bullish reversal signal.

### Double tops and double bottoms

- **Double top** — price reaches a resistance level, pulls back, then rallies to the same resistance level again and fails to break through. The pullback level between the two peaks is the "neckline." A break below the neckline confirms the pattern and signals a bearish reversal.
- **Double bottom** — price falls to a support level, bounces, then falls back to the same level again and holds. A break above the neckline (the bounce high) confirms a bullish reversal.

A rule of thumb: the two peaks (or troughs) should be roughly equal in price and separated by at least a few weeks, with a meaningful pullback between them.

### Triple tops and triple bottoms

Less common but more significant — price fails at the same level three times. Each additional test reinforces the significance of the level. Confirmation requires a break of the neckline.

### Key principles for trading reversal patterns

1. **Wait for confirmation** — the pattern is not complete until the neckline breaks. Trading anticipatory of the break leads to many failed trades.
2. **Volume confirms** — the breakout should be accompanied by above-average volume.
3. **Measure the target** — subtract (or add) the height of the pattern from the neckline to estimate the minimum expected move.
4. **Retest is common** — after a neckline break, price often retests the neckline from below (in a top reversal) before continuing lower. This retest can be an entry opportunity.
5. **Failed reversals exist** — not every head-and-shoulders completes. If price rallies above the right shoulder, the pattern has failed.

### The psychology behind reversal patterns

Reversal patterns work because they reflect shifting momentum between buyers and sellers. In a head-and-shoulders top, each successive high attracts fewer new buyers, and the neckline represents the level where enough holders decide to exit that selling pressure overcomes buying interest. The pattern is visible because it describes human behavior at inflection points.

### Why it matters for a trader

Reversal patterns identify potential turning points in the market — the moments where a trend change may be underway. For swing traders and position traders, catching an early entry after a confirmed reversal pattern is one of the highest reward-to-risk opportunities available. Missing the distinction between a continuation consolidation and a genuine reversal is one of the most common and costly mistakes in trend-following strategies.
