## In plain words

**Candlestick patterns** are specific shapes formed by one or more candles that traders use as signals about near-term direction. A long green candle with no upper wick suggests strong buying; a small body with long wicks on both sides suggests indecision. Recognizing these patterns is one of the first practical skills traders develop — though no single pattern is a guarantee.

## Quick demo

Gold has been falling for three days. On day four, it opens lower, drops sharply during the session, then completely reverses — closing near its high and back above the prior day's close. This candle is called a **bullish engulfing**: its body fully "engulfs" the prior red candle's body. Traders read it as evidence that buyers overpowered sellers, and many use it as an entry signal for a long position.

## Full explanation

### Single-candle patterns

Some of the most reliable signals come from individual candles:

- **Doji** — open and close are at almost the same price, leaving almost no body. The wicks may be long. A doji signals indecision and is most significant after a strong trend.
- **Hammer** — small body near the top, long lower wick (at least twice the body), little or no upper wick. Appears after a downtrend; signals that buyers pushed price back up from the lows. The bullish version is green/white; the **hanging man** is the same shape at the top of an uptrend (bearish signal).
- **Shooting star** — small body near the bottom, long upper wick. Appears after an uptrend; sellers rejected higher prices. The **inverted hammer** is similar but appears at bottoms (bullish signal).
- **Marubozu** — a full-bodied candle with no wicks. Signals complete dominance by one side: a green marubozu is very bullish, a red one is very bearish.
- **Spinning top** — small body with wicks on both sides. Signals indecision; context matters.

### Two-candle patterns

- **Bullish engulfing** — a red candle followed by a larger green candle that fully contains the prior body. Strong reversal signal at bottoms.
- **Bearish engulfing** — the reverse: a large red candle engulfs a smaller green candle at a top. Suggests sellers have taken over.
- **Tweezer tops / bottoms** — two candles with the same high (top) or low (bottom). Signals price failed to break a level twice in a row.

### Three-candle patterns

- **Morning star** — three candles: a large red, a small-bodied candle (the "star") gapping down, then a large green candle. A classic bottom-reversal signal.
- **Evening star** — the mirror image at the top: large green, small star, large red. A top-reversal signal.
- **Three white soldiers** — three consecutive large green candles with higher opens and closes. Strong bullish momentum signal.
- **Three black crows** — three consecutive large red candles. Strong bearish momentum signal.

### Context is everything

Candlestick patterns work best when:
1. They appear at significant price levels (support, resistance, moving average).
2. Volume confirms the move (higher volume on the signal candle adds conviction).
3. They align with the broader trend context.

A hammer at the bottom of a downtrend after hitting a major support level is a far stronger signal than the same candle appearing in the middle of a range.

### Why it matters for a trader

Candlestick patterns give traders a visual shorthand for reading who is winning — buyers or sellers — over a given period. They are most useful not as standalone signals but as a trigger: once your broader analysis points you in a direction, a confirming candlestick pattern gives you a precise, low-risk entry point.
