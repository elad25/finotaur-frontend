## In plain words

**Volume** is the number of shares, contracts, or units traded during a given period. It is the fuel that drives price moves — high volume means many participants are actively buying or selling, which gives a price move credibility. Low volume means few participants, which makes a move suspect. The core principle: **volume confirms price action**. A breakout on high volume is far more reliable than one on thin volume.

## Quick demo

Gold breaks above a long-standing resistance level at $2,000 for the first time in months. But volume is 40% below its 20-day average. Three days later, price slips back below $2,000. Compare this to a scenario where the breakout came on triple the average volume — suggesting major institutional participation. The same price move carries completely different meaning depending on whether volume confirms it.

## Full explanation

### Why volume matters

Price tells you *what* happened. Volume tells you *how significant* it was. When a price move is accompanied by heavy volume, it indicates broad conviction — many traders and institutions agree on the direction. When a move happens on light volume, it may reflect low interest or be driven by a small number of participants, making a reversal more likely.

### Volume and trend confirmation

Classic relationships between volume and trend:

- **Rising price + rising volume** — healthy uptrend. Buyers are enthusiastic and increasing.
- **Rising price + falling volume** — weakening uptrend. Fewer participants are driving the price higher; a reversal may be near.
- **Falling price + rising volume** — strong downtrend. Sellers are pressing hard. Confirms the decline.
- **Falling price + falling volume** — weakening downtrend. Sellers are losing conviction. A potential bottom.

These relationships are not rules — they are tendencies. Context and timeframe matter.

### Volume at breakouts

Breakouts deserve special attention to volume:

- **High-volume breakout** — price clears a key level (support, resistance, chart pattern boundary) with volume well above the 20-day average. This is the confirmation traders want; it suggests genuine demand (or supply in a breakdown).
- **Low-volume breakout** — price moves through a level but volume is below average. More likely to be a false breakout. Many traders wait for volume confirmation before entering.
- **Climactic volume (volume spike)** — a sudden massive surge in volume — often signals the exhaustion of a move, especially at the end of a long trend. The crowd has piled in all at once; when there are no more buyers (or sellers), the move reverses.

### Volume-based indicators

Beyond raw volume, several indicators process volume for additional insight:

- **On-Balance Volume (OBV)** — adds a period's volume if price closed up, subtracts if it closed down. The resulting line shows whether volume is flowing into or out of an asset. OBV trending higher while price consolidates suggests accumulation; divergence between OBV and price is a warning signal.
- **Volume Profile** — shows how much volume was traded at each price level over a period (rather than per time period). Identifies high-volume nodes (where most trading occurred — major support/resistance) and low-volume nodes (where price tends to move through quickly).
- **Chaikin Money Flow (CMF)** — combines price position within the bar's range with volume to measure buying vs. selling pressure.

### Relative volume (RVOL)

Absolute volume numbers are not meaningful on their own — 10 million shares traded means very different things for a small-cap stock vs. a mega-cap. **Relative volume (RVOL)** compares current volume to the average volume for the same time period: RVOL = current volume / average volume. An RVOL of 3.0 means the stock is trading three times its normal pace — a signal of unusual activity worth investigating.

### Volume in different markets

- **Equities**: Volume data is reliable and widely available.
- **Futures**: Volume is available per contract; open interest is also valuable.
- **Forex**: True volume is not available (decentralized market). Traders use tick volume (number of price updates) as a proxy; it correlates reasonably with actual volume but is not identical.
- **Crypto**: Volume is available per exchange; total "reported" volume is often inflated by wash trading on smaller exchanges.

### Why it matters for a trader

Volume is one of the few truly independent data streams alongside price. While every indicator is derived from price, volume is recorded separately and provides an external check on whether price action is genuine. Ignoring volume is like watching a sports highlight without knowing the score — you can see what happened, but you don't know how meaningful it was. Combining volume analysis with price patterns, support/resistance, and indicators dramatically improves the reliability of every signal.
