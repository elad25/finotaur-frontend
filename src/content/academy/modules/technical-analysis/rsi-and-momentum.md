## In plain words

**Momentum** measures how fast price is moving in a given direction. The **Relative Strength Index (RSI)** is the most widely used momentum indicator: it produces a reading between 0 and 100 that tells you whether recent gains have been larger than recent losses (high RSI) or vice versa (low RSI). Traditionally, a reading above 70 is considered **overbought** — price may have moved too far, too fast — and below 30 is considered **oversold**. But context matters enormously.

## Quick demo

Bitcoin rallies 40% in two weeks. Its RSI climbs to 82. A trader sees the reading above 70 and considers taking partial profits, knowing that sustained RSI above 80 is rare and often precedes a pullback. Sure enough, over the next week Bitcoin drops 15%. The RSI had not predicted the exact top, but it flagged that buying pressure was extreme and the risk of a correction was elevated.

## Full explanation

### How RSI is calculated

RSI was developed by J. Welles Wilder Jr. in 1978. The formula:

1. Calculate the average gain and average loss over the last 14 periods.
2. Relative Strength (RS) = average gain / average loss.
3. RSI = 100 − (100 / (1 + RS)).

The result oscillates between 0 and 100. A 14-period RSI is the standard, though traders use shorter periods (7–9) for more sensitivity or longer periods (21–25) for smoother, less noisy readings.

### Interpreting RSI levels

- **Above 70** — traditionally "overbought." Buyers have dominated recent periods. Does not mean price *must* fall; strong trending markets can stay above 70 for extended periods.
- **Below 30** — traditionally "oversold." Sellers have dominated. Again, strong downtrends can stay below 30.
- **50 line** — in a bullish trend, RSI tends to hold above 50. In a bearish trend, it tends to stay below 50. The 50 crossover can be used as a trend signal.

Overbought and oversold do not mean "sell" and "buy" by themselves. In a strong uptrend, overbought conditions are normal and the correct response is often to stay long, not exit.

### RSI divergence

One of the most powerful RSI signals is **divergence** — when price and RSI move in opposite directions:

- **Bearish divergence** — price makes a higher high, but RSI makes a lower high. Momentum is weakening even as price rises. Signals potential reversal.
- **Bullish divergence** — price makes a lower low, but RSI makes a higher low. Selling pressure is exhausting itself. Signals potential recovery.

Divergence is not a timing signal — it can persist for weeks before price reverses. It is most useful as a warning, not a trigger.

### RSI as a trend filter

In trending markets, RSI can be used differently:

- In an uptrend, RSI pulling back to the 40–50 zone (rather than to 30) may be a buying opportunity — the trend is maintaining elevated momentum.
- In a downtrend, RSI rallying to the 50–60 zone before turning down again may be a shorting opportunity.

This "trend-adjusted" use of RSI reduces false signals compared to the simple overbought/oversold interpretation.

### Other momentum indicators

RSI is not the only momentum tool:

- **Stochastic oscillator** — compares the closing price to the high-low range over a period, generating two lines (%K and %D). Useful for timing entries in ranging markets.
- **Rate of Change (ROC)** — measures the percentage change in price over N periods directly. Simpler than RSI.
- **Momentum indicator** — the raw difference between current price and N-period-ago price.

### Why it matters for a trader

Momentum indicators like RSI add a dimension that price alone does not show: *how fast* price is moving and whether that speed is accelerating or decelerating. A price making new highs with declining RSI is a warning that fewer and fewer participants are driving the move — a sign to manage risk, not add to it. RSI is one of the most versatile tools in TA: useful for timing entries, identifying divergences, and gauging trend strength.
