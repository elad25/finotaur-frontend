## In plain words

**Dow Theory** is the oldest framework in technical analysis — developed in the late 1800s by Charles Dow — and argues that market prices move in identifiable primary, secondary, and minor trends. **Elliott Wave Theory**, proposed by Ralph Nelson Elliott in the 1930s, extends this idea: it claims that markets move in a repeating pattern of five waves in the direction of the trend and three corrective waves against it. Both frameworks offer ways to think about market structure, but both are also highly subjective and disputed.

## Quick demo

The S&P 500 rallied in five distinct waves from 2020 to 2021: three upward "impulse" waves separated by two smaller pullbacks, followed by a three-wave correction in 2022 (labeled A-B-C). An Elliott Wave analyst in 2022 would count the completed five-wave structure, recognize the A-B-C correction, and potentially argue that a new five-wave cycle upward should follow — which broadly happened in 2023. The framework gave a narrative; the outcome was uncertain at the time.

## Full explanation

### Dow Theory: the foundation of technical analysis

Charles Dow, co-founder of the Wall Street Journal, laid out principles in his editorials that form the bedrock of TA:

**Six tenets of Dow Theory:**
1. **The market discounts everything** — all known information is already priced in (same assumption TA rests on broadly).
2. **Three trends exist simultaneously** — the primary trend (months to years), secondary trend (weeks to months), and minor trend (days to weeks).
3. **Primary trends have three phases** — accumulation (smart money buying), public participation (broad awareness and buying), and distribution (smart money selling).
4. **The indices must confirm each other** — originally, both the Dow Jones Industrial Average and the Dow Jones Transportation Average had to signal the same direction for a primary trend to be confirmed. One index alone was not sufficient.
5. **Volume must confirm the trend** — volume should expand in the direction of the trend.
6. **A trend is in effect until it gives a definitive reversal signal** — do not assume a trend has changed without clear evidence.

Dow Theory remains relevant not as a mechanical trading system but as a philosophical framework: the idea of confirming signals across multiple instruments (intermarket analysis) and the three-phase model of market cycles are concepts active traders and analysts still reference.

### Elliott Wave Theory: structure and controversy

Elliott proposed that markets move in a repeating fractal pattern driven by crowd psychology:

**Impulse waves (trend direction):**
- Wave 1: Initial move, often unnoticed.
- Wave 2: Correction of Wave 1, often retraces 50-61.8%.
- Wave 3: Typically the longest and strongest wave — the main move. Cannot be the shortest of waves 1, 3, 5.
- Wave 4: Correction of Wave 3, typically shallower than Wave 2.
- Wave 5: Final move in the trend direction, often with weakening momentum (RSI divergence).

**Corrective waves (counter-trend):**
- Wave A: First leg of correction.
- Wave B: Partial recovery, often traps bulls (or bears).
- Wave C: Final and often powerful leg of the correction.

The entire structure repeats at larger and smaller timeframes — the fractal nature of Elliott waves.

### The honest critique

Elliott Wave is among the most controversial tools in technical analysis, for legitimate reasons:

- **Labeling is highly subjective** — two experienced Elliott Wave analysts will often count the same chart differently. There are many rules and guidelines, but enough flexibility that almost any wave count can be rationalized.
- **Retrospective clarity** — wave counts often look obvious in hindsight but were genuinely ambiguous in real time.
- **Alternate counts** — experienced practitioners always have "alternate" counts for when their primary count fails. This makes the theory difficult to falsify in practice.
- **No proven edge in controlled testing** — academic studies have not consistently found that Elliott Wave labeling provides statistically significant predictive power beyond chance.

Dow Theory is less subjective but also less precise — it provides a general framework rather than specific entry and exit rules.

### Practical uses

Even traders who do not use Elliott Wave as a system find value in the underlying concepts:
- The idea that corrections in a trend tend to retrace 38-62% (consistent with Fibonacci levels).
- The three-phase model (accumulation, public participation, distribution) as a mental model for where in a cycle markets might be.
- Wave 3 behavior — the strongest move in a trend — as a reminder to position larger during confirmed trend momentum.

### Why it matters for a trader

Dow Theory and Elliott Wave are worth understanding because they are part of the language of TA and they shaped modern thinking about trends and market phases. The critical mindset is equally important: knowing *when* a framework is too subjective to trade with precision is a skill. Many traders reference Elliott Wave concepts loosely — especially the five-wave structure and Fibonacci relationships — without rigidly following wave counts. That selective, critical use of classic frameworks is generally more practical than either blind faith or total rejection.
