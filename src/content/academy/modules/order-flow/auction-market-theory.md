## In plain words

**Auction Market Theory (AMT)** describes every financial market as a continuous two-sided auction — buyers bid, sellers offer, and the market searches for a price where both sides are willing to trade. Price moves *away* from areas where trade is too easy (high volume, "fair value") and toward areas where trade is too restricted, until two-sided participation is restored. The market's job is to **advertise price** and facilitate trade, not to predict direction.

## Quick demo

Picture an open-air fruit market. Apples are priced at $2; everyone wants them at that price, so they sell out in minutes. The vendor raises the price to $3 — some buyers walk away. At $3.50 most buyers disappear, and the vendor is forced back toward $2.80, where buying and selling is balanced again. A financial market works the same way: price rises to find sellers and falls to find buyers, always seeking the level where the most business gets done.

## Full explanation

### The two core drives

AMT, developed by J. Peter Steidlmayer (creator of Market Profile), rests on two mechanics:

- **Trend**: price moves away from "value" — an area where both sides traded comfortably. It will keep trending until it reaches a price that attracts the opposite side.
- **Range / balance**: once both sides are active, the market consolidates, building a value area. Volume concentrates at the price of maximum acceptance.

These two states alternate. Recognizing which one you are in determines strategy: trends favor continuation trades; ranges favor mean-reversion trades.

### Value, balance, and imbalance

- **Value Area**: the price range where roughly 70% of the day's volume traded. Both buyers and sellers found it acceptable. Prices inside the value area move slowly.
- **Point of Control (POC)**: the single price with the most volume — the market's clearest agreed fair value.
- **Imbalance**: price outside the value area. The market is advertising a new price to attract the other side. If no response comes, it continues. If the other side shows up aggressively, price snaps back to value.

### Time and opportunity

AMT views time as important as price. A price level visited briefly with low volume is *rejected* — the market did not find enough participants there. A price level visited repeatedly with high volume is *accepted*. This distinction explains why some levels hold as strong support or resistance repeatedly: they are deeply embedded in the collective value perception of all participants.

### Directional reads from AMT

- Price **above value** with low volume: advertisement that's not attracting sellers — bullish continuation until sellers respond.
- Price **below value** with low volume: advertisement that's not attracting buyers — bearish continuation until buyers respond.
- Price returning to value area after a range extension: normal mean-reversion; expect rotation within the known value range.

### Why it matters for a trader

AMT gives you a framework to classify market context before choosing a strategy. It replaces the question "which direction will price go?" with the more answerable question "is the market in balance or imbalance, and where is value relative to current price?" Traders who understand AMT spend less time chasing random moves and more time waiting for the market to advertise price at levels where the auction logic favors their edge.
