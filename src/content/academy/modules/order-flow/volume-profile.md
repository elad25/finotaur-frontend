## In plain words

A **volume profile** is a histogram displayed alongside a price chart that shows how much volume traded at each price level over a selected period. Unlike standard volume bars (which show total volume per time period), volume profile shows volume *per price* — revealing where the market spent the most time and energy. The resulting shape identifies **high-value areas** where price was accepted and **thin areas** where price moved quickly through.

## Quick demo

On a daily NQ (Nasdaq futures) chart, the previous week's volume profile shows 60% of all contracts traded between 19,800 and 20,100. The **Point of Control** — the single busiest price — was 19,950. At the boundaries, volume drops to nearly zero. This week, if NQ opens at 20,400 and drifts back toward 20,100, a volume-profile trader sees a high-probability mean-reversion trade: the market is returning to the area where it last found the most balanced two-sided activity.

## Full explanation

### Core components

**Point of Control (POC)**: the price level with the highest volume during the chosen period. This is the market's "fairest" price — where the most willing buyers and sellers met. Price gravitates toward the POC because both sides agreed on it the most.

**Value Area (VA)**: the range of prices containing approximately 70% of the total volume for the period. This threshold (70%) comes from the statistical definition of one standard deviation in a normal distribution. The boundaries are the **Value Area High (VAH)** and **Value Area Low (VAL)**.

**High-volume nodes (HVN)**: price levels or narrow bands where unusually large volume traded. These act as magnets — price tends to return to them and often consolidates there. They also represent potential support/resistance.

**Low-volume nodes (LVN)**: thin areas where very little volume traded. Price moves quickly through these zones because there are few resting orders there and little two-sided interest. LVNs often mark the transition between accepted value ranges.

### Types of volume profile

- **Session profile**: volume for a single trading day. Used by day traders to find today's POC and value area.
- **Visible range profile**: volume for whatever portion of the chart is visible on screen. Adjusts dynamically as you scroll.
- **Fixed range profile**: volume for a manually selected date or price range. Used to study specific market events or consolidation periods.
- **Composite profile**: volume accumulated over weeks or months. Shows the market's long-term value consensus and powerful structural levels.

### Common setups

**Value area retest**: price moves outside the value area and returns into it. Statistically, price revisits the value area roughly 70–80% of the time after an opening outside it — a principle used by many session-day traders.

**POC magnet**: when price is above the POC and drifts lower, or below it and drifts higher, traders often target the POC as a mean-reversion level. The logic: the market seeks where it last found the most participation.

**LVN breakout acceleration**: when price breaks through a low-volume node, the lack of resting orders means it can travel quickly to the next HVN. LVNs explain the "air pocket" effect — price that seems to move for no reason is often traversing a thin zone.

**Developing POC**: intraday, watching which price level accumulates the most volume as the session progresses. The developing POC tracks the market's evolving fair-value estimate in real time.

### Relationship to Market Profile and AMT

Volume Profile is the modern quantitative successor to Market Profile (which tracked time at price rather than volume at price). Both derive from Auction Market Theory — the idea that price searches for the level of maximum two-sided participation. Volume profile is generally considered more precise because volume matters more than time: a price level visited briefly with enormous volume is more significant than one visited for hours with trivial activity.

### Why it matters for a trader

Volume profile gives you a structural map of the market's memory. Every session, week, and month leaves behind a record of where participants agreed on value. Entries near the edge of a value area with confluence from other signals (delta, absorption, trend) are among the highest-probability setups in order-flow trading. More broadly, volume profile prevents the mistake of treating all price levels equally — some prices are deeply embedded in market consensus; others are passing experiments.
