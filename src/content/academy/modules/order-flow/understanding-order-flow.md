## In plain words

**Order flow** is the mechanism by which aggressive buying or selling actually *moves* price. When a buyer wants in immediately, they lift the ask — consuming a resting sell order and pushing price up. When a seller wants out immediately, they hit the bid — consuming a resting buy order and pushing price down. The **direction and size** of this aggression is what drives price; the passive limit orders just define where price stops temporarily.

## Quick demo

Gold futures are quoted at $2,350 bid / $2,350.10 ask. A large institution needs to buy 500 contracts immediately. It sweeps all available offers at $2,350.10, then $2,350.20, then $2,350.30 — exhausting each resting limit order until it fills all 500. The price has risen $0.30 not because sentiment changed, but because aggressive buyers systematically consumed passive sellers. A trader watching the footprint or time-and-sales would have seen the sweeping happen in real time.

## Full explanation

### Passive vs. aggressive participants

Every trade has two sides, and they behave very differently:

- **Passive (limit orders)**: a trader posts an order to buy at $100 or sell at $101. They wait. They provide liquidity to the market and earn the spread when filled.
- **Aggressive (market orders or marketable limits)**: a trader wants to transact *now* and takes the available price. They consume liquidity and cause price to move.

Price only moves when aggressive participants overwhelm the passive orders at a given level.

### Absorption and the weight of evidence

Sometimes price *tries* to move but fails. Heavy sell aggression hits a level, yet price barely drops. Why? Because passive buyers are absorbing each sell order as fast as it arrives. This is called **absorption**. Absorption at a level is a strong signal that large, patient buyers have a line in the sand — a potential long entry or support level.

The opposite — **exhaustion** — occurs when aggressive buyers push price higher but their buying weakens. Fewer and fewer contracts trade at the new highs; the momentum dries up. This is visible in a falling delta near price highs and is a warning of a pending reversal.

### The role of market makers

Market makers continuously post bids and offers, earning the spread. They do not intend to hold directional positions. When a flow of orders is heavily one-sided, market makers adjust their quotes upward (to sell at higher prices) or downward (to buy at lower prices) to manage their inventory risk. This is a second mechanism by which sustained order flow pressure shifts price.

### Reading aggression in real time

Traders use three primary signals to interpret order flow aggression:

- **Delta**: buy volume minus sell volume. Rising delta into a move confirms it; diverging delta warns of exhaustion.
- **Volume at price**: which individual price levels attracted the most contracts? Heavy volume at a level signals acceptance; thin volume signals rejection.
- **Speed and clustering**: a large block traded in a single second vs. the same volume spread over five minutes carry different urgency. Speed indicates institutional intent.

### Why it matters for a trader

Understanding how aggressive orders move price prevents two common mistakes: (1) entering a breakout that is driven by thin, low-conviction volume — a false move likely to reverse — and (2) fading a move driven by relentless, high-volume aggression — fighting the actual buyer or seller. Order flow gives you evidence of *who* is in control of price at this moment, making your entries and exits more informed than candle patterns alone can provide.
