## In plain words

Order flow has its own vocabulary. Terms like **delta**, **absorption**, and **imbalance** appear constantly in footprint charts and trading platforms, and they all describe specific measurable events. Knowing exactly what each term means — not just loosely — is what separates a trader who can read the tape from one who just sees numbers on a screen.

## Quick demo

During a 5-minute candle on crude oil futures, 8,400 contracts traded at the ask (buys) and 3,200 at the bid (sells). The **delta** is +5,200 — strongly bullish. But at the candle's high, 600 contracts sold aggressively into 600 resting bids that absorbed every one. That's **absorption**: price stopped rising not because buyers disappeared, but because large passive sellers stood their ground. The delta was bullish; the absorption was bearish. Both pieces of information matter.

## Full explanation

### Delta

**Delta** = buy volume − sell volume for any given period (a candle, a session, a price level).

- Positive delta: more aggressive buying than selling.
- Negative delta: more aggressive selling than buying.
- **Cumulative delta (CVD)**: delta summed continuously over a session. If price makes a new high but CVD makes a lower high, that divergence often precedes a reversal.

Delta is a flow indicator, not a direction predictor. High positive delta in a downtrend simply means buyers tried; it doesn't mean they succeeded unless price actually moved up.

### Absorption

**Absorption** occurs when aggressive orders on one side are met by an equal or greater quantity of passive orders on the other side — and price does not move meaningfully. It signals that a large participant is willing to take the other side of every incoming order at that level.

- Sell absorption at support: large buyers are absorbing every aggressive sell. Bullish.
- Buy absorption at resistance: large sellers are absorbing every aggressive buy. Bearish.

Absorption is one of the most reliable order-flow signals because it reveals the presence of a committed participant.

### Imbalance

An **imbalance** in a footprint chart appears when the buy volume at a price level is significantly larger than the sell volume at the same level (or vice versa), typically by a ratio set by the trader (e.g., 3:1 or more). Platforms often highlight these cells in a contrasting color.

- **Bid imbalance** (sellers overwhelm buyers): more selling than buying at that price — bearish pressure.
- **Ask imbalance** (buyers overwhelm sellers): more buying than selling at that price — bullish pressure.
- **Stacked imbalances**: multiple consecutive price levels all showing the same imbalance direction — a particularly strong signal.

### Exhaustion

**Exhaustion** is the tail end of a trend where the dominant side is losing firepower. Signs include:

- Delta declining while price still advances.
- Volume shrinking at new highs/lows.
- Large aggressive orders failing to push price further.

Exhaustion does not guarantee reversal, but it does remove the evidence that supports the trend.

### Sweep

A **sweep** occurs when a market order (or a series of large orders) moves through multiple price levels, consuming all resting limit orders along the way. Sweeps leave a distinctive footprint of high volume across consecutive price levels. Institutional buyers sweeping supply is often the starting gun of a move.

### Pulling liquidity

Sometimes the order book shows large orders that vanish the moment price approaches. This is called **pulling (or spoofing in its illegal form)** — placing large visible orders to create a false impression of support or resistance, then canceling them. Legitimate version: a market maker adjusting quotes rapidly as price moves. Either way, the DOM can mislead, which is why traders combine it with actual traded volume.

### Why it matters for a trader

These terms are the grammar of the tape. A trader who conflates delta with direction, or absorption with thin supply, will misread setups constantly. Precise definitions turn a confusing stream of numbers into a structured story: who is aggressive, who is absorbing, and where commitment is real vs. manufactured.
