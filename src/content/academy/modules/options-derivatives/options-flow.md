## In plain words

**Options flow** refers to the stream of options trades being executed in the market, with particular attention to unusually large, aggressive, or unusual orders. Because buying a call or put requires taking a position before a price move, large options orders can signal that a sophisticated trader — often an institution — is positioning for a specific outcome. Monitoring options flow means looking for these "smart money" footprints: big trades, high-conviction bets, and repeated accumulation in a specific expiration or strike that stands out from normal activity.

## Quick demo

A quiet Tuesday morning: shares of a mid-cap biotech are flat at $40 and average options volume is 1,000 contracts per day. Suddenly, 5,000 $45 call contracts expiring in three weeks trade in a single block for $0.80 each — a $400,000 bet that the stock jumps 12% in three weeks. No news is out. Three days later, a merger is announced at $50 per share. The calls are worth $5.00. That unusual call sweep was visible to anyone watching flow — it was a signal, whether from insider activity, an informed bet, or a well-timed hedge.

## Full explanation

### What options flow shows

The options market processes thousands of trades per second. Most are routine hedges, small retail orders, and market-maker adjustments. Options flow tools filter for what stands out:

- **Large block trades**: Single orders of hundreds or thousands of contracts at once.
- **Unusual volume**: A ticker showing 10× its average daily options volume.
- **Sweeps**: Aggressive orders that fill across multiple exchanges simultaneously, indicating urgency.
- **Skew changes**: Sudden increases in put or call demand at specific strikes, shifting the volatility surface.

### Reading the tape: what flow can reveal

**Bullish flow indicators**:
- Large call buying, especially in OTM strikes.
- Put selling (selling puts is bullish — the seller wants shares to stay above the strike).
- Unusual call sweeps in a stock with no obvious news.

**Bearish flow indicators**:
- Large put buying, especially in OTM strikes or protective structures.
- Call selling against existing positions (covered calls are neutral, but unusual call selling can signal a hedge).
- Block purchases of index puts — a sign institutions are hedging portfolios.

### What options flow cannot tell you

Flow is a signal, not a certainty. Key limitations:

- **You cannot see intent**: A large block trade could be a hedge on an existing position, not a new directional bet.
- **Delta-neutral trades are invisible to casual analysis**: An institution might buy calls and simultaneously short stock — the flow looks bullish but the net position is neutral.
- **Timing is uncertain**: A large options position might be opened weeks before a catalyst, or it might never pay off.
- **False signals**: Market makers, algorithmic traders, and ETF rebalancing all generate flow that looks meaningful but is mechanical.

### Practical use for traders

Options flow works best as a confirmation tool, not a standalone signal. When flow aligns with technical analysis, macro context, or upcoming catalysts, it adds a layer of conviction. Common approaches:

- Watch for **call sweeps** in sectors or individual stocks already showing momentum.
- Track **put buying in indices** as a macro hedging signal.
- Identify **repeat accumulation** in a specific strike across multiple days — suggesting ongoing conviction rather than a one-off bet.

Many professional trading platforms provide real-time options flow dashboards that filter and score unusual activity. Learning to read this data accurately — distinguishing signal from noise — is a distinct skill that takes time to develop.
