## In plain words

An **option** is a contract that gives you the right — but not the obligation — to buy or sell an asset at a specific price before a specific date. A **call option** gives the right to buy; a **put option** gives the right to sell. The price locked in by the contract is called the **strike price**, and the last date you can use it is the **expiration date**. You pay a fee called the **premium** to own that right.

## Quick demo

Suppose Apple stock trades at $180. You buy a call option with a $185 strike expiring in one month for a $3 premium (that's $300 per contract, which covers 100 shares). If Apple climbs to $200 before expiration, you can buy at $185 and immediately have $15 per share of value — a $1,500 gain on a $300 outlay. If Apple stays below $185, the option expires worthless and you lose only the $300 premium you paid.

## Full explanation

### The contract structure

Every option contract specifies four things:

- **Underlying asset** — the stock, ETF, index, or commodity the option controls.
- **Strike price** — the fixed price at which you can buy (call) or sell (put).
- **Expiration date** — the deadline for exercising the right. Most equity options expire on Fridays.
- **Premium** — the price you pay to acquire the contract. One standard equity contract covers 100 shares.

### Calls and puts

A **call option** profits when the underlying rises above the strike price. Buyers of calls are bullish; sellers of calls are neutral to bearish.

A **put option** profits when the underlying falls below the strike price. Buyers of puts are bearish — or are hedging a position they already hold. Sellers of puts are neutral to bullish.

### Moneyness

Options are described by their relationship to the current price of the underlying:

- **In the money (ITM)** — exercise would be immediately profitable (call with strike below current price; put with strike above).
- **At the money (ATM)** — strike is at or very near the current price.
- **Out of the money (OTM)** — exercise would not be profitable at current price.

OTM options are cheaper but require a larger move to become profitable. ITM options cost more but already have intrinsic value.

### American vs. European style

Most U.S. equity options are **American-style**: you can exercise any time before expiration. Index options (SPX, NDX) are typically **European-style**: exercise only at expiration. In practice, most traders never exercise early — they close positions by selling the contract back.

### Buyer vs. seller obligations

The option **buyer** pays the premium and holds a right, not an obligation. Maximum loss is limited to the premium paid. The option **seller** (the writer) collects the premium but takes on obligation — to sell shares (call seller) or buy shares (put seller) if the buyer exercises. Sellers face potentially much larger losses.

### Why options matter for traders and investors

Options are used for three purposes: **speculation** (leveraged directional bets), **income generation** (selling premium against existing positions or cash), and **hedging** (protecting a portfolio from adverse moves). Understanding how they are priced and how they behave across time and volatility is the foundation for using them effectively.
