## In plain words

**Open interest (OI)** is the total number of outstanding options contracts that have not yet been closed, exercised, or expired. It measures how much money is committed to a particular strike and expiration — the more open interest, the more significant that level may be as a market reference point. **Max pain** is a related concept: the price at which the most options contracts (by dollar value) would expire worthless, theoretically causing the maximum financial loss to option buyers — and maximum gain to sellers. Both are tools for understanding where positioning pressure may influence price near expiration.

## Quick demo

SPY (S&P 500 ETF) has its monthly expiration this Friday. Open interest is highest at the $520 strike: 80,000 call contracts and 60,000 put contracts outstanding. The "max pain" calculation finds that $518 is the price where total option premium expiring worthless is maximized. SPY is currently at $522. Some traders expect a modest pull toward $518–$520 as market makers who sold those contracts delta-hedge and reduce their exposure. This is not a guarantee — but it is a known gravitational field in the price around expiration.

## Full explanation

### Open interest — what it measures

Open interest increases when a new contract is opened (one new buyer and one new seller create a new position). It decreases when a position is closed — either by an offsetting trade, expiration, or exercise.

- **Rising OI at a strike**: New money is flowing into positions at that level — could be hedging, speculation, or income selling. The meaning depends on context.
- **Falling OI**: Participants are unwinding positions. Often occurs as expiration approaches and traders close or roll.
- **High OI vs. high volume**: Volume shows activity on a given day; OI shows total accumulated exposure. Both are useful, but OI indicates where the weight of positioning sits.

### Using OI as a market reference

Strikes with very high open interest often act as price magnets or resistance/support levels, particularly near expiration:

- **Gamma pinning**: When a stock approaches a strike with massive OI near expiration, market makers who sold options at that strike must hedge by buying or selling the underlying as price approaches. This hedging activity can "pin" the stock near the strike as expiration nears — a phenomenon called gamma pinning.
- **Strike clusters**: A chain with heavy OI clustered between $95 and $105 on a $100 stock suggests that range has significant positioning significance. Moves that clear those strikes may accelerate as hedging flow reverses.

### Max pain theory

Max pain is calculated by summing the total dollar value of all in-the-money calls and puts at each possible expiration price, across all strikes. The price where that total is minimized (where the most options expire worthless) is max pain.

**The theory**: Since option sellers (who collect premium) want options to expire worthless, and market makers are often net-short options (having sold to customers), there is a theoretical incentive for price to gravitate toward max pain near expiration.

**The reality**:
- Max pain works occasionally, especially on heavily traded index ETFs or large-cap stocks with high options open interest.
- It is not a reliable directional predictor on its own — broader market forces often override it.
- It is more useful as a reference frame ("max pain is at $95 and we're at $100 — are there forces that might pull price down?") than as a trade signal.

### Practical framework

Open interest data is most useful when combined with other factors:

- Check OI on the days approaching expiration (especially monthly OPEX Friday), when gamma effects are strongest.
- Watch for large OI at round-number strikes — $400, $450, $500 — which often act as psychological and mechanical gravity.
- Use max pain not as a prediction but as one data point in a larger picture of positioning, volatility, and price action.

Understanding what open interest represents and its limitations helps traders avoid over-relying on it while still capturing the genuine information embedded in options positioning data.
