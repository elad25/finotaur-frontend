## In plain words

**Alternative assets** are investments outside the traditional categories of publicly traded stocks, bonds, and cash. The main types are **private equity** (ownership stakes in companies not listed on a stock exchange), **hedge funds** (pooled vehicles that can use almost any strategy), and **collectibles** (art, wine, classic cars, rare watches, trading cards). They are typically less liquid, less regulated, and accessible mainly to institutional investors and high-net-worth individuals — though retail access is slowly expanding.

## Quick demo

A private equity fund raises $500 million from pension funds and endowments. It acquires a struggling regional retail chain for $800 million (using $300 million in debt — a leveraged buyout). Over five years, the fund cuts costs, closes unprofitable stores, and grows e-commerce. It sells the company for $1.4 billion. After repaying the $300 million debt and paying fees, the fund returns roughly 2.5× the invested $500 million. The pension funds benefited — but their capital was locked up for those five years with no ability to exit early. Illiquidity was the price of the premium.

## Full explanation

### Private equity

Private equity (PE) encompasses several strategies:

- **Venture capital (VC)** — early-stage investments in startups. High failure rate; a few big winners drive returns. Long lock-up periods (7–10 years).
- **Growth equity** — investing in established, profitable companies that want capital to scale but are not yet ready for an IPO.
- **Buyouts (LBO)** — acquiring mature companies with significant debt financing, improving operations, and selling at a profit. The largest segment by capital deployed.
- **Secondaries** — buying existing PE fund stakes from other investors, providing liquidity at a discount.

PE firms charge a "2 and 20" fee structure: 2% annual management fee on committed capital and 20% of profits above a hurdle rate (the "carried interest"). Returns historically exceeded public equity but come with illiquidity, opacity, and survivorship bias in reported data.

### Hedge funds

Hedge funds are pooled investment vehicles with wide latitude to use leverage, short selling, derivatives, and cross-asset strategies. Common strategies include:

- **Long/short equity** — long stocks expected to rise, short stocks expected to fall.
- **Global macro** — takes large directional bets on currencies, rates, and commodities based on macroeconomic themes.
- **Event-driven** — merger arbitrage, distressed debt, activist investing around corporate events.
- **Quantitative / algorithmic** — systematic, model-driven strategies.

Hedge funds are typically limited to **accredited investors** (in the U.S., those with $1M+ net worth or $200K+ annual income). Fees are also "2 and 20" or higher. After fees, most hedge funds underperform simple equity indices over long periods, though top funds (Bridgewater, Renaissance, D.E. Shaw) have delivered exceptional long-term returns.

### Collectibles and tangible alternatives

Physical collectibles — art, wine, whisky, classic cars, rare sneakers, trading cards — have produced strong returns in certain categories over certain periods. Key considerations:

- **No income** — a Basquiat painting pays no dividends.
- **Expertise required** — authentication, provenance, and storage costs are non-trivial.
- **Illiquid** — selling at fair value takes time and access to the right buyers.
- **Platforms like Masterworks** now allow fractional ownership of art, lowering the entry barrier.

### Infrastructure and real assets

Infrastructure (roads, airports, pipelines, renewable energy) and timberland are alternative real assets sought by pension funds for their stable, inflation-linked cash flows and low correlation to equities. Retail access is mainly through listed infrastructure ETFs.

### Why it matters for a trader or investor

Understanding alternatives matters even if you never invest in one. Private equity influences which companies go public and when, directly affecting IPO pipelines. Hedge fund positioning (visible through 13F filings and options flow) moves markets. Macro hedge funds are often among the largest participants in currency and rates markets. And as retail platforms make fractional alternatives more accessible, knowing their real risk profile — fees, illiquidity, leverage — prevents costly surprises.
