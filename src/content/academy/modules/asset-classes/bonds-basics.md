## In plain words

A **bond** is a loan you make to a government or company. In return, the borrower promises to pay you a fixed **interest rate** (the **coupon**) at regular intervals and return your original money (the **principal**) on a set date called the **maturity date**. Bonds are sometimes called **fixed income** because the payments are known in advance.

## Quick demo

You buy a 10-year U.S. Treasury bond for $1,000 with a 4% annual coupon. Every year you receive $40. After 10 years you get your $1,000 back. Total income: $400 plus your original $1,000. If interest rates rise to 6% shortly after you buy, your bond — still paying only $40 a year — is worth less than $1,000 on the open market because new bonds pay more. This inverse relationship between rates and bond prices is the core concept to memorize.

## Full explanation

### Bond anatomy

Every bond has five key features:

- **Face value (par)** — the principal returned at maturity, typically $1,000.
- **Coupon rate** — annual interest as a percentage of face value.
- **Maturity** — when the principal is repaid (short: <3 years; intermediate: 3–10; long: >10).
- **Price** — what the bond trades for in the secondary market, which can be above or below par.
- **Yield** — the actual return if you buy today and hold to maturity (accounts for the current price vs. face value).

### Types of bonds

- **Government bonds** — issued by national governments. U.S. Treasuries are considered essentially risk-free; developing-country bonds carry more risk and pay higher rates.
- **Municipal bonds ("munis")** — issued by states and cities; often tax-exempt for U.S. investors.
- **Corporate bonds** — issued by companies. Higher yield than Treasuries; risk depends on the issuer's credit rating.
- **High-yield ("junk") bonds** — corporate bonds rated below investment grade. Higher coupons compensate for higher default risk.

### Duration: interest-rate sensitivity

**Duration** measures how sensitive a bond's price is to interest-rate changes. A bond with a duration of 7 will fall roughly 7% in price if rates rise 1%. Longer-maturity bonds have higher duration, meaning they swing more when rates move. This is why long-dated bonds are more volatile than short-term bills.

### Credit ratings

Rating agencies (Moody's, S&P, Fitch) grade bonds from AAA (lowest default risk) down to C/D (default). Investment grade is BBB-/Baa3 and above. Below that is speculative, or "junk." Ratings affect both price and how institutional investors are allowed to hold the bonds.

### Why bonds matter for a trader or investor

Bonds serve several roles: they generate income, they tend to cushion equity drawdowns (when stocks fall, money often flows to Treasuries), and they are the primary tool through which central bank policy transmits to the economy. Understanding yield, duration, and credit risk lets you interpret market signals — like yield curve moves — that affect every other asset class.
