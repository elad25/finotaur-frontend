## In plain words

**Cryptocurrencies** are digital assets that use cryptography and a **blockchain** — a distributed, tamper-resistant ledger — to record ownership without a central bank or government. **Bitcoin** was the first and remains the largest by market value; **Ethereum** introduced programmable contracts that enable decentralized applications. The crypto market operates 24 hours a day, 365 days a year, is globally accessible, and is significantly more volatile than traditional financial markets.

## Quick demo

Bitcoin launched in 2009 at essentially zero value. By late 2021 it traded above $68,000 per coin — then fell below $16,000 in 2022, a drop of over 75%. By early 2024 it exceeded $73,000 again. A single four-year cycle delivered enormous gains for early holders, devastating losses for those who bought at the top, and then recovery. This kind of volatility — unthinkable for a stock index — is normal in crypto. Position sizing and risk management matter more here than in almost any other asset class.

## Full explanation

### What a blockchain is

A blockchain is a chain of records (blocks) that are cryptographically linked. Once a block is confirmed and added, altering it would require re-computing every subsequent block — computationally impractical at scale. This makes blockchain records effectively permanent and auditable by anyone. No central authority holds the ledger; thousands of nodes hold identical copies.

### Bitcoin (BTC)

Bitcoin was designed as **digital sound money** — a fixed supply of 21 million coins, a predictable issuance schedule (halved roughly every four years in an event called the "halving"), and no central controller. Its value proposition is scarcity, censorship resistance, and portability. It is increasingly held by institutions and ETFs as a store of value and inflation hedge.

### Ethereum (ETH)

Ethereum added **programmability** to blockchain. **Smart contracts** are code that runs automatically on-chain when conditions are met — no intermediary needed. This enabled:
- **DeFi** (decentralized finance) — lending, borrowing, and trading without banks.
- **NFTs** (non-fungible tokens) — provably unique digital assets.
- **Layer 2 networks** — scaling solutions that reduce fees and speed up transactions.

Ethereum's model moved to **proof-of-stake** in 2022 ("The Merge"), dramatically cutting its energy use.

### Market structure

Crypto markets differ from equities in important ways:

- **No circuit breakers** — prices can fall 20–30% in hours with no halt.
- **24/7 trading** — liquidity varies; weekends and off-hours can be thin.
- **Centralized exchanges (CEXs)** — Coinbase, Binance, Kraken. Regulated to varying degrees.
- **Decentralized exchanges (DEXs)** — Uniswap, dYdX. No account needed; trades execute via smart contracts.
- **Custody risk** — if an exchange fails (as FTX did in 2022), assets held on it can be lost. "Not your keys, not your coins."

### Key drivers of crypto prices

- **Bitcoin halving cycles** — historically correlated with bull markets.
- **Macro environment** — crypto often trades like a high-beta risk asset; rising rates and dollar strength tend to pressure prices.
- **Regulatory news** — SEC actions, ETF approvals, government bans move prices sharply.
- **On-chain data** — active addresses, exchange inflows, whale movements offer signals unique to crypto.
- **Narrative** — sector rotation between DeFi, NFTs, Layer 2s, and AI tokens drives altcoin cycles.

### Correlation and diversification

Bitcoin's correlation to the S&P 500 has risen over time as institutional participation grew, particularly during stress events (both can sell off together in liquidity crunches). It is not a reliable hedge against equity drawdowns in the short run, but over very long horizons it has moved largely independently of traditional assets.

### Why it matters for a trader or investor

Crypto is now large enough — Bitcoin alone has a market cap comparable to major economies — that it cannot be ignored as a macro factor or as a trading opportunity. The volatility that makes it dangerous for the underprepared makes it attractive for traders who manage risk well. Understanding the structural differences from traditional markets — custody, liquidity, 24/7 pricing, on-chain data — is the minimum required before allocating any capital.
