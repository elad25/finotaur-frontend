## In plain words

**Leverage** means controlling a larger position than your own capital would normally allow, by borrowing the rest. A **CFD** (Contract for Difference) is a contract between you and a broker where you agree to exchange the difference in price of an asset from when you open to when you close the trade — without ever owning the underlying asset. **Margin** is the deposit you put up as collateral to hold a leveraged position. Leverage amplifies both gains and losses proportionally.

## Quick demo

Gold is trading at $2,000 per ounce. You open a CFD for 10 ounces (notional value $20,000) with 10:1 leverage, so you post $2,000 in margin. Gold rises 1% to $2,020. Your position gained $200 on $2,000 of capital — a 10% return on your actual money from a 1% move. But if gold falls 1% to $1,980, you lose $200 — also 10% of your capital. At 10:1 leverage, a 10% adverse move wipes your entire margin. Most retail CFD traders lose money; this math is why.

## Full explanation

### How leverage works

Leverage is expressed as a ratio: 5:1 means you control $5 of exposure for every $1 of capital. The broker lends you the remaining $4.

- **Notional value** = your capital × leverage ratio.
- **Required margin** = notional value ÷ leverage ratio (your deposit).
- **Profit/loss** is calculated on the full notional, not your deposit.
- **Margin call** — if losses reduce your equity below the maintenance margin level, the broker will demand more funds or close your position automatically.

### CFDs (Contracts for Difference)

CFDs are available on stocks, indices, forex, commodities, and crypto from specialized brokers. Key characteristics:

- **No ownership** — you never receive shares or physical goods. You settle the price difference in cash.
- **Long or short** — easy to profit from falling prices by selling CFDs short.
- **Overnight financing (swap)** — holding a long CFD past market close incurs a daily interest charge (since the broker effectively lent you money to fund the position). Short positions may earn a credit.
- **Availability** — CFDs are widely offered in Europe, Asia, and Australia. They are **banned for retail traders in the United States**.

### Margin trading on exchanges

In the U.S., retail traders access leverage through margin accounts with stock brokers (Regulation T allows 2:1 on equities overnight, 4:1 intraday for day traders) or through futures accounts (where exchange-set margins provide higher leverage on indices, commodities, and currencies).

### Leveraged and inverse ETFs

These are exchange-traded products that use derivatives internally to deliver 2× or 3× the daily return of an index (or its inverse). They are designed for intraday use, not long-term holding: **daily rebalancing causes performance decay** (called "volatility drag") over time, meaning a 2× ETF held for weeks or months typically underperforms 2× the index's total return even if the index ends flat.

### The risk profile of leverage

Leverage does not change the probability distribution of the underlying asset — it magnifies it. With 10:1 leverage on a 20% volatile asset, your position's annual volatility is 200%. This means:

- **Sequence of returns matters enormously** — a string of moderate losses can wipe out a leveraged position before it recovers.
- **Risk of ruin** — the probability of a total loss is not theoretical; many retail leveraged traders experience it.
- **Discipline requirements** — leveraged trading demands strict stop-loss rules and position sizing. Without them, a single bad trade is catastrophic.

### Why it matters for a trader or investor

Leverage is not inherently bad — professional firms use it routinely and manage it with sophisticated risk controls. For retail traders, the danger is the speed at which it can destroy capital. Before using any leveraged instrument, a trader must understand the margin requirements, the cost of carry, how margin calls work at their specific broker, and what percentage drawdown their account can survive. Sizing a leveraged position as if it were an unleveraged one is one of the most common — and most costly — mistakes in retail trading.
