## In plain words

**Real estate** as an investment means owning property to earn rental income or to sell later at a profit. A **REIT** (Real Estate Investment Trust) lets you access real estate returns without buying actual property — it is a company that owns income-producing real estate and trades on a stock exchange like a regular share. REITs are required by law to distribute at least 90% of their taxable income to shareholders as dividends.

## Quick demo

You want real estate exposure but have $2,000, not $200,000. You buy shares in a REIT that owns 400 apartment complexes across the U.S. Your $2,000 buys a proportional slice of all that rental income. When tenants pay rent, the REIT collects it, pays expenses, and distributes most of the remainder to you quarterly. If the properties appreciate, the REIT's share price rises too. You got real estate cash flow and potential capital gain — with no landlord calls at midnight.

## Full explanation

### Direct real estate investment

Owning property directly provides:

- **Rental income** — a monthly cash flow stream from tenants.
- **Capital appreciation** — properties tend to rise in value over long periods, partially tracking inflation.
- **Leverage** — mortgages allow you to control a $500,000 asset with $100,000 down, amplifying returns (and losses).
- **Tax advantages** — depreciation deductions, 1031 exchanges (in the U.S.) can defer capital gains.

The drawbacks are illiquidity (you cannot sell a bedroom), concentration risk, and the operational burden of managing tenants, maintenance, and vacancies.

### REITs — the stock-market equivalent

REITs were created in the U.S. in 1960 to give ordinary investors access to large-scale real estate. Key characteristics:

- **Equity REITs** own and operate physical properties (apartments, offices, warehouses, retail centers, data centers, hospitals).
- **Mortgage REITs (mREITs)** lend money to real estate owners or invest in mortgage-backed securities. They carry more interest-rate risk.
- **Hybrid REITs** combine both.

### REIT sectors

Real estate covers a wide range of property types, each with different risk profiles:

- **Residential** — apartments and single-family rentals.
- **Industrial** — warehouses and logistics (strong secular tailwind from e-commerce).
- **Office** — challenged post-pandemic with remote work trends.
- **Retail** — malls and shopping centers (pressured by e-commerce).
- **Data centers** — high demand from cloud computing growth.
- **Healthcare** — hospitals, senior housing, medical offices.
- **Self-storage** — defensive; demand stays relatively stable through recessions.

### REITs and interest rates

REITs are sensitive to interest rates for two reasons: (1) they are valued partly like bonds because of their high dividends — rising rates make bonds more competitive, pressuring REIT prices; (2) REITs carry debt, and refinancing at higher rates squeezes margins. When rates fall, REITs often outperform.

### Metrics for evaluating REITs

- **FFO (Funds From Operations)** — adjusts net income for depreciation, the key REIT earnings metric.
- **AFFO (Adjusted FFO)** — further strips out capital expenditures; closer to free cash flow.
- **Dividend yield** — annual dividend divided by share price.
- **Occupancy rate** — what percentage of units or space is rented.

### Why it matters for a trader or investor

REITs offer inflation-linked income and diversification away from technology-heavy equity indices. Data center and industrial REITs have been among the strongest performers of the past decade. For income-focused portfolios, the mandated 90% payout is a structural advantage — and for active traders, REIT sectors respond visibly to rate decisions and economic data, creating tradeable patterns around Fed events.
