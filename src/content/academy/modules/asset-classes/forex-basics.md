## In plain words

**Forex** (foreign exchange) is the market where currencies are bought and sold against each other. It is the largest financial market in the world by volume — over $7 trillion traded per day. Currencies are quoted in **pairs** (e.g., EUR/USD), where the price tells you how many units of the second currency one unit of the first currency buys. Forex runs 24 hours a day, five days a week, across overlapping global sessions.

## Quick demo

EUR/USD is quoted at 1.0850. That means 1 euro buys 1.0850 U.S. dollars. You believe the euro will strengthen against the dollar, so you buy 10,000 euros (a "mini lot"). If the pair rises to 1.0950, each euro is now worth $0.01 more. On 10,000 euros that is a $100 gain — on a move of only 100 **pips** (the smallest standard price increment). With 10:1 leverage that $100 gain was earned on just $1,000 of capital deployed. But the same leverage means a 100-pip move against you costs $100 too.

## Full explanation

### Currency pairs

Currencies always trade in pairs because buying one currency means selling another.

- **Major pairs** involve the USD and the world's most liquid currencies: EUR/USD, USD/JPY, GBP/USD, USD/CHF, USD/CAD, AUD/USD, NZD/USD.
- **Cross pairs** exclude USD: EUR/GBP, EUR/JPY, GBP/JPY.
- **Exotic pairs** combine a major currency with a smaller economy's: USD/TRY, USD/MXN.

The first currency in a pair is the **base**; the second is the **quote**. The price shows how much of the quote currency buys one unit of the base.

### Pips and lots

- **Pip** — price interest point. For most pairs, 1 pip = 0.0001 (fourth decimal place). For JPY pairs, 1 pip = 0.01.
- **Lot sizes**: standard lot = 100,000 units; mini lot = 10,000 units; micro lot = 1,000 units.
- **Pip value** depends on lot size and the quote currency. For EUR/USD standard lot, 1 pip = $10.

### Leverage and margin

Forex brokers routinely offer leverage of 10:1 to 50:1 (retail) and higher for professionals. Leverage amplifies both gains and losses. **Margin** is the deposit required to hold a leveraged position — if losses eat into your margin below the maintenance level, the broker issues a **margin call** and may close your position.

### What moves currencies

- **Interest rate differentials** — higher rates attract capital, strengthening a currency.
- **Inflation** — high inflation erodes purchasing power and weakens a currency.
- **Economic data** — GDP, employment, trade balance reports move pairs at release.
- **Central bank policy and language** — Fed, ECB, BoJ statements can move majors 100+ pips instantly.
- **Risk sentiment** — in risk-off episodes, safe-haven currencies (USD, JPY, CHF) strengthen; high-beta currencies (AUD, NZD) weaken.

### Trading sessions

Forex operates across four main sessions — Sydney, Tokyo, London, and New York — with the London-New York overlap (8 AM–12 PM ET) producing the highest volume and tightest spreads.

### Why it matters for a trader or investor

Even if you never trade a currency pair directly, forex context is essential. A strong dollar suppresses commodities, pressures emerging-market debt, and dents the overseas earnings of multinational corporations. Currency moves can explain why a stock that beat earnings still fell — or why gold spiked on a quiet news day. Forex literacy is part of reading any global market.
