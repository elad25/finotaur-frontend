## In plain words

A **stock** is a small ownership stake in a company. When a company issues stock, it splits itself into millions of tiny pieces called **shares** and sells them to raise money. **Common stock** gives you voting rights and a share of profits; **preferred stock** pays a fixed dividend first but usually has no vote.

## Quick demo

Suppose a company is worth $1,000,000 and issues 1,000,000 shares. Each share represents one-millionth ownership. You buy 100 shares for $1 each. If the company grows and the market values it at $2,000,000, your 100 shares are now worth $200 — you doubled your money without doing anything extra. That upside is why investors buy stocks.

## Full explanation

### Common stock

Common stock is the standard share most people mean when they say "stock." Holders get:

- **Voting rights** — one vote per share on major decisions such as board elections and acquisitions.
- **Residual claim** — after debts and preferred dividends are paid, common shareholders split whatever is left (profits or liquidation proceeds).
- **Dividends** — optional; the board declares them. Not all companies pay dividends; growth companies typically reinvest profits instead.

Because common shareholders are last in line if a company fails, common stock carries the highest risk — and, historically, the highest long-run return.

### Preferred stock

Preferred stock sits between debt and common equity:

- **Fixed dividend** paid before any common dividend.
- **Priority in liquidation** — preferred holders are paid before common holders (but after debt).
- **Usually no vote** on company matters.
- **Convertible** — some preferred shares can convert to common at a set ratio.

Preferred stock behaves more like a bond than a common share. It attracts income-focused investors who want predictability over growth.

### Why stock prices move

A stock's price reflects what buyers and sellers currently think the company is worth. Prices shift when:

- **Earnings** beat or miss expectations.
- **Macro conditions** change (interest rates, inflation, recession).
- **Sector news** re-rates the whole industry.
- **Supply and demand** at the moment of trade.

### Share classes

Some companies create multiple classes of common stock with different voting weights. Google's parent Alphabet, for example, has Class A (1 vote), Class B (10 votes, not publicly traded), and Class C (no vote). This lets founders keep control after going public.

### Why it matters for a trader or investor

Stocks are the most liquid, most data-rich asset class available to retail participants. Understanding whether you own common or preferred — and where each sits in the capital structure — clarifies your actual risk. A common shareholder in a distressed company may get nothing; a preferred holder may recover some capital. Knowing the difference matters long before a company hits trouble.
