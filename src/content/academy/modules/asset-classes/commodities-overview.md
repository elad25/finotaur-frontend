## In plain words

**Commodities** are raw physical goods that are interchangeable regardless of who produced them — a barrel of crude oil from Texas is essentially the same as one from Saudi Arabia. The main categories are **energy** (oil, natural gas), **metals** (gold, silver, copper), and **agriculture** (wheat, corn, soybeans, coffee). Commodity prices are driven by global supply and demand, weather, geopolitics, and the strength of the U.S. dollar.

## Quick demo

In early 2022 Russia invaded Ukraine. Both countries are major wheat exporters. Within weeks, Chicago wheat futures jumped from roughly $750 to over $1,300 per bushel — a 70%+ move — because traders repriced the supply shortfall. A farmer in Kansas who had locked in a price by selling futures before the invasion was protected. A food company that had not hedged suddenly faced dramatically higher input costs. Physical commodities connect global events to market prices almost instantly.

## Full explanation

### The three main sectors

**Energy**
- **Crude oil** (WTI and Brent are the two benchmarks) is the world's most actively traded commodity. Gasoline, diesel, jet fuel, and petrochemicals all derive from it. OPEC production decisions, U.S. shale output, and global growth forecasts drive price.
- **Natural gas** is used for heating, electricity, and industrial processes. Prices can spike sharply in winter or with supply disruptions.

**Metals**
- **Precious metals** (gold, silver, platinum) are held as stores of value, hedges against inflation, and safe-haven assets in crises. Gold in particular moves inversely to real interest rates.
- **Industrial metals** (copper, aluminum, nickel) are economic barometers. Copper is nicknamed "Dr. Copper" because its price tends to rise with global growth (more construction, more wiring) and fall with slowdowns.

**Agriculture**
- Grains (wheat, corn, soybeans), softs (coffee, cocoa, sugar, cotton), and livestock. Prices are heavily influenced by weather, crop reports from the USDA, and export demand from large buyers like China.

### How commodities trade

Most commodity trading happens via **futures contracts** — agreements to buy or sell a specific quantity at a set price on a future date. Retail investors can also access commodities through:
- **Commodity ETFs** (e.g., GLD for gold, USO for oil).
- **Commodity producer stocks** (e.g., oil majors, mining companies).
- **Commodity mutual funds or indices**.

Physical delivery is possible for futures but rare for retail participants — most traders roll their contracts before expiry.

### The dollar relationship

Commodities are priced in U.S. dollars globally. When the dollar strengthens, commodities become more expensive for foreign buyers, which can suppress demand and push prices lower. A weakening dollar has the opposite effect. This makes the DXY (dollar index) a useful companion chart when analyzing commodities.

### Why it matters for a trader or investor

Commodities add a dimension that stocks and bonds cannot: direct exposure to physical supply and demand. They tend to perform well during inflationary periods when financial assets struggle. Understanding energy and metals also helps you interpret macroeconomic signals — oil prices feed directly into CPI, and copper often leads equity markets by a quarter or two.
