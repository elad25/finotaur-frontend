## In plain words

A **derivative** is a financial contract whose value is derived from an underlying asset — a stock, index, commodity, currency, or interest rate. Rather than owning the asset directly, you hold a contract that specifies a price, a quantity, and a future date. The three main types are **options** (the right but not obligation to buy or sell), **futures** (the obligation to buy or sell at a set price on a set date), and **forwards** (a private, customized version of a futures contract).

## Quick demo

You think Apple stock will rise from $180 to $200 over the next month. Instead of buying 100 shares for $18,000, you buy a call option — the right to purchase 100 shares at $185 any time in the next 30 days — for a premium of $3 per share, or $300 total. If Apple reaches $200, your option is worth at least $15 per share ($200 − $185), so $1,500 — a 5× return on your $300 outlay. If Apple falls to $160, you lose only the $300 premium, not $1,800 like a stock buyer would. Leverage and capped downside in one contract.

## Full explanation

### Options

An option gives the buyer the **right, not the obligation**, to buy (call) or sell (put) an asset at a predetermined **strike price** before or on the **expiration date**.

- **Call option** — the right to buy. Profitable when the underlying rises above the strike.
- **Put option** — the right to sell. Profitable when the underlying falls below the strike.
- **Premium** — the price paid for the option. It is the maximum a buyer can lose.
- **American vs. European style** — American options can be exercised any time before expiry; European only at expiry.

Options are covered in depth in the Options & Advanced Derivatives module.

### Futures

A futures contract is a **binding agreement** to buy or sell a specific quantity of an asset at a set price on a specific future date. Unlike options, both parties are obligated to perform.

- **Standardized** — futures trade on exchanges (CME, ICE, CBOE) with standardized contract sizes and settlement dates.
- **Marked to market daily** — gains and losses are credited or debited to your account each day.
- **Margined** — you post a small initial margin (often 3–10% of contract value) rather than the full notional.
- **Uses** — hedging (an airline locks in jet fuel prices), speculation (a trader bets on oil direction), or arbitrage.

A key concept is the **roll**: futures have expiration dates. If you want to hold a position beyond expiry, you close the near-month contract and open the next one — a process with its own cost or benefit depending on the **term structure** (contango vs. backwardation).

### Forwards

Forwards are structurally similar to futures but are:
- **Private, bilateral contracts** — not exchange-traded.
- **Customizable** — any asset, any size, any date.
- **Not marked to market** — settlement happens at expiry.
- **Counterparty risk** — if one party defaults, there is no exchange clearinghouse to step in.

Banks and corporations use forwards extensively to hedge currency and commodity exposures. Retail traders rarely use them directly.

### Why derivatives exist

Derivatives serve two primary economic functions:

1. **Hedging** — reducing or eliminating price risk. A corn farmer sells futures to lock in price before harvest; an airline buys oil futures to cap fuel costs.
2. **Speculation and price discovery** — derivatives markets are often more liquid and faster-moving than spot markets. Prices set here transmit information to underlying asset prices.

### Why it matters for a trader or investor

You do not need to trade derivatives actively to benefit from understanding them. Options flow reveals institutional bets and hedges before they appear in stock prices. Futures markets in bonds and equity indices run around the clock and price in news before stock exchanges open. Even if you only trade stocks, knowing what the derivatives market is pricing gives you an edge in reading where money is positioned.
