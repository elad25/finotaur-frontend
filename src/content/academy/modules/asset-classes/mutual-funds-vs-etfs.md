## In plain words

A **mutual fund** and an **ETF (exchange-traded fund)** are both ways to own a basket of securities — stocks, bonds, or other assets — through a single purchase. The key difference is how they trade: mutual funds are priced once a day after market close, while ETFs trade on an exchange throughout the day just like a stock. Both provide instant **diversification** but with different costs, taxes, and flexibility.

## Quick demo

You have $500 and want exposure to 500 U.S. large-cap companies. Buying each stock directly would cost thousands and require enormous time. Instead you buy one share of an S&P 500 ETF for around $500 — or invest $500 into an S&P 500 mutual fund. Both give you proportional ownership in all 500 companies. The ETF fills at whatever price it trades at 10:43 AM; the mutual fund fills at the price calculated at 4:00 PM. Same underlying basket, different wrapper.

## Full explanation

### How mutual funds work

A mutual fund pools money from many investors and a professional manager buys a portfolio on their behalf. Key traits:

- **Priced once daily** at the **NAV** (net asset value), calculated after markets close.
- **No intraday trading** — orders placed during the day execute at that evening's NAV.
- **Minimum investments** often $500–$3,000.
- **Actively managed** or index-tracking (passive). Active funds charge higher fees; index mutual funds can be as cheap as ETFs.
- **Less tax-efficient** — the manager's trading inside the fund can trigger capital gains distributions passed to all shareholders.

### How ETFs work

ETFs are structured like mutual funds internally but listed on an exchange:

- **Intraday trading** — buy or sell any time the market is open, at the live market price.
- **No minimums** beyond one share (some brokers allow fractional shares).
- **Typically passive** — most ETFs track an index, keeping expenses low (many under 0.10% per year).
- **Tax-efficient** — a unique "creation/redemption" mechanism lets the fund swap in-kind with large institutions, avoiding most capital gains events inside the fund.
- **Spread cost** — you pay the bid-ask spread when trading, which can add up for thinly traded ETFs.

### Comparing costs

| Feature | Mutual Fund (active) | Mutual Fund (index) | ETF (index) |
|---|---|---|---|
| Expense ratio | 0.5–1.5% | 0.01–0.20% | 0.01–0.20% |
| Trading cost | None (direct) | None (direct) | Bid-ask spread |
| Min investment | Often $500+ | Often $500+ | 1 share |
| Tax efficiency | Lower | Medium | Higher |

### Index funds vs. active funds

Both mutual funds and ETFs can be **index funds** (passive, tracking a benchmark like the S&P 500) or **actively managed**. Decades of data show that most active managers underperform their benchmark net of fees over long periods. This is the principal argument for low-cost index ETFs.

### Why it matters for a trader or investor

For long-term investors, low-cost index ETFs are the most efficient vehicle for broad exposure. For active traders, ETFs offer intraday flexibility unavailable in mutual funds. Understanding the wrapper — not just the underlying — helps you pick the right tool for your time horizon, tax situation, and strategy.
