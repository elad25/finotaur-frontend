## In plain words

Most major commodities — oil, gold, copper, wheat, soybeans — are **priced in US dollars** on global markets. This creates a mechanical relationship: when the dollar strengthens, it takes fewer dollars to buy the same barrel of oil or ounce of gold, so dollar-denominated prices tend to fall. When the dollar weakens, commodity prices tend to rise, all else equal. This inverse relationship is one of the most consistent in financial markets, though it is not absolute and can be overridden by powerful supply or demand shocks.

## Quick demo

In 2022, the US Federal Reserve embarked on the most aggressive rate-hiking cycle in four decades. The US Dollar Index (DXY) surged roughly **15% between January and September 2022**, rising from about 96 to above 114. Over the same period, gold fell from approximately **$1,830 to $1,620 per ounce** despite record inflation — the very scenario where many expected gold to shine. The dollar's strength offset the inflation hedge argument, reminding traders that the dollar relationship can dominate even powerful fundamental tailwinds for extended periods.

## Full explanation

### Why the dollar matters

When a commodity is priced in dollars, any move in the dollar changes the effective price for every non-US buyer and seller in the world:

- A **stronger dollar** means foreign buyers (who convert local currency to dollars to purchase commodities) face higher real costs. This suppresses demand from outside the US, which puts downward pressure on the dollar price of commodities.
- A **weaker dollar** makes commodities cheaper for foreign buyers, stimulating demand and supporting prices.

This mechanism is most direct for gold, oil, and broadly traded grains. It is less dominant for commodities where physical supply-demand conditions are extremely tight or where geopolitical shocks are in play.

### The DXY as a commodity input

The **US Dollar Index (DXY)** — which measures the dollar against a basket of major currencies — is a standard reference for commodity traders. A rising DXY is generally a headwind for commodities; a falling DXY is a tailwind. Tracking the DXY alongside a commodity chart is basic practice.

### When the relationship breaks down

The dollar-commodity inverse relationship is a tendency, not a law. It breaks down when:

- **Supply shocks dominate** — If a major oil-producing region goes offline, oil can rally sharply even as the dollar strengthens.
- **Demand surges** — A sudden spike in global industrial activity (e.g., post-COVID restocking) can overwhelm dollar headwinds.
- **Safe-haven flows conflict** — During extreme financial stress, both the dollar and gold can rise simultaneously because investors flee to safety in multiple forms.

### Dollar strength, inflation, and commodities

There is also a second-order relationship worth understanding: commodities are often seen as an inflation hedge. Rising inflation can weaken real interest rates, which tends to weaken the dollar, which in turn supports commodity prices. This creates a reinforcing cycle — but it also means the cycle can reverse sharply when central banks hike rates aggressively (strengthening the dollar and pressuring commodities simultaneously).

### Producer currencies

Some currencies are so closely tied to commodity exports that they move in tandem with commodity prices — these are called **commodity currencies**. Examples include the Australian dollar (AUD, driven by iron ore and coal exports), the Canadian dollar (CAD, closely correlated to oil), and the Norwegian krone (NOK, tied to North Sea oil). Traders who understand this linkage can use commodity trends to anticipate currency moves, or vice versa.

### Why it matters for a trader

Ignoring the dollar when trading commodities is like trading tech stocks without watching the Nasdaq. The DXY is a non-negotiable input into any commodity analysis. A commodity that is rising against a rising dollar is showing exceptional intrinsic demand strength — a powerful signal. A commodity that can barely hold its level against a weakening dollar is flagging fundamental weakness beneath the surface. Always contextualize commodity price action against what the dollar is doing.
