## In plain words

Every commodity price is ultimately a tug-of-war between **supply** (how much is available) and **demand** (how much is wanted). But commodities add a layer that most financial assets don't have: **seasonality** — predictable, recurring patterns in supply and demand that repeat year after year because of the physical world. Heating oil gets used in winter. Gasoline demand peaks in summer. Corn harvests happen in autumn. Knowing these rhythms doesn't guarantee a profit, but trading against them without knowing about them is a common and avoidable mistake.

## Quick demo

Natural gas futures almost always weaken in late spring and early summer in the Northern Hemisphere. Here's why: winter heating demand has just ended, but summer cooling demand hasn't yet peaked — storage facilities refill rapidly. In 2023, US natural gas prices fell from about **$3.50 per MMBtu in March to under $2.00 by June**, roughly a 43% drop, following a pattern that repeats most years. Traders who bought the "gas rally" in April without accounting for the seasonal inventory build were trading against a known headwind.

## Full explanation

### Supply-side fundamentals

The supply side of any commodity is captured in two key concepts:

- **Production** — How much is being extracted, grown, or refined. Production levels are often known in advance (OPEC sets targets; USDA projects planting acreage) but are disrupted by weather, accidents, and geopolitics.
- **Inventories (stocks)** — The buffer between production and consumption. Inventory data — EIA for energy, USDA WASDE for agriculture, LME warehouse data for metals — is published on a regular schedule and is watched intensely by the market. **Low inventories amplify price volatility** because there is no buffer; any supply disruption or demand spike goes straight to price.

### Demand-side fundamentals

- **Economic growth** — Industrial metals and energy demand correlates with GDP growth; weakness in major economies suppresses it.
- **Substitution** — When a commodity becomes expensive enough, buyers shift to alternatives. This creates a natural ceiling on prices over time, though the transition is slow.
- **Policy-driven demand** — Biofuel mandates boost corn and sugar demand; EV adoption drives copper and nickel demand. Policy can create secular demand shifts that override seasonal patterns.

### Seasonality in detail

| Commodity | Seasonal driver | Typical pattern |
|-----------|----------------|-----------------|
| Natural gas | Winter heating / summer cooling | Low spring, spike into winter |
| Gasoline (RBOB) | Summer driving season | Rises into Memorial Day, weakens after Labor Day |
| Heating oil | Winter demand | Rises Aug–Jan, weakens spring |
| Corn | Planting/harvest cycle | Often weakens at harvest (Oct); can rally spring if planting concerns emerge |
| Wheat | Northern/Southern Hemisphere harvests | Two crop cycles create multiple seasonal price windows |
| Soybeans | US harvest (Oct) / South American harvest (Jan–Mar) | Two major supply events per year |
| Crude oil | Summer demand / OPEC policy | Less predictable than refined products |

### The carry structure as a signal

Commodity futures markets often signal the supply-demand balance through the shape of the futures curve:

- **Contango** — Futures prices for later months are higher than nearby months. This typically signals ample supply or weak near-term demand; storage is being paid for.
- **Backwardation** — Nearby months are priced higher than later months. This signals tight supply and strong immediate demand — buyers need the commodity now and will pay a premium.

Backwardation in a commodity market is often a bullish structural signal.

### Why it matters for a trader

Seasonality is one of the few edges in commodity markets that is genuinely structural and repeatable. It doesn't work every year — an unusually warm winter, a geopolitical shock, or a policy change can override it — but it provides a probabilistic baseline. Before sizing into a commodity position, ask two questions: Where are inventories relative to the 5-year average? And am I trading with or against the seasonal tendency? These two data points alone will improve the quality of your setups.
