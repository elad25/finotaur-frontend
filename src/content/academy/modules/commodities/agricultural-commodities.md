## In plain words

**Agricultural commodities** — called **"ags"** by traders — include the grains (corn, wheat, soybeans), the softs (coffee, cocoa, sugar, cotton), and livestock (cattle, hogs). They are the raw inputs for the food and beverage supply chain. What makes ag markets unique is that their prices are driven not just by supply and demand, but by factors that no one controls: **weather, disease, and the biological limits of growing seasons**. The result is a market that can be calm for months and then violently repriced by a single frost, drought, or export ban.

## Quick demo

In 2022, Russia's invasion of Ukraine — two countries that together account for roughly **30% of global wheat exports** — sent wheat futures from about **$7.70 per bushel to over $13.00** in less than three weeks. Importing nations scrambled for alternative supplies; governments panicked about food security. A geopolitical event in one region repriced the food bill for billions of people. No fundamental analysis or technical setup predicted it — it was an abrupt supply shock, which is the defining risk of agricultural markets.

## Full explanation

### The grains complex

- **Corn (ZC)** — The world's most produced grain by volume, used for animal feed, ethanol production, and food processing. Corn prices are sensitive to US crop conditions (the US is the largest exporter), energy prices (ethanol demand links corn to oil), and Chinese import demand.
- **Soybeans (ZS)** — The primary protein source in animal feed globally. The US and Brazil are the dominant exporters. Soybeans follow a strong seasonal pattern around the South American harvest (January–March), which often creates price inflection points.
- **Wheat (ZW)** — Grown across many regions, but exports are concentrated in the US, Russia, Ukraine, Australia, and Canada. Wheat is acutely sensitive to geopolitical disruptions in key exporting regions.

### The softs

- **Coffee (KC)** — Weather in Brazil and Vietnam (the two largest producers) dominates price moves. Frosts in Brazil's coffee belt have triggered multi-year bull markets in arabica coffee.
- **Cocoa (CC)** — Over 60% of global cocoa comes from West Africa (Ivory Coast and Ghana). Disease, political instability, and rainfall patterns in that region drive the market.
- **Sugar (SB)** — Sensitive to Brazilian production (Brazil controls roughly 20% of global supply), weather, and ethanol economics (in Brazil, sugar can be diverted to ethanol when energy prices are high).
- **Cotton (CT)** — US crop reports, Indian government export policies, and global textile demand are the primary drivers.

### Weather and seasonal patterns

Agricultural commodities follow predictable seasonal rhythms tied to planting, growing, and harvest cycles. The **USDA's World Agricultural Supply and Demand Estimates (WASDE) report**, released monthly, is the single most important scheduled data release in agricultural markets — equivalent to the jobs report for equities traders.

### Key risk: supply shocks

Unlike financial assets, agricultural commodities cannot be manufactured to meet demand. When a drought hits, there is no alternative — the supply is simply smaller. This creates asymmetric price behavior: supply shocks produce sharp, fast spikes that can take months or years to unwind as the next crop cycle begins.

### Why it matters for a trader

Agricultural markets offer genuine diversification because their primary drivers (weather, crop cycles, geopolitics of food) are largely independent of interest rates, equity valuations, or corporate earnings. For macro traders, sharp ag rallies — especially in wheat and corn — feed directly into global food price inflation, which influences CPI readings and emerging market political risk. Understanding the seasonal structure of a grain market before entering a trade can mean the difference between being early and being right.
