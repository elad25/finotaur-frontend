## In plain words

**Crude oil** is the single most traded commodity in the world and the backbone of the global energy system. Its price ripples into gasoline, diesel, plastics, fertilizers, and airline tickets — touching almost every other sector. Energy markets include oil, natural gas, heating oil, and gasoline, all of which trade as futures contracts and respond to a predictable set of supply, demand, and geopolitical drivers.

## Quick demo

In the second half of 2022, European natural gas prices surged to roughly **€340 per megawatt-hour** — about ten times their historical average — after Russia curtailed pipeline flows following the invasion of Ukraine. The shock didn't stay in gas markets: it pushed electricity prices to records across Europe and accelerated a scramble for LNG cargoes worldwide. One geopolitical event in one energy market repriced energy across an entire continent within months.

## Full explanation

### The benchmarks

Oil does not have a single global price. Three key benchmarks dominate:

- **WTI (West Texas Intermediate)** — US crude, delivered at Cushing, Oklahoma. The benchmark for North American markets. Trades as the CL futures contract on the NYMEX.
- **Brent Crude** — North Sea crude, the international benchmark. Most global oil contracts are priced off Brent. The spread between WTI and Brent reflects US storage conditions, export infrastructure, and transport costs.
- **Heating Oil (HO) and RBOB Gasoline** — Refined products derived from crude; they trade on their own futures markets and are watched as indicators of end-consumer demand.

### What moves oil prices

- **OPEC+ production decisions** — The cartel of major oil-producing nations sets output targets that directly affect global supply. Surprise cuts are bullish; surprise increases are bearish.
- **EIA Weekly Inventory Report** — Published every Wednesday, this US government report shows crude oil, gasoline, and distillate stockpiles. A drawdown (fewer barrels in storage) typically lifts prices; a build pushes them lower. It is one of the most watched scheduled data releases in commodity markets.
- **Economic growth data** — Oil demand is tightly correlated with global GDP growth, particularly in emerging markets. Recession fears crush oil.
- **Geopolitical risk** — Conflict in oil-producing regions (the Middle East, Russia, Nigeria, Venezuela) adds a risk premium to prices. This premium can evaporate quickly when the risk fades.
- **The US dollar** — Because oil is priced in dollars, dollar strength tends to suppress oil prices and vice versa.

### Natural gas

Natural gas trades on its own supply-demand dynamics, driven by weather (heating and cooling demand), storage levels (tracked in the weekly EIA Natural Gas Storage Report), and LNG export capacity. Gas and oil prices do not always move together, especially in the short term.

### Energy and the broader market

Energy costs feed directly into corporate margins, consumer spending, and inflation. High oil prices act as a tax on the economy — they raise costs across transportation, manufacturing, and agriculture, and they typically lift the CPI. That relationship makes energy central to macro analysis, not just commodity trading.

### Why it matters for a trader

Oil is one of the most liquid futures markets in the world, offering tight spreads and nearly 24-hour access. But its sensitivity to geopolitical events, scheduled reports, and OPEC decisions means you must track the catalyst calendar carefully. A position held into an OPEC meeting or an EIA report without awareness of the risk is an unmanaged bet. Energy traders who survive long-term treat the fundamental backdrop — storage levels, demand trends, geopolitical risk premium — as essential context for every technical setup.
