## In plain words

A **commodity** is a raw material or primary good — oil, wheat, copper, gold — where one producer's output is interchangeable with another's. Prices are set by **supply and demand** at a global level, which means a drought in Brazil, a war in the Middle East, or a slowdown in Chinese manufacturing can all move the price you see on a screen in seconds. Unlike a stock, a commodity has no earnings or management team — its price is purely a reflection of the physical world.

## Quick demo

In the spring of 2020, global lockdowns crushed demand for jet fuel and gasoline overnight. The world was physically running out of places to store crude oil. WTI crude oil futures briefly traded at **negative $37 per barrel** on April 20, 2020 — meaning producers were paying buyers to take oil away. That extreme is unusual, but it illustrates the core driver: when supply vastly exceeds demand and storage is full, price collapses regardless of what any central bank does.

## Full explanation

### Supply factors

The supply side of a commodity is shaped by:

- **Production capacity** — How many barrels OPEC members agree to pump, how much wheat a growing season yields, how fast miners can extract copper.
- **Inventory levels** — Stockpiles act as a buffer. When inventories are high, any supply shock is absorbed; when they are low, even a small disruption drives prices sharply higher.
- **Geopolitics** — Sanctions, wars, and cartel decisions (especially OPEC+ for oil) can restrict supply regardless of market forces.
- **Weather and natural events** — Hurricanes disrupt Gulf of Mexico oil production; droughts destroy grain harvests; floods block shipping routes.

### Demand factors

- **Economic growth** — Strong GDP growth in large economies (especially China and the US) boosts industrial demand for energy and metals.
- **Substitution** — If oil becomes too expensive, buyers shift to natural gas or renewables over time. Demand is not fixed.
- **Currency effects** — Most commodities are priced in US dollars. When the dollar strengthens, commodities become more expensive for foreign buyers, which suppresses demand.
- **Seasonal demand** — Heating oil demand peaks in winter; gasoline demand peaks in summer; agricultural demand follows crop cycles.

### The role of futures markets

Most commodity price discovery happens in futures markets — standardized contracts to buy or sell a set quantity at a set price on a future date. These markets serve two types of participants:

- **Hedgers** — Producers (oil companies, farmers) and consumers (airlines, food processors) who lock in prices to reduce business risk.
- **Speculators** — Traders and funds who provide liquidity and absorb risk in exchange for potential profit.

### Macro forces

Commodity prices are also driven by broader macro signals: dollar strength, interest rates, inflation expectations, and global trade flows. This is why commodities are often treated as an asset class and a macro signal, not just a market for physical goods.

### Why it matters for a trader

Understanding what drives a commodity helps you avoid being blindsided. An oil trader who ignores OPEC meeting dates, EIA inventory reports, and dollar strength is trading blind. Commodity markets react fast to real-world events — and that speed creates both the opportunity and the risk. Before sizing into any commodity position, knowing the current supply-demand balance and the key near-term catalysts is non-negotiable.
