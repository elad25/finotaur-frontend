## In plain words

**Industrial metals** — primarily **copper, aluminum, zinc, nickel, and steel** — are the raw materials that build everything: wiring, pipes, vehicles, buildings, and electronics. Unlike gold, these metals have almost no monetary function. Their prices rise and fall almost entirely with the health of the global economy, making them powerful real-time barometers of industrial activity and growth expectations.

## Quick demo

Copper has earned the nickname **"Dr. Copper"** because its price has historically tracked global economic cycles so closely that economists treat it as a leading indicator. In early 2020, when COVID-19 shuttered factories worldwide, copper fell from about **$2.88 per pound to $2.10** in just two months. Then, as stimulus packages and infrastructure spending kicked in, copper surged to an all-time high of nearly **$5 per pound by May 2021**. No press release was needed — the copper market priced the global recovery before most economists had declared it.

## Full explanation

### Copper — the macro signal

Copper is used in virtually every form of construction and manufacturing: electrical wiring, plumbing, motors, and circuit boards. Because demand touches so many sectors simultaneously, the price of copper is one of the best real-time reads on whether the global economy is expanding or contracting.

Key drivers of copper:

- **Chinese demand** — China accounts for roughly 50-55% of global copper consumption. Any sign of Chinese industrial slowdown or stimulus measures moves copper immediately.
- **Supply disruptions** — The world's largest copper mines are in Chile and Peru. Labor strikes, natural disasters, or regulatory shutdowns can tighten supply rapidly.
- **Energy transition** — Electric vehicles require roughly four times more copper than internal combustion engines. Solar and wind infrastructure is also copper-intensive. Structural demand from the clean energy buildout is increasingly a long-term bullish argument for copper.
- **Inventory levels** — The London Metal Exchange (LME) publishes daily warehouse stock data. Low inventories signal tight supply and tend to support prices.

### Aluminum

Aluminum is the world's most used metal after steel. It is lightweight, corrosion-resistant, and highly recyclable — critical for aerospace, automotive, packaging, and construction. Key aluminum-specific factors include:

- **Energy costs** — Aluminum smelting is extremely energy-intensive. Rising electricity prices directly squeeze production margins and can curtail output.
- **Capacity in China** — China produces more than half the world's aluminum, and policy-driven production cuts or expansions have an outsized impact on global prices.

### Nickel

Nickel is essential for stainless steel and, increasingly, for lithium-ion battery cathodes. Its price has become tied to the electric vehicle supply chain, adding a new layer of volatility to what was historically a quiet industrial market.

### Steel (iron ore)

Steel is not traded as prominently as copper on major futures exchanges, but **iron ore** (the primary input) is a key commodity, especially as a proxy for Chinese infrastructure spending. Iron ore and Chinese housing data are closely watched together.

### How industrial metals trade

Most industrial metals trade on the **London Metal Exchange (LME)** and the **COMEX** (for copper futures, ticker HG). The LME's forward pricing structure — contracts for 3-month delivery are the benchmark — means the market is always pricing future supply and demand, not just current conditions.

### Why it matters for a trader

Industrial metal prices are among the best coincident and leading macro indicators available. A copper rally confirms global growth; a copper selloff raises a warning flag. Before entering macro trades or sector positions in industrials, materials, or emerging markets, checking the copper chart is a discipline many experienced traders treat as mandatory. Knowing whether industrial metals are leading or lagging the equity market often reveals whether a macro move has real economic substance or is purely sentiment-driven.
