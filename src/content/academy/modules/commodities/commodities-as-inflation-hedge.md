## In plain words

**Inflation** erodes the purchasing power of money — a dollar buys less over time when prices rise. **Commodities** are real, physical assets, and their prices often rise with inflation because they are inputs to the very goods that make up inflation measures. Owning a commodity (directly or through a futures-based instrument) means owning something whose price tends to increase when money loses value — which is why commodities are a classic inflation hedge. But the relationship is imperfect, and understanding when it works — and when it doesn't — is critical before treating it as a reliable portfolio tool.

## Quick demo

Between 2021 and mid-2022, the US Consumer Price Index (CPI) surged from below 2% to a peak of **9.1% year-over-year** — the highest in four decades. Over the same period, the Bloomberg Commodity Index — which tracks a basket of energy, metals, and agricultural commodities — rose approximately **65% from early 2021 to its June 2022 peak**. Investors who rotated into commodities in early 2021, when inflation was just beginning to accelerate, captured a major portion of that move as equities and bonds both lost real value.

## Full explanation

### Why commodities hedge inflation

Inflation, at its core, is the price of goods and services rising. Many of those goods are made from — or transported using — commodities: energy powers factories and logistics; metals go into buildings and machines; agricultural commodities become food. When the prices of these raw inputs rise, they flow through to the finished goods tracked by CPI and PPI. Owning those raw inputs means your asset's price rises alongside the inflation that would otherwise erode your purchasing power.

### The main categories as hedges

- **Energy (oil, natural gas)** — Energy costs are embedded in almost everything: manufacturing, transport, food production. Oil is one of the most direct inflation inputs in the modern economy. Energy has historically been the most effective commodity hedge against surprise inflation spikes.
- **Gold** — Gold's inflation-hedge reputation is more nuanced. Over very long periods (decades), gold has preserved purchasing power. But over shorter periods (years), gold's price is more driven by real interest rates than by inflation per se. Gold works best as a hedge when inflation is accompanied by low or negative real rates.
- **Agricultural commodities** — Food prices are directly in the CPI basket. Rising grain and soft commodity prices feed directly into the headline inflation numbers they are supposed to hedge.
- **Industrial metals** — Copper, aluminum, and zinc prices rise when industrial activity is strong, which often coincides with inflationary conditions.

### Instruments for commodity exposure

Direct commodity ownership is usually impractical (you cannot store barrels of oil in your account). Common instruments include:

- **Commodity futures** — Direct price exposure. Requires understanding margin, roll costs, and contract specs.
- **Commodity ETFs** — Products like DJP (Bloomberg Commodity Index), PDBC (Invesco Optimum Yield), or individual commodity ETFs (GLD for gold, USO for oil). Note that futures-based ETFs carry **roll costs** that can erode returns over time in contango markets.
- **Commodity-linked equities** — Mining stocks, oil majors, and agribusiness companies provide indirect commodity exposure with the added layer of company-specific risk and leverage.

### When the hedge fails

The inflation hedge argument has limits:

- **Rate-hiking cycles** — When central banks respond to inflation by raising interest rates aggressively, the dollar typically strengthens and real rates rise. This combination can hammer commodity prices even as inflation remains elevated — as happened in the second half of 2022 when gold fell despite still-high CPI.
- **Demand-driven vs. cost-push inflation** — Commodities hedge "cost-push" inflation (rising input costs) better than "demand-pull" inflation (rising demand for final goods). When inflation is primarily demand-driven and commodities are not in tight supply, the hedge is weaker.
- **Roll costs and tracking error** — Futures-based commodity instruments in contango markets can significantly underperform the spot price over time, reducing the practical hedge value.

### Why it matters for a trader

Commodities as inflation hedges matter both for portfolio construction and for macro trading. When inflation expectations are rising — as signaled by breakeven inflation rates in the bond market — commodity positions often become attractive from a macro thesis perspective. Understanding whether the current inflation is supply-driven (bullish for commodities) or being countered by rate hikes (bearish for commodities via the dollar) is the key analytical question. Getting that macro read right turns a commodity trade from a guess into a thesis.
