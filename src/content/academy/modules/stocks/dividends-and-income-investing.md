## In plain words

A **dividend** is a cash payment a company makes to shareholders, typically every quarter, as a share of its profits. Instead of reinvesting all earnings back into growth, these companies return capital directly to owners. **Income investing** is a strategy centered on building a portfolio of stocks that generate reliable, growing dividend payments — creating cash flow from equities without needing to sell shares.

## Quick demo

You buy 500 shares of a consumer-staples company at $60 per share ($30,000 invested). The company pays a quarterly dividend of $0.45 per share, or $1.80 annually. Your **dividend yield** is $1.80 ÷ $60 = 3%. Each year, without selling a single share, you receive $900 in cash ($1.80 × 500). If the company raises its dividend 5% annually, that payment grows to $1,148 in five years — purely from holding.

## Full explanation

### Key dividend metrics

- **Dividend yield** = annual dividend per share ÷ share price. A 4% yield on a $100 stock means $4 per year per share.
- **Payout ratio** = dividends paid ÷ net earnings. A 40% payout ratio means the company returns 40 cents of every dollar earned. Below 60–70% is generally sustainable; above 80–90% can signal risk.
- **Dividend per share (DPS)** — the actual dollar amount paid per share per period.

### The dividend calendar

Four dates matter:

1. **Declaration date** — the board announces the dividend.
2. **Ex-dividend date** — you must own the stock *before* this date to receive the payment. Shares typically drop by approximately the dividend amount on this day.
3. **Record date** — the company checks who is on the shareholder list (usually one day after the ex-date).
4. **Payment date** — cash arrives in your account.

### Dividend growers vs. high yielders

A common mistake is chasing the highest yield. An unusually high yield (e.g., 12%) often signals the market expects a dividend cut — the price has fallen because the business is deteriorating, pushing the yield up mechanically. Focus on:

- **Dividend growth rate** — companies raising their dividend 5–10% annually compound your income far better than static high yielders.
- **Dividend aristocrats** — S&P 500 companies that have raised dividends for 25+ consecutive years (examples: Coca-Cola, Johnson & Johnson, Procter & Gamble). These are benchmarks of dividend durability.

### DRIP: dividend reinvestment plans

Many brokers allow automatic **dividend reinvestment** (DRIP), where dividends are used to purchase additional fractional shares instead of paid out as cash. Over long periods, compounding through DRIP dramatically amplifies total returns.

### Tax treatment

In the US, **qualified dividends** (from domestic corporations held for a minimum period) are taxed at the lower long-term capital gains rate (0%, 15%, or 20%). **Ordinary dividends** are taxed as regular income. Foreign dividends may have withholding taxes applied at the source.

### Why this matters for a trader

Even active traders benefit from understanding dividend mechanics. Going short a stock through its ex-dividend date means you owe the dividend to whoever you borrowed shares from — a hidden cost. For long-term investors, dividend growth stocks have historically outperformed the broader market with lower volatility, making them a cornerstone of any income-focused or defensive portfolio allocation.
