## In plain words

**Market capitalization** (market cap) is simply the total dollar value of a company's outstanding shares: share price multiplied by the number of shares. It is the market's estimate of what the entire business is worth right now. Companies are grouped into **size tiers** — mega, large, mid, small, and micro-cap — because size strongly influences how a stock behaves, how liquid it is, and how much risk it carries.

## Quick demo

Apple (AAPL) has roughly 15 billion shares outstanding. At $200 per share, its market cap is $3 trillion — firmly mega-cap. A regional bank with 10 million shares at $15 has a market cap of $150 million — small-cap territory. Same stock market, completely different creatures: Apple fills institutional portfolios and trades billions of shares daily; the regional bank may trade only 50,000 shares on a quiet afternoon and move 5% on a single news headline.

## Full explanation

### The size tiers

The boundaries are approximate and shift with market levels, but the standard US classifications are:

| Tier | Typical market cap |
|---|---|
| **Mega-cap** | $200 billion + |
| **Large-cap** | $10 billion – $200 billion |
| **Mid-cap** | $2 billion – $10 billion |
| **Small-cap** | $300 million – $2 billion |
| **Micro-cap** | $50 million – $300 million |
| **Nano-cap** | Below $50 million |

### What size actually changes

**Liquidity** scales with size. Mega-caps trade hundreds of millions of shares daily; micro-caps may trade 10,000. Low liquidity means wider bid-ask spreads, more slippage, and the risk that you cannot exit quickly without moving the price against yourself.

**Volatility** is inversely related to size. A bad earnings report might move a mega-cap 5%; the same disappointment in a micro-cap can cause a 30% drop. Smaller companies have less analyst coverage, narrower revenue bases, and fewer institutional shock absorbers.

**Research coverage** drops sharply below large-cap. Thousands of analysts cover Apple; a $400 million company may have two — or zero. Less coverage means bigger information gaps and more opportunity for those willing to do the work.

**Institutional ownership** is constrained by size. A pension fund managing $50 billion cannot take a meaningful position in a micro-cap without owning half the company. This limits the buyer pool but also means institutional conviction in a small-cap is a stronger signal.

### The index connection

Major indices are size-segmented: the S&P 500 is large-cap, the S&P 400 is mid-cap, the Russell 2000 is small-cap. Passive fund flows follow index composition, so when a stock is added to or removed from an index, its price often reacts purely from forced buying or selling — regardless of business fundamentals.

### Why this matters for a trader

Your strategy must match the size of the stocks you trade. Tight scalping strategies need deep liquidity — large-caps or mega-caps. Seeking momentum from a catalyst (earnings surprise, FDA approval, contract win) often leads to small- and mid-caps where the move can be violent. Understanding size tiers also helps you read institutional behavior: a large fund accumulating a mid-cap position leaves footprints that show up in volume and price action long before any public announcement.
