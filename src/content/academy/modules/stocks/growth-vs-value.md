## In plain words

**Growth investing** means buying companies expected to grow revenues and earnings significantly faster than average — even if their stock looks expensive relative to current profits. **Value investing** means buying companies that appear cheap relative to their current fundamentals — looking for businesses trading below what they are actually worth. These two approaches have each dominated at different points in history, and most successful investors develop a clear opinion on which lens fits their temperament and time horizon.

## Quick demo

In 2020, Zoom Video grew revenue 326% year-over-year. The stock traded at a price-to-sales ratio above 80 — meaning investors paid $80 for every $1 of annual revenue. A pure value investor would never touch it. A growth investor saw a winner accelerating into a massive market and bought at $200; the stock reached $559 in October 2020. Then rates rose, growth expectations fell, and Zoom fell back below $90 by 2022. Both the growth investor who timed the entry and the value investor who avoided it could claim to have been right — just at different points in time.

## Full explanation

### Growth investing

Growth investors buy companies with superior revenue and earnings growth rates, often in expanding markets (technology, healthcare, emerging sectors). Key characteristics:

- Revenue growing 20%+ annually, ideally accelerating.
- High or expanding profit margins.
- Large addressable market (TAM) with room to expand.
- Strong competitive positioning (moat).
- Willing to pay premium multiples: high P/E, high P/S, even negative current earnings.

The core belief: if a company can sustain 25% annual earnings growth for a decade, paying 30× earnings today is cheap compared to what those earnings will look like in 10 years.

**Risk**: valuation is doing a lot of work. When growth slows — or when interest rates rise, increasing the discount rate applied to future earnings — premium-valued growth stocks can fall 50–80% even if the business remains healthy.

### Value investing

Pioneered by Benjamin Graham and popularized by Warren Buffett, value investing looks for a **margin of safety**: buying a dollar's worth of value for seventy cents. Key characteristics:

- Low P/E, P/B, or EV/EBITDA relative to the market or historical average.
- Strong balance sheet (low debt, high cash).
- Consistent free cash flow generation.
- Often out-of-favor industries or temporarily depressed earnings.

The core belief: Mr. Market is often irrational. When a business is temporarily misunderstood, patient investors who understand intrinsic value can buy cheap and profit when price eventually catches up to reality.

**Risk**: value traps. Some stocks are cheap for a permanent reason — declining business, structural disruption, management failure. The discipline is distinguishing a temporarily cheap stock from one that deserves to be cheap.

### GARP: growth at a reasonable price

Many practitioners blend both: seeking companies with strong growth but at a price that does not require perfection. The PEG ratio (P/E ÷ earnings growth rate) is the canonical GARP metric — a PEG below 1.0 suggests a company is not being fully valued for its growth.

### Cyclical behavior

Growth tends to outperform during low-interest-rate, expanding-economy environments. Value tends to outperform during rate-rising cycles and economic recoveries. The 2010s were dominated by growth; value had a strong comeback in 2021–2022. Neither style wins all the time.

### Why this matters for a trader

Your style determines everything: which screeners you build, which multiples you care about, how you react to earnings beats, and how long you hold. A growth trader and a value investor looking at the same stock see completely different things. Knowing which lens you are using — and applying it consistently — is far more important than which approach is theoretically superior.
