## In plain words

**Float** is the number of shares actually available for public trading — the total shares outstanding minus those held by insiders, locked-up investors, and the company itself. **Short interest** is the number of shares currently sold short (borrowed and sold, with a promise to buy them back later). When short interest is high relative to float and buyers suddenly rush in, the shorts are forced to buy to cover their losses, amplifying the upward move into what is called a **short squeeze**.

## Quick demo

GameStop (GME) in January 2021 had about 50 million shares of float and roughly 70 million shares sold short — meaning more shares were shorted than even existed in the float. When retail traders coordinated buying, the price shot from $20 to nearly $500 in days. Short sellers had to buy shares at any price to close their positions, which pushed the price higher, which forced more short sellers to buy, which pushed it higher still. The squeeze happened precisely because the float was small and short interest was extreme.

## Full explanation

### Float explained

- **Total shares outstanding** = every share the company has ever issued.
- **Restricted shares** = insider holdings, pre-IPO investors, and shares subject to lock-up agreements; these cannot be freely traded.
- **Float** = shares outstanding minus restricted shares. This is the actual supply available on the open market.

A company with 100 million shares outstanding but 80 million held by founders and institutions has a float of only 20 million. On a 1-million-share trading day, that float turns over in 20 days — a relatively illiquid stock that can move sharply on any surge in demand.

### Why low float drives volatility

Supply and demand in a thin float is extreme. If 5 million shares trade in a day on a 10-million-share float, price discovery is rapid — the market needs to find a price that clears half the entire available supply. News, earnings beats, or viral social media attention can trigger moves of 20–50% intraday in low-float names.

### Short interest

Short interest is reported twice monthly by exchanges. Key metrics:

- **Short interest ratio (days to cover)** = shares short ÷ average daily volume. A ratio of 10 means it would take 10 days of average volume for all shorts to buy back their shares. Higher = more squeeze potential.
- **Short interest as % of float** — a figure above 20–30% is considered elevated; above 50% is extreme.

### The mechanics of a short squeeze

1. A stock rises, hurting short sellers (who profit only when price falls).
2. Rising prices trigger stop-losses or margin calls on short positions.
3. Shorts must buy shares to close — this buying drives the price higher.
4. More shorts hit their stops, creating a feedback loop.
5. The squeeze ends when most short sellers have covered or new supply (insiders selling, secondary offerings) overwhelms demand.

### How to find float and short data

Most financial data platforms provide float and short interest. Days-to-cover is one of the most useful single numbers: anything above 5–7 in a stock with a narrative catalyst is worth watching for squeeze potential.

### Why this matters for a trader

Float and short interest are momentum multipliers. A catalyst (earnings beat, FDA approval, acquisition rumor) that hits a high-short-interest, low-float stock can produce moves that seem irrational but are mathematically inevitable given the positioning. Understanding this dynamic lets you read volume spikes and price action with much greater accuracy — and avoid being the short seller caught on the wrong side of a squeeze.
