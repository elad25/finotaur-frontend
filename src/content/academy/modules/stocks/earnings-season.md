## In plain words

Every three months, publicly traded companies are required to report their financial results to shareholders. This period — **earnings season** — is when companies disclose revenue, profit, and guidance for the next quarter. The market reacts to how actual results compare to **analyst expectations**, not to the raw numbers themselves. A company can post record profits and still fall if traders expected even more.

## Quick demo

Netflix reported Q4 2022 earnings: revenue of $7.85 billion, slightly above the $7.82 billion consensus estimate. But the company added 7.66 million new subscribers — far above the 4.57 million expected. The stock surged 8% after-hours on that single metric, not because revenue was remarkable but because subscriber growth crushed the consensus. The entire move was about the gap between expectation and reality — what traders call the **earnings surprise**.

## Full explanation

### The earnings calendar

Earnings season runs approximately:
- **Q1 results**: mid-April through mid-May
- **Q2 results**: mid-July through mid-August
- **Q3 results**: mid-October through mid-November
- **Q4 results**: late January through mid-February

Most companies report either before market open (BMO) or after market close (AMC). Pre-market reports react in pre-market trading; AMC reports play out in after-hours and then the regular session open.

### What the report contains

- **Revenue** (top line) — total sales for the quarter.
- **Earnings per share (EPS)** — net profit divided by shares outstanding, the single most-watched number.
- **Operating income and margins** — a read on business efficiency.
- **Guidance** — management's forecast for the next quarter or full year. Often more market-moving than the historical results.
- **Non-GAAP adjustments** — many companies strip out stock-based compensation, restructuring charges, etc. to report "adjusted" EPS. Analysts often forecast on this basis.

### Beat, miss, and in-line

- **Beat**: actual result exceeds analyst consensus → typically a positive reaction.
- **Miss**: actual result falls short of consensus → typically a negative reaction.
- **In-line**: meets consensus → often a muted or negative reaction because the stock had already priced in the expected result.

### The "sell the news" dynamic

When a stock runs up strongly into earnings on positive expectations, it is already pricing in a beat. If the company merely meets — not exceeds — expectations, longs take profit and the stock falls even on good results. This is the most common beginner trap: buying a stock before earnings because "the numbers will be great," only to watch it drop 5% on a solid beat.

### Implied move and options

Options traders use the earnings event to price **implied volatility (IV)**, which reflects how much the market expects the stock to move. A stock with 4% implied move may move only 2% — IV collapses after the event (the "IV crush"), often destroying the value of options bought before earnings.

### Why this matters for a trader

Earnings season is the single highest-impact recurring event in stock trading. It creates the largest single-day moves in individual names, sets new technical levels that hold for months, and redefines narratives. Whether you trade through earnings or deliberately avoid them, understanding the mechanics — consensus, surprise, guidance, IV crush — is essential for managing risk and identifying post-earnings setups.
