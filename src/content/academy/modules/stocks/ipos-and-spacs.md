## In plain words

An **IPO** (Initial Public Offering) is the first time a private company sells shares to the public on a stock exchange. Before the IPO, only founders, employees, and private investors can own stock. After it, anyone can buy. A **SPAC** (Special Purpose Acquisition Company) is an alternative path to the public markets: a blank-check shell company raises money through its own IPO first, then uses that cash to acquire a private company — effectively taking it public without the full IPO process.

## Quick demo

In November 2021, Rivian — an electric truck manufacturer — went public via traditional IPO, selling shares at $78. On its first day of trading it surged to $100, briefly valuing the company at over $100 billion despite having virtually no revenue. Within a year, the stock had fallen below $20. The IPO was wildly oversubscribed and priced at a level that assumed years of perfect execution. Most retail investors who bought on day one experienced severe losses. The lesson: "going public" does not mean "going up."

## Full explanation

### The IPO process

1. **Selection of underwriters** — the company hires investment banks (Goldman, Morgan Stanley, JPMorgan) to manage the offering.
2. **S-1 filing** — a detailed prospectus is filed with the SEC, disclosing financials, risks, and business model. Reading the S-1 is the most important due-diligence step for any IPO investment.
3. **Roadshow** — company executives present to institutional investors to gauge demand and set a price range.
4. **Pricing** — the underwriters set the final IPO price the night before trading begins, based on orders from institutional investors.
5. **First day of trading** — shares open on the exchange. The "pop" (if any) goes to institutional investors who received shares at the IPO price, not to retail investors buying on the open.
6. **Lock-up expiry** — insiders and pre-IPO investors are typically prohibited from selling for 90–180 days. When the lock-up expires, selling pressure often intensifies.

### Why IPOs are often treacherous for retail

- Institutional investors get the IPO allocation at the offering price; retail buys on the secondary market after any pop has already occurred.
- IPO euphoria drives extreme first-day valuations that the business may take years — or never — to justify.
- The company's financial history in the S-1 may be short, making fair valuation very difficult.
- Management and VC firms have strong incentives to time the IPO at peak valuations.

### SPACs: the alternative route

A SPAC raises capital through its own IPO with no defined target — investors essentially back a management team's ability to find and acquire a good private business. Key mechanics:

- SPAC shares typically price at $10 with embedded warrants.
- Investors can redeem at $10 if they dislike the proposed merger (the "de-SPAC").
- The target company avoids much of the traditional IPO scrutiny and regulatory process.
- SPAC deals surged in 2020–2021; the majority of SPAC mergers from that era underperformed significantly in the years that followed.

### Direct listings and other alternatives

Some companies skip the traditional IPO entirely by listing existing shares directly on an exchange (Spotify, Palantir, Coinbase used this approach). No new shares are issued and no underwriter takes fees — but also no guaranteed institutional buyers.

### Why this matters for a trader

IPOs and SPACs generate intense media coverage and first-day volatility. Understanding the structure explains why you should rarely buy at the open on IPO day (institutional sellers are taking profits), why the lock-up expiration date belongs on your calendar, and why SPAC enthusiasm rarely produces lasting value for public shareholders. The most reliable IPO trade for most investors is patience: wait 3–6 months for lock-up expiry, price discovery, and the first couple of earnings reports before forming a real view.
