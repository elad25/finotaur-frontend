## In plain words

**Government bonds** are issued by sovereign nations and are generally treated as the safest bonds available in that currency — the benchmark against which everything else is priced. **Corporate bonds** are issued by companies and pay a higher yield to compensate for the extra risk that a business, unlike a government with a printing press, can run out of money. The choice between them is always a trade-off between **safety and yield**.

## Quick demo

In a stable environment, a 10-year government bond might yield 4.5% while a comparable investment-grade corporate bond from a blue-chip company yields 5.8%. That 1.3% spread — the **credit spread** — is your reward for lending to a company instead of a government. In a recession, investors rush into government bonds ("flight to safety"), their prices spike, their yields fall, and the corporate spread widens dramatically — sometimes to 3% or more — as corporate default risk rises.

## Full explanation

### Government bonds — the risk-free benchmark

Major government bond markets include:

- **US Treasuries**: T-bills (under 1 year), T-notes (2–10 years), T-bonds (30 years). Backed by the full faith and credit of the US government. The global risk-free rate reference.
- **UK Gilts**, **German Bunds**, **Japanese JGBs**: equivalent sovereign debt in their respective markets.
- **Emerging market sovereigns**: government bonds issued by developing countries, often in US dollars. These carry significant credit and currency risk — far from risk-free.

Government bond yields set the **risk-free rate** used in virtually every financial calculation, from stock valuations (discounted cash flows) to option pricing models.

### Corporate bonds — credit risk in exchange for yield

Corporate bonds range from **investment grade** (solid companies with healthy balance sheets) to **high yield** (highly leveraged companies or those in cyclical industries). Key categories:

- **Senior secured**: first claim on specific assets in a default. Highest recovery rate.
- **Senior unsecured**: general claim on assets, behind secured debt. Standard corporate bond type.
- **Subordinated / junior**: paid after senior creditors. Higher yield, lower recovery.

The **indenture** — the legal contract of a bond — specifies covenants that restrict certain borrower behaviors (taking on more debt, paying excessive dividends), protecting bondholders.

### Comparing the two

| Feature | Government bonds | Corporate bonds |
|---|---|---|
| Default risk | Near zero (major economies) | Credit-dependent |
| Yield | Lower (risk-free baseline) | Higher (credit spread added) |
| Liquidity | Extremely high | High to moderate |
| Covenants | None | Yes — protective clauses |
| Sensitivity to economy | Mainly rate-driven | Rate-driven + credit-driven |

### The credit spread and what moves it

The credit spread (yield of corporate bond minus yield of same-maturity Treasury) reflects:

- **Credit risk**: probability of default × expected loss given default.
- **Liquidity premium**: compensation for the fact that corporate bonds are less liquid.
- **Business cycle**: spreads compress in expansions, widen sharply in recessions.

### Why this matters for a trader or investor

The interplay between government and corporate bonds is a macro signal. Widening credit spreads often precede equity market stress; tight spreads signal risk appetite. A bond investor must constantly decide: is the extra yield worth the extra risk, especially when that risk spikes precisely when portfolios are under pressure? Diversifying across both categories — with government bonds acting as a stabilizer — is a cornerstone of traditional portfolio construction.
