## In plain words

A bond is a loan you give to a government or company, and the price you pay for it on the open market moves **opposite** to its **yield** (the interest rate it effectively pays). When rates rise, existing bonds are worth less; when rates fall, they're worth more. That inverse relationship is the single most important mechanical fact about fixed income.

## Quick demo

Imagine you hold a bond that pays $50 a year on a $1,000 face value — a 5% coupon. Then new bonds start offering 6%. Nobody will pay you $1,000 for yours when they can get $60 a year elsewhere. To compete, your bond's price has to drop to roughly $833, because $50 ÷ $833 ≈ 6%. Price fell; yield rose. Same logic works in reverse when rates drop.

## Full explanation

### What determines a bond's price

A bond has a **face value** (the amount repaid at maturity, typically $1,000), a **coupon rate** (the fixed annual interest payment expressed as a percentage of face value), and a **maturity date**. In the primary market you buy at or near face value. In the secondary market, the price floats constantly.

The price is simply the **present value of all future cash flows** — the coupon payments plus the final face-value repayment — discounted at the current market interest rate:

- **Higher discount rate → lower present value → lower bond price**
- **Lower discount rate → higher present value → higher bond price**

### Why the inverse relationship exists

Think of it this way: the coupon is fixed in dollar terms. If you paid $950 for a $1,000 bond paying $50 a year, your effective yield is higher than 5% because you're getting $50 plus a $50 gain at maturity. The market reprices bonds every day so that every bond of similar credit quality and maturity offers roughly the same yield, regardless of its original coupon.

### Par, premium, and discount

- **Par**: the bond trades at face value — coupon equals market yield.
- **Premium**: the bond trades above face value — coupon is higher than current market yield (investors pay up for the above-market income).
- **Discount**: the bond trades below face value — coupon is below current market yield.

### Clean price vs. dirty price

Bond prices are often quoted as **clean prices** — excluding accrued interest that has built up since the last coupon payment. What the buyer actually pays is the **dirty price** (clean price + accrued interest). Knowing this distinction matters when you see a quote on a platform that differs from what you actually pay at settlement.

### Why this matters for a trader or investor

The bond market is far larger than the stock market, and its pricing mechanism ripples through everything else. When central banks raise rates, bond prices fall — which raises the yield investors demand for *all* assets, pressuring stock valuations. Understanding bond pricing means you can read a rate move and instantly know whether it will lift or weigh on the portfolios around you.
