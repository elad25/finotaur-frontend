## In plain words

The **yield curve** is a graph that plots the interest rates (yields) of bonds with identical credit quality — typically government bonds — across different maturities, from short-term (3 months) to long-term (30 years). Its shape tells you what the bond market collectively expects about **economic growth, inflation, and central bank policy** in the years ahead. An **inverted yield curve**, where short-term yields exceed long-term ones, has preceded most major recessions in modern economic history.

## Quick demo

In a normal environment, a 3-month Treasury yields 4.0%, a 2-year yields 4.4%, and a 10-year yields 4.8% — investors demand more for tying up money longer. In 2022–2023, the curve inverted: the 2-year yielded 5.0% while the 10-year yielded only 3.9%. The market was saying: "rates are high right now because the Fed is fighting inflation, but we expect the economy to slow, rates to fall, and long-term growth to be weaker." Equity markets paid attention.

## Full explanation

### The three classic shapes

- **Normal (upward-sloping)**: long-term yields are higher than short-term yields. This reflects the expectation of continued growth, modest inflation, and compensation for the uncertainty of lending money further into the future.
- **Flat**: short- and long-term yields converge. Often a transition state — can signal that the economy is slowing or that rate cuts are expected.
- **Inverted (downward-sloping)**: short-term yields exceed long-term yields. Historically a powerful recession indicator. Represents market expectations of future rate cuts as the economy weakens.

### Key yield spreads traders watch

- **2s10s spread**: the 10-year yield minus the 2-year yield. The most widely cited measure. When this goes negative, the yield curve has inverted.
- **3-month / 10-year spread**: also tracked by the Federal Reserve's own research as a recession predictor.

### What drives the curve's shape

- **Short end**: dominated by central bank policy. The overnight rate set by the central bank anchors the front end. Market expectations for future rate moves ripple out to 2-year yields.
- **Long end**: driven by longer-term growth and inflation expectations, plus the **term premium** — the extra yield investors demand for holding a bond for decades rather than rolling short-term bills.

### Steepening vs. flattening

- **Bear steepening**: long rates rise faster than short rates — often signals inflation fears or supply concerns at the long end.
- **Bull steepening**: short rates fall faster — often occurs after a rate-cutting cycle begins.
- **Bear flattening**: short rates rise faster — typical when a central bank is hiking aggressively.
- **Bull flattening**: long rates fall faster — flight-to-safety demand drives long yields down.

### The curve as a market signal

The yield curve's predictive record is imperfect but notable. In the US, an inverted 2s10s spread has preceded every recession since the 1970s, typically with a 6–18 month lag. However, the length of that lag means the inversion is a warning, not a trigger — stocks have continued to rally for months after an inversion before eventually rolling over.

### Why this matters for a trader or investor

The yield curve is a live read on the bond market's collective economic forecast. For equity traders, it shapes sector rotation: a steepening curve typically benefits banks (wider net interest margins); a flattening curve pressures them. For bond investors, the curve's shape informs duration management — where to be on the curve to capture the best risk-adjusted yield. For macro traders, the curve is one of the most reliable single indicators of where the economic cycle stands.
