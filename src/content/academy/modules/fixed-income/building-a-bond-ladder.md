## In plain words

A **bond ladder** is a portfolio of bonds with staggered maturity dates — for example, one bond maturing each year over the next five to ten years. As each rung of the ladder matures, you reinvest the proceeds in a new bond at the long end. This structure manages two of the biggest bond risks simultaneously: it smooths out **interest-rate risk** (you're never fully committed to today's rate) and it controls **reinvestment risk** (you're never reinvesting everything at once).

## Quick demo

You invest $50,000 by buying five $10,000 bonds maturing in 1, 2, 3, 4, and 5 years. At the end of year 1, the first bond matures and you reinvest $10,000 in a new 5-year bond — now the ladder again spans 5 years. If rates have risen, you lock in the higher rate. If rates have fallen, only one-fifth of your portfolio is affected. Each year you collect a predictable stream of income and principal, regardless of what the market does.

## Full explanation

### Why laddering works

A bond ladder is a practical response to the two-edged sword of interest-rate uncertainty:

- **If you buy only short-term bonds**, you earn lower yields, and all your capital rolls over at the new rate when rates change — heavy reinvestment risk.
- **If you buy only long-term bonds**, you capture higher yields, but all your capital is exposed to price swings if rates rise — heavy duration risk.
- **A ladder splits the difference**: each maturity bucket absorbs rate changes incrementally, with never more than one rung maturing in any given period.

### Building a bond ladder

**Step 1 — Define the time horizon and rung spacing.** Common ladders span 5, 10, or even 30 years, with rungs spaced annually. Tighter spacing (6-month rungs) provides more liquidity; wider spacing (2-year rungs) reduces transaction costs.

**Step 2 — Choose bond types.** A ladder can be built with:
- **Treasuries**: maximum safety, deep liquidity, lower yield.
- **CDs (certificates of deposit)**: FDIC-insured in the US, often yielding slightly more than Treasuries.
- **Investment-grade corporate bonds**: higher yield, adding credit risk.
- **Municipal bonds**: tax-advantaged for investors in higher brackets.
- **Mixed**: combining types for diversification by credit and sector.

**Step 3 — Allocate equal amounts to each rung.** A classic ladder invests equal dollars per maturity. Alternatively, you can weight rungs by cash-flow needs — heavier near-term rungs if you anticipate expenses.

**Step 4 — Roll maturing rungs.** When a bond matures, reinvest proceeds at the far end of the ladder to maintain the structure. This is the ongoing management step that keeps the ladder in shape.

### Benefits of a bond ladder

- **Predictable income**: regular coupon payments and principal returns create a cash-flow schedule you can plan around.
- **Flexibility**: maturing bonds provide periodic liquidity without selling at market prices.
- **Rate-environment adaptability**: the rolling reinvestment naturally captures higher rates in rising environments and maintains existing rates in falling ones.
- **Reduced decision pressure**: the systematic nature removes the temptation to time the market.

### Limitations

- **Transaction costs**: building a ladder of individual bonds involves spreads and potentially commissions at each purchase.
- **Minimum investment**: individual bonds often require $10,000+ per position; small investors may find bond ETFs or defined-maturity ETFs (which simulate ladder rungs) more practical.
- **Opportunity cost**: in a steeply falling-rate environment, a locked ladder earns above-market rates — but in a steeply rising environment, only one rung per year is available to capture the new rates.

### Why this matters for a trader or investor

The bond ladder is one of the most time-tested structures in personal and institutional fixed-income management. For investors generating income from their portfolios — retirees, endowments, or anyone managing a cash-flow-driven portfolio — it transforms the complexity of interest-rate management into a simple mechanical system. Even active traders benefit from understanding the ladder concept as it informs how institutions allocate across the yield curve, which in turn drives yield curve dynamics and spread movements.
