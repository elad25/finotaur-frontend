## In plain words

A **credit rating** is an independent assessment of how likely a bond issuer is to make its interest payments and repay the principal on time. Ratings run from **AAA** (virtually no default risk) down through **investment grade**, then into **high yield** (also called **junk bonds**), and eventually to **default**. The lower the rating, the higher the yield the market demands to compensate for the extra risk.

## Quick demo

Suppose two 10-year bonds both pay their coupons annually. A US Treasury (effectively AAA) yields 4.5%. A BB-rated corporation in the same environment might yield 7%. That extra 2.5% — called the **credit spread** — is the market's price for the risk that the corporation might struggle to pay. In a recession, that spread could widen to 5% or more as investors flee to safety, hammering the corporate bond's price even if Treasury yields stay flat.

## Full explanation

### The major rating agencies

Three agencies dominate global credit assessment:

- **Moody's**: uses Aaa, Aa, A, Baa, Ba, B, Caa, Ca, C
- **Standard & Poor's (S&P)** and **Fitch**: use AAA, AA, A, BBB, BB, B, CCC, CC, C, D

Modifiers (+ and − for S&P/Fitch, or 1/2/3 for Moody's) fine-tune positions within each grade. Despite the notation difference, the scales are broadly comparable.

### Investment grade vs. high yield

The dividing line sits between **BBB−/Baa3** (lowest investment grade) and **BB+/Ba1** (highest high yield):

- **Investment grade** (AAA to BBB−): lower default probability. Required for most pension funds, insurance companies, and index ETFs with investment-grade mandates.
- **High yield** (BB+ and below): higher default probability, higher coupons, more equity-like price behavior. Sometimes called "junk bonds," though many high-yield issuers are profitable companies with substantial leverage.

### What ratings measure

Agencies examine:

- **Financial metrics**: leverage ratios, interest coverage, free cash flow, liquidity.
- **Business profile**: industry position, revenue stability, competitive moat.
- **Macro sensitivity**: how the issuer would perform in a downturn.
- **Structural factors**: whether your bond is senior, subordinated, secured, or unsecured — which determines recovery in a default.

### Rating changes and their effects

A **downgrade** — especially one that crosses from investment grade to high yield ("fallen angel") — can trigger forced selling by funds that are mandated to hold only investment-grade bonds, causing a sharp price drop. An **upgrade** has the opposite effect. Watching credit-rating actions is a legitimate market catalyst.

### Limitations of ratings

Ratings are opinions, not guarantees. They are inherently backward-looking, relying heavily on reported financials. The 2008 financial crisis exposed how wrong AAA ratings on structured products can be when the underlying assumptions fail. Market participants increasingly use **credit default swap (CDS) spreads** as a real-time, forward-looking alternative to agency ratings — CDS widening can signal credit stress days or weeks before an official downgrade.

### Why this matters for a trader or investor

Credit spreads move with the economic cycle and risk sentiment. When spreads widen (credit stress), it is often an early warning signal for equity markets. Traders watch the **high-yield spread index** (like the ICE BofA US High Yield Index OAS) as a barometer of systemic risk. For bond investors, rating discipline — staying within a defined rating band — is the most common form of credit-risk management.
