## In plain words

Bonds are often called "safe" assets, but they carry several distinct risks that can cause real losses. The four main ones are **interest-rate risk** (prices fall when rates rise), **credit risk** (the issuer might not pay), **inflation risk** (your fixed payments lose purchasing power), and **liquidity risk** (you might not be able to sell at a fair price). Understanding each helps you choose the right bond for the right purpose — and avoid the painful surprises that come from treating "bond" as synonymous with "safe."

## Quick demo

In 2022, a holder of long-term US Treasury bonds — supposedly the safest asset on earth — lost more than 30% of their investment in under a year as the Fed raised rates sharply. No default occurred; no credit event happened. Pure **interest-rate risk**, magnified by the bonds' long duration, caused losses larger than many equity bear markets. The lesson: every type of risk is real, even in government bonds.

## Full explanation

### Interest-rate risk (duration risk)

The most pervasive bond risk. When market interest rates rise, existing bonds with fixed coupons become less attractive — their prices must fall until their yields match the new rate environment. Longer-maturity bonds are far more exposed than short-term ones (see the Duration & Convexity chapter). This risk is unavoidable for any fixed-income holder; it can only be managed through duration selection.

### Credit risk (default risk)

The risk that the issuer fails to make a scheduled coupon payment or fails to return principal at maturity. For highly rated government bonds in major economies, this risk is minimal. For corporate and high-yield bonds, it is material and grows significantly during economic downturns. Credit risk is managed through diversification, rating discipline, and credit analysis.

### Inflation risk

Bonds pay fixed nominal amounts. If inflation is 4% and your bond yields 3%, you are earning a **negative real return** — your purchasing power is declining even as your account balance grows in nominal terms. This was the hidden catastrophe of the 1970s for bond investors. **Treasury Inflation-Protected Securities (TIPS)** and similar inflation-linked bonds in other markets explicitly address this risk by adjusting principal with inflation.

### Reinvestment risk

As discussed in the YTM chapter, the yield-to-maturity calculation assumes all coupon payments are reinvested at the same rate. In practice, rates fluctuate. If rates fall, you reinvest at lower yields, and your actual total return falls below the promised YTM. This risk is greater for high-coupon bonds and longer maturities, and it is particularly relevant in falling-rate environments.

### Call risk (prepayment risk)

Many corporate and municipal bonds are **callable** — the issuer can repay them early, typically after a few years. Issuers call bonds when rates fall, because they can refinance at lower cost. But this is precisely when you most want to keep earning those above-market coupons. Called bonds leave you holding cash at the worst possible moment to reinvest. **Yield to worst** (YTW) accounts for this by calculating yield under the most disadvantageous call scenario.

### Liquidity risk

Not all bonds trade freely. Government bonds of major economies are extremely liquid. Many corporate bonds — especially smaller issues — trade infrequently, with wide bid-ask spreads. In a market panic, even investment-grade corporate bond liquidity can evaporate. Being forced to sell an illiquid bond in a stress scenario can mean accepting a price well below fair value.

### Currency risk

When you hold a bond denominated in a foreign currency, its return in your home currency depends not just on the bond's yield but on exchange-rate movements. A 6% bond can produce a negative return if the issuing currency depreciates 8% against yours.

### Why this matters for a trader or investor

A comprehensive bond analysis requires assessing all these risks simultaneously, not just yield. A bond offering 2% more yield than a comparable government bond may be offering that premium because of credit risk, liquidity risk, or call risk — and the premium may not adequately compensate. The best bond investors are risk-type-aware: they know which risks they are taking, how they interact with the rest of the portfolio, and whether the compensation is fair.
