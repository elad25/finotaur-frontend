## In plain words

**Absorption** happens when a large participant on one side of the market quietly soaks up all the aggressive orders coming from the other side — without letting price move through. **Exhaustion** is the moment when one side runs out of ammunition: they've been hitting bids or lifting offers for so long that there are no participants left willing to continue. Both are signals of an impending reversal, but they look different on the tape.

## Quick demo

NQ futures are falling hard. The tape shows 400, 600, 500, 350 contracts hitting the bid every second. Yet price refuses to drop more than two ticks. For 90 seconds, nearly 15,000 contracts trade and price is glued in place. That is absorption: a massive buyer is resting limit orders and taking the other side of every seller. When the selling finally slows, price explodes upward 30 points in under a minute — the absorbed pressure becomes the fuel for the reversal.

## Full explanation

### What absorption looks like

Absorption is most visible when you combine three tools:
1. **Footprint charts** — large bid volume trading at a level without price falling through reveals buyers absorbing sellers.
2. **Delta** — a large negative delta (sellers aggressive) on a candle that ends flat or slightly higher is the mathematical fingerprint of absorption.
3. **Tape** — heavy, consistent selling prints at the same level without a tick-down.

On the footprint, absorption at a low looks like this: multiple bars with high selling volume (ask side thin, bid side heavy) but no progress lower. The bar closes flat or higher despite the selling.

### Absorption at support vs. resistance

- **Absorption at support** (buyers absorbing selling pressure): sellers are persistent but can't push price through. A large patient buyer is on the other side. When selling exhausts, the trapped sellers become forced buyers on the way up — amplifying the reversal.
- **Absorption at resistance** (sellers absorbing buying pressure): a large resting seller soaks up every attempt to break through. When buyers eventually give up, the trapped buyers become forced sellers — adding fuel to a drop.

### What exhaustion looks like

Exhaustion is different from absorption. In exhaustion, there is no large patient participant on the other side. Instead, the aggressive side simply runs out of orders:

- **Volume dries up** — after a sustained move, the number of contracts per candle shrinks significantly. The tape slows.
- **Delta flips without much trading** — a trend candle prints a small, opposite delta even on low volume, showing the directional flow has ceased.
- **Widening spread** — in less liquid instruments, the spread widens when participants step back, another sign of exhaustion.

Exhaustion often looks like a series of smaller and smaller candles at the extreme of a move — "staircase" slowing. Price isn't necessarily held by a big participant; it's simply that no one is left willing to chase.

### Absorption vs. exhaustion: the key difference

| | Absorption | Exhaustion |
|---|---|---|
| Volume at the level | High — lots of trades printing | Low — tape goes quiet |
| Price movement | Minimal despite high activity | Minimal because activity dried up |
| Who causes it | A large passive participant soaking up aggression | The aggressive side simply runs out |
| Signal quality | High — a real participant is defending | Medium — can be a pause, not a reversal |

In practice, the most powerful setups combine both: exhaustion on the attacking side + absorption from a large defender = high-probability reversal.

### Common mistakes

- **Confusing slow tape with absorption**: a slow tape at a level might just be thin participation, not a large defender. Require visible volume for absorption to be credible.
- **Trading absorption too early**: absorption can last longer than expected. Wait for the tape to slow and delta to confirm before entering.

### Why it matters for a trader

Absorption and exhaustion are the two ways a trend ends at a specific price. Understanding the difference tells you whether to expect a sharp reversal (absorption: the spring is coiled) or a grinding chop-and-fade (exhaustion: the energy simply dissipates). Getting this right before the price chart shows any confirmation is the edge that separates tape readers from ordinary chart traders.
