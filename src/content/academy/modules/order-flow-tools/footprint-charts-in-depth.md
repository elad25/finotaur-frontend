## In plain words

A **footprint chart** breaks each price candle open and shows you exactly how many contracts traded at the **bid** versus the **ask** at every single price level inside that bar. Instead of seeing a candle close at a price, you see the full battle — how many buyers hit offers and how many sellers hit bids, row by row. This makes **imbalances** and **absorption** visible in a way that no standard chart can show.

## Quick demo

A 5-minute ES candle closes at 5,415. A plain candlestick tells you almost nothing about what happened inside. The footprint shows that at 5,410, 2,300 contracts traded on the ask (buyers) versus only 180 on the bid (sellers) — a massive bid imbalance. At 5,414, the numbers flip: 90 on the ask, 1,800 on the bid — sellers stepped in. That internal structure explains why price stalled at 5,414 and didn't push higher: sellers absorbed the run-up.

## Full explanation

### Structure of a footprint bar

Each footprint bar is a vertical column of price levels. At every level, two numbers are shown side by side:

- **Left number** — volume traded at the **bid** (sellers initiating, hitting the bids).
- **Right number** — volume traded at the **ask** (buyers initiating, lifting the offers).

Some platforms display just one number per cell (delta = ask volume − bid volume) or show percentages. The most common format is **bid × ask** at each price row.

### Types of footprint charts

- **Volume footprint** — shows raw traded volume on each side at each price. The most widely used.
- **Delta footprint** — shows net delta (ask volume minus bid volume) per price level. Positive = net buying; negative = net selling.
- **Bid/ask imbalance** — highlights cells where one side dominates the other by a chosen ratio (e.g., 3:1 or 4:1). Imbalances are often color-coded and indicate potential future price magnets or continuation zones.

### Reading key patterns

**Bid imbalance (bullish)**
When ask volume dramatically exceeds bid volume at a price level — e.g., 2,000 on the ask, 300 on the bid — aggressive buyers overwhelmed sellers there. These cells often act as support on a retest; price may skip through them quickly on a pullback because sellers don't re-appear.

**Ask imbalance (bearish)**
The opposite: far more selling than buying at a level. These cells act as resistance.

**Absorption**
A large amount of volume trades at a level but price does not move through it. On the footprint, you see big numbers on both sides with the bar stalling. A large participant is on the other side, soaking up all the incoming aggression — the market is absorbing the pressure rather than moving.

**Unfinished auction**
When a candle's high or low prints with zero volume on one side at the extreme (no trades printed on the ask at the very high, or no trades on the bid at the very low), the auction is considered incomplete. Markets tend to return to finish the auction.

**Stacked imbalances**
Two or more consecutive levels with the same directional imbalance suggest strong, sustained one-sided aggression — a high-conviction move rather than a random spike.

### Combining footprint with other tools

Footprint is most powerful when combined with:
- **Volume Profile** — to see whether the footprint imbalances align with high-volume or low-volume nodes.
- **Delta divergence** — when price makes a new high but delta is declining (fewer buyers at each successive level), momentum is weakening. This is a leading indicator of reversal, visible in the footprint before the price chart shows it.

### Why it matters for a trader

Standard charts show you *what* price did. Footprint charts show you *how hard* the market fought to get there and *where* the real participants committed. A breakout candle with stacked ask imbalances at every level is a very different beast from a breakout candle with scattered small prints. The footprint is the microscope of order flow — it gives you resolution that no other tool provides.
