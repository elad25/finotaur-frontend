## In plain words

A **scaling plan** is a firm's structured mechanism for growing a funded trader's account size over time. Rather than staying at the original funded amount forever, traders who demonstrate consistent profitability can qualify for larger accounts — sometimes doubling or more — without paying new evaluation fees. Scaling rewards discipline and consistency; it is the prop firm's way of deploying more capital toward traders who have proven they can manage it.

## Quick demo

You start with a $50,000 funded account. The firm's scaling plan says: earn 10% profit over three months while staying within all rules, and your account grows to $75,000. Reach 10% on the $75,000 account in another three months, and it scales to $100,000. Each step keeps the same profit split — 80/20 — but your 80% share now applies to a larger base, so the same percentage return generates more absolute dollars.

## Full explanation

### Why scaling plans exist

Scaling plans serve both sides of the relationship:

- **For the firm** — rather than funding all traders at maximum size from the start, the firm allocates more capital incrementally to traders who have demonstrated they can manage it. This reduces the firm's aggregate risk exposure.
- **For the trader** — a realistic path to trading larger size without paying for a new, larger-account evaluation. Earning $1,000 from a $50,000 account feels different from earning $2,000 from a $100,000 account, but the latter is accessible through consistent performance rather than a bigger upfront fee.

### How scaling triggers typically work

Conditions for a scaling upgrade vary by firm but commonly include a combination of:

- **Profit threshold** — reaching a set percentage gain (e.g., 10% or 15%) on the current account.
- **Time period** — sustaining that performance over a minimum number of months (typically 2–3) rather than achieving it in a single lucky week.
- **Rule compliance** — no rule breaches during the qualifying period. Some firms invalidate a scaling cycle if any daily loss limit or drawdown limit is touched, even if not breached.
- **Payout behavior** — some plans require that the trader has already made at least one or two successful withdrawals.

### The size limits

Scaling is not infinite. Most firms have a maximum account size — often $200,000–$400,000 per account, though some advertise up to $1,000,000 or more across multiple accounts. Once the ceiling is reached, further scaling requires applying for an additional account or is simply not available.

### Scaling plans vs. multiple accounts

Some traders attempt to scale by simply purchasing and managing multiple funded accounts simultaneously. Firms' policies on this vary: some explicitly permit it, some cap the total funded capital per trader (e.g., maximum $300,000 across all accounts), and some prohibit it entirely in their terms of service. Check the policy before running multiple accounts.

### What to watch for

- **Automatic vs. manual** — some firms scale automatically when conditions are met; others require the trader to submit a request and wait for approval.
- **Rule reset on scaling** — confirm whether the drawdown limit resets to the new account size or remains tied to the original. This matters significantly if the trailing drawdown model is used.
- **Fee on scaling** — most firms do not charge a fee to scale, but some charge an administrative fee or require a subscription plan upgrade.

### Why it matters for a trader

A scaling plan changes the long-term economics of prop trading substantially. A trader who starts at $50,000 and scales to $200,000 over a year — with the same strategy and the same percentage returns — earns four times the absolute income. Understanding the specific triggers, timelines, and caps of a firm's scaling plan is part of evaluating whether that firm's total offer matches a trader's growth ambitions.
