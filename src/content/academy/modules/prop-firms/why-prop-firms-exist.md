## In plain words

Prop firms exist because talented traders and large pools of capital are two things that rarely sit in the same place. A firm can earn money by identifying skilled traders, funding them at scale, and taking a slice of the upside — while limiting its downside through strict risk rules. The evaluation fee and the drawdown limits are not arbitrary hurdles; they are the firm's core risk-management tools.

## Quick demo

A firm charges $500 for a $100,000 challenge. Of every 100 traders who attempt it, perhaps 10 pass and get funded. That's $50,000 in fees collected. If those 10 funded traders collectively generate $20,000 in profits over the next month and the firm keeps a 20% cut, it earns another $4,000 — on top of the fees. Meanwhile, the firm's worst-case loss per trader is capped by the drawdown rule, say 10%, or $10,000 max.

## Full explanation

### The problem prop firms solve

Two groups exist in financial markets who need each other:

- **Skilled retail traders** who have a consistent edge but limited capital — a $5,000 personal account cannot generate meaningful income even with a 10% monthly return.
- **Capital owners** (the prop firms) who have large pools of money but need reliable operators to deploy it without blowing it up.

Prop firms create a marketplace that connects these two groups, using the evaluation process as a filter.

### The business model — three revenue streams

Most retail prop firms operate on one or more of the following:

1. **Evaluation fees** — the primary and most reliable revenue source. Fees are collected upfront, whether or not the trader passes. With thousands of applicants, this alone can be substantial.
2. **Profit splits on funded accounts** — if funded traders generate returns, the firm captures its percentage. This is less predictable than fees but scales with good trader performance.
3. **Subscription or monthly account fees** — some firms charge an ongoing fee to maintain a funded account, which provides recurring revenue.

### How risk is managed

The firm is exposed to funded traders losing money. Its core protection is the **drawdown rule**: if a trader's account drops by a set percentage, the account is closed immediately. This hard cap on loss is what makes the model viable — the firm is not betting on every trader succeeding, only on the aggregate.

### A note on transparency

The prop-firm industry is not uniformly regulated. Firms vary significantly in how they actually deploy capital, how reliably they pay out, and what their terms mean in practice. Some firms have faced criticism for opaque payout processes or rule structures designed to be difficult to meet. Checking independent trader reviews and regulatory registrations before choosing a firm is important.

### Why it matters for a trader

Understanding the business model tells you what the firm actually wants: traders who are consistent and rule-compliant, not traders who swing for enormous gains. The rules are not there to trick you — they define the risk tolerance the firm can operationally sustain. Aligning your trading style with that reality is the first step to a productive relationship with any prop firm.
