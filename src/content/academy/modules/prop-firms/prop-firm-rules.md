## In plain words

Every prop firm account — whether in the evaluation or the funded phase — operates under a set of hard rules. The three that matter most are the **overall drawdown limit** (the maximum you can lose from the account before it is closed), the **daily loss limit** (the maximum you can lose in a single day), and the **profit target** (the minimum gain required to pass the evaluation). Breaching any of them typically terminates the account immediately.

## Quick demo

You are on a $100,000 challenge with a 10% overall drawdown limit ($10,000), a 5% daily loss limit ($5,000), and an 8% profit target ($8,000). One morning the market moves against you sharply. By midday you are down $4,800 on the day. You have $200 of daily limit remaining. Even though your overall account is healthy, you must stop trading for the day — or risk breaching the daily limit and losing the entire challenge, regardless of your overall equity.

## Full explanation

### The three core rules

#### 1. Overall (maximum) drawdown

This is the percentage or dollar amount the account can fall from its starting balance — or from its peak — before the account is closed.

- **Static drawdown** — measured from the original starting balance only. If you start at $100,000 and the limit is 10%, the account closes if balance hits $90,000 at any point, regardless of whether you were up $5,000 yesterday.
- **Trailing drawdown** — the limit follows the highest balance the account has ever reached. If you grow to $105,000, the floor rises to $95,000. This is stricter: a drawdown limit that keeps moving up is harder to stay above during a pullback.

Understanding which type a firm uses is critical, because trailing drawdown can close an account even when equity is still above the starting balance.

#### 2. Daily loss limit

The maximum a trader can lose in a single calendar or trading day. Once hit, all positions must be closed and no new trades may be opened until the next trading session. This rule:

- Prevents revenge trading after a bad session.
- Stops a single catastrophic day from wiping out the entire challenge.
- Is calculated differently by different firms — some use end-of-day equity, some monitor intraday floating P&L in real time.

#### 3. Profit target

Applies during the evaluation phase, not in the ongoing funded phase. The target is the minimum total gain required to pass and advance. It sets the floor on ambition: you cannot stay in the evaluation indefinitely making tiny gains to avoid triggering any limits.

### Other common rules

Beyond the three core rules, firms commonly add:

- **Minimum trading days** — prevents a lucky single-trade pass; demonstrates consistency.
- **Maximum position size** — limits leverage per trade or in aggregate.
- **News trading restrictions** — some firms prohibit opening or holding positions during major scheduled news events (NFP, CPI, Fed meetings).
- **Prohibited instruments** — certain highly volatile instruments may be excluded.
- **Consistency rules** — some firms flag accounts where a disproportionate share of profits came from a single day, and require more balanced performance.

### Why the rules are designed this way

Each rule addresses a specific failure mode that would damage the firm's capital. The daily loss limit stops one bad session from compounding. The drawdown limit stops an overall losing streak from going too deep. Together they define a risk envelope that the firm can operate within — and that, if internalized by the trader, actually protects the trader from their own worst decisions.

### Why it matters for a trader

Memorizing the rules is not enough. A trader needs to know their own worst-case daily swings and make sure their normal trading behavior stays comfortably inside the limits — not right against them. A strategy that averages $300 a day with occasional $400 down days looks fine against a $500 daily limit on paper but leaves almost no buffer for bad periods. Building in a personal cushion below the firm's stated limits is standard practice among experienced prop traders.
