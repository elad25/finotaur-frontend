## In plain words

There are two main paths to a funded prop-firm account. The **challenge model** requires you to prove your trading skills over days or weeks on a practice account before receiving real funding — lower fee, but a gate to pass. **Instant funding** skips the evaluation: you pay a higher upfront fee and receive immediate access to a funded account. Both lead to the same destination; they differ in cost, speed, and the ongoing conditions attached.

## Quick demo

Consider a $100,000 account at a typical firm. The challenge path costs $500, takes up to 30 days to pass two phases, and awards an 80% split if you succeed. The instant-funding path at the same firm costs $2,000 but grants access to the $100,000 account immediately — though with a tighter daily loss limit (2% instead of 5%) and a lower initial profit split of 60% (rising to 80% after the first payout). The faster path costs more upfront and imposes stricter ongoing constraints.

## Full explanation

### The challenge (evaluation) model

The challenge is the original and still-dominant prop-firm structure.

**How it works:**
- Pay an evaluation fee (typically $100–$700 depending on account size).
- Trade a simulated account while hitting a profit target and staying within defined loss limits.
- Pass one or two phases of evaluation, then advance to a funded account.

**Advantages:**
- Lower upfront cost to access large capital.
- The evaluation period itself tests whether your strategy is actually ready.
- Widely available across dozens of firms with competitive pricing.

**Disadvantages:**
- Time investment — passing a two-phase challenge can take weeks.
- Failure means paying again (or using a reset fee, which is cheaper but still a cost).
- Psychological pressure during the evaluation often causes traders to behave differently than they would in a funded account.

### The instant funding model

Instant funding (sometimes called "straight-to-funded" or "direct funding") was popularized in the early 2020s as a faster alternative.

**How it works:**
- Pay a larger upfront fee (often 3–5x the challenge fee for the same account size).
- Immediately receive access to a funded account — no evaluation phase.
- Begin trading and earning immediately; rules apply from day one.

**Advantages:**
- No evaluation period — income-eligible from the first trade.
- Removes the psychological distortion of "challenge mode" trading.
- Often offered with more flexible time restrictions.

**Disadvantages:**
- Significantly higher upfront cost — capital-intensive before any income is earned.
- Rules during the funded phase are often tighter, reflecting the firm's higher risk (no prior vetting of the trader).
- The profit split may start lower and require proof of consistency to unlock higher percentages.
- Not universally available; fewer firms offer this model than the challenge model.

### Which model fits which trader

| Situation | Likely better fit |
|---|---|
| Limited capital, willing to prove skill first | Challenge model |
| Consistent profitability already demonstrated | Either, evaluate cost vs. speed |
| Wants income-generating potential immediately | Instant funding |
| New to disciplined rule-based trading | Challenge model (evaluation forces discipline) |
| Has failed multiple challenges due to evaluation psychology | Instant funding worth considering |

### Why it matters for a trader

The choice is fundamentally a trade-off between upfront cost and time-to-income. Neither model is universally superior — it depends on a trader's available capital, confidence in their edge, and tolerance for evaluation risk. Comparing the total cost (fees paid on average attempts to pass), the ongoing rules, and the profit split side by side across both models at any given firm is the most reliable way to make the decision.
