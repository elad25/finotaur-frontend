## In plain words

**Dollar-cost averaging (DCA)** means investing a fixed amount of money at regular intervals — say, $200 every month — regardless of whether the market is up, down, or sideways. Because you invest the same dollar amount each time, you automatically buy more shares when prices are low and fewer shares when prices are high. The alternative is **lump-sum investing**: putting all available money in at once, which performs better on average but is psychologically harder and requires good timing.

## Quick demo

You invest $300 per month for three months in a stock that moves around:

| Month | Price | Shares bought |
|-------|-------|---------------|
| 1     | $30   | 10.0          |
| 2     | $15   | 20.0          |
| 3     | $25   | 12.0          |

Total invested: $900. Total shares: 42. Average cost per share: $21.43. The average market price over those three months was $23.33. DCA produced a lower average cost simply because it bought more shares when the price dipped — no market timing required.

## Full explanation

### How DCA works

DCA is a systematic buying strategy, not a market-timing strategy. The fixed-dollar rule is what gives it its property: the same dollar amount buys different quantities depending on price. In a declining market, each installment buys more shares. In a rising market, each installment buys fewer. Over time, this mechanical process produces an average cost per share that is lower than the simple average of the prices paid.

The formal term for this is the **harmonic mean of prices** — which is always less than or equal to the arithmetic mean, provided prices vary. DCA exploits this mathematical property automatically.

### DCA vs. lump-sum investing

Research consistently shows that **lump-sum investing outperforms DCA in approximately two-thirds of historical periods**, because markets go up more often than they go down. If you have a $12,000 windfall, investing it all in January produces better average returns than spreading it over 12 months.

However, DCA has meaningful advantages despite this:

- **Behavioral**: most people do not have a lump sum — they earn income over time. DCA matches the reality of how money is accumulated.
- **Regret reduction**: committing a lump sum at a market peak produces deep regret. DCA reduces both the risk and the psychological sting of bad timing.
- **Volatility capture**: in sideways or declining markets followed by recovery, DCA outperforms lump-sum because it accumulates shares cheaply before the recovery.

### When DCA makes the most sense

DCA is the natural structure for:

- **Regular income**: contributing a percentage of each paycheck to a retirement account is DCA by design.
- **New investors**: building a position in a new asset class without having to guess the entry point.
- **Volatile assets**: crypto, small-caps, and other high-volatility assets benefit more from DCA than stable assets, because the price variation is larger — and DCA's advantage grows with volatility.
- **Periods of uncertainty**: when macro conditions are unclear and the risk of a poor lump-sum entry is elevated.

### Common misconceptions

**"DCA guarantees profits"**: no — if an asset is in a permanent downtrend (a company going bankrupt, for example), DCA accelerates losses. DCA works best in assets that are volatile but trend upward over the long term.

**"Spreading over 12 months is enough diversification"**: DCA is time diversification of entry price, not asset diversification. You still need to own uncorrelated assets.

### Why it matters for a trader and investor

DCA matters most to long-term investors building wealth steadily. For traders, the concept is less directly applicable — but the underlying logic is valuable: forced systematic behavior removes the paralysis of "is now the right time?" and replaces it with process. The discipline of committing regardless of market conditions trains the muscle of acting on plan rather than emotion — a habit that transfers to every part of active trading.
