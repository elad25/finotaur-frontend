## In plain words

**Rebalancing** is the act of periodically returning your portfolio to its original target allocation by selling assets that have grown too large and buying assets that have shrunk. Over time, markets move your allocation away from its intended mix — a strong stock rally might push equities from 60% to 75% of your portfolio, raising your risk beyond your original plan. Rebalancing restores the intended **risk profile** and, as a side effect, enforces a systematic buy-low, sell-high discipline.

## Quick demo

You start with a 60% stocks / 40% bonds portfolio worth $10,000: $6,000 in stocks and $4,000 in bonds. After a strong year, stocks are up 25% and bonds are up 3%. Your portfolio is now worth $11,630 — but stocks have grown to $7,500 (64.5%) and bonds to $4,120 (35.4%). To restore the 60/40 mix, you sell roughly $520 of stocks and buy $520 of bonds. You have just mechanically sold some of what went up and bought more of what lagged — the opposite of what emotion would suggest.

## Full explanation

### Why portfolios drift

Markets do not move in unison. When equities outperform bonds for several years, the equity portion naturally takes up a larger share of the total portfolio. This drift is a feature of holding different assets — but left unchecked, it silently increases risk. A target allocation is not just an aesthetic choice; it reflects a deliberate decision about how much volatility and drawdown you can tolerate and afford. Drift erodes that decision invisibly.

### Rebalancing methods

There are two main approaches, and many portfolios combine both:

**Calendar rebalancing**: rebalance on a fixed schedule — quarterly, semi-annually, or annually. Simple to implement, predictable, and avoids overtrading. The main risk is that drift grows large between scheduled dates in volatile markets.

**Threshold (bands) rebalancing**: rebalance whenever any asset class drifts beyond a set tolerance — for example, sell when stocks rise more than 5 percentage points above target. More responsive to actual drift but requires ongoing monitoring. A common setup is a 5/25 rule: rebalance if an allocation is more than 5 absolute percentage points off target, or more than 25% of its target weight.

### The return impact of rebalancing

Rebalancing does not always improve returns — in a straight-up trending market, it repeatedly trims the winner and buys the laggard, which can drag returns. Its primary job is **risk control**, not return enhancement.

That said, rebalancing can add return in **mean-reverting** or volatile, choppy markets — buying dips in an asset and selling into its rallies over time creates a structural advantage. Academic studies generally find that rebalancing adds 0.1–0.5% per year in risk-adjusted return over long periods, primarily from this effect.

### Tax and transaction cost considerations

Rebalancing in a taxable account triggers capital gains taxes on positions sold at a profit. Several techniques reduce this cost:

- **Direct new contributions** to underweight assets rather than selling overweight ones.
- **Tax-advantaged accounts** (401k, IRA, ISA) for most rebalancing activity — no tax event when you sell within the account.
- **Dividend and interest reinvestment** directed to underweight assets.
- **Harvest losses** in taxable accounts to offset gains generated by rebalancing elsewhere.

### Rebalancing as behavioral discipline

Beyond the mechanics, rebalancing enforces a counterintuitive discipline: it requires selling what feels good (recent winners) and buying what feels bad (recent underperformers). This is precisely backwards from what fear and greed push most investors to do. A systematic rebalancing rule removes the emotional decision from the equation and replaces it with process.

### Why it matters for a trader and investor

Rebalancing is one of the few portfolio management actions with a clear, evidence-backed rationale that is accessible to any investor at any account size. For long-term investors, it keeps risk within bounds and can modestly enhance risk-adjusted returns. For active traders, the principle translates into position sizing discipline — periodically trimming large winners to prevent any single position from dominating risk exposure. In both contexts, the habit of restoring balance is a practical application of the foundational idea that return and risk must always be managed together.
