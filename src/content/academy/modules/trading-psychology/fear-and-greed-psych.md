## In plain words

**Fear** and **greed** are the two emotional forces that distort nearly every trading decision. Fear makes you exit winners too early, avoid valid setups after a loss, and freeze when you should act. Greed makes you hold losers too long, size up recklessly, and chase moves that have already run. Both emotions are normal human responses — in trading, they are also your most reliable enemies.

## Quick demo

A trader buys a stock at $50 with a target of $60 and a stop at $47. At $56 the stock pauses and the trader, afraid of giving back the gain, sells for a $6 profit. The stock then runs to $62. A week later, greed takes over: after reading bullish headlines, the trader sizes three times larger than normal on a new trade — and the stop hits for a $21 loss, wiping out three previous wins. Fear and greed, taking turns, produced a net negative result from a winning strategy.

## Full explanation

### Why fear shows up

Fear in trading usually traces back to one source: **undefined or excessive risk**. When you risk more than you can emotionally afford to lose, fear becomes a physical sensation that overrides rational judgment. Your brain, designed to protect you from threats, starts treating the open trade like a predator — and the instinct is to escape.

Common fear-driven behaviors:
- **Early exits** — cutting winners before the target because the gain "feels good enough."
- **Hesitation** — missing a valid setup because the last similar one lost.
- **Moving stops tighter** — breaking your own rules to reduce the discomfort of risk.
- **Paralysis after a loss** — refusing to enter the next valid trade, missing its recovery.

### Why greed shows up

Greed arrives in two flavors: **impatience** (wanting gains faster than the market delivers them) and **overconfidence** (believing recent winners have proven permanent skill). Both cause the same mistakes: holding losers in the hope they recover, adding to losing positions, or sizing up beyond your plan after a winning streak.

Common greed-driven behaviors:
- **Holding past the target** — expecting just a little more profit, then watching the trade reverse.
- **Averaging down** — adding to a losing position to "lower the cost basis," ignoring that the thesis may simply be wrong.
- **Oversizing after wins** — treating a hot streak as proof of skill, increasing risk at exactly the wrong time.

### Managing the pendulum

Neither fear nor greed can be eliminated — they are part of the brain's architecture. The professional response is to build rules that fire *before* emotions have time to interfere:

- **Pre-set position size** based on a fixed percentage of capital (e.g., 1 % risk per trade). Size is chosen when you are calm, not when you are excited.
- **Hard targets and stops** entered at order placement, not decided mid-trade.
- **A rule against adjusting stops or targets after entry** unless price action creates an objective reason (e.g., a major level breaks).
- **A cool-down rule** — no trading for a defined period (30 minutes, rest of the day) after an emotionally charged loss.

### The broader picture

Fear and greed are not unique to weak traders. Professionals feel them too. The difference is that professionals have systems that run independent of those feelings. Their rules make the decision; they just execute. Over time, running a structured process builds a track record that genuinely reduces fear (because you have real evidence your edge works) and keeps greed in check (because the rules cap the upside you can chase).

Understanding that fear and greed are universal also builds humility: when the whole market is greedy — extreme bullishness, euphoric headlines, everyone buying — history shows that is precisely when risk is highest. And when fear peaks — capitulation, margin calls, despair — that is often when the best opportunities appear. Reading the crowd's emotions from the outside is a skill. Managing your own is what lets you act on it.
