## In plain words

**Revenge trading** is entering new trades specifically to recover a loss you just took — driven by anger or frustration rather than by a valid setup. It is the market equivalent of doubling your bet at a casino after a bad hand. The trade is not about the market; it is about your emotions, which means you are no longer trading your edge. Of all the psychological traps in trading, revenge trading is the one most likely to turn a manageable loss into an account-ending event.

## Quick demo

A trader takes a $300 loss when a setup moves against them cleanly. Instead of stepping away, they immediately jump into another position — larger this time, reasoning they need to make the $300 back fast. There is no clear setup; the entry is rushed. The second trade loses $500. Now furious, they take a third trade at twice the original size. This one loses $900. In 45 minutes, a $300 loss became a $1,700 drawdown. The original loss was fine. The revenge trades were the catastrophe.

## Full explanation

### The anatomy of a revenge trade

Revenge trading follows a reliable pattern:

1. A loss occurs — sometimes a fair stop-out, sometimes a loss due to a mistake.
2. Frustration or anger rises immediately.
3. The trader feels a compulsive need to "fix" the loss quickly.
4. A new position is entered, usually with less analysis, often with larger size.
5. The new position loses, amplifying the emotional state.
6. The cycle repeats.

The core error is that the motivation is **backward-looking** (I need to undo what just happened) rather than **forward-looking** (is there a valid setup in front of me right now?). The market has no memory of your previous trade. It will not give you your money back out of fairness.

### Why it escalates

Revenge trading is particularly dangerous because it tends to escalate in size. The urgency to recover grows with each loss, so each subsequent trade risks more. This creates a compounding disaster: the losing streak that follows a single bad trade is almost always worse than the initial loss itself.

Neuroscience explains part of this: the frustration state associated with a loss activates the brain's threat-response system, which simultaneously increases risk appetite (to "fix" the threat) and reduces prefrontal cortex function (the rational planning area). You are biologically less capable of good decision-making in the minutes immediately following an emotional loss.

### Recognizing the trigger

Revenge trading has a distinct feel that you can learn to identify before it becomes a trade:

- You are checking the charts within seconds of closing a losing position.
- You feel angry, not analytical.
- You are thinking about the dollar amount you lost, not about the next setup.
- You are considering a larger position than your plan allows.
- You are entering a market or instrument you were not watching before the loss.

Any one of these signals is a warning. Multiple signals together mean you should close the platform entirely.

### Breaking the cycle

The structural fixes are straightforward, but require pre-commitment (they cannot be imposed in the heat of the moment):

- **Hard stop after two consecutive losses.** Write this rule down. Two losses in a row = session over. No exceptions.
- **Mandatory waiting period after any loss.** A minimum of 20–30 minutes away from the screen before re-entering.
- **Size lockout after a loss.** Many traders use a rule: after a loss, the next trade must be at or below normal size — no "making it back" with a larger bet.
- **Platform closure.** Physically closing the trading platform and opening a journal or notebook is a powerful interruption. You cannot revenge trade if you cannot click.
- **Daily loss limit.** Set a maximum daily loss — a hard number you will not exceed. When it is hit, trading stops. This is not a suggestion; it is a circuit breaker.

Why this matters: revenge trading is the direct cause of the majority of account blow-ups in retail trading. The original strategy rarely destroys accounts; the emotional response to normal losses does. Protecting yourself from your own reaction to loss is not a soft skill — it is the single most important risk management tool you have.
