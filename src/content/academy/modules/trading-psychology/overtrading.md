## In plain words

**Overtrading** is taking more trades than your edge justifies — either too many in a day, too large in size, or both. It usually feels like staying active, being engaged, and working hard, but it is the opposite: each low-quality trade erodes your capital and your clarity. The market does not reward effort; it rewards selectivity. **Doing less is often the higher-skill move.**

## Quick demo

A trader's strategy produces roughly 3 solid setups per day. On a slow Tuesday, nothing clean appears by noon. Restless, the trader takes 7 more trades through the afternoon — all marginal, all forced. By the close, the trader is down $400. The 3 genuine setups that appeared the next morning would have covered that loss easily, but now the trader is in a confidence hole and too cautious to take them properly. One day of overtrading cost two days of opportunity.

## Full explanation

### What overtrading actually is

Overtrading is not just about the number of trades. It includes:

- **Frequency overtrading** — entering and exiting repeatedly with no genuine setup.
- **Size overtrading** — trading a position so large that every tick movement triggers an emotional response, leading to poor decisions.
- **Scope overtrading** — trading in too many markets simultaneously, spreading attention thin.
- **Boredom trading** — entering the market because waiting feels intolerable, not because a valid signal exists.

All four share the same root cause: the trader is seeking stimulation or control rather than executing an edge.

### Why traders overtrade

The psychological drivers are predictable:

- **Boredom and restlessness** — markets move constantly; not participating feels like missing out.
- **Recovery urgency** — after a loss, the fastest path back to even seems to be more trades.
- **Overconfidence** — after a winning streak, the trader begins to believe every intuition is valid.
- **Commission-based confusion** — the act of trading *feels* like doing something productive.

Trading platforms are also designed to encourage activity. Watchlists that blink, P&L that updates in real time, one-click execution — all of these reduce the friction between an impulse and an order.

### The cost of overtrading

Every trade costs something: spread, slippage, commissions. More importantly, low-probability trades directly reduce your **expectancy** — the average amount you make per trade. A strategy with a strong expectancy can be turned negative purely through overtrading, because the added trades are below the quality threshold the expectancy was calculated on.

There is also a mental cost. More decisions mean more decision fatigue. The trader who takes 20 trades in a session is measurably less focused on trade 20 than on trade 1. Quality degrades with volume.

### How to stop overtrading

The solution is structural, not motivational:

- **Set a maximum daily trade limit.** Three to five trades per session is sufficient for most strategies. When you hit the limit, you are done — regardless of how good the next setup looks.
- **Require written reasons before entry.** If you cannot write a clear one-sentence rationale that matches your criteria, you do not enter.
- **Create a waiting ritual.** Before entering any trade, take 60 seconds to check: does this meet every criterion? Is this boredom, or is this a genuine setup?
- **Track your trade quality separately from results.** Rate each trade 1–5 on setup quality at the time of entry. Over time, you will see that your 4s and 5s drive all the profit, and your 1s and 2s destroy it.
- **Use session reviews.** At the end of each trading day, identify trades taken outside your plan. The pattern of where you overtrade (time of day, market conditions, emotional state) becomes a signal you can act on.

Why this matters: your edge is only expressed in trades that meet your criteria. Every trade outside those criteria is a random bet that degrades your results. The discipline to sit on your hands when no valid setup exists is one of the most valuable — and underrated — skills in trading.
