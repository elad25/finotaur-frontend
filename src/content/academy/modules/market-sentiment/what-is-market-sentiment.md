## In plain words

**Market sentiment** is the collective mood of all investors at a given moment — the sum of their fear, confidence, greed, and uncertainty expressed as buying and selling decisions. When most participants feel optimistic, prices tend to rise even without new fundamental news; when fear dominates, prices can fall sharply. Sentiment is not the same as value, but it drives price in the short run.

## Quick demo

In late 2021, retail investors poured billions into growth stocks and speculative assets, convinced markets would keep climbing. The S&P 500 peaked in January 2022. There was no single piece of bad news — just a slow shift from euphoria to anxiety as the Fed signaled rate hikes. By October 2022 the index was down 25%. The fundamentals of most S&P 500 companies had not fallen 25% — but the mood of investors had changed dramatically, and prices followed the mood.

## Full explanation

### What sentiment actually is

Sentiment is the aggregate of how millions of market participants feel about the future. It lives on a spectrum from extreme fear to extreme greed, and it shifts continuously as traders digest news, data, price action, and each other's behavior.

It does not operate in a vacuum. Sentiment interacts with:

- **Fundamentals** — a great earnings report can still send a stock lower if the crowd expected even more.
- **Technicals** — a key support level breaking can flip sentiment from cautious to panicked in minutes.
- **Positioning** — how much exposure investors actually have in the market shapes how far sentiment can swing prices.

### Why sentiment diverges from value

A stock's intrinsic value changes slowly — it is driven by earnings, growth rates, and discount rates. Sentiment can change in hours. This gap is what creates both bubbles (prices far above value because optimism is extreme) and crashes (prices far below value because fear is extreme). Understanding this lag is the entire foundation of contrarian investing.

### How sentiment spreads

- **Media and social networks** — fear and excitement are contagious. A viral negative story can shift millions of small investors simultaneously.
- **Price action itself** — a falling price makes holders nervous, which creates more selling, which makes more holders nervous. This feedback loop is the mechanism behind momentum and panic.
- **Institutional flows** — when large funds reduce exposure, prices drop, which changes the mood of retail participants even before the fund's reasoning is public.

### Measuring sentiment

Traders do not guess at mood — they read it through instruments and data:

- **Volatility indexes** (VIX) — the market's priced-in expectation of future swings.
- **Put/call ratios** — whether traders are buying protection or speculating on upside.
- **Surveys** — the AAII Investor Sentiment Survey polls retail investors weekly.
- **Breadth indicators** — whether gains or losses are broad or narrow.
- **Fear & Greed indexes** — composite gauges that combine multiple signals.

### Why it matters for a trader

Ignoring sentiment is like navigating a river without knowing the current. Even if your fundamental or technical thesis is correct, you can lose money if sentiment is violently against your position in the short term. Reading sentiment tells you the environment you are trading in — trending and confident, or choppy and fearful — and it allows you to size, time, and manage trades more intelligently than those reacting on instinct.
