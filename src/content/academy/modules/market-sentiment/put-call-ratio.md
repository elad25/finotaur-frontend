## In plain words

The **put/call ratio** compares the number of put options (bets that a market or stock will fall) to the number of call options (bets it will rise) traded on a given day. A ratio above 1.0 means more puts than calls are being purchased, suggesting elevated fear or hedging. A ratio well below 1.0 means calls dominate, signaling speculative optimism. Traders use this **options positioning data** as a real-time window into collective market sentiment.

## Quick demo

In January 2021, retail traders flooded into GameStop call options, pushing the equity put/call ratio to one of the lowest readings in years — far below 0.5. The ratio was screaming that speculative greed had reached an unusual extreme. Within days of those lows, GameStop peaked above $480 and then crashed below $50 in a matter of weeks. The put/call ratio did not cause the collapse, but it accurately reflected the lopsided positioning that made a violent reversal likely.

## Full explanation

### What puts and calls reveal about positioning

- A **put option** gives the buyer the right to sell at a set price. Traders buy puts to hedge long positions or to speculate on declines.
- A **call option** gives the buyer the right to buy at a set price. Traders buy calls to speculate on gains or to gain leveraged upside exposure.

When participants collectively buy more puts, they are either more fearful or more actively hedging. When they buy more calls, they are more optimistic or more willing to chase.

### Types of put/call ratios

There are several versions, and they measure different things:

- **Equity put/call ratio** — only includes options on individual stocks. This is a cleaner read on speculative retail sentiment because retail traders account for a large share of single-stock options activity.
- **Index put/call ratio** — includes options on indexes like the S&P 500. This tilts heavily toward institutional hedging, so a high reading here can mean professional money is protecting gains rather than expecting disaster.
- **Total put/call ratio** — combines both, giving a broad market-wide view. The CBOE publishes this daily.

### How to interpret the numbers

There are no universal thresholds, but as a general guide:

- **Total ratio above 1.2–1.3** — elevated fear; historically associated with market bottoms or near-term bounces (contrarian bullish).
- **Total ratio around 0.7–1.0** — neutral zone, baseline noise.
- **Total ratio below 0.6–0.65** — speculative call-buying excess; historically associated with complacency or short-term tops (contrarian bearish).

These are not mechanical signals. A spike in the ratio can also be institutional hedging around an event (earnings, Fed meeting), which is different from genuine retail panic.

### Smoothing the signal

Day-to-day readings are noisy because volume varies. Most traders use a **10-day or 21-day moving average** of the ratio to smooth out the noise and identify genuine extremes rather than reacting to a single-day reading.

### Using it alongside other data

The put/call ratio works best in combination:

- A high put/call ratio alongside a very elevated VIX strengthens the contrarian bullish case.
- A low put/call ratio alongside a low VIX and a stretched price chart strengthens the contrarian bearish case.
- Conflicting readings (high put/call but low VIX) suggest mixed signals and reduce confidence in either direction.

### Why it matters for a trader

The put/call ratio shows you where other market participants have actually put money, not where they say they are. It is harder to fake than a survey. Extreme readings have historically corresponded to points where the crowd was most wrong — precisely when contrarian opportunities are largest. Watching it regularly gives you a habit of reading the market's positioning rather than just its price.
