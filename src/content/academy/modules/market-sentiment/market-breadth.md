## In plain words

**Market breadth** measures how widely a market move is shared across its individual components. An index like the S&P 500 can rise even when most of its 500 stocks are falling — if just a handful of very large companies rally hard enough. Breadth indicators reveal whether a rally or decline is **broad and healthy** (most stocks participating) or **narrow and fragile** (carried by a small group). Narrow moves are often warning signs; broad moves tend to be more durable.

## Quick demo

In the first half of 2023, the S&P 500 gained roughly 16% — but underneath the surface, only about 20–25 stocks (mostly AI-related mega-caps like NVIDIA and Microsoft) drove almost all of that gain. The median stock in the index was nearly flat. Breadth indicators at the time were flashing divergence: the index looked strong while most stocks were treading water. Traders who watched breadth knew the rally's foundation was thinner than the headline number suggested.

## Full explanation

### Why breadth matters

An index is a weighted average. In a market-cap-weighted index, the largest companies have the most influence. When a few giants surge, the index can hit new highs while thousands of smaller stocks languish or decline. This is not a hypothetical edge case — it is a recurring feature of late-cycle bull markets.

Breadth indicators cut through the averaging and ask: **how many stocks are actually participating?**

### Key breadth indicators

**Advance/Decline Line (A/D Line)**

The most widely watched breadth tool. Each day, subtract the number of declining stocks from the number of advancing stocks on a given exchange (typically the NYSE). Add that daily number to the previous cumulative total. The result is a running line.

- When the A/D Line trends up alongside the index → broad participation → healthy market.
- When the index makes a new high but the A/D Line does not → **bearish divergence** → a warning.
- When the A/D Line makes a new high while the index lags → breadth leading price → typically bullish.

**Percentage of stocks above their 200-day moving average**

This measures the share of stocks in the index that are in longer-term uptrends. Readings above 70–75% indicate a broad bull market. Readings below 30% indicate broad damage — conditions sometimes associated with bottoms.

**New 52-week highs vs. new 52-week lows**

If the index is at highs but the number of stocks making 52-week highs is shrinking while lows are expanding, participation is deteriorating. This divergence has historically preceded market corrections.

**Up/down volume ratio**

Compares the volume traded in advancing stocks to the volume in declining stocks on a given day. A 9:1 up-volume day (nine times more volume in advancing than declining stocks) is called a "breadth thrust" — a historically bullish signal suggesting strong underlying demand.

### Breadth in bear markets

Just as narrowing breadth can warn of a top, **broad deterioration** can confirm a bear market:

- A majority of stocks breaking below their 200-day moving averages signals systemic selling, not just rotation.
- Persistent negative A/D readings even on up days indicate that rallies are being sold into broadly.

### Common mistakes

- **Using breadth on small samples** — breadth is more reliable on large universes (NYSE, S&P 500) than on narrow ones. A sector-level breadth reading can be misleading.
- **Ignoring the timeframe** — a single day's breadth reading is noise. Trends in breadth over weeks are signal.
- **Treating divergence as a sell trigger** — breadth divergence can persist for months before price catches up. It is a warning, not a precise timing tool.

### Why it matters for a trader

Breadth tells you whether the environment supports your strategy. A broad, healthy market lifts most boats and supports momentum trades. A narrow market rewards selectivity and signals that index-level moves can be deceptive. If the breadth is deteriorating while the headline index grinds higher, you are watching the foundation crack before the ceiling — and that awareness is worth a great deal.
