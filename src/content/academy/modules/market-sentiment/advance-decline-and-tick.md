## In plain words

The **Advance/Decline line** (A/D line) and the **NYSE TICK** are two real-time internal gauges that reveal actual market participation beneath the surface of an index number. The A/D line tracks how many stocks are rising versus falling each day. The TICK measures, at any instant, how many NYSE-listed stocks just had an uptick in price versus a downtick. Together, they tell you whether the index's movement reflects genuine broad buying or selling — or whether it is being distorted by a small number of large-cap movers.

## Quick demo

Imagine the S&P 500 is up 0.4% at noon. Sounds fine. But the NYSE TICK is reading –800, meaning 800 more stocks just ticked down than ticked up — suggesting broad selling despite the green index. Checking the A/D line confirms: 1,800 stocks are declining while only 900 are advancing. A trader watching these internals recognizes the index is being lifted by a few big names while the majority of the market is actually weak. They avoid chasing the "green day" and wait for confirmation.

## Full explanation

### The Advance/Decline Line in detail

The A/D line is built by subtracting declining issues from advancing issues each day and adding the result to the prior cumulative total. It is typically calculated on the NYSE because of its large and diverse membership.

**Reading the A/D line:**

- A rising A/D line alongside a rising index confirms a healthy, broad bull market.
- A **bearish divergence** — index making new highs while the A/D line fails to confirm — suggests the rally is narrowing and may be running out of fuel.
- A **bullish divergence** — A/D line making new highs while the index lags — suggests underlying accumulation and often precedes index strength.

The A/D line is most useful for spotting these divergences over days to weeks, not for intraday trading.

### NYSE TICK — the intraday internal

The NYSE TICK is calculated every second. It counts the number of NYSE stocks that just had an **uptick** (last trade above the prior trade) minus those that just had a **downtick** (last trade below the prior trade). The result is a real-time number, typically ranging from about –1,500 to +1,500 during normal sessions.

**Key TICK zones:**

- **+600 to +1,000** — broad intraday buying pressure; institutions are putting money to work.
- **–600 to –1,000** — broad intraday selling pressure; institutional distribution or risk-off.
- **Extreme readings (+1,200 or –1,200 and beyond)** — often mark short-term intraday exhaustion or capitulation; price can reverse near these levels.

Many traders use a **TICK moving average** (e.g., a 10- or 20-period average on a 1-minute chart) to smooth the erratic second-by-second readings into a more interpretable trend.

### TICK and institutional behavior

The TICK is particularly sensitive to program trading. When large institutional orders execute simultaneously across many stocks, the TICK can spike sharply. A TICK reading of +1,000 in a matter of seconds often reflects a large basket order hitting the market — a signal that a major participant just committed capital.

### Using A/D and TICK together

- Both positive (A/D line rising, TICK positive) → genuine broad buying → supports long bias.
- A/D line positive but TICK persistently negative intraday → possible late-cycle narrowing → caution.
- Index flat or down, but TICK printing above +600 repeatedly → hidden accumulation → potential for upside reversal.
- Index up, but TICK repeatedly negative → distribution into strength → potential for reversal lower.

### Common mistakes

- **Reacting to every TICK spike** — isolated spikes are noise. Look at the average and the trend over the session, not individual prints.
- **Using TICK alone for entries** — the TICK is an environmental gauge, not a precise entry trigger. Combine it with a level, setup, or pattern.
- **Ignoring time of day** — TICK tends to be most extreme at the open and close; mid-session readings are typically quieter and therefore more meaningful when they spike.

### Why it matters for a trader

Internals are the market's vital signs. A doctor does not diagnose only from how the patient looks; they read heart rate and blood pressure. The A/D line and TICK give you the equivalent for markets — quantified signals of real participation that the headline index cannot provide on its own. Active traders who incorporate these internals consistently make fewer mistakes by not chasing moves that lack real underlying support.
