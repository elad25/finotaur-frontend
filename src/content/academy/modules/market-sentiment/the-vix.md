## In plain words

The **VIX** (officially the CBOE Volatility Index) measures how much volatility options traders expect in the S&P 500 over the next 30 days. When markets are calm and confidence is high, the VIX tends to be low — around 12–16. When fear spikes and investors rush to buy protective options, the VIX surges. Because of this relationship, traders call it the **"fear index."**

## Quick demo

In March 2020, as COVID-19 shutdowns hit the United States, the VIX closed above 82 — the highest reading in its history. That number meant options traders were pricing in potential S&P 500 moves of roughly 5% per day. Anyone who watched the VIX crossing 40, then 60, had a real-time gauge of how terrified institutional money had become — well before many retail investors fully understood what was happening.

## Full explanation

### How the VIX is calculated

The VIX is not based on any single option or past price moves. It is derived from the **implied volatility of a wide range of S&P 500 options** — calls and puts across many strike prices and two near-term expirations — and weighted to represent a 30-day forward-looking view.

The key word is "implied." The VIX does not measure what volatility has been; it measures what the options market expects it to be. It is the market pricing the cost of insurance.

### What VIX levels mean

While no level is fixed, practitioners use these rough zones:

- **Below 15** — calm, trending market; low fear, high complacency.
- **15–25** — normal to mildly elevated; moderate uncertainty.
- **25–35** — elevated stress; participants are hedging actively.
- **Above 35** — real fear; spikes here often correspond to market crises or sharp selloffs.
- **Above 50** — extreme fear; rare, associated with 2008, 2020, or acute systemic shocks.

### The VIX's inverse relationship with the S&P 500

In almost all short-term environments, the VIX moves **opposite** to the S&P 500:

- S&P 500 falls sharply → investors buy puts for protection → put prices rise → implied volatility rises → VIX rises.
- S&P 500 grinds higher steadily → fewer hedges needed → put premiums fall → VIX falls.

This inverse relationship is strong but not mechanical. Occasionally both rise together (a "confused" or bifurcated market), which itself is a signal worth noting.

### VIX as a mean-reverting instrument

Unlike stocks, volatility tends to revert to its historical average. A VIX at 70 almost always falls back toward 20 eventually — it cannot stay at panic levels indefinitely, because markets eventually stabilize. This mean-reverting property is why many traders watch for "VIX spikes" as potential entry signals for contrarian long positions.

### Derivatives on volatility

The VIX itself is not directly tradable, but several products let traders express volatility views:

- **VIX futures** — contracts on future VIX levels, with their own term structure.
- **VIX options** — calls and puts on VIX futures.
- **ETPs** (UVXY, SVXY, etc.) — leveraged or inverse volatility products; extremely dangerous due to decay and roll costs.

### Why it matters for a trader

The VIX gives you the market's real-time temperature. A VIX below 15 tells you the market is complacent — good for premium-selling strategies, but a warning to be cautious about holding naked risk. A VIX spiking above 30 tells you institutions are scrambling — moves will be larger and faster than normal, spreads widen, and mean reversion setups become attractive. Reading the VIX alongside price action is one of the fastest ways to calibrate your risk in any session.
