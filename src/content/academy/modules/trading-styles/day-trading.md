## In plain words

**Day trading** means opening and closing all positions within a single market session — you hold nothing overnight. The goal is to profit from intraday price movements using **technical analysis, order flow, and strict risk controls**. Because individual moves are small, day traders typically use higher position sizes or leverage and execute multiple trades per day.

## Quick demo

It is 9:45 AM. A biotech stock gapped up 12% at the open on positive trial data and is now pulling back to VWAP with declining volume. A day trader buys 500 shares at $24.80, targeting the morning high at $26.50, with a stop at $24.20. By 11:10 AM the stock re-tests the high; the trader sells at $26.40 for a $1.60 gain per share ($800 total). By 3:30 PM all positions are flat. No overnight risk is carried.

## Full explanation

### The mechanics

Day traders operate within a single session, using intraday charts (1-minute to 15-minute) and real-time data. Key elements:

- **Speed** — entries and exits happen in seconds to minutes. Execution quality matters; slippage costs add up across dozens of trades.
- **Leverage** — many brokers offer day traders intraday buying power of 4:1 (in the US, under the PDT rule, accounts with $25,000+ can use this). Leverage amplifies both gains and losses.
- **High frequency** — a day trader may take 3 to 20 trades per day, each with a defined setup and stop.

### Common day trading setups

- **Opening-range breakout** — the first 15–30 minutes establish a range; a break above or below it triggers the trade.
- **VWAP reclaim or rejection** — price bouncing off or failing at the VWAP (Volume-Weighted Average Price) is a widely-used intraday signal.
- **Momentum plays** — stocks in play (high relative volume, news catalyst, large gap) that trend cleanly in one direction for part of the day.
- **Scalps at levels** — rapid trades at specific support or resistance levels, holding for minutes or seconds.

### The PDT rule (US-specific)

In the United States, traders with less than $25,000 in a margin account are classified as **Pattern Day Traders** after making four or more day trades in five business days. They are restricted from day trading until the account is above the threshold. This rule does not apply to cash accounts or non-US brokers.

### The real difficulty

Day trading has a high failure rate among retail participants. The documented reasons:

- **Transaction costs** — spreads, commissions, and slippage compound across hundreds of trades. A trader who is marginally profitable before costs may be negative after.
- **Cognitive load** — real-time decision-making under financial pressure degrades judgment. Tilt (emotional trading after a loss) is especially damaging intraday.
- **Competition** — intraday price movements often reflect high-frequency algorithms, professional prop traders, and market makers who have structural advantages in speed and data.
- **Survivorship bias** — the successful day traders who write books or post returns are not representative of the distribution.

### Minimum requirements

To have a realistic chance:
- A funded account (ideally $25,000+ in the US)
- Fast, reliable execution platform
- Real-time level 2 data and a quality news feed
- A tested, rule-based strategy with documented edge
- Strict daily loss limits (e.g., stop trading after losing 2% in a day)

### Why it matters for a trader

Even if you do not become a day trader, understanding intraday dynamics teaches you how price actually moves — who is on the other side of a trade, how liquidity shifts through the session, and how institutional participation shapes patterns. This knowledge improves decision-making in any time frame.
