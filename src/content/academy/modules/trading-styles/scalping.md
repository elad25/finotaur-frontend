## In plain words

**Scalping** is the fastest active trading style: you enter a position, take a small profit — sometimes just a few cents or ticks — and exit, often within seconds to a few minutes. You repeat this process many times per session. The goal is not one large winner but a high volume of **small, consistent edges** that accumulate over dozens or hundreds of trades.

## Quick demo

A futures scalper is watching the S&P 500 E-mini contract (ES). The bid is 5,412.25 and the offer is 5,412.50. She notices a large limit order sitting at 5,412.00 absorbing selling pressure. She buys one contract at 5,412.50 targeting 5,413.50 (one full point, worth $50 on ES) with a stop at 5,412.00 ($25 risk). The price ticks up in 40 seconds; she exits at 5,413.25 for a $37.50 gain. She repeats this 15 times over the morning session.

## Full explanation

### How scalping works

Scalpers exploit very short-term price inefficiencies — small supply-demand imbalances that last seconds or minutes before the market corrects. The math is different from other styles:

- **High win rate** — scalpers often target 60–70%+ win rates with small average wins.
- **Tiny risk per trade** — stops are tight, sometimes just 2–5 ticks from entry.
- **Volume** — profitability depends on executing many trades; a single trade's outcome is nearly irrelevant.

### What scalpers trade

The best instruments for scalping share common characteristics: high liquidity, tight bid-ask spreads, and predictable intraday patterns.

- **Index futures** (ES, NQ, MES, MNQ) — deep order books, liquid 24/5.
- **Forex majors** (EUR/USD, GBP/USD) — extremely tight spreads, constant volume.
- **Large-cap stocks with high float** — AAPL, TSLA, SPY — enough liquidity to enter and exit without moving the price.
- **Crypto spot or perpetual futures** — liquid pairs like BTC/USDT on deep exchanges.

### Tools scalpers use

- **Level 2 / order book** — to see where large orders are stacked and where price is likely to stall or bounce.
- **Time and sales (tape)** — to track the actual sequence of executed trades and detect aggressive buying or selling.
- **VWAP** — the intraday fair value; scalpers often fade extensions away from VWAP or buy reclaims of it.
- **Footprint charts** — candles that show buy-side vs. sell-side volume at each price level.

### The cost problem

Scalping is uniquely sensitive to transaction costs. Consider:

- A scalp targeting $0.10 per share on a stock with a $0.01 spread costs $0.01 each way — a 20% round-trip cost overhead.
- If commissions add another $0.01 round-trip per share, the net target is now $0.08, requiring even more precision.

This is why professional scalpers use direct market access (DMA) brokers with low per-share or per-contract commissions, and why scalping a wide-spread instrument is often unprofitable by construction.

### Mental demands

Scalping requires extraordinary focus and discipline. Mistakes compound fast:
- **Revenge trading** — doubling size after a loss in an attempt to recover immediately.
- **Moving stops** — widening a stop because you "feel" it will come back, turning a scalp into an unintended overnight hold.
- **Overtrading** — executing setups that do not meet the defined criteria because of boredom or eagerness.

Most scalpers define a maximum daily loss (e.g., $200 or 1% of account) and stop for the day the moment it is hit.

### Why it matters for a trader

Scalping teaches execution precision, cost awareness, and the discipline of rule-following under real-time pressure. Even traders who do not scalp benefit from understanding how scalpers behave — they are a significant part of intraday liquidity, and knowing how they enter and exit helps you read price at key levels.
