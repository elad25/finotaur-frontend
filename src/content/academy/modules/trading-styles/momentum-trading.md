## In plain words

**Momentum trading** is based on one observation: assets that have been rising recently tend to keep rising for a while, and assets that have been falling tend to keep falling. A momentum trader **buys strength and sells weakness**, entering after a move has already started and exiting before it exhausts. The edge is not prediction — it is participation in an already-moving market.

## Quick demo

A semiconductor stock reports earnings, beats by 20%, and jumps 15% in a single day on the highest volume in six months. A momentum trader does not buy before the announcement; they buy the stock the next morning after it holds gains and continues higher, adding to a position that is already working. They hold as long as the stock keeps making new short-term highs, then sell into the first sign of distribution — a high-volume down day or a close below the 10-day moving average. The key: they were never trying to predict the earnings; they followed the evidence of strength.

## Full explanation

### The academic foundation

Momentum is one of the best-documented anomalies in financial markets. Academic research going back to Jegadeesh and Titman (1993) found that stocks with strong 3–12 month returns outperformed over the subsequent 3–12 months. This "cross-sectional momentum" has been replicated across asset classes including equities, bonds, currencies, and commodities.

The likely explanations include:
- **Investor under-reaction** — investors initially underestimate the significance of positive news, causing a slow drift higher.
- **Herding** — as more investors notice the trend, capital flows in, extending the move.
- **Index rebalancing** — rising stocks gain more weight in indices, forcing passive fund buying.

### Types of momentum

- **Cross-sectional momentum** — ranking assets relative to each other. Buy the top 20% of performers; sell short the bottom 20%. Used by systematic funds.
- **Time-series momentum** — an asset compared only to its own history. If it is above its 200-day average, it is in an uptrend; if below, a downtrend. Used by trend-following funds.
- **Earnings momentum** — stocks with accelerating earnings growth or positive analyst revisions tend to continue outperforming.
- **Price momentum** — pure price-based, often combined with relative strength (RS) rankings as popularized by William O'Neil's CANSLIM system.

### Practical entry criteria

Momentum traders look for:
- **Relative strength** — the stock or asset is outperforming its sector, its index, or peer group.
- **Catalyst** — a fundamental reason for the move (earnings, product launch, macro shift) that could attract further buyers.
- **Technical confirmation** — the asset is breaking to new highs, holding above key moving averages, or bouncing from support in a strong trend.
- **Volume** — high-volume moves signal genuine institutional participation, not a thin-market spike.

### The risks of momentum

- **Crashes fast** — momentum stocks that reverse do so violently. A stock up 100% in three months can give back 50% in two weeks if sentiment shifts.
- **Crowding** — widely followed momentum names can become dangerously over-owned. When everyone decides to sell, liquidity dries up and the drop is severe.
- **Late-cycle danger** — buying a stock that has already risen 200% means you are buying near the top of many investors' gains. The next catalyst to disappoint triggers mass exits.
- **Requires active management** — momentum is not a buy-and-forget strategy. Losing positions must be cut quickly; the whole thesis is that strength begets strength, so weakness is a sell signal.

### Why it matters for a trader

Momentum is probably the most naturally profitable instinct a trader can develop — but it must be disciplined. The untrained version is chasing, entering after a large move with no defined exit. The trained version uses systematic criteria, defined risk, and an exit plan. Understanding momentum also explains a lot of observed market behavior: why certain sectors dominate for years, why earnings surprises continue to compound, and why the market rarely stops at "fair value."
