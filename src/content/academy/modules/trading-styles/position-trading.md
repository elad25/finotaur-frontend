## In plain words

**Position trading** means holding a trade for weeks to months — sometimes longer — to capture a large, sustained directional move in a market. Unlike buy-and-hold, position traders actively select entries and exits based on technical and macro analysis. Unlike swing traders, they are not targeting a single multi-day bounce; they are riding a **structural trend** and staying in until the trend shows signs of ending.

## Quick demo

In early 2023, crude oil breaks out of a three-month base and begins trending higher on supply cuts and recovering global demand. A position trader buys a crude oil ETF at $72, places a wide stop at $64, and targets a measured move to $90 based on the prior trend structure. Over the next four months, price grinds to $88. The trader exits as the trend loses momentum, booking a $16 gain while tolerating multiple $3–5 pullbacks along the way without exiting.

## Full explanation

### Distinguishing position trading from related styles

| Feature | Swing Trading | Position Trading | Buy & Hold |
|---|---|---|---|
| Hold period | Days to weeks | Weeks to months | Years |
| Analysis basis | Technical-primary | Technical + macro | Fundamental-primary |
| Active management | Yes | Yes | Minimal |
| Number of trades | High | Medium | Low |

Position traders use a blend of technical trend identification (higher highs and higher lows, moving average structure, trend channel) with macro context (sector rotation, commodity cycle, central bank policy) to confirm that the trend has staying power.

### How position traders find setups

- **Trend identification** — they look for markets making sustained new highs or lows across a weekly or monthly chart, not just a daily spike.
- **Macro tailwind** — a currency trade backed by a central bank divergence thesis, or a commodity long backed by a supply deficit, gives the trend a fundamental engine that can persist.
- **Technical entry** — even with a multi-month thesis, entry timing matters. Most position traders buy pullbacks to a rising moving average (e.g., the 20-week or 50-day EMA) rather than chasing new highs.
- **Wide stops** — a position held for months must absorb normal volatility. Stops are placed below major structural support, not at 1–2% below entry.

### Risk management in position trading

Because stops are wide, position size must be smaller to keep dollar risk at an acceptable level. A trader risking 5% from entry to stop on a large-cap stock would size down to risk only 1–2% of their account on that single trade.

Key risks:
- **Trend reversal** — a macro regime change (unexpected rate hike, geopolitical shock) can terminate a trend quickly. Position traders must define in advance what constitutes a trend break.
- **Drawdown tolerance** — a 3-week pullback inside a 4-month trend can feel like the trend is over, leading traders to exit early and miss the continuation.
- **News events** — earnings, central bank decisions, and geopolitical events can gap price past a stop, especially in individual stocks or concentrated positions.

### The role of patience

Position trading's edge is patience. Most market participants cannot hold a winner for four months while it oscillates. Those who can — and who entered with a valid thesis and disciplined risk management — capture the majority of a trend's value while shorter-term traders are shaken in and out repeatedly.

### Why it matters for a trader

Position trading is a useful bridge between active trading and long-term investing. It forces you to think at a higher time frame, to distinguish between noise and signal, and to define a thesis with a falsifiable exit condition. These habits improve decision-making across all styles.
