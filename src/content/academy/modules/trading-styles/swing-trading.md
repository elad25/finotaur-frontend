## In plain words

**Swing trading** means holding a position for several days to a few weeks, aiming to capture a single meaningful price "swing" — a move from one turning point to the next. You are not trying to catch every tick, and you are not waiting years for a thesis to play out. Your edge sits in **reading short-to-medium-term momentum** and exiting before the move reverses.

## Quick demo

A stock has been consolidating between $48 and $52 for three weeks. On Monday it breaks above $52 with strong volume. A swing trader buys at $52.50, places a stop at $50 (below the prior range), and targets $58 based on a measured move. Four days later the stock hits $58. The trade is closed for an $5.50 gain — roughly a 10% return in under a week — and the trader starts looking for the next setup.

## Full explanation

### How swing trading works

Swing traders sit between day traders and long-term investors on the time spectrum. They typically:

- Hold from 2 days to 6 weeks per position.
- Use **technical analysis** (chart patterns, moving averages, support/resistance) as the primary entry and exit tool.
- Often layer in a **catalyst** — earnings, a sector rotation, a macro event — to explain why the move may happen now.
- Close positions before fundamental thesis plays out fully; they want the swing, not the entire move.

### Common setups

- **Breakout from consolidation** — price compresses into a range, then breaks out on volume. The swing trader buys the breakout or the pullback to the breakout level.
- **Pullback in a trend** — a stock trending higher dips to a support level (moving average, prior resistance turned support). The swing trader buys the dip and sells the next push toward the prior high.
- **Earnings continuation** — a strong earnings beat sends a stock up 8%. The swing trader buys the gap-and-go or the first constructive consolidation after the gap.

### Time commitment

Swing trading does not require watching screens all day. Most swing traders:

- Spend 30–90 minutes per day reviewing charts and adjusting orders.
- Set limit orders, stop losses, and alerts, then step away.
- Review positions in the evening and plan for the next day.

This makes it compatible with a full-time job, unlike day trading.

### Risk considerations

- **Gap risk** — a stock held overnight can gap sharply on news. Stop losses do not protect against gaps; position size does.
- **Sequence of losses** — a string of failed setups that each cost 1–2% adds up. Swing traders need a positive expectancy to survive long drawdown runs.
- **Whipsaws** — in choppy, low-trend markets, breakouts fail frequently. The style works best when a directional bias is present in the broader market.

### Position sizing and risk management

Most swing traders risk 0.5–1.5% of their account on any single trade. With a clear stop, the position size is calculated from that fixed dollar risk divided by the distance to the stop. This keeps individual losses manageable regardless of the trade's profit potential.

### Why it matters for a trader

Swing trading is one of the most accessible active styles because it does not demand real-time execution or ultra-fast infrastructure. It forces discipline around entry criteria, stop placement, and target definition — skills that transfer to any other style. It also exposes you to the core skill of reading price structure, which has value whether you end up as a long-term investor or a day trader.
