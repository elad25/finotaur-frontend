## In plain words

**Trend following** is a systematic style that buys markets that are trending up and sells (or holds cash in) markets that are trending down — and keeps doing so until the trend reverses. Unlike momentum trading, which often focuses on short bursts of strength, trend following aims to **stay in a winning trade for as long as the trend persists**, sometimes for months or years. The philosophy: cut losers quickly, let winners run indefinitely.

## Quick demo

A systematic trend follower applies a simple rule: buy any market when its 50-day moving average crosses above its 200-day moving average (a "golden cross"), and sell when the 200-day crosses back above the 50-day (a "death cross"). In 2022, this rule kept the system short or flat on equities from January to October — avoiding most of a 25% drawdown — while generating strong gains in long commodity and short bond positions that were trending clearly. The year was excellent not because any individual call was brilliant, but because the system stuck to the rule and let each trend play out.

## Full explanation

### The philosophy

Trend following is arguably the oldest systematic trading strategy in existence. The core belief is that markets are driven by behavioral and economic forces that persist over time — supply/demand imbalances, policy cycles, investor herding — and that these forces create trends that can be exploited with simple, rules-based systems.

The approach is:
1. **Define a signal** — a rule that identifies when a market is in an uptrend or downtrend.
2. **Enter when the signal triggers** — without trying to predict how far the move will go.
3. **Hold until the signal reverses** — tolerating pullbacks within the trend without exiting.
4. **Accept that most trades will be small losers** — the edge comes from the few large winners that pay for everything else.

### Common trend signals

- **Moving average crossovers** — the 50-day crosses above the 200-day (bullish) or below it (bearish).
- **Breakout from a range** — price closes above a 52-week high, signaling a new bullish trend.
- **Channel breakout** — the Donchian channel system buys when price makes a new N-day high and sells at a new N-day low.
- **ATR-based trailing stop** — a stop set at a multiple of the Average True Range below the highest close, which rises with the price and locks in gains as the trend extends.

### Where trend following works best

Trend following performs best in markets with clear directional moves:
- **Commodities** — long supply/demand cycles create multi-year trends.
- **Currencies** — monetary policy divergence creates extended trends in forex pairs.
- **Fixed income** — interest rate cycles move over years, not days.
- **Equities** — sector rotation and bull/bear market cycles.

It performs worst in choppy, range-bound markets where signals trigger frequently but reverse before a trend develops, generating a sequence of small losses.

### The return profile

Trend following has a distinctive return profile that sets it apart from most strategies:

- **Many small losses** — the majority of trades will be entered at the start of a potential trend that quickly fails. Each loss is small because the exit rule is defined.
- **Few large winners** — when a real trend develops, the system stays in. The largest wins can be 10–30 times larger than the average loss.
- **Positive skew** — the distribution of trade returns is skewed toward large positive outliers, which is the opposite of most mean-reversion and premium-selling strategies.
- **Low correlation to equities** — trend following often does well in crisis periods (2008, 2022) when equities fall sharply, because it can go short falling markets.

### Why traders fail at trend following

- **Giving up after a losing period** — trend following can underperform for 12–24 months during choppy markets. Most traders abandon it just before markets enter a trending regime.
- **Overriding signals** — letting personal views ("this must be a fake breakout") override the rule defeats the entire purpose.
- **Position sizes too large** — the frequent small losses feel painful at large size, even though the math works over time at appropriate size.

### Why it matters for a trader

Whether you adopt trend following as your primary style or not, the concept of "letting winners run" is universal. Many traders are good at identifying a setup but exit winners too early out of fear, only to watch the trend continue for weeks or months. Trend following makes the discipline of holding explicit, mechanical, and learnable.
