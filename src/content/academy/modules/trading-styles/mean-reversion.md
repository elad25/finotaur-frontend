## In plain words

**Mean reversion** is based on the idea that prices tend to return to an average level after moving too far in one direction. When a stock or market gets unusually stretched — either overbought above its average or oversold below it — a mean reversion trader bets that it will **snap back toward normal**. It is the opposite of momentum trading: you sell strength and buy weakness, expecting extremes to correct.

## Quick demo

A large-cap stock closes 4.5% below its 20-day moving average — a rare extreme in its historical distribution. Scanning shows that over the past two years, every time it fell more than 4% below its 20-day average, it recovered to the average within 5 trading days 78% of the time. A mean reversion trader buys a small position that afternoon, targeting the 20-day average (roughly 4% higher) as the exit, with a stop at 7% below the average. The stock rebounds over three days; the trader exits near the moving average for a 3.8% gain.

## Full explanation

### Why mean reversion exists

Markets exhibit mean-reverting behavior for two interconnected reasons:

- **Overreaction** — human investors tend to overreact to news. Bad news sends prices below fundamental value; good news sends them above. As calmer assessment takes over, prices correct.
- **Supply and demand mechanics** — extremely low prices attract value buyers; extremely high prices attract sellers and short sellers. These flows push prices back toward equilibrium.

Mean reversion works differently at different time scales. Very short-term (intraday to a few days), it reflects statistical noise and overreaction. Over years, fundamental value anchors price.

### Common mean reversion signals

- **Bollinger Band extremes** — when price touches or breaks the outer Bollinger Band (typically 2 standard deviations from a 20-day moving average), it is statistically stretched. Traders look for price to return to the middle band.
- **RSI extremes** — an RSI reading below 30 (oversold) or above 70 (overbought) signals a stretched move that may reverse.
- **Z-score deviation** — systematic traders compute how many standard deviations price is from its rolling mean. A z-score of 2 or more triggers a reversion trade.
- **Pairs trading** — two historically correlated instruments (e.g., Coke and Pepsi, gold and silver) diverge in relative price. The trade is to buy the laggard and sell the leader, expecting the spread to close.

### Mean reversion in practice

The style is common in:

- **Equity statistical arbitrage** — quantitative hedge funds running pairs or factor-neutral portfolios.
- **Index rebalancing plays** — stocks added to an index spike; stocks removed drop. Mean reversion traders fade those moves.
- **Options selling** — implied volatility is mean-reverting. Options sellers (premium sellers) exploit high-IV environments expecting volatility to normalize, which is conceptually a mean reversion bet.
- **Fixed income** — yield spreads between similar bonds tend to revert after dislocations.

### The central risk: trending markets

Mean reversion strategies fail catastrophically in trending markets. If a stock falls 4% below its 20-day average and then keeps falling — because of genuine fundamental deterioration, a macro shock, or a structural change in the business — the reversion never comes. The "cheap" stock gets cheaper.

This is the core tension: the more extreme the deviation, the more a reversion trader wants to buy, but the more extreme the deviation, the more likely something fundamental has changed. This is why mean reversion traders:

- Use tight stops to limit losses on positions that keep moving against them.
- Avoid trading stocks with recent negative fundamental catalysts (earnings misses, guidance cuts, accounting fraud).
- Prefer liquid, diversified instruments (indices, ETFs) where the underlying does not go to zero.

### Why it matters for a trader

Mean reversion thinking is essential even for traders who do not use it as a primary style. It explains why parabolic moves tend to correct, why oversold markets often bounce, and why chasing a stock up 40% in a week usually ends badly. Understanding when a market is stretched — and when it is not — improves timing across every other style.
